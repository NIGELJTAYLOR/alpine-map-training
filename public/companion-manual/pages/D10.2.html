Virtual Day on a Tour

Page 29 / 29 D10.2 Learning aim set

Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to follow a six-leg route across a 1:25,000 alpine map and produce a sustained narrative of the planning thinking, the instrument use at each leg, the decision points and escape routes, and the response to one "what if" scenario per leg. This is the closing exercise of Level 3 and the bridge into the next phase of the IMS / EMS pathway.

Why this matters

The virtual day on a tour is the closest the workbook gets to a full assessment-style narration. It integrates:

  • the route card (D9) as the planning artefact,
  • the compass and altimeter (D1 to D7) as the navigation instruments,
  • the barometer (D6) as the weather indicator,
  • the poor-visibility procedures (D8) as the contingency response,
  • the leadership communication patterns (D8.3) as the working voice.

The IMS / EMS assessor's most demanding test is to ask the candidate to lead a tour while narrating each decision. D10.2 is the rehearsal, on paper, with the trainer alongside.

How to use this page

Working materials: the Section D10.2 map extract (provided by your trainer; 1:25,000, 10 m contours), with a six-leg route pre-marked. The route should span the kinds of terrain the candidate met in Section D9.2 (col, summit, hut, alternative descent).

For each leg, the candidate narrates:

  • the route card entry (using the eleven-field structure from D9.1),
  • the instrument plan (which instruments are primary, which are secondary, calibration timing),
  • the decision-point criteria (using the three-component standard),
  • the escape route (with worsening-conditions check),
  • and the response to a "what if" scenario injected by the trainer.

The narration is sustained: each leg should run to roughly five minutes of spoken or written content, with all five elements addressed. A six-leg tour produces 30 minutes of structured narration.

Why this matters for IMS

D10.2 is the closing rehearsal of Level 3. Every component skill the IMS / EMS assessor will probe in the field has appeared somewhere in the narration, and the sustained format is the form the assessor will hear it in. After D10.2 the workbook hands the candidate over to the next phase of the IMS / EMS pathway: real-tour practice, scenario response with a real group, and the practical drill days that lead to the assessment.

If the readiness check at R4 returns "not quite" or "no", that is the workbook working as intended. The error log from D10.1 plus the closing reflection here gives a clear list of what to do. Do that work before moving on.

Quick questions

  • Why should you always inspect the legend before using an unfamiliar map?

- Model idea: Because orienting the map to north helps you match the map to the real terrain.

  • How do grid lines help with navigation?

- Model idea: They allow you to use grid references to give and find precise positions.

Exercise 1

Match the spacing

  • Contours 8 mm apart on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m intervals: gentle (approximately 8 to 10 degrees).
  • Contours 2 mm apart on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m intervals: steep / very steep (approximately 38 to 45 degrees; in the prime avalanche release range and above).
  • Contours 4 mm apart on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m intervals: moderate to steep (approximately 22 to 25 degrees, just below the 30 degree threshold).
  • Contours touching or merged: very steep / cliff (approximately 45 degrees and above; usually too steep for slab formation).

  • show two side walls rising at moderate angles,

"If I stood here"

  • the steepness of the side walls expressed in the C1.2 vocabulary (gentle, moderate, steep, very steep),
  • and an awareness of overhead exposure (for a valley, this is usually low; for a gully, this is the central winter hazard).

Coach learners who slip into describing the symbol rather than the imagined view. The point is the 2D-to-3D drill.

Exercise 5

Following a linear feature in poor visibility

A strong answer should capture both points:

  • Why a valley floor or stream is useful: it is a continuous linear feature, it falls in one direction, and it is unambiguous to follow even when visibility is poor. Mountaineering Scotland's navigation guidance lists valley floors and water features as standard handrails for poor-visibility navigation.
  • Why a tight gully is not: even though it is also a linear feature, a gully on the descent in winter concentrates moving snow into a narrow channel, often has no safe exit point, and may end in a cliff band, terrain trap or terminal cone. Following one in poor visibility means committing to an unknown line in the most exposed part of the slope. The IMS / EMS expectation is to use linear features as handrails when they reduce risk, not when they increase it.

If a learner says "because gullies are dangerous", push for the specific reason. The teaching point is that linear-feature thinking is conditional, not automatic.

Exercise 2

Read the col height

For the standard extract, with point A as the col:

  • Lowest contour value through the saddle: 2,720 m (accept any reading consistent with the trainer's print of the extract).
  • Spot height: 2,718 m (accept any reading consistent with the trainer's print).

Exercise 3

Side profile sketch

A strong sketch should:

  • show two summits at different heights with a saddle between them,
  • show the saddle as a clearly lower point, not just a flat ridge,
  • label both summits and the col,
  • mark approximate heights or relative heights,
  • and indicate the two valleys falling away on either side of the saddle.

The sketch does not need to be artistic. It does need to make the inverse-of-summit relationship visible: a col is the local minimum on the ridge, the same way a summit is the local maximum.

Exercise 4

"If I stood here"

A strong answer should include:

  • a description of the ground at the col itself, which on most maps is slightly hollow rather than truly flat (the saddle dips between the two summits),
  • the direction of rising ground in front of the learner, identified by compass aspect or by a feature reference,
  • the two falling directions on left and right, given as the two valleys with their compass aspects,
  • and an awareness of wind exposure: cols funnel wind because they are the low point in the ridge, and learners should expect stronger wind at the col than at either summit.

If the learner describes the col as "flat ground", coach them: it is locally flat in the sense that the saddle bottoms out, but it is shaped by the rising summits on either side. The descriptive precision matters at IMS / EMS assessment.

Exercise 5

Route choice

A strong answer should include:

  • Horizontal distance. Route 1 (over the summit) is usually slightly shorter in horizontal distance than Route 2 (over the col), because the col is offset from the direct line. On the standard extract, accept either answer with a sound reason.
  • Total climbing. Route 2 (over the col) involves less total climbing in almost every case, because the col is by definition lower than either summit. On the standard extract, the saving is 122 m on the lower summit and 202 m on the higher summit.
  • Touring choice. A strong answer should choose Route 2 (the col) for a winter touring ascent and explain at least one of:

- the climbing saving,

- the lower exposure compared with a summit traverse,

- the wind protection of the col flanks vs. the summit ridge,

- or the route-card simplicity of crossing at the col.

If the learner chooses Route 1 (over the summit), accept it only with a strong justification (for example, summit visit as the day's objective, or summit weather window). The default IMS / EMS instinct is to cross at the col when the climbing budget allows.

Exercise 2

Headwall steepness

For the bowl identified at point A in Exercise 1:

  • Headwall contour spacing: approximately 1 to 2 mm.
  • Headwall slope angle (using the C1.2 rule): approximately 40 to 50 degrees.
  • At or above 30 degrees: Yes, comfortably above the avalanche-release threshold.
  • Flank contour spacing: approximately 4 to 6 mm.

Exercise 3

Side profile sketch

A strong sketch should:

  • show a steep headwall at the back (steep curve or near-vertical line on the sketch, depending on the actual angle),
  • show the two flanks meeting the headwall and opening out into the floor of the bowl,
  • show the floor as comparatively flat or gently sloping, with the mouth opening downhill,
  • label all four parts (headwall, two flanks, floor / mouth),
  • and mark wind-loaded snow accumulation in the upper bowl, just below the headwall.

The wind-loading mark is the IMS / EMS-relevant detail. A bowl sketch that shows even snow distribution misses the most important point about why bowls are terrain traps.

Exercise 4

"If I stood here"

A strong answer should include:

  • a description of the headwall rising in front of the learner, with steepness expressed in the C1.2 vocabulary (steep or very steep),
  • the two flanks on left and right with at least one of them named as steeper or more wind-affected,
  • a description of visible wind-loading signs (cornice on the rim, snow pillow on the upper headwall, snow plume blowing over the lip),
  • and an explicit statement that standing at the mouth puts the learner in the runout zone if a slab releases from the headwall.

The runout-zone awareness is what turns this from a description exercise into a route-choice exercise. Coach learners who omit it.

Exercise 5

Aspect and snow

A strong answer should include:

  • Northern hemisphere. A north-facing bowl typically holds the most consistent winter snow. Reasons: less direct sun in winter, slower melt-freeze cycles, longer-lasting cold dry snow. Accept also "north-east" or "north-north-west" with sound reasoning.
  • Southern hemisphere. The relationship inverts: a south-facing bowl holds the most consistent winter snow there.

If the learner gives the wrong direction without reasoning, mark as wrong. If they give the right direction with weak reasoning ("because it's colder"), prompt for the specific mechanism (low sun angle in winter, less radiation, slower snowpack metamorphosis).

Build the cross-section

A correct cross-section will show:

  • a horizontal axis 8.4 cm long (matching the section line),
  • a vertical axis covering 2,190 m to 2,310 m (one 10 m margin top and bottom),
  • vertical exaggeration 2:1, with the page distance per metre of elevation set to 20 mm per 100 m (giving 100 m of elevation = 2 cm on the page, which is twice what 100 m would occupy at true 1:25,000 scale of 4 mm per 100 m),
  • both axes labelled with scale and the vertical-exaggeration figure (2:1),
  • and 11 plotted points joined by a smooth line that is convex upwards in places and concave in others, depending on the standard extract.

For the standard extract, the cross-section between X and Y reads as a slope that steepens slightly past the halfway point and then eases back towards Y, with no major breaks. The line is approximately concave upwards from X to crossing 6, then approximately straight from crossing 6 to Y.

  • Vertical scale not labelled.
  • Vertical exaggeration not stated.
  • Contour crossings plotted at uniform spacing along the horizontal axis instead of at their actual distances from X (a common simplification that destroys the shape).

First description

Exercise 2

Second description, on a different feature type

Mark on the same criteria as Exercise 1. The point P2 is set on a different feature type by design; the test is whether the learner adapts the structure to the new feature without the structure breaking down.

Common errors when the feature changes:

  • on a summit, learners forget the four-part structure because "I'd just see lots of mountains";
  • on a ridge, learners describe only one side rather than both flanks;

Exercise 3

Third description, with poor visibility added

Mark on the same criteria as Exercises 1 and 2, plus the poor-visibility adaptation:

  • the underfoot sentence becomes more important (the learner can still feel the ground),
  • the slope and aspect sentence becomes a map-derived statement,
  • near features may or may not be visible at 30 m (a contour feature within 30 m might be visible; one further away is inferred from the map),

Exercise 2

Choose the preferred line

For a stable winter day, the preferred line is line 3 (zig-zag with kick turns). A strong justification will include:

  • the average gradient (9°) sits inside the comfortable skinning band (8 to 12°),
  • the total length (2,000 m) is longer but the energy cost is lower because the gradient is sustained at the comfortable level,
  • the kick turns are placed deliberately on the gentlest sections,
  • and the line avoids any features that would force a steeper section.

Accept line 2 (contour-following) as a defensible alternative if the learner argues that the absence of kick turns simplifies the line for a tired group, the gradient is still in band, and the trade-off in length is acceptable.

Line 1 (direct) should be rejected with a sound reason. If a learner picks line 1 because "it's shortest", coach: shortest distance is not the same as easiest. The IMS / EMS expectation is energy-efficient route choice, not distance-minimisation.

Exercise 3

Place the kick turns

For the standard extract, the three kick turns should be placed:

Kick turn Standard placement Reason
1 At a small bench at approximately 1,890 m Gentlest ground on the lower part of the slope; contour spacing locally widens to approximately 6 mm.
2 At a slope-crest transition at approximately 1,975 m Bench-like transition where contour spacing widens before the next steeper section.
3 At a near-flat shoulder at approximately 2,065 m Wide contour spacing immediately below the final pitch to B.

Mark by checking that each placement sits on locally wider contour spacing rather than in the middle of a steep section. A kick turn placed in the middle of tight contours is awkward to execute and slow to recover from.

Exercise 4

Conditions change the choice

Scenario 1 (heavy fresh snow on wind-loaded north aspect, danger 3):

  • Line 3 may still be the preferred line only if the slope itself is not on the heightened-danger north aspect at heightened-danger elevation; if it is, none of the three lines on this slope is the right line and the right answer is to abandon the slope.
  • Line 2 (contour-following) may become the preferred line if it crosses to a less-loaded aspect.
  • Line 1 (direct) is not appropriate in any reading; the direct line maximises exposure on the avalanche-loaded slope.

A strong answer should note the bulletin-driven aspect and elevation question and treat slope choice as the upstream decision.

Scenario 2 (spring conditions, firm refrozen surface):

  • Line 3 is still preferred for the gradient, but on a firm refrozen surface skin grip is reduced and the comfortable gradient band contracts (8 to 10° rather than 8 to 12°). The traverse legs of line 3 should be checked against the contracted band.
  • Line 1 is firmly out: skin grip cannot hold a 14° direct line on refrozen snow.
  • Line 2 may become preferable because its average gradient sits below the contracted band.

Mark for the surface-conditions awareness, not for a specific line choice.

Exercise 5

Descent vs ascent

A strong answer should recognise that:

  • The preferred descent line is often different from the ascent line because the gradient that is too steep for skinning may be ideal for skiing.
  • A direct or near-direct line that was rejected for the ascent may be the right descent line.
  • Specific features that change the descent choice include cliff bands (which may force the descent away from the direct line entirely), narrow gullies (which become terrain traps on the descent), and aspect transitions that change snow quality.

If the learner answers that the descent line is the same as the ascent line without engaging with these factors, coach the gradient asymmetry: skin tracks favour gentle gradients, ski lines favour steeper ones.

Identify a runout zone

For the standard extract, the runout zone of the central gully is the basin at 1,900 m where the gully terminates. A learner crossing this basin during skinning is at risk from any slide that releases on the upper slope or in the gully above, even if they themselves are on flat ground.

Find a convex roll

For the standard extract, the most prominent convex roll is between 2,300 m and 2,200 m on the slope above the central gully:

  • Location: above the gully head on the central flank.
  • Contour spacing immediately above the roll: approximately 6 mm.
  • Contour spacing immediately below the roll: approximately 3 mm.
  • Route relevance: the slope steepens going down, concentrating stress at the steep transition. A skier crossing the roll will be heaviest exactly where the slope is steepest, which is where avalanche-trigger forces are highest. The convex shape combined with the gully below produces a textbook trigger-and-funnel configuration.

Accept any convex roll the learner identifies on the extract, with the same three pieces of information.

  • Extra distance. The shoulder line is approximately 400 m longer than the gully line.

Exercise 9

Col vs summit

A strong answer captures one of:

  • A summit shows closed contour loops with the highest contour at the centre; a col shows two opposite sides of contours rising (towards two summits) and two opposite sides falling (towards two valleys).
  • The summit is the local maximum on a ridge; the col is the local minimum on a ridge between two summits.

If the learner answers "a summit is high, a col is low", coach for specificity. Both features are "high" relative to the surrounding valleys; the difference is the contour pattern, not the elevation.

Exercise 10

Bowl reading

Pattern: a U-shape or arc of contours opening downhill, with tightly spaced contours at the back (the headwall) and wider-spaced contours on the flanks.

Exercise 11

Cliff band

The two cues are:

  • Rock symbology overlaid (hatching on IGN, stippling on Swisstopo, rock-face shading on Alpenvereinskarte).

Either cue alone can mislead. Both together are diagnostic.

Exercise 13

D1.1 — Answer Key

Exercise 1

Label the compass

The six numbered leader lines on the unlabelled diagram point at:

  • Baseplate — the transparent flat body of the compass.
  • Direction-of-travel arrow — the fixed arrow printed on the baseplate, pointing forward.
  • Rotating bezel — the dial marked 0 to 360 that turns around the body.
  • Orienting arrow — the arrow printed on the floor of the rotating bezel.
  • Magnetic needle — the free-floating needle, red end to magnetic north.
  • Parallel orienting lines — the lines on the bezel floor, parallel to the orienting arrow.

Mark each candidate label in order (Q1 through Q6). If a label is correct in meaning but uses the wrong vocabulary (for example "north arrow" instead of "orienting arrow"), accept and note the correct vocabulary. Common error: confusing the orienting arrow (4) with the magnetic needle (5) or the parallel orienting lines (6) — these are all on the bezel floor and easily mixed up.

Exercise 2

Match each part to its function

Correct matches:

Part Function letter
Baseplate b
Direction-of-travel arrow c
Rotating bezel d
Orienting arrow a
Magnetic needle e
Parallel orienting lines f

Mark any wrong match. The most common error is swapping the orienting arrow (a) and the parallel orienting lines (f); the orienting arrow aligns with the magnetic needle in the field, while the orienting lines align with the grid lines on the map.

Exercise 3

The two most-confused parts

A strong answer should:

  • name the direction-of-travel arrow and the orienting arrow as the two parts,
  • explain that both are arrows on the same compass and both point,
  • distinguish the function of each (direction-of-travel = where you are going; orienting = the alignment target for the needle),
  • and state the consequence of confusing them (a bearing that is wrong, often by 90 degrees or more depending on the bezel position; in the worst case, a candidate walks the wrong direction).

If a learner gives a vague answer ("they look similar"), coach for the specific functional distinction.

Exercise 4

Quick check

For a compass with the bezel set to 90 and the magnetic needle sitting freely:

  • The direction-of-travel arrow points 90 degrees away from where the needle is currently sitting (because the bezel has rotated the orienting arrow 90 degrees from where the needle is, but the direction-of-travel arrow on the baseplate is fixed). In practical terms: the direction-of-travel arrow points where the user is currently facing; the needle still points to magnetic north.
  • To set the compass to 90 degrees and walk on it, rotate the body until the needle sits inside the orienting arrow on the bezel. The bezel has already been set to 90; rotating the body brings the needle into alignment.
  • To show a bearing of 270 degrees, the bezel must show 270 at the index mark (where the direction-of-travel arrow meets the bezel scale).

Mark by checking the candidate has not confused which thing rotates at which step. The bezel is set first (to the bearing), then the body rotates (to align the needle).

Exercise 3

Holding the compass

A strong answer should name all three habits and give one concrete example of failure for each:

  • Hold the compass flat. Failure example: a tilted compass lets the needle drag against the bezel; the bearing reads slow or sticks at the wrong figure.
  • Hold away from steel. Failure example: a compass next to a phone reads several degrees off; the candidate walks on the wrong line.
  • Wait for the needle to settle. Failure example: a candidate reads the bezel before the needle has stopped swinging and gets a different figure on the next reading.

Mark for the explicit pairing of habit and failure; a vague answer ("hold it properly") is not enough.

Exercise 4

Quick-check drill

There is no fixed answer; the drill produces the candidate's own reading. Mark by checking that:

  • the candidate has stated the expected bearing for the known direction (for example, due east = 90 degrees),
  • the candidate has noted the actual reading,
  • and the candidate has identified at least one nearby object or condition that might explain any difference.

If the difference is more than 10 degrees and no source is identified, the compass itself may be faulty; suggest a re-test in a different location.

Exercise 5

When the needle is sluggish

Correct answers:

  • Cold temperature. Action: allow extra settling time (up to 10 seconds in temperatures well below freezing); warm the compass inside a glove if the needle is unusable.
  • Magnetic interference. Action: move the compass away from the suspect source (phone, ice axe, ski binding); re-read; if the bearing changes, the source is confirmed.
  • Damaged compass. Action: check for visible damage to the pivot or bezel; if the compass no longer settles reliably, replace it.

Mark by checking each cause is paired with the correct action. A candidate who names the cause but gives the wrong action ("cold temperature: replace the compass") has not internalised the troubleshooting hierarchy.

D2.2 — Answer Key

Exercise 1

Look up the variation

There is no fixed answer; the variation depends on the candidate's chosen map area and date. Mark by checking that:

  • the source named is one of the three official sources (BGS WMM, NOAA / NCEI, or swisstopo),
  • the coordinates entered match the candidate's map area,
  • the date entered is current (within a few months of the workbook session),
  • the variation returned is reasonable for the area (1 to 3 degrees east in the Alps; 0 to 3 degrees west in the UK; up to 15 degrees east or west in some North American locations).

A candidate who copies a figure from an old map margin without checking the date has slipped the discipline. Coach the look-up habit.

Exercise 2

Convert grid to magnetic

There is no fixed answer; the magnetic bearings depend on the candidate's variation lookup from Exercise 1 and the grid bearings from D2.1.

Mark by checking the arithmetic. For an east variation (positive), each magnetic bearing should equal the grid bearing plus the variation. For a west variation (negative), each magnetic bearing should equal the grid bearing minus the variation.

For trainer reference, with grid bearings from the standard D2.1 extract and a variation of +2 degrees east:

1 218 220
2 045 047
3 312 314
4 178 180
5 003 005

A candidate who subtracts the variation when they should add (or vice versa) has the rule wrong. Drill the chosen mnemonic until it is automatic.

Exercise 3

Reflect on the consequence

For a 1,500 m leg with a 2-degree variation:

  • Lateral drift = 1,500 × tan(2 degrees) ≈ 1,500 × 0.0349 ≈ 52 metres.

Mark for the right order of magnitude (40 to 60 m is acceptable for a 2-degree variation). Adjust for the candidate's actual variation:

  • 1 degree → approximately 26 m drift over 1,500 m.
  • 3 degrees → approximately 79 m drift over 1,500 m.
  • 5 degrees → approximately 131 m drift over 1,500 m.

The point of the exercise is for the candidate to see that the variation is not a small detail. A 50 m drift is enough to miss a col, a hut, or a critical waypoint.

D2.3 — Answer Key

Exercise 1

Practical drill in any open space

There is no fixed answer; the drill produces the candidate's own measurement. Mark by checking that:

  • the candidate has stated the bearing they set,
  • the candidate has paced 100 paces (roughly 70 to 100 m of ground depending on pace length),
  • the candidate has measured lateral drift at the end of the leg,
  • and the drift is expressed as metres per 100 m of travel.

The IMS / EMS target is less than 15 m of lateral drift per 100 m of travel. A candidate inside this target on the first attempt is at working level. A candidate consistently outside it should run Exercise 3 to diagnose the cause and re-attempt.

For comparison, the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF gives 15 m per 100 m vertical as the target on a snow drill (Exercise 6 of the Programme). The horizontal-on-flat-ground figure used here is the same number applied to horizontal distance.

Exercise 2

Repeat with a deliberate offset

For a deliberate 5-degree offset over 100 paces:

  • Predicted drift = 100 × tan(5 degrees) ≈ 100 × 0.0875 ≈ 8.7 m.

Mark for the right order of magnitude. A candidate measuring drift well in excess of 10 m has compounded the deliberate offset with their own drift causes; coach the diagnostic from Exercise 3.

The point of the exercise is for the candidate to see that small bearing errors produce predictable, calculable drift. A 3-degree variation slip would produce approximately 5 m drift per 100 m, which is inside the IMS / EMS target but builds up over a longer leg.

Exercise 3

Diagnose the drift

There is no fixed answer; the diagnosis depends on the candidate's own drill. Mark by checking that the candidate has worked through the three causes systematically and identified at least one as the most likely culprit.

Common patterns:

  • Tilted compass: drift is consistently to one side (the side the compass tilted to). Often present in candidates new to the technique.
  • Body off-axis: drift is variable and unpredictable. Often present in candidates who turn at the moment of stepping forward.
  • Leaning into a slope: drift is uphill (away from the intended line on the downhill side). Most relevant on snow rather than on flat ground; flag for the snow drill in D8.1.

If the candidate identifies an "other" cause, mark for the candidate naming a specific mechanism (for example, gusty wind, soft ground producing pace-length variation, distraction). Vague "other" answers are coaching opportunities.

Exercise 4

The intermediate aiming point

A strong answer should make two points:

  • Walking with the eyes on the compass produces drift because the body does not naturally walk in a straight line without an external reference; the compass needle is too small a reference to track at walking pace.
  • Walking with the eyes on a fixed feature ahead is reliable because the feature is large and external; the body tracks the target naturally and the candidate corrects automatically for minor side-step errors.

If the learner answers only "because looking at the compass is awkward", coach for the body-tracking-target mechanism.

Exercise 5

When there is no aiming point

Correct backup strategies:

  • A partner sent forward on the bearing. The partner becomes a moving aiming point. Used in poor visibility; basis of the pole-and-cord technique covered in D8.2.
  • Tracks in the snow. If the candidate is following a known line, the tracks themselves are the aiming reference. Not always available; not reliable in fresh snow or wind that may have erased or obscured the track.

Mark for the explicit naming of both. A learner who only names one has half the toolkit.

D3.1 — Answer Key

Exercise 1

Take three field bearings

There is no fixed answer; the bearings depend on the candidate's position and chosen features. Mark by checking that:

  • the candidate took bearings to features that are uniquely identifiable on the map,
  • the magnetic-to-grid conversion uses the right rule for the candidate's local variation (subtract for east; add for west),
  • and the bearings are within 2 degrees of the correct value (the candidate can verify by re-taking from the same position).

If the candidate consistently takes bearings to features they cannot uniquely identify on the map, the exercise has not surfaced the parallax test. Coach the choice of identifiable features.

Exercise 2

Identify the feature on the map

There is no fixed answer; the identifications depend on the candidate's bearings and position. Mark by checking that:

  • each feature on the map lies along the grid bearing from the candidate's position,
  • the feature identified matches the feature observed in the field (cross-check by description, not just by bearing),
  • and the candidate has verified the identification by checking that the feature looks right from the position.

A candidate who identifies the wrong feature on the map (a feature that lies on the bearing but is not the feature they were looking at) has fallen for the multiple-feature trap. Exercise 3 surfaces this.

Exercise 3

When more than one feature lies on the bearing

A strong answer should:

  • Name the other feature on the line. This depends on the candidate's chosen feature and bearing.
  • Distinguish using a second source of information. Acceptable distinguishing factors include:

- estimated distance (the closer feature looks larger; the further one is harder to see clearly),

- elevation (the candidate can estimate elevation from the visible terrain and cross-check against the spot height on the map),

- shape and surrounding features (a summit with two neighbouring spurs is different from a summit on its own),

- or a third bearing taken to a different feature, which would intersect with the first bearing only at the candidate's position.

If the candidate names only one distinguishing factor, accept it; if no factor is named, prompt for at least one.

Exercise 4

Diagnose the parallax error

For the worked scenario (87 magnetic from one position, 92 magnetic after walking 50 m to the side):

  • No, the bearing has not genuinely changed by 5 degrees over 50 m. A genuine bearing change of 5 degrees over 50 m would imply the feature is at very close range; for a distant feature (a ridge, a summit) the bearing should be approximately constant over a 50 m base.
  • Most likely cause: parallax. The candidate's eye was not aligned with the feature when sighting along the baseplate edge on at least one of the two readings. The 5-degree difference is a sighting error rather than a real bearing change.
  • What to do differently: establish a consistent sighting habit. Hold the compass at chest height with both hands, square to the body. Look along the baseplate edge with one eye closed. A sighting compass with a built-in mirror makes this easier.

If the candidate identifies the cause as drift or compass damage rather than parallax, coach: the controlled experiment (same compass, same operator, two consecutive readings) rules out compass-side causes. The variable is the sighting position.

Exercise 1

Mark for the arithmetic. The single most common error is failing to wrap (for example, 045 + 180 = 225 is correct; 312 + 180 = 492, which should wrap to 132 by subtracting 360). Drill the rule: less than 180, add 180; 180 or more, subtract 180.

Exercise 2

Practical drill

There is no fixed answer; the drill produces the candidate's own measurement. Mark by checking that:

  • the candidate has stated their forward bearing,
  • the calculated back bearing matches the forward bearing plus or minus 180 (with wrap if needed),
  • the actual back bearing read in the field is close to the calculated back bearing (within 5 degrees if the walking was straight),
  • and the candidate has used the angular-to-linear relationship from D2.3 to estimate the implied lateral drift.

If the actual back bearing is more than 10 degrees off the calculated, the candidate has drifted significantly during the walk; coach the D2.3 walking habits.

Exercise 3

The poor-visibility sanity check

A strong answer should make two points:

  • Why every 5 to 10 minutes: at typical walking speeds (3 to 5 km per hour), 5 to 10 minutes is 250 to 800 m of travel. A drift accumulating over that distance is still small enough to correct. A drift accumulated over an hour is large enough to be lost.
  • What action if the back bearing does not align: stop, take a fresh forward bearing (not the original one), and re-orient. The corrective action is not to "push harder on the original bearing"; it is to recognise the drift, accept the new position, and recalculate from there.

If the candidate answers only "to check direction", coach the explicit timing (5 to 10 minutes, not arbitrary) and the explicit action (re-orient, not push harder).

Exercise 4

Back bearing for relocation

For the scenario (col visible behind, candidate uncertain of position on a broad slope):

  • The candidate would take a forward bearing to the col itself. From the candidate's current position, the col is the visible feature; the bearing is taken using the D3.1 procedure.
  • The bearing identifies the line on which the candidate's position lies. From the col, drawing the back bearing on the map produces a line; the candidate is somewhere on that line. To find where on the line, cross-reference with altimeter (D7.2) or with a second bearing to another visible feature (D3.3 resection).
  • More reliable than estimating distance walked: distance estimation on snow is unreliable (pacing does not work; time is affected by surface conditions and gradient). A bearing to a visible feature is exact in direction even if the distance is unknown.

A candidate who suggests pacing or timing as the alternative has the right idea but the wrong tool for snow. Coach the why-distance-fails point.

D3.3 — Answer Key

Exercise 1

Plot a back bearing

There is no fixed answer; the line plotted depends on the candidate's chosen feature and back bearing. Mark by checking that:

  • the back bearing is correctly drawn from the named feature on the map,
  • the parallel orienting lines on the bezel are aligned with the map's grid lines when the line is drawn,
  • the line is drawn in the correct direction (away from the feature, on the back-bearing heading),
  • and the line passes through plausible terrain (not, for example, ending in mid-air or running through a cliff face that is not on the candidate's likely route).

A candidate who draws the line in the wrong direction has the bearing-on-map procedure inverted. Recycle D2.1 and D3.2.

Exercise 2

Practical resection

There is no fixed answer; the resection depends on the candidate's chosen position and visible features. Mark by checking that:

  • two features were chosen that are uniquely identifiable on the ground and the map,
  • the magnetic-to-grid conversions are correct,
  • the back bearings are correctly calculated,
  • the two lines are correctly plotted on the map,
  • the intersection point is identified as the candidate's position,
  • and the candidate has compared the resection result with their known position.

A resection within 100 m of the known position at 1:25,000 is at IMS / EMS working standard. A resection more than 200 m off indicates a bearing error worth investigating; check the chosen-features step (one feature may have been mis-identified on the map) and re-attempt.

Exercise 3

The 90-degree rule

A strong answer should explain that:

  • two features at 90 degrees produce two lines that cross at a sharp angle, giving a clean and unambiguous intersection point,
  • two features at less than 30 degrees apart (or more than 150 degrees) produce two lines that cross at a shallow angle, where small bearing errors translate into large position errors,
  • the candidate should choose features that are roughly 90 degrees apart from their position whenever possible.

If the candidate's answer is geometric without naming the IMS / EMS-relevant consequence (small bearing error causing large position error), coach for the practical implication.

Exercise 4

When resection does not work

Scenario Resection viable? Alternative if not
Clear day, summit visible to the north and hut visible to the east Yes (90 degrees apart, both identifiable). n/a
Whiteout on a broad slope; no features visible beyond 30 m No (no distant features visible). Aspect-of-slope relocation (D7.2) using compass and altimeter.
Flat light; one ridge visible to the south but no second feature No (only one feature; resection requires two). Aspect-of-slope relocation, or wait for visibility to improve and retry.
Clear day on a wide plateau; nearest features all on the same skyline 5 km away No (features are too close together angularly; the resection is geometrically poor). Aspect-of-slope relocation, or use altimeter alone with the known plateau elevation.

Mark for the candidate having identified the failure mode in each "no" case (no features, only one feature, features too close together angularly). The aspect-of-slope alternative should be named; if the candidate has not yet read D7.2, accept "altimeter and compass technique we will cover in D7.2" or similar.

D4.1 — Answer Key

Exercise 1

Walk a square

There is no fixed answer; the closure error depends on the candidate's compass-and-pacing accuracy. Mark by checking that:

  • the candidate has stated their starting bearing,
  • the four sides walked correspond to bearings of 90, 180 and 270 degrees added to the starting bearing (with wrap),
  • the closure error is measured (in paces) and converted to metres using the candidate's calibrated pace length,
  • and the candidate has identified the side that contributed most to the error.

Working interpretation:

Closure error Standard Trainer action
Less than 5 paces IMS / EMS working standard. Confirm. Move on to the triangle and hexagon.
5 to 10 paces Good but not excellent. Identify the problem side; refine.
10 to 20 paces A habit to fix. Run the D2.3 diagnostic. Re-attempt.
More than 20 paces A bearing or pacing problem. Recycle D1.2 (compass holding) and D2.3 (walking technique).

A candidate who reports closure error in metres without stating their pace length has skipped the calibration. Make sure the pace length is recorded.

Exercise 2

Walk a triangle

There is no fixed answer; the closure error depends on the candidate's accuracy. Mark on the same criteria as Exercise 1.

The triangle is typically harder than the square because:

  • the 120-degree turns are less intuitive than 90-degree turns,
  • there are only three sides over which errors can average out,
  • and small bearing errors at the turns compound faster.

A candidate whose triangle closure is markedly worse than their square closure has a turn-discipline issue. Watch the candidate at the corners during the next drill; the body-square-up habit at the moment of changing direction is the fix.

Exercise 3

Walk a hexagon

There is no fixed answer; the closure error depends on the candidate's accuracy. Mark on the same criteria as Exercise 1.

The hexagon is typically gentler than the square because:

  • the 60-degree turns are small,
  • there are six sides over which errors can average out,
  • and the longer total distance means the closure error is a smaller fraction of the path length.

A candidate whose hexagon closure is markedly worse than their square closure has a pacing-consistency issue (errors accumulate over the longer total distance). Coach the consistent-stride habit.

Exercise 4

Deliberate offset

A 3-degree deliberate offset on every side of a 100-pace square produces approximately:

  • per-side lateral drift = tan(3 degrees) × 100 paces ≈ 5 paces.
  • four sides with the same offset: errors compound but partially cancel; closure error typically 10 to 15 paces.

The exact closure error depends on the geometry of the offset (whether the offset is consistently to the right or to the left). For a uniform 3-degree offset, the four corners of the "square" are not at the intended right angles; the shape becomes a slight parallelogram. The closure error reflects the parallelogram's diagonal misalignment.

Mark by checking:

  • the candidate has compared the deliberate-offset closure with their accurate-bezel closure from Exercise 1,
  • the difference is in the order of magnitude predicted (10 to 15 paces of additional closure error),
  • and the candidate has reflected on the consistency of the prediction.

A candidate who finds the deliberate-offset closure is much smaller than predicted may have not actually maintained the 3-degree offset on every side; coach the discipline of setting the bezel at the new value before each side.

Exercise 5

Implications for tour legs

The lateral drift on a 500 m tour leg with the same compounding errors is approximately the closure error from Exercise 1 scaled to 500 m. For example:

  • closure error 7 paces over 100-pace sides (about 70 m sides, total path 280 m): drift rate is approximately 7 / 280 = 2.5 percent.
  • on a 500 m tour leg: implied drift is approximately 12.5 m.

Mark for the right order of magnitude and the right scaling. A candidate who does not scale (and reports the same closure-error figure for the longer leg) has missed the proportional relationship.

The point of the exercise is to make the bearing-shapes drill numbers feel relevant to actual route legs. A candidate at 5-paces closure error on a 100-pace square is at approximately 1 percent drift, which is excellent on any tour leg.

Exercise 2

Choose the safe side

For the standard extract:

Target Standard safe side Reason
Target 1 North of the hut (so when the candidate hits the stream they walk south, downhill, towards the hut) Walking downhill on the stream is easier than walking uphill; the south side of the hut is open ground with no hazard

Exercise 3

Calculate the aim-off bearing

There is no fixed answer; the bearings depend on the candidate's chosen targets and offsets. Mark by checking:

  • the direct grid bearing is taken correctly (using the D2.1 procedure),
  • the offset is in the correct direction (towards the chosen safe side, away from the hazard),
  • the offset is reasonable (5 to 10 degrees is typical; more produces a longer walk, less produces less reliability),
  • and the aim-off grid bearing is correctly calculated.

For Target 1 (hut at 2,450 m, direct bearing 220 grid, aim off 10 degrees north):

  • offset direction: north of the direct line. North of bearing 220 is towards 220 - 90 = 130, which is the perpendicular direction. Subtracting from 220 moves the bearing towards north. So 220 - 10 = 210 grid is the aim-off bearing.

Mark the arithmetic and the direction. A candidate who adds when they should subtract has the offset direction inverted.

Exercise 4

Aim-off in poor visibility

A strong answer should make two points:

  • Aim-off matters more in poor visibility because visual confirmation is unavailable. In good visibility the candidate sees the target as they approach and can correct; in poor visibility they cannot. The aim-off provides the certainty that visual confirmation would otherwise provide.
  • The safe-side choice becomes more critical in poor visibility because the candidate cannot see hazards on the wrong side. A wrong-side aim-off in good visibility is recoverable; in poor visibility it may put the candidate on a hazard before they realise.

If the candidate answers only with the first point, prompt for the safe-side implication.

Exercise 3

Quick-check habit

The four instrument-hygiene checks:

  • Inspect the baseplate for cracks. A cracked baseplate flexes when held flat and tilts the compass. Replace if cracked.
  • Check the damping fluid. A clouded or pierced compass capsule means the needle will not settle reliably. Replace.
  • Check the bezel turns smoothly. A stuck or sluggish bezel produces inconsistent bearings. Try cleaning gently; replace if persistent.
  • Run a quick-check against a known direction. Set the compass to a known direction (a building edge, a road, a ridge crest) at the start of every tour and confirm it agrees. Disagreement is the cue to investigate.

Diagnose the discrepancy

There is no fixed answer; the diagnosis depends on the candidate's largest-discrepancy slope. The three causes from the page:

  • Map estimate averaged over a longer slope (the map shows the average; the inclinometer measures the specific point).
  • Slope is non-uniform (the inclinometer reading at one point is unrepresentative).
  • Measurement at a poor point (a small bump or hollow gives an unrepresentative reading).

To verify: take three or four inclinometer measurements at different points on the slope and average. If the average matches the map estimate, the slope is non-uniform. If a single re-measurement matches the map estimate, the original measurement was at a poor point. If the average still differs significantly from the map, the map estimate was based on contour spacing that was misread.

A candidate who proposes only one verification action has missed the systematic diagnosis. Coach the multiple-measurement approach.

Exercise 4

The 30-degree threshold

There is no fixed answer; the slopes depend on the candidate's exam-area map. For the inclinometer-action column:

  • For slopes well below 30 degrees (under 25): inclinometer measurement is informative but not critical; can be skipped if time-pressed.
  • For slopes near the 30-degree threshold (25 to 30): inclinometer measurement is the deciding figure; take it.
  • For slopes well above 30 (over 35): inclinometer measurement confirms the obvious; take it as confirmation but treat the slope as in the release range regardless.
  • For convex rolls: take multiple measurements to find the steepest section.

Mark for the candidate having matched the action to the map estimate sensibly.

Exercise 5

Convex roll detection

There is no fixed answer; the figures depend on the candidate's chosen convex roll. For trainer reference, typical pattern:

  • Top of the roll: estimated at perhaps 20 to 25 degrees from contour spacing.
  • Rollover (steepest section): estimated at perhaps 35 to 40 degrees from contour spacing.
  • Below the rollover: estimated at perhaps 30 degrees from contour spacing.

The inclinometer measurements should be taken at three points: top, rollover, and below. The pattern of measurements (gentle, steeper, moderate) confirms the convex-roll diagnosis.

A candidate who proposes only one inclinometer measurement on a convex roll has missed the multi-measurement detection technique. Coach the three-point pattern.

D8.1 — Answer Key

Exercise 1

Walk through the whiteout sequence

There is no fixed answer; the work depends on the candidate's chosen leg from D7.1. Mark by checking that:

  • the candidate has applied all six steps in order,
  • step 3 (last known position) references a specific named feature, not a vague "where we just were",

  • and step 6 (move forward) references the appropriate technique for the visibility (intermediate aiming points if visibility allows; pole-and-cord with terrain hazard).

A candidate who tries to take a fresh bearing from the map in step 4 has missed the point of the sequence; coach the route-card-as-foundation principle.

Exercise 2

The in-scope / out-of-scope distinction

A strong answer should make two points:

  • Out of IMS scope: setting out at the start of the day with the intention of leading a group in poor visibility. Example: forecast for whiteout all day, candidate plans to lead a group on a tour anyway. This is not an IMS-level decision.
  • In IMS scope: managing poor visibility encountered en route. Example: morning forecast was good, conditions deteriorate at midday, candidate must lead the group safely back to a known point. This is the IMS-relevant test.

The distinction matters because it affects the candidate's planning thinking. A pre-departure forecast of poor visibility is grounds for not setting out; a mid-tour visibility drop is a managed problem with a procedure.

If the learner answers vaguely ("you should not navigate in poor visibility"), coach the explicit distinction. The IMS / EMS expectation is competent management, not avoidance.

Exercise 3

Why a pre-prepared route card matters

A strong answer should make two points:

  • The pre-prepared bearings are calculated calmly, in good light, with full attention. Bearings calculated in the field in poor visibility, with cold fingers and a panicked group, are produced under conditions that compromise the four-step procedure (D2.1) and almost certainly produce errors.
  • The route card is the trusted reference. A candidate who refers to the card is following a known good plan. A candidate who improvises is making a fresh plan under the worst possible conditions.

Bonus point: a candidate who has prepared the route card has thought through the route in advance and knows it well; this familiarity helps even before the candidate consults the written card.

Exercise 4

The most-skipped step under stress

There is no fixed correct answer; the most-skipped step varies by candidate. Common patterns:

  • Step 1 (Stop). Many candidates keep walking on instinct; the visibility drop feels like a momentary blip and they assume it will improve. The consequence is moving away from the last known position, making relocation harder.
  • Step 2 (Group together). A scattered group is easy to miss in poor visibility; the candidate may not notice they have lost a member until after the next leg.
  • Step 6 (Use intermediate aiming points / pole-and-cord). The candidate executes the bearing without the technique discipline and drifts off line.

Mark for the candidate's specific identification and the consequence reasoning. A vague "the candidate might forget to do something" is not enough.

Exercise 3

Why "temporarily unsure" works

A strong answer should make two points:

  • For the candidate's own thinking: "temporarily unsure" frames the situation as a managed problem with a known process. "Lost" frames it as a crisis with no plan. The mental framing affects the response.
  • For the group's confidence: "temporarily unsure" plus a stated action ("I am stopping to relocate") tells the group that the leader has a plan. "Lost" tells the group that there is no plan.

Bonus point: "temporarily unsure" is also more accurate. A candidate who knows the last confirmed position and has the tools to relocate is not "lost"; they are temporarily uncertain about a specific question (where am I now?) for which they have a procedure.

Exercise 4

The most common error

There is no fixed correct answer; common errors include:

  • Continuing to walk while consulting the map. The most common error. Walking moves the candidate further from the last known position and makes relocation harder. The fix is the explicit "stop" instruction at step 1.
  • Avoiding the relocation entirely by hoping the next feature will resolve the uncertainty. Sometimes this works (the next feature is recognisable); often it does not (the candidate continues on a wrong bearing for another 20 minutes before admitting the uncertainty).
  • Skipping the leadership communication. The candidate runs the procedure silently and the group becomes anxious; the procedure works but the leadership impression is poor.
  • Trusting the GPS over the map-and-compass procedure. The candidate skips the systematic relocation and just reads the GPS coordinates. This works in the moment but does not build the skill.

Mark for the candidate's specific identification and consequence reasoning.

Exercise 1

Identify decision points on your tour

There is no fixed answer; the decision points depend on the candidate's chosen tour. Mark by checking:

  • between three and five decision points are identified,
  • each is at a meaningful location (col, point of no return, major slope crossing, time / weather checkpoint),
  • and each "why this location" reason names a specific terrain or operational factor (not just "good place to stop").

For trainer reference, typical decision points on a six-leg tour:

  • The summit or main objective (descent route choice).
  • An afternoon waypoint (time / weather checkpoint).

Exercise 2

Write criteria for each decision point

There is no fixed answer; the criteria depend on the candidate's decision points. Mark by checking that each criterion has all three components:

  • a specific trigger (a measurable condition: time of day, barometer change, group fitness, snow observation),
  • a specific action (turn around, take alternative line, abort, etc.),
  • a named decision point (which point on the route the criterion applies at).

A candidate whose criteria all use the same trigger (typically time of day) has a one-dimensional contingency plan. Coach for trigger variety: time, weather (barometer trend from D6.2), group condition, snow observation, terrain-trap concern.

  • Time: "If arrival at the col exceeds 10:30am, skip the summit and traverse via the south col."

Exercise 1

Identify escape routes for each leg

For trainer reference, typical patterns:

  • A morning ascent line via a north-facing slope: becomes unavailable as a descent line if the slope is wind-loaded by afternoon.

  • A specific trigger (a measurable condition).
  • A specific action (turn around, take alternative line, etc.).
  • A named decision point (where the criterion is checked).

Mark for all three. A candidate missing any component has not internalised the standard; recycle D6.2 and D9.3.

Confidence score and error log

There is no fixed answer; mark by checking the candidate has scored each skill area honestly and that the scores correlate with their performance on the relevant questions. The error log should name specific pages to revisit and specific actions to take.

D10.2 — Answer Key

Note: model answers depend on the trainer's chosen six-leg route on the Section D10.2 extract. The marking framework below applies to any standard configuration.

Each leg — shared five-element marking framework

For each of the six legs (Exercises 2 to 7), mark by checking that all five elements are populated:

  • Route card entry: all eleven fields from D9.1, with the under-description criteria from D9.2 met.
  • Instrument plan: primary and secondary instruments named for the leg, calibration timing specified.
  • Decision-point criteria: at least one criterion meeting the three-component standard from D6.2 / D9.3.
  • Escape route: named, with one condition under which it would not work.
  • "What if" scenario: trainer-injected scenario with a three-to-four-sentence response.

Common patterns to watch for:

  • Route card entry vague or incomplete: the candidate has not internalised the eleven-field discipline. Recycle D9.1 / D9.2.
  • Instrument plan not differentiated by leg: every leg uses "compass and altimeter" generically. The IMS / EMS-relevant distinction is which is primary and which is secondary for each specific leg. Coach the explicit choice.
  • Decision-point criteria vague: "if the weather looks bad, turn around" is not the three-component standard. Coach D9.3.
  • Escape route as reversal of planned route: the candidate has not engaged with alternative-line thinking. Coach D9.4.
  • "What if" response generic: "stop and reassess" is not enough. The IMS / EMS-relevant response uses the specific procedures (whiteout sequence from D8.1, systematic relocation from D8.3, etc.).
Exercise 1

Pre-departure briefing

Mark by checking that all seven elements are populated:

  • objective stated in one sentence,
  • forecast referenced (single sentence),
  • barometer reading recorded,
  • altimeter calibration point named,
  • expected duration noted,
  • group composition assumed,
  • headline contingency criteria stated.

A candidate who skips the briefing or completes it superficially has missed the IMS / EMS-relevant pre-departure habit. Coach the full briefing.

Exercise 2

Leg 1: Lift station to first waypoint

Apply the shared five-element marking framework above.

Suggested "what if" scenario (trainer-injected): the barometer has fallen 1 hPa in the first hour. A strong response uses the D5/D6 vocabulary: 1 hPa/hr is on the boundary between steady and slow fall; the action is to recalibrate the altimeter at the next known point, note the trend in the route card, and watch for sustained fall over the next hour before triggering a contingency.

Exercise 3

Leg 2: First waypoint to summit

Apply the shared five-element marking framework above.

Suggested "what if" scenario: a group member is significantly behind pace at the first major slope. A strong response uses the D8.3 leadership communication: regroup, assess the cause (fitness, kit, conditions), adjust pace or alter the route plan, and re-time the route card before continuing.

Exercise 4

Leg 3: Summit descent

Apply the shared five-element marking framework above.

Suggested "what if" scenario: visibility drops to approximately 30 m at the start of the descent. A strong response triggers the D8.1 poor-visibility sequence: stop, group up, switch to compass-primary navigation, slow to a controlled pace, and apply the bearing-walked protocol with pole-and-cord if conditions deteriorate further.

Exercise 5

Leg 4: Mid-tour traverse

Apply the shared five-element marking framework above.

Suggested "what if" scenario: the altimeter reads 50 m higher than the expected contour at a known point. A strong response uses the D5.1 rule of thumb (1 hPa = approximately 8 m) to infer roughly a 6 hPa pressure fall, recalibrates the altimeter to the known point, and re-evaluates the barometric trend for the rest of the day.

Exercise 6

Leg 5: Second col

Apply the shared five-element marking framework above.

Suggested "what if" scenario: an unmapped steep slope appears mid-traverse. A strong response uses the D7 aspect-of-slope relocation procedure to confirm position, evaluates the new slope against the planned line, and either skirts the slope or applies a pre-agreed go/no-go criterion before crossing.

Exercise 7

Leg 6: Final descent to lift station

Apply the shared five-element marking framework above.

Suggested "what if" scenario: the group is approximately 45 minutes behind schedule by the start of the final descent. A strong response references the route-card time estimates, evaluates the daylight margin and lift-close time, considers the pre-mapped alternative line / escape route, and communicates the decision to the group with a clear trigger (e.g. "if we are not at point X by HH:MM, we take the alternative line").

For trainer reference, the full "what if" reference table:

Leg Suggested scenario
1 The barometer has fallen 1 hPa in the first hour.
2 A group member is significantly behind pace at the first major slope.
3 Visibility drops to approximately 30 m at the start of the descent.
4 The altimeter reads 50 m higher than the expected contour at a known point.
5 An unmapped steep slope appears mid-traverse.

Substitute trainer-chosen scenarios as appropriate. The point is to test the candidate's response across the full Level 3 toolkit.

Exercise 8

R1: Strongest leg

Mark by checking that the candidate has named a specific leg and what they did well there (not just "I did well at Leg 3").

Exercise 9

R2: Weakest leg

Mark by checking that the candidate has named a specific leg and what specifically was missing or vague (not just "Leg 4 was hard").

Exercise 10

R3: The hardest "what if"

Mark by checking that the candidate has named a specific scenario and articulated why the response was difficult (the specific Level 3 skill that was not yet automatic).

Exercise 11

R4: Level 4 readiness check

The answer is the candidate's, not the trainer's. The trainer's role is to verify the answer is honest:

  • A "yes" answer should be supported by a clean D10.1 quiz, a strong D10.2 narration with all five elements populated for every leg, and an error log of minor items only.
  • A "not quite" answer should name specific pages.
  • A "no" answer should name a specific section or block of sections to redo.

If the candidate's answer disagrees with the evidence (a "yes" with multiple wrong answers in D10.1 or weak narration in D10.2), coach the discrepancy openly rather than override the answer. The honesty of the readiness check is what makes it useful.

For the trainer's working framework:

  • Ready for next phase if: D10.1 quiz score 12 or more out of 15; D10.2 narration with all five elements at every leg; "what if" responses use specific Level 3 procedures; closing reflection is honest.
  • Not quite ready if: D10.1 quiz score 9 to 11; D10.2 narration with one or two element-gaps; some "what if" responses generic.
  • Substantial review needed if: D10.1 quiz score under 9; D10.2 narration with multiple element-gaps; "what if" responses generic across the board.
Exercise 12

R5: The two-instruments-in-one habit

Mark by checking that the candidate has reflected honestly on whether the dual use of the altimeter (altitude reference and weather signal) is becoming automatic. A candidate who claims it is automatic but the D10.2 narration does not show it has the wrong self-assessment; coach.
