### Exercise 1 — Identify the bowl

Mark by checking that the learner has:

- recognised that a bowl shows a U or arc-shaped pattern of contours opening downhill,
- distinguished the headwall (close-spaced contours at the back) from the flanks (wider-spaced contours on the sides),
- distinguished a bowl from a valley by checking that the bowl is enclosed on three sides whereas a valley runs through with high ground on only two sides,
- and given a one-line reason that refers to at least two of: the U-shape, the headwall, the flanks, the open mouth.

For trainer reference, the standard Section C3.2 extract is configured as:

| Feature | Answer | Model reason |
|---|---|---|
| A | Bowl | Clear U opening south; tight headwall contours at 2,800 to 2,900 m; wider-spaced flanks; mouth opens onto a basin at 2,500 m. |
| B | Valley | V-up pattern with stream symbol; runs through, not enclosed at the head. |
| C | Bowl | Smaller corrie pattern; tight arc-shaped headwall on the north-east side; open mouth to the south-west. |
| D | Neither | A simple slope with no enclosing pattern; contours run uniformly without a U or arc. |

### Exercise 2 — Headwall steepness

For the bowl identified at point A in Exercise 1:

1. Headwall contour spacing: approximately **1 to 2 mm**.
2. Headwall slope angle (using the C1.2 rule): approximately **40 to 50 degrees**.
3. At or above 30 degrees: **Yes**, comfortably above the avalanche-release threshold.
4. Flank contour spacing: approximately **4 to 6 mm**.
5. Flank slope angle: approximately **15 to 20 degrees**.

Mark by checking the learner has applied the C1.2 rule of thumb correctly (3 mm or closer at 1:25,000 with 10 m contours indicates 30 degrees or more) and has read both the headwall and the flanks rather than treating the bowl as a single uniform slope.

### Exercise 3 — Side profile sketch

A strong sketch should:

- show a steep headwall at the back (steep curve or near-vertical line on the sketch, depending on the actual angle),
- show the two flanks meeting the headwall and opening out into the floor of the bowl,
- show the floor as comparatively flat or gently sloping, with the mouth opening downhill,
- label all four parts (headwall, two flanks, floor / mouth),
- and mark wind-loaded snow accumulation in the upper bowl, just below the headwall.

The wind-loading mark is the IMS / EMS-relevant detail. A bowl sketch that shows even snow distribution misses the most important point about why bowls are terrain traps.

### Exercise 4 — "If I stood here"

A strong answer should include:

- a description of the headwall rising in front of the learner, with steepness expressed in the C1.2 vocabulary (steep or very steep),
- the two flanks on left and right with at least one of them named as steeper or more wind-affected,
- a description of visible wind-loading signs (cornice on the rim, snow pillow on the upper headwall, snow plume blowing over the lip),
- and an explicit statement that standing at the mouth puts the learner in the runout zone if a slab releases from the headwall.

The runout-zone awareness is what turns this from a description exercise into a route-choice exercise. Coach learners who omit it.

### Exercise 5 — Aspect and snow

A strong answer should include:

1. **Northern hemisphere.** A north-facing bowl typically holds the most consistent winter snow. Reasons: less direct sun in winter, slower melt-freeze cycles, longer-lasting cold dry snow. Accept also "north-east" or "north-north-west" with sound reasoning.
2. **Southern hemisphere.** The relationship inverts: a south-facing bowl holds the most consistent winter snow there.

If the learner gives the wrong direction without reasoning, mark as wrong. If they give the right direction with weak reasoning ("because it's colder"), prompt for the specific mechanism (low sun angle in winter, less radiation, slower snowpack metamorphosis).
