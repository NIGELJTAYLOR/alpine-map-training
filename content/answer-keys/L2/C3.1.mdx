### Exercise 1 — Identify the col

Mark by checking that the learner has:

- recognised that a col shows a low point on a ridge with rising contours on two opposite sides (the summits) and falling contours on the other two opposite sides (the valleys),
- distinguished a col from both a summit (closed loops, no falling contours on either side) and a valley (V-up but no rising contours on opposite sides),
- and given a one-line reason that refers to the saddle pattern.

For trainer reference, the standard Section C3.1 extract is configured as:

| Point | Answer | Model reason |
|---|---|---|
| A | Col | Low point on the main ridge with two summits visible to north and south; saddle pattern with valleys falling east and west. |
| B | Summit | Closed contour loops with the highest contour at the centre; no saddle. |
| C | Col | Smaller secondary col on a side ridge; clear rising contours on two opposite sides. |
| D | Neither | A bend in the ridge crest that has not formed a clear low point; ground continues to rise on both sides. |

### Exercise 2 — Read the col height

For the standard extract, with point A as the col:

1. Lowest contour value through the saddle: **2,720 m** (accept any reading consistent with the trainer's print of the extract).
2. Spot height: **2,718 m** (accept any reading consistent with the trainer's print).
3. Higher summit: **2,920 m**.
4. Lower summit: **2,840 m**.
5. Climb saved by going over the col rather than over the lower summit: **122 m** (2,840 m minus 2,718 m).

Mark by checking the learner has used the spot height where present, has identified both summits correctly, and has done the subtraction in the right direction (col is below summit; the saving is positive).

### Exercise 3 — Side profile sketch

A strong sketch should:

- show two summits at different heights with a saddle between them,
- show the saddle as a clearly lower point, not just a flat ridge,
- label both summits and the col,
- mark approximate heights or relative heights,
- and indicate the two valleys falling away on either side of the saddle.

The sketch does not need to be artistic. It does need to make the inverse-of-summit relationship visible: a col is the local minimum on the ridge, the same way a summit is the local maximum.

### Exercise 4 — "If I stood here"

A strong answer should include:

- a description of the ground at the col itself, which on most maps is slightly hollow rather than truly flat (the saddle dips between the two summits),
- the direction of rising ground in front of the learner, identified by compass aspect or by a feature reference,
- the two falling directions on left and right, given as the two valleys with their compass aspects,
- and an awareness of wind exposure: cols funnel wind because they are the low point in the ridge, and learners should expect stronger wind at the col than at either summit.

If the learner describes the col as "flat ground", coach them: it is locally flat in the sense that the saddle bottoms out, but it is shaped by the rising summits on either side. The descriptive precision matters at IMS / EMS assessment.

### Exercise 5 — Route choice

A strong answer should include:

1. **Horizontal distance.** Route 1 (over the summit) is usually slightly shorter in horizontal distance than Route 2 (over the col), because the col is offset from the direct line. On the standard extract, accept either answer with a sound reason.
2. **Total climbing.** Route 2 (over the col) involves less total climbing in almost every case, because the col is by definition lower than either summit. On the standard extract, the saving is 122 m on the lower summit and 202 m on the higher summit.
3. **Touring choice.** A strong answer should choose Route 2 (the col) for a winter touring ascent and explain at least one of:
   - the climbing saving,
   - the lower exposure compared with a summit traverse,
   - the wind protection of the col flanks vs. the summit ridge,
   - or the route-card simplicity of crossing at the col.

If the learner chooses Route 1 (over the summit), accept it only with a strong justification (for example, summit visit as the day's objective, or summit weather window). The default IMS / EMS instinct is to cross at the col when the climbing budget allows.
