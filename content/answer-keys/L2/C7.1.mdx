### Question 1 — Contour intervals

Crossings: **8**. Height gained: **80 m**.

(2,180 m to 2,260 m is 80 m of climb at a 10 m contour interval, which is 8 contour-line crossings.)

If the learner answers "9" for crossings, the most likely error is counting both the start and the end contour as crossings. Coach: a crossing is a transition from one contour band to the next; the eight crossings between 2,180 m and 2,260 m correspond to the eight transitions, not to the nine contour lines.

### Question 2 — Steepness from spacing

**Yes**, this slope is at or above 30 degrees. 2 mm contour spacing on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m contours sits well inside the C1.2 30-degree threshold (which sits at approximately 3 mm spacing on these parameters).

### Question 3 to 5 — Identify the feature

For the standard snippet sheet:

| Snippet | Feature |
|---|---|
| A | Spur (V-down) |
| B | Bowl |
| C | Plateau |

Mark by checking the family is right. Sub-type errors within the correct family are less serious; family errors are the ones to recycle.

### Question 6 — Aspect to eight directions

For the standard extract with grid north up the page and Q2 falling towards the lower-right corner: aspect is **SE (south-east)**.

If the learner answers "south" or "east" alone, the four-direction default has crept back in. Recycle C4.2.

### Question 7 — Fall line

A correct fall-line arrow at Q2 runs perpendicular to the contour at Q2, pointing from the higher contour towards the lower contour, in the south-east direction.

Mark by checking the perpendicular rule was applied (not parallel to the contour).

### Question 8 — Inverse pairing

A V-shape of contours pointing downhill is a **spur** (or a ridge if the feature is long enough; both answers are accepted, since the test of family vs sub-type is on the matching questions, not here).

If the learner answers "valley" or "gully", the C2.3 inverse-pairing rule has not stuck. Recycle C2.3.

### Question 9 — Col vs summit

A strong answer captures one of:

- A summit shows closed contour loops with the highest contour at the centre; a col shows two opposite sides of contours rising (towards two summits) and two opposite sides falling (towards two valleys).
- The summit is the local maximum on a ridge; the col is the local minimum on a ridge between two summits.

If the learner answers "a summit is high, a col is low", coach for specificity. Both features are "high" relative to the surrounding valleys; the difference is the contour pattern, not the elevation.

### Question 10 — Bowl reading

Pattern: a **U-shape or arc of contours opening downhill**, with **tightly spaced contours at the back (the headwall) and wider-spaced contours on the flanks**.

Wind-loaded snow accumulates: in the **upper part of the bowl, just below the headwall**, on the lee side of the prevailing wind; cornices may build on the rim above.

### Question 11 — Cliff band

The two cues are:

1. **Merged or near-merged contours** drawn as a single thick line.
2. **Rock symbology** overlaid (hatching on IGN, stippling on Swisstopo, rock-face shading on Alpenvereinskarte).

Either cue alone can mislead. Both together are diagnostic.

### Question 12 — Skinning gradient

Gradient range: **8 to 12 degrees comfortable; up to 15 degrees as the upper sustained limit**.

Upper-end contour spacing: **5 to 6 mm** on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m contours (using the C1.2 rule).

If the learner answers "8 to 15" without the comfortable / upper-limit distinction, coach: the comfortable band and the upper limit are different concepts. The 8 to 12 figure is where the skin track works without effort; up to 15 is acceptable on a single traverse leg with kick turns.

### Question 13 — Terrain trap

A strong answer captures: **a piece of ground where a small avalanche or fall produces disproportionate consequences**, often because the configuration concentrates moving snow (gully), removes the exit (single-mouth bowl), or adds a vertical drop (cliff band below the slope).

If the learner answers "a steep slope" or "a dangerous slope", coach: terrain trap is about the consequence, not just the slope angle. A 30-degree slope is not a terrain trap; a 30-degree slope above a cliff is.

### Question 14 — Sketch task

A strong sketch should:

- show a sharp peak (narrow, pointed, steep flanks),
- show two spurs running out from the peak in different directions,
- label the summit and the two spurs,
- and indicate that the two spurs face in different aspects (a brief direction note for each is helpful).

Mark for the relationship between the features (spurs as projections from a parent peak) more than for the artistry. A sketch that shows two summits with no obvious spur projection has missed the structural point.

### Question 15 — Written description

Mark on the same criteria as C5.3 Exercise 1:

- all four parts of the structure used,
- compass-aspect terms (eight directions),
- C1.2 steepness vocabulary,
- specific feature references rather than generic ones,
- imagined view rather than symbol description.

For trainer reference, the standard Q3 in the standard extract is configured on a moderate north-east-facing slope at approximately 2,250 m, with a summit at 2,500 m to the south-west and a broad valley with a stream at 1,950 m to the north-east. A model description is in the same form as the C5.3 Exercise 1 model answer; adapt to the trainer's actual Q3.

### Confidence score

There is no fixed answer. Mark by checking that the learner has scored each of the six skill areas honestly and that the scores correlate with their performance on the relevant questions. A learner who scores 5 across the board after several wrong answers has not engaged with the self-assessment honestly; coach.

### Error log

There is no fixed answer. The error log is the page's most useful artefact. Mark by checking that:

- every wrong answer is logged,
- every slow-but-right answer is logged,
- each entry names a specific page to revisit,
- and each entry names a specific action.

A vague entry ("review contours") is not enough. Push for "redo C2.3 Exercise 1 with a fresh extract by Friday".
