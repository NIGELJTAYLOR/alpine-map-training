### Exercise 1 — Find the plateau

For the standard Section C3.3 extract:

1. Plateau dimensions: approximately **1.2 km by 0.8 km**.
2. Plateau spot height: **2,140 m**.
3. Sharpest drop-off: **east** (the plateau falls steeply onto the cliff band on the eastern side; the other three sides drop more moderately).

Mark by checking the learner has shaded an area where contours are absent or widely spaced, and has correctly identified the cardinal direction(s) of the steepest drop-off. Accept any reading consistent with the trainer's print of the extract.

### Exercise 2 — Mark the cliff bands

For the standard extract, expect the learner to identify two cliff bands:

| Cliff band | Length | Aspect | Continuity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main band below the plateau | approximately 200 m | east-facing | continuous (no break) |
| Secondary band south of the plateau | approximately 80 m | south-east-facing | broken (one gully cuts through it) |

Mark by checking the learner has used the merged-contours-plus-rock-symbology cue, and has noted whether each band is continuous or has a break. The break is the route-relevant detail: a continuous cliff band has to be avoided; a broken cliff band may be navigable through the gap.

### Exercise 3 — Easiest and hardest ground

For the standard extract:

1. Easiest ground (tick): the plateau itself; widely spaced or absent contours, no rock symbology, gentle gradient.
2. Hardest ground to descend (cross): the cliff band immediately east of the plateau; merged contours and rock symbology indicate impassable terrain in normal conditions.
3. Reason: contour spacing alone gives the answer. Wide spacing equals easy ground; merged contours equal cliff. The rock symbology confirms the cliff reading and excludes the alternative interpretation that the merger is just a printing artefact.

### Exercise 4 — Side profile sketch

A strong sketch should:

- show the plateau as a flat or near-flat top,
- show the bounding slopes falling away from the plateau,
- show the cliff band as a sharp vertical or near-vertical face on the steepest side,
- label the plateau, the bounding slopes, and the cliff band,
- and indicate at least one route from the valley to the plateau, marked on the side that does not have the cliff band, or through the gap in a broken cliff band.

If the learner draws the cliff band as merely "very steep ground", coach them: a cliff is a sudden vertical break. The sketch should show that break as a distinct line, not as a steeper version of a normal slope.

### Exercise 5 — "If I stood here" (poor visibility)

A strong answer should include:

- a description of the immediate surroundings dominated by the absence of features (flat ground, no obvious lines, low visibility removes any distant landmark),
- a clear explanation of why a plateau is hard to navigate in poor visibility: there are no linear handrails (no streams, ridges, or spurs), so the learner cannot use the standard contour-feature handrails from C2.2 and C2.3,
- and a piece of equipment beyond map and compass. Acceptable answers include altimeter (essential for verifying height when no contour features are visible), GPS, pacing in combination with a bearing, or a known timing for the leg.

If the learner names "phone" or "watch" without specifying the function (altimeter or GPS), prompt for the specific feature being relied on.
