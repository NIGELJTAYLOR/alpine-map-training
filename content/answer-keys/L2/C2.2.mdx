### Exercise 1 — Valley or gully

Mark by checking that the learner has:

- recognised that the V or U shape of the contour bend points uphill,
- distinguished a broad bend (valley) from a narrow, sharp bend (gully),
- referred to both the width of the V and the spacing of the side-wall contours in their reason.

For trainer reference, the standard Section C2.2 extract is configured as:

| Feature | Answer | Model reason |
|---|---|---|
| A | Valley | Wide V shape with a broad floor; side-wall contours moderately spaced; visible blue stream line through the bend. |
| B | Gully | Sharp, narrow V; side-wall contours close together (around 2 mm); cuts across a steeper slope. |
| C | Valley | Broad U shape with a near-flat floor; side-wall contours wider apart; classic glacial valley profile. |
| D | Gully | Tight V with merged or near-merged side-wall contours indicating very steep walls; no flat floor. |

### Exercise 2 — Drainage direction

For the standard extract:

| Question | Feature A (valley) | Feature B (gully) |
|---|---|---|
| Highest contour value at point of V | 2,180 m | 2,520 m |
| Lowest contour value at open end | 2,040 m | 2,460 m |
| Direction of water flow | from 2,180 m towards 2,040 m | from 2,520 m towards 2,460 m |

Mark by checking the learner has used the contour values, not the visible blue line, as the primary cue. Some real maps will not show a stream line where one exists, especially in karst terrain or on north-facing winter slopes. The contour pattern is the reliable cue.

### Exercise 3 — Side profile sketch

A strong valley sketch should:

- show a flat or near-flat floor,
- show two side walls rising at moderate angles,
- mark the stream or water flow on the floor,
- and label the floor width in metres (broadly consistent with the map width × scale calculation).

A strong gully sketch should:

- show no flat floor (V-shaped cross-section),
- show steep, close side walls,
- mark the line of the gully along the bottom of the V,
- and label the floor width as small (typically 5 to 15 m for an alpine gully).

The sketch does not need to be artistic. It needs to show that the learner has translated the contour pattern into a 3D mental picture and can quantify the width.

### Exercise 4 — "If I stood here"

A strong answer should include:

- a clear description of the floor surface (gravel, stream-bed, snow-filled bowl, broad meadow) consistent with the contour width,
- the direction of the stream stated as upstream / downstream or in compass terms,
- the steepness of the side walls expressed in the C1.2 vocabulary (gentle, moderate, steep, very steep),
- and an awareness of overhead exposure (for a valley, this is usually low; for a gully, this is the central winter hazard).

Coach learners who slip into describing the symbol rather than the imagined view. The point is the 2D-to-3D drill.

### Exercise 5 — Following a linear feature in poor visibility

A strong answer should capture both points:

1. **Why a valley floor or stream is useful:** it is a continuous linear feature, it falls in one direction, and it is unambiguous to follow even when visibility is poor. Mountaineering Scotland's navigation guidance lists valley floors and water features as standard handrails for poor-visibility navigation.
2. **Why a tight gully is not:** even though it is also a linear feature, a gully on the descent in winter concentrates moving snow into a narrow channel, often has no safe exit point, and may end in a cliff band, terrain trap or terminal cone. Following one in poor visibility means committing to an unknown line in the most exposed part of the slope. The IMS / EMS expectation is to use linear features as handrails when they reduce risk, not when they increase it.

If a learner says "because gullies are dangerous", push for the specific reason. The teaching point is that linear-feature thinking is conditional, not automatic.
