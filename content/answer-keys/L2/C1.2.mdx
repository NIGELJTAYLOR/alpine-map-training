### Exercise 1 — Match the spacing

1. Contours 8 mm apart on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m intervals: **gentle** (approximately 8 to 10 degrees).
2. Contours 2 mm apart on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m intervals: **steep / very steep** (approximately 38 to 45 degrees; in the prime avalanche release range and above).
3. Contours 4 mm apart on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m intervals: **moderate to steep** (approximately 22 to 25 degrees, just below the 30 degree threshold).
4. Contours touching or merged: **very steep / cliff** (approximately 45 degrees and above; usually too steep for slab formation).

### Exercise 2 — Apply the 30 degree rule

Answers depend on the specific Section C1.2 map extract. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- measured spacing in millimetres rather than estimating by eye,
- correctly applied the rule "3 mm or closer at 1:25,000 with 10 m contours = 30 degrees or more",
- ticked Yes for slopes where measured spacing is 3 mm or closer, and No for slopes where it is wider.

For trainer reference, the standard Section C1.2 extract is configured as:

| Slope | Spacing | At or above 30 degrees? |
|---|---|---|
| A | approximately 5 mm | No (around 18 degrees) |
| B | approximately 2 mm | Yes (around 38 degrees) |
| C | approximately 3 mm | Yes (right at the threshold) |

### Exercise 3 — Why the interval matters

The spacing between contour lines only tells you about steepness if you also know the **contour interval**, which is shown in the map **legend**.

### Exercise 4 — Reflection

A strong answer should capture two or three of the following:

- contour spacing is the visual cue that translates a flat map into a 3D sense of the ground,
- it is the core link between contour reading and route choice,
- it underpins the 30 degree avalanche threshold,
- it works on any topographic map regardless of country or scale, provided you know the interval.
