### Exercise 1 — Ridge, spur or neither

Mark by checking that the learner has:

- recognised that the V or U shape of the contour bend points downhill,
- used the contour values (falling towards the point of the V) as the confirming check,
- distinguished a long, continuous crest (ridge) from a shorter projection (spur),
- and given a reason that draws on at least two of: V direction, contour values, length of the feature.

For trainer reference, the standard Section C2.3 extract is configured as:

| Feature | Answer | Model reason |
|---|---|---|
| A | Spur | V points downhill from a parent summit; values fall from 2,380 m at the high end to 2,200 m at the point of the V; feature is short (around 600 m). |
| B | Ridge | Continuous line of paired contour bends running across the sheet; spot heights along the crest; divides two drainage systems. |
| C | Spur | Short downhill projection from a ridge; tight V; values clearly falling towards the point. |
| D | Neither | A high point that does not form a V shape; closed-loop summit (treat as a C2.1 feature, not a ridge or spur). |

### Exercise 2 — Aspect of each side

For the standard extract spur (Feature A from Exercise 1):

1. Crest direction: north-east to south-west (running from 2,380 m down to 2,200 m).
2. Aspect of slope on the left of the crest (facing downhill from 2,380 m towards 2,200 m): roughly **north-west facing**.
3. Aspect of slope on the right of the crest: roughly **south-east facing**.
4. The **north-west facing** side would be expected to hold colder, drier snow in mid-winter because it receives less direct sun in the northern hemisphere. The opposite side warms more, and is more prone to melt-freeze cycles.

Accept any answer where the two named aspects are 180 degrees apart and the candidate's reasoning links sun exposure to snow condition correctly.

### Exercise 3 — Side profile sketch

A strong ridge sketch should:

- show a long high line with multiple high points along it,
- show two flank slopes falling away on either side,
- label the highest point on the crest,
- and indicate clearly which flank is steeper if the contour spacing differs.

A strong spur sketch should:

- show a single high point at one end and a falling line extending out from it,
- show two flank slopes converging at the point of the V,
- label the high end and the low end,
- and indicate which flank is steeper.

The sketch should make the inverse-of-valley relationship visually obvious. If the learner sketches a spur that looks like a valley, return to Exercise 1's contour-value check.

### Exercise 4 — "If I stood here"

A strong answer should include:

- a description of the ground underfoot consistent with a spur crest (typically narrow, sometimes broad on a rounded spur, occasionally rocky on a sharp spur),
- the aspect or direction of the slope falling on the left, expressed in compass terms,
- the aspect or direction of the slope falling on the right, expressed in compass terms,
- and a stated descent direction along the crest itself (towards the low end of the spur, or back the way they came towards the parent feature).

Coach learners who describe both flanks as the same direction; that is a sign they are reading the spur as a valley.

### Exercise 5 — Inverse pairing with C2.2

A strong answer should capture both points:

1. **V direction.** In a valley or gully the V points uphill (towards higher contour values); in a ridge or spur the V points downhill (towards lower contour values).
2. **Contour values.** As you move along the feature towards the point of the V, contour values rise in a valley and fall in a ridge or spur.

If the learner gives only one of the two points, prompt for the other. Both checks together are what makes the rule robust on awkward map extracts where the V direction alone can be ambiguous.
