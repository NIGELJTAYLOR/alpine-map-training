### Exercise 1 — Match the contour snippet to the photograph

For the standard photo-matching set, the correct pairings are:

| Contour snippet | Matching photograph |
|---|---|
| 1 | C |
| 2 | A |
| 3 | F |
| 4 | E |
| 5 | B |
| 6 | D |

Mark by family first (high ground photograph paired with closed-loop snippet; valley photograph paired with V-up snippet; bowl photograph paired with arc-shape snippet). A learner who pairs by family but mismatches within a family has a calibration problem, not a vocabulary problem; a learner who pairs by family wrongly has a vocabulary problem. Treat the two errors differently.

For the standard set, snippets 1 and 4 are deliberately similar (both summit families), as are snippets 2 and 5 (both drainage families). The within-family discrimination is the test.

### Exercise 2 — Articulate the cues

A strong cue answer will:

- name a specific contour feature in the snippet (closed loops, V-up, V-down, arc with tight headwall, merged contours, etc.),
- name the corresponding feature visible in the photograph,
- and link the two with a short explanation.

Examples of strong answers:

- "Pair 3 (snippet 3, photo F): the snippet has tight closed loops with a spot height at the centre, indicating a sharp peak. Photo F shows a recognisable rock summit with steep flanks falling away on all sides."
- "Pair 4 (snippet 4, photo E): the snippet shows widely spaced closed loops with a broad central spot height, indicating a rounded summit or plateau-like high. Photo E shows a broad flat-topped hill with no obvious sharp peak."

Examples of weak answers to coach:

- "Pair 3: it just looks similar." (No cue named.)
- "Pair 3: I picked the most obvious one." (No reasoning.)

If a learner produces three weak answers, recycle. The cue articulation is what makes the calibration durable; without it the matching is guess-work.

### Exercise 3 — Where the picture surprised you

There is no fixed answer. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- named a specific surprise rather than a generic "I didn't expect that",
- identified the contour feature that produced the wrong expectation,
- and stated what they will change in their reading of similar features in future.

Common surprises in the standard set:

- learners draw cliff bands as dramatically vertical when the actual ground in photo D shows the cliff band as a rough rock face that looks less geometric and more broken than the merged-contours-plus-rock-symbology image suggests,
- learners picture rounded summits as gentle when photo E shows a rounded summit with surprisingly strong flank slopes,
- learners picture bowls as more symmetrical than reality (photo B in the standard set).

If the learner cannot identify a surprise, prompt them: "look at the photograph for a feature you would not have drawn that way". Most learners can produce one with a small nudge.

### Exercise 4 — Sketch from the photograph back to a contour

There is no single correct sketch. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- drawn contours, not a side profile (the exercise is photograph-to-map, not photograph-to-sketch),
- represented the family of feature correctly (closed loops for a summit, V-up for a valley, arc for a bowl, etc.),
- represented the relative steepness using contour spacing,
- and produced something that resembles the actual contour snippet when compared.

If the learner produces a side-profile sketch by accident, return to the exercise wording and have them redo. The reverse direction (image to map) is a different cognitive skill from the forward direction (map to image) and is worth practising in its own right.
