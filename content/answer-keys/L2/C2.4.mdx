### Exercise 1 — Match the snippets to the names

For trainer reference, the standard C2.4 snippet sheet is configured as below. The mix is deliberate: each of the seven feature names is represented at least once, with two summit sub-types and two ridge / spur examples to test the sub-type discrimination established in C2.1 and C2.3.

| Snippet | Feature name | One-word reason |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sharp peak | Loop, tight |
| 2 | Valley | V-up, broad |
| 3 | Spur | V-down, short |
| 4 | Rounded summit | Loop, wide |
| 5 | Gully | V-up, tight |
| 6 | Ridge | V-down, long |
| 7 | Plateau-like high | Loop, flat |
| 8 | Spur | V-down, short (off a different parent feature from snippet 3) |

Mark by checking that the learner has:

- placed the snippet in the correct family (closed loop = summit; V-up = drainage; V-down = high-ground crest),
- correctly named the sub-type within the family (sharp / rounded / plateau, broad / tight, ridge / spur),
- and given a single-word reason that is consistent with the answer.

If the learner has the family right but the sub-type wrong, treat that as a partial pass and recycle the relevant earlier exercise (C2.1 for summit sub-types, C2.2 for valley vs gully, C2.3 for ridge vs spur).

If the learner has the family wrong (for example, calling a spur a valley), that is a sign the V-direction or contour-value check is not yet automatic. Return to the inverse-pairing diagram in C2.3 before moving on.

### Exercise 2 — Match the snippets to side-view sketches

For the standard snippet sheet:

| Snippet | Sketch |
|---|---|
| 1 | C |
| 2 | A |
| 3 | F |
| 4 | E |
| 5 | H |
| 6 | B |
| 7 | G |
| 8 | D |

Mark by checking that the learner has paired contour family with sketch family first (high ground sketches show a crest or peak; drainage sketches show a floor and side walls). Sub-type errors within the correct family are minor; family errors are the ones to correct.

If the standard snippet sheet uses a different lettering, accept any pairing that matches the same feature on both sides. The point of the exercise is the contour-to-3D-view link, not the lettering.

### Exercise 3 — Reflection

There is no model answer for this exercise. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- named a specific feature (not just "contours" or "the hard ones"),
- identified a specific source of confusion (V-direction, sub-type, scale, spacing),
- and named a specific page or exercise to revisit.

A weak answer ("I'll do more practice") should be coached towards specificity. Ask the learner: which feature, which page, which exercise, by when?

This exercise is the diagnostic that closes Section C2. Treat it as a learning tool, not a quiz question. The trainer should record the learner's answer in the trainer manual's progress log so it can be checked against improvement at the start of Section C3.

### Exercise 4 — Stretch task: preview of Section C3

This is a deliberately unscored exercise. The learner has not yet been taught the formal vocabulary for cols / saddles or cliff bands.

For snippet 9 (col / saddle): a strong sketch should show two high points with a clear low point between them. Some learners will sketch the col as a smooth dip; some will sketch it as a sharp notch. Either is fine at this stage. Coach learners who draw two unrelated peaks (without the linking low point) towards looking again at the connected nature of the feature.

For snippet 10 (cliff band): a strong sketch should show a sudden steep face running across the slope, with gentler ground above and below. Some learners will sketch the cliff face square-on; some will draw it foreshortened. Either is fine. Coach learners who draw the cliff as merely "very steep ground" towards recognising that merged contours indicate ground too steep to walk on, not just steep ground.

Do not introduce the names "col" or "cliff band" formally during marking. Save the formal teaching for C3.1 and C3.3 respectively. The point here is the visual seed.
