### Exercise 1 — Draw the section line

For the standard extract:

1. Section line length on the map: **8.4 cm**.
2. Equivalent ground distance: **2,100 m** (8.4 × 250).

Mark by checking that the learner has drawn a straight line between X and Y using a ruler. A wobbly or freehand line will produce wrong contour-crossing readings later. Insist on the ruler.

| Crossing # | Distance from X (cm) | Contour value (m) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0.0 | 2,200 |
| 2 | 0.4 | 2,210 |
| 3 | 1.0 | 2,220 |

Mark by checking that the learner has noted **every** contour crossing rather than skipping ones that look close together, and that the recorded distance increases monotonically along the line. Skipped crossings are the most common cause of incorrectly steep or flat sections later.

### Exercise 3 — Build the cross-section

A correct cross-section will show:

- a horizontal axis 8.4 cm long (matching the section line),
- a vertical axis covering 2,190 m to 2,310 m (one 10 m margin top and bottom),
- vertical exaggeration 2:1, with the page distance per metre of elevation set to **20 mm per 100 m** (giving 100 m of elevation = 2 cm on the page, which is twice what 100 m would occupy at true 1:25,000 scale of 4 mm per 100 m),
- both axes labelled with scale and the vertical-exaggeration figure (2:1),
- and 11 plotted points joined by a smooth line that is convex upwards in places and concave in others, depending on the standard extract.

For the standard extract, the cross-section between X and Y reads as a slope that steepens slightly past the halfway point and then eases back towards Y, with no major breaks. The line is approximately concave upwards from X to crossing 6, then approximately straight from crossing 6 to Y.

Common errors to mark down:

- Vertical scale not labelled.
- Vertical exaggeration not stated.
- Contour crossings plotted at uniform spacing along the horizontal axis instead of at their actual distances from X (a common simplification that destroys the shape).
- Smoothing through important breaks (a plateau read as a continuous slope, for example).

### Exercise 4 — Read the cross-section

For the standard extract:

1. Highest point: **2,300 m at 8.4 cm from X (point Y)**.
2. Lowest point: **2,200 m at 0.0 cm from X (point X)**.
3. Steepest section: between crossings 1 and 2 (0.0 to 0.4 cm from X), where contour spacing on the section line is approximately 0.4 cm. On a 1:25,000 map with 10 m contours, 0.4 cm = 4 mm spacing, which puts that section in the moderate-to-steep range (around 22 to 25 degrees).
4. Gentlest section: between crossings 6 and 11, where spacing is approximately 1 cm per contour (10 mm). That puts the section at gentle ground (around 9 degrees).
5. Named features the learner can identify: the cross-section shape suggests a slope with a slight steepening near the bottom, easing as the slope rises. No closed loops on the section, no V-shape: the section is a single flank rather than a feature in its own right.

Mark by checking the learner has read steepness from contour spacing on the section line (not from the visual angle of the cross-section drawing, which is exaggerated). The C1.2 rule of thumb still governs.

### Exercise 5 — Compare with a model

There is no fixed answer. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- produced a comparison rather than a generic "looks similar",
- identified at least one specific section where their cross-section deviates from the model,
- attributed the deviation to a specific cause (missed crossing, wrong scale, ruler off the X-Y line, smoothing),
- and stated what they would change next time.

The single most common cause of error is a missed contour crossing in a steep section. The second most common is a vertical-scale labelling slip. Both are easy to fix once named.
