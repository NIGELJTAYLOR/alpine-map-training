### Exercise 1 — Identify the terrain traps

For the standard Section C6.2 extract, the terrain traps are:

| # | Feature type | Location |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Narrow gully | Central north-east section, running from approximately 2,150 m down to 1,900 m, with rock-walled lower section |
| 2 | Bowl with single exit | Upper west bowl, opening to the south through a constricted mouth at approximately 2,200 m |
| 3 | Slope-above-cliff | Open slope at 2,400 m feeding directly onto the cliff band on the eastern flank |
| 4 | Convex-roll-into-gully | Slope steepening from 2,300 m down to the head of the central gully |

Mark by checking that the learner has:

- named the feature type (gully, bowl, slope-above-cliff, convex roll),
- given a location reference (a contour value, a compass bearing, a named feature),
- and recognised at least three of the four configurations on the standard extract.

A learner who identifies only one or two has not yet read the extract critically. Coach to look for combinations: a steep slope alone is not a trap; a steep slope above a cliff is.

### Exercise 2 — Identify the committing terrain

For the standard extract:

1. **The slope-above-cliff on the eastern flank.** Once entered, the cliff below means the line cannot be reversed by skinning back up.
2. **The mouth of the western bowl.** Once skied into, the constricted mouth is the only exit.

Accept also the upper section of the central gully, if the learner argues that lateral exit becomes impossible once the gully narrows below 2,000 m.

### Exercise 3 — Identify a runout zone

For the standard extract, the runout zone of the central gully is the basin at 1,900 m where the gully terminates. A learner crossing this basin during skinning is at risk from any slide that releases on the upper slope or in the gully above, even if they themselves are on flat ground.

A strong one-sentence answer should identify the runout zone and link the risk to the upstream slope rather than to the basin itself. If the learner says "the basin is safe because it's flat", coach: the basin is the runout for the slope above; flat does not mean safe.

### Exercise 4 — Find a convex roll

For the standard extract, the most prominent convex roll is between 2,300 m and 2,200 m on the slope above the central gully:

1. Location: above the gully head on the central flank.
2. Contour spacing immediately above the roll: approximately 6 mm.
3. Contour spacing immediately below the roll: approximately 3 mm.
4. Route relevance: the slope steepens going down, concentrating stress at the steep transition. A skier crossing the roll will be heaviest exactly where the slope is steepest, which is where avalanche-trigger forces are highest. The convex shape combined with the gully below produces a textbook trigger-and-funnel configuration.

Accept any convex roll the learner identifies on the extract, with the same three pieces of information.

### Exercise 5 — Plan a safer alternative

For the standard extract, a safer alternative to the central gully descent is a line down the western shoulder, descending the broader open slope to the basin at 1,900 m. The trade-offs:

1. **Extra distance.** The shoulder line is approximately 400 m longer than the gully line.
2. **Extra ascent on the day's overall route**, because the shoulder requires a brief contour-following traverse to reach.
3. **Different aspect.** The shoulder is a south-east aspect; the gully line was on a north-east aspect. The aspect change has snow-quality implications.

A strong answer will explain that the alternative still meets the day's objective (reaching the basin at 1,900 m) and will name a condition under which even the alternative would be unsuitable (for example, danger level 4 across all aspects, or a heightened-danger band that extends to the shoulder aspect at the relevant elevation).

If the learner cannot find a safer alternative, accept the answer "no safer alternative exists on this extract" if it is supported by an explicit reading of the extract. The right IMS / EMS response to that finding is to abandon the day's objective.
