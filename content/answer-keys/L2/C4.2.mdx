### Exercise 1 — Add the north arrow

Mark by checking that the north arrow has been drawn parallel to the eastings (the vertical grid lines), pointing up the page if the map is in conventional orientation. If the map has a printed north arrow already, the learner should have copied it accurately.

If the learner draws the arrow towards a corner of the page or in the direction of a named feature, coach: the north arrow is geometric, not local. It always points up the grid.

### Exercise 2 — Label the aspect

For the standard Section C4.2 extract:

| Slope | Fall-line arrow direction | Aspect |
|---|---|---|
| A | South-east | South-east |
| B | South | South |
| C | West | West |
| D | North-east | North-east |

The fall-line direction and the aspect are the same: the slope faces in the direction it falls.

Mark by checking the learner has used eight directions (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW) rather than four (N, E, S, W). The four-direction reading is too coarse for IMS / EMS work and the avalanche bulletin will use eight. Accept finer divisions (NNE, ENE, etc.) without penalty if the learner volunteers them.

### Exercise 3 — Aspect and snow

Model answer for two slopes from the standard extract:

**Slope A (south-east):**

1. Sunny mid-winter day: morning sun, warming through the day; snow can become wind-glazed or melt-affected by mid-afternoon depending on temperature. Lower-quality skiing in the afternoon.
2. After a stormy north-westerly: this slope is on the lee side of the prevailing wind, so it accumulates wind-loaded snow. The combination of sun and wind-loading is unfavourable; potential cornice build-up.
3. Skiing preference depends on the time. Early morning before sun-affect: acceptable. Mid-afternoon: not preferred.

**Slope D (north-east):**

1. Sunny mid-winter day: morning sun then afternoon shade; snow stays drier and longer-lasting through the day. Often the best mid-winter aspect.
2. After a stormy north-westerly: also on the lee side of the prevailing wind, so it accumulates wind-loaded snow. The combination of cold shade and wind-loading is the classic persistent-weak-layer aspect.
3. Skiing preference: better than slope A on a cold day; less attractive than slope A in late season because it stays cold and unforgiving.

A strong learner answer does not need to match the model exactly. Mark for:

- the right hemisphere reasoning (north-facing cold and snow-holding in the northern hemisphere),
- recognition that wind-loading depends on the prevailing wind direction not the aspect alone,
- and a specific reason for the preferred-slope choice rather than "because I prefer powder".

### Exercise 4 — Bulletin to terrain

For the standard extract, the heightened-danger band (aspect NE through NW going through N, elevation above 2,200 m) covers approximately:

- the upper north-facing flank in the north of the extract,
- the upper north-east bowl below the main summit,
- the north-west spur of the secondary ridge.

A strong route answer should:

- name a specific destination point on the extract (a col, a hut, a junction),
- describe a route that stays on aspects from E to W going through S, or below 2,200 m, or both,
- and acknowledge a place where the route gets close to the heightened-danger band and what they do about it (descend, traverse below, choose an alternative).

If the learner shades the extract correctly but cannot find a viable route, that is information about the extract, not about the learner. Some extracts will be heavily shaded; the right answer in that case is to choose a different objective for the day. Coach this point: the bulletin's role is to direct the route, not just to inform it.

### Exercise 5 — Aspect on a ridge or spur

For trainer reference, using the C2.3 standard extract spur (Feature A in C2.3 Exercise 1):

1. Left flank (facing along the crest from 2,380 m towards 2,200 m): **north-west**.
2. Right flank: **south-east**.
3. Northern hemisphere winter ascent preference: the **north-west** flank holds colder, drier snow and is less affected by sun. It is also more likely to hold consistent snow cover where the south-east flank may have bare patches or melt-freeze surfaces. Strong learners may also note that the prevailing wind direction can complicate this default; if the prevailing wind is from the north-west, the south-east flank may be wind-loaded and the north-west flank scoured.

Accept either flank as the answer if the reasoning is sound. The IMS / EMS-relevant skill is the explicit aspect reading and the snow reasoning, not the specific choice.
