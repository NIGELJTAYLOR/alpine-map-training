*Note: model answers depend on the trainer's choice of A, B, C, D points and pre-sketched lines. The marking framework below applies to any standard configuration.*

### Stops A, B, C, D — shared marking framework

For each stop, mark by checking that the learner has:

1. **Identified the feature correctly** in one phrase, with a contour-pattern reason.
2. **Used the four-part description structure** (underfoot, slope and aspect, near features, far features).
3. **Used eight-direction aspect language** consistently.
4. **Used C1.2 steepness vocabulary** consistently.
5. **Referred to specific features** visible from the stop (named summit, valley, col, spur), not generic ones.

A learner who produces a strong description at A but lets the structure collapse by B or C has lost discipline under length. Coach back to the structure each time it slips.

For trainer reference, the standard A → B → C → D route is configured to span four feature families:

| Stop | Standard feature type |
|---|---|
| A | Col or saddle (start of the day, on a ridge between two valleys) |
| B | Bowl (mid-route, on a slope facing into a corrie) |
| C | Spur (descending towards a valley floor) |
| D | Valley floor with stream (end of the day, near a hut or junction) |

The four families test different aspects of the description structure. The col tests aspect on a saddle; the bowl tests structured reading (headwall, flanks); the spur tests two-flank aspect; the valley floor tests the underfoot detail and overhead-exposure awareness.

### Legs A→B, B→C, C→D — shared marking framework

Mark by checking that the learner has:

1. **Identified the line type** (direct, contour-following, zig-zag).
2. **Read the average gradient** from contour spacing using the C1.2 rule.
3. **Applied the additional check for that leg** (the comfortable skinning band on A→B, the bulletin on B→C, the terrain-trap reading on C→D).

For the standard configuration:

| Leg | Standard line type | Standard gradient |
|---|---|---|
| A → B | Contour-following round the side of the bowl | Approximately 8 to 9 degrees |
| B → C | Zig-zag with kick turns down the spur head | Approximately 10 to 12 degrees on the kick-turn approaches |
| C → D | Direct down the spur to the valley | Approximately 12 to 15 degrees, depending on where the line meets the lower flank |

### Exercise 1 — Stop A

Standard feature type: **Col or saddle** (start of the day, on a ridge between two valleys). Apply the shared stops marking framework above. The col tests aspect on a saddle: a strong description names the two valleys either side and the two summits on the ridge, with eight-direction aspect language for the saddle itself.

### Exercise 2 — Leg A → B

Standard line type: **contour-following round the side of the bowl**. Standard gradient: **approximately 8 to 9 degrees**. Apply the shared legs marking framework above.

Additional check (comfortable skinning band): a strong answer confirms the gradient sits inside the 8 to 12 degree comfortable band and justifies the contour-following choice against a direct line that would force a steeper gradient.

### Exercise 3 — Stop B

Standard feature type: **Bowl** (mid-route, on a slope facing into a corrie). Apply the shared stops marking framework above. The bowl tests structured reading: a strong description names the headwall, the flanks, and the bowl floor separately, with aspect read at the headwall (the steepest part).

### Exercise 4 — Leg B → C

Standard line type: **zig-zag with kick turns down the spur head**. Standard gradient: **approximately 10 to 12 degrees on the kick-turn approaches**. Apply the shared legs marking framework above.

Additional check (bulletin: "Danger level 3. Heightened danger on N through E aspects above 2,200 m"): for the standard extract, the leg passes briefly through the upper part of the bowl on a north-east aspect at 2,250 m, which is inside the heightened-danger band. A strong answer notes this and proposes one of: avoid the bowl entirely, traverse below 2,200 m, or accept the brief crossing as a calculated risk with stated mitigations (timing, group spacing, terrain selection within the band).

### Exercise 5 — Stop C

Standard feature type: **Spur** (descending towards a valley floor). Apply the shared stops marking framework above. The spur tests two-flank aspect: a strong description names the two flanks of the spur with different aspect readings for each, and identifies the fall line down the spur crest.

### Exercise 6 — Leg C → D

Standard line type: **direct down the spur to the valley**. Standard gradient: **approximately 12 to 15 degrees, depending on where the line meets the lower flank**. Apply the shared legs marking framework above.

Additional check (terrain trap): for the standard extract, the lower spur runs above a small cliff band that would catch a slide from above. A strong answer identifies the slope-above-cliff configuration and proposes either a slightly different line (further along the spur to a gap in the cliff) or a stated awareness that the line is safe only if the upper slope is stable.

### Exercise 7 — Stop D

Standard feature type: **Valley floor with stream** (end of the day, near a hut or junction). Apply the shared stops marking framework above. The valley floor tests underfoot detail (stream, snowpack at the bottom) and overhead-exposure awareness (what slopes run down towards this point).

### Exercise 8 — R1: Hardest section to picture

There is no fixed answer; the reflection is the diagnostic. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- named a specific stop or leg, not "all of it",
- named the specific component skill that was hard (contour pattern, aspect, route choice, terrain trap),
- and articulated the difficulty in their own words.

### Exercise 9 — R2: Strongest moment

There is no fixed answer. Mark by checking that the learner has named a specific moment and a specific habit they want to keep. Vague "I did well at the col" is less useful than "the four-part structure held at the col because I had used it twice already and it had become automatic".

### Exercise 10 — R3: Level 3 readiness

The answer is the learner's, not the trainer's. The trainer's role is to verify the answer is honest:

- A "yes" answer should be supported by a clean C7.1 quiz, a strong C7.2 narration, and an error log of minor items only.
- A "not quite" answer should name specific pages.
- A "no" answer should name a specific section or block of sections to redo.

If the learner's R3 answer disagrees with the evidence (a "yes" with five wrong answers in C7.1, for example), coach the discrepancy openly rather than override the answer. The honesty of the readiness check is what makes it useful.
