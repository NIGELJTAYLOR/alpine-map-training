*Note: model answers depend on the trainer's choice of A, B, C, D points and pre-sketched lines. The marking framework below applies to any standard configuration.*

### Stops A, B, C, D — feature identification and standing description

For each stop, mark by checking that the learner has:

1. **Identified the feature correctly** in one phrase, with a contour-pattern reason.
2. **Used the four-part description structure** (underfoot, slope and aspect, near features, far features).
3. **Used eight-direction aspect language** consistently.
4. **Used C1.2 steepness vocabulary** consistently.
5. **Referred to specific features** visible from the stop (named summit, valley, col, spur), not generic ones.

A learner who produces a strong description at A but lets the structure collapse by B or C has lost discipline under length. Coach back to the structure each time it slips.

For trainer reference, the standard A → B → C → D route is configured to span four feature families:

| Stop | Standard feature type |
|---|---|
| A | Col or saddle (start of the day, on a ridge between two valleys) |
| B | Bowl (mid-route, on a slope facing into a corrie) |
| C | Spur (descending towards a valley floor) |
| D | Valley floor with stream (end of the day, near a hut or junction) |

The four families test different aspects of the description structure. The col tests aspect on a saddle; the bowl tests structured reading (headwall, flanks); the spur tests two-flank aspect; the valley floor tests the underfoot detail and overhead-exposure awareness.

### Legs A→B, B→C, C→D — line type, gradient, and additional checks

Mark by checking that the learner has:

1. **Identified the line type** (direct, contour-following, zig-zag).
2. **Read the average gradient** from contour spacing using the C1.2 rule.
3. **Applied the additional check for that leg** (the comfortable skinning band on A→B, the bulletin on B→C, the terrain-trap reading on C→D).

For the standard configuration:

| Leg | Standard line type | Standard gradient |
|---|---|---|
| A → B | Contour-following round the side of the bowl | Approximately 8 to 9 degrees |
| B → C | Zig-zag with kick turns down the spur head | Approximately 10 to 12 degrees on the kick-turn approaches |
| C → D | Direct down the spur to the valley | Approximately 12 to 15 degrees, depending on where the line meets the lower flank |

The bulletin check on B → C asks whether the leg passes through heightened-danger ground (N through E aspects above 2,200 m). For the standard extract, the leg passes briefly through the upper part of the bowl on a north-east aspect at 2,250 m, which is inside the heightened-danger band. A strong answer notes this and proposes one of: avoid the bowl entirely, traverse below 2,200 m, or accept the brief crossing as a calculated risk with stated mitigations (timing, group spacing, terrain selection within the band).

The terrain-trap check on C → D asks whether the leg passes near a terrain trap. For the standard extract, the lower spur runs above a small cliff band that would catch a slide from above. A strong answer identifies the slope-above-cliff configuration and proposes either a slightly different line (further along the spur to a gap in the cliff) or a stated awareness that the line is safe only if the upper slope is stable.

### Closing reflection — R1, R2, R3

There are no fixed answers; the reflection is the diagnostic.

For **R1 (hardest section to picture)**, mark by checking that the learner has:

- named a specific stop or leg, not "all of it",
- named the specific component skill that was hard (contour pattern, aspect, route choice, terrain trap),
- and articulated the difficulty in their own words.

For **R2 (strongest moment)**, mark by checking that the learner has named a specific moment and a specific habit they want to keep. Vague "I did well at the col" is less useful than "the four-part structure held at the col because I had used it twice already and it had become automatic".

For **R3 (Level 3 readiness)**, the answer is the learner's, not the trainer's. The trainer's role is to verify the answer is honest:

- A "yes" answer should be supported by a clean C7.1 quiz, a strong C7.2 narration, and an error log of minor items only.
- A "not quite" answer should name specific pages.
- A "no" answer should name a specific section or block of sections to redo.

If the learner's R3 answer disagrees with the evidence (a "yes" with five wrong answers in C7.1, for example), coach the discrepancy openly rather than override the answer. The honesty of the readiness check is what makes it useful.
