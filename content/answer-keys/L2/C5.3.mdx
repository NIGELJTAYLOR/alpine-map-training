### Exercise 1 — First description

There is no fixed answer; the model depends on the trainer's chosen point P1. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- used all four parts of the structure (underfoot, slope and aspect, near features, far features),
- written at least one sentence per part,
- used compass-aspect terms (eight directions, not four),
- used the C1.2 steepness vocabulary (gentle, moderate, steep, very steep),
- referred to specific features visible from the chosen point on the map (named summit, named col, named valley if labelled, otherwise contour-shape features),
- and avoided describing the symbol rather than the imagined view (a common slip is "there are lots of contours" rather than "the ground is steep").

A strong description will read like the worked example: a point of view in plain English, structured by the four parts, accurate to the map.

For trainer reference, the standard P1 in the standard extract is on the eastern flank of a 2,650 m summit at approximately 2,300 m. A model description for P1:

1. **Underfoot.** I am standing on a moderate east-facing snow slope, with the ground rising steeply to my west and falling to my east into a broad valley.
2. **Slope and aspect.** The slope falls east at approximately 22 to 25 degrees on the C1.2 rule of thumb, just below the 30 degree avalanche threshold.
3. **Near features.** The summit is about 350 m above me to the west; the valley floor with a stream is approximately 600 m below me to the east.
4. **Far features.** Across the valley, a continuous ridge runs north to south at approximately 2,500 m; on the horizon to the north-east, the spot height of a peak at 3,150 m is just visible.

### Exercise 2 — Second description, on a different feature type

Mark on the same criteria as Exercise 1. The point P2 is set on a different feature type by design; the test is whether the learner adapts the structure to the new feature without the structure breaking down.

Common errors when the feature changes:

- on a summit, learners forget the four-part structure because "I'd just see lots of mountains";
- on a ridge, learners describe only one side rather than both flanks;
- in a valley, learners forget the far-features sentence because the surrounding high ground blocks the distant view.

The structure should adapt rather than collapse. Coach the adaptation explicitly.

### Exercise 3 — Third description, with poor visibility added

Mark on the same criteria as Exercises 1 and 2, plus the poor-visibility adaptation:

- the underfoot sentence becomes more important (the learner can still feel the ground),
- the slope and aspect sentence becomes a map-derived statement,
- near features may or may not be visible at 30 m (a contour feature within 30 m might be visible; one further away is inferred from the map),
- far features are entirely inferred from the map and should be phrased "I cannot see X but the map tells me X is at...".

A learner who writes the same description for poor visibility as for clear visibility has not engaged with the visibility constraint. Coach this explicitly.

### Exercise 4 — Self-criticism

There is no fixed answer. Mark by checking that the learner has:

- engaged with the self-criticism honestly rather than declaring all three descriptions equally good,
- identified a specific weakness in the underfoot sentence and a specific weakness in the far features sentence,
- and named one specific habit they want to make automatic before Section C6.

The self-criticism is the diagnostic that closes Section C5. If a learner cannot identify a weakness, ask them to read aloud and listen for repetitive language ("there is", "you can see") or for missing parts of the structure. The act of reading aloud usually surfaces the problem.
