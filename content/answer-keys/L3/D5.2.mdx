### Exercise 1 - Calibration drill at home

There is no fixed answer; the discrepancy depends on the candidate's altimeter, route, and the day's weather. Mark by checking that:

- the candidate set the altimeter to their known home elevation,
- the candidate walked a route of known elevation change,
- the discrepancy between expected and read elevation is recorded,
- and the candidate has named a likely explanation (drift since calibration, calibration error, route uncertainty).

A discrepancy of less than 5 m indicates the altimeter is performing well in stable conditions. A discrepancy of 10 to 20 m indicates moderate drift, common in active weather. A discrepancy of more than 30 m suggests calibration error or a significantly active weather system.

If the candidate cannot identify a plausible explanation for the discrepancy, prompt for the three options on the page (drift, calibration error, route uncertainty).

### Exercise 2 - Identify reliable known points

There is no fixed answer; the points depend on the candidate's exam-area map. Mark by checking that:

- five points are listed,
- each point has a precisely known altitude (a marked spot height, a hut elevation, a lift station altitude),
- and each point is something the candidate would physically stand on (not just be near).

For trainer reference, typical reliable known points on an alpine map:

- The morning lift station with its published altitude.
- A summit spot height at the top of the day's main ascent.
- A col spot height where the day's tour crosses a ridge.
- A hut or refuge with its marked elevation, used as a lunch stop or overnight.
- The afternoon lift station for the descent.

A candidate who lists "the col" without naming an altitude is using an unreliable point; the col may be 10 m above or below a marked spot height.

### Exercise 3 - Distinguish reliable from unreliable

| Point | Reliable? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Marked summit at 2,847 m, candidate at the spot height marker | **Yes** | Precisely known altitude; candidate physically at the point. |
| Col labelled with a spot height of 2,718 m | **Yes** | Spot height is precisely known; col is a clearly identifiable point. |
| Junction of two paths at approximately 2,400 m (no marker) | **No** | "Approximately" is not a precisely known altitude; the candidate may be 5 m above or below. |
| Hut labelled "Refuge des Ecrins, 2,340 m" | **Yes** | Hut elevation is published; candidate is physically at the hut level. |
| A point on a slope where the altimeter happens to read 2,500 m | **No** | Calibrating to whatever the altimeter currently reads is circular; no independent reference. |

Mark for the right yes/no judgements and for reasons that name the specific failure mode (approximate altitude, no independent reference, etc.).

### Exercise 4 - Why recalibrate so often

A strong answer should make two points:

1. **Drift between calibrations is unavoidable** because weather pressure changes during the day; an altimeter calibrated only at the start is wrong by mid-afternoon in active weather.
2. **The cost of recalibrating is small** (15 seconds at each known point) and the benefit is large (the altimeter stays accurate to within a few metres throughout the day).

A bonus point: the recalibration moment is also a weather observation. The discrepancy between the altimeter and the known point at recalibration tells the candidate how much pressure has changed since the last calibration, which is information about how the weather is evolving.

If the candidate answers only "to keep the altimeter accurate", coach the explicit pressure-drift mechanism and the discrepancy-as-weather-observation use.
