### Exercise 1 - Three-day pressure log

There is no fixed answer; the readings depend on the candidate's barometer and the local weather. Mark by checking that:

- the candidate recorded readings every two hours during waking hours,
- three full days of data are present,
- and the readings vary across the period (any barometer shows some change over three days; complete consistency suggests careless reading).

If the candidate cannot record at every two-hour interval, accept four readings per day as a minimum (8am, noon, 4pm, 8pm) provided the rate calculations in Exercise 3 still work.

### Exercise 2 - Plot the readings

Mark by checking that the plot:

- has time on the horizontal axis and pressure on the vertical axis,
- uses a sensible vertical scale (typically 5 to 20 hPa range covers most weather variation),
- shows three distinct day-curves (or one continuous curve over the three days),
- and is readable at a glance.

A candidate who plots the data correctly but with no scale or labels has missed the point of the plot. Coach the labelling.

### Exercise 3 - Classify the trends

There is no fixed answer; the trend depends on the candidate's data. For trainer reference, the classification rules:

- **Rising:** any positive rate sustained for at least 3 hours.
- **Steady:** less than 0.1 hPa/hr change sustained for at least 3 hours.
- **Slow fall:** rate between -0.1 and -0.5 hPa/hr.
- **Moderate fall:** rate between -0.5 and -1.0 hPa/hr.
- **Rapid fall:** rate steeper than -1.0 hPa/hr.

If a day shows mixed trends (rising in the morning, falling in the afternoon), classify the dominant trend (the longer or steeper section) and note the variation.

### Exercise 4 - Predict the next 12 hours

There is no fixed answer; the prediction depends on the candidate's data. Mark by checking:

- the prediction is consistent with the trend at the end of Day 3,
- the prediction names a specific weather expectation (improving / stable / deteriorating; possibly with intensity),
- and the prediction includes a time element (the next 12 hours, with possible deterioration starting in 6 to 8 hours, for example).

A vague "weather might change" is not enough; push for specificity.

### Exercise 5 - Cross-check with a forecast

There is no fixed answer; the comparison depends on the candidate's data and the forecast they look up. Mark by checking:

- the forecast is from a reputable source (national weather service, established mountain weather service, not a generic phone weather app),
- the forecast covers the same three-day period as the candidate's data,
- the candidate has identified specific points where their barometer led or lagged the forecast,
- and the candidate has reflected on what the comparison tells them about the value of the barometer for short-term forecasting.

A typical pattern: the barometer leads the forecast for short-term events (an unexpected pressure drop at midday gives 1 to 2 hours of warning that a local forecast may not capture), but the forecast leads for longer-term events (a system 24 hours away is forecast accurately but is not yet visible in the barometer).

If the candidate concludes the barometer is "more accurate than the forecast" or "less accurate than the forecast", coach the both-have-their-uses framing.
