### Exercise 1 - Run the interference test

There is no fixed answer; the distances depend on the candidate's specific equipment. Mark by checking that:

- each piece of equipment listed is something the candidate routinely carries,
- the first-deflection distance is measured (not estimated),
- a 20 cm margin is added for the personal safe distance,
- and the personal safe distance is at least the workbook-recommended figure (50 cm for steel objects, 30 cm for electronics).

For trainer reference, typical first-deflection distances:

| Equipment | Typical first-deflection distance |
|---|---|
| Mobile phone | 15 to 25 cm |
| Steel ski binding | 25 to 40 cm |
| Ice axe | 25 to 35 cm |
| Avalanche transceiver (transmit) | 10 to 20 cm |

A candidate whose figures are markedly different from these may have a particularly strong (or weak) magnet in their specific equipment, or may have measured at an unusual angle. Re-test if the figure looks anomalous.

### Exercise 2 - Identify the highest-risk source

There is no fixed answer; the highest-risk source depends on the candidate's equipment. Most candidates find that **steel ski bindings** are the highest-risk source because they are physically close to the compass when the candidate is wearing skis and using the compass at chest height.

A strong management plan should include at least one of:

- holding the compass at chest height (well above the binding),
- removing skis when taking a precise bearing if practical,
- noting that on-ski bearings are slightly less accurate and accepting the trade-off,
- or using the compass on a flat surface (a notebook on the snow) rather than near the bindings when taking critical bearings.

Mark for the candidate having a specific management action, not just an awareness statement.

### Exercise 3 - Quick-check habit

The four instrument-hygiene checks:

1. **Inspect the baseplate for cracks.** A cracked baseplate flexes when held flat and tilts the compass. Replace if cracked.
2. **Check the damping fluid.** A clouded or pierced compass capsule means the needle will not settle reliably. Replace.
3. **Check the bezel turns smoothly.** A stuck or sluggish bezel produces inconsistent bearings. Try cleaning gently; replace if persistent.
4. **Run a quick-check against a known direction.** Set the compass to a known direction (a building edge, a road, a ridge crest) at the start of every tour and confirm it agrees. Disagreement is the cue to investigate.

Mark for all four named with a one-sentence action for each. A candidate who names only the quick-check has missed the equipment-condition checks.

### Exercise 4 - When the compass disagrees with a known direction

For the worked scenario (compass reads 178 when expected 180):

1. **Yes, a 2-degree discrepancy is acceptable.** It is at the boundary of the IMS / EMS reading-accuracy standard. Note the discrepancy and proceed.
2. **Action before using the compass for the day:** re-check in a different location, confirm no nearby interference source. If the discrepancy persists in interference-free conditions, accept the 2-degree offset as the compass's calibration and apply it consistently to all bearings for the day.
3. **Do not use the compass at all if the discrepancy is more than approximately 5 degrees** in interference-free conditions. The compass is then sufficiently inaccurate to produce bearings that cannot be trusted; use the candidate's backup compass or postpone the tour.

Mark for:

- the right judgement on the 2-degree case (acceptable; proceed),
- the right diagnostic (re-check in interference-free conditions before accepting),
- and the right threshold for "do not use" (around 5 degrees).

A candidate who would not use the compass at all on a 2-degree discrepancy is over-cautious; coach the working standard. A candidate who would use the compass on a 10-degree discrepancy without troubleshooting is under-cautious; coach the limit.
