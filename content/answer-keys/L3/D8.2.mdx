### Exercise 1 - When the pole-and-cord adds value

There is no fixed answer; the scenario depends on the candidate's exam-area map. Mark by checking that the scenario:

- has visibility low enough that a partner cannot serve as a standard intermediate aiming point (typically less than 10 m),
- has a hidden terrain hazard (drop, cornice, sudden steepening) that simple partner-walking would not detect,
- and is one where the candidate would actually need to move (rather than shelter and wait).

A scenario that meets only one or two of the three conditions is not the right scenario for the pole-and-cord technique. Coach the conditional use.

### Exercise 2 - When simpler techniques would be preferable

| Scenario | Pole-and-cord, or simpler? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Visibility 30 m, broad gentle snow slope | **Simpler (intermediate aiming points)** | Visibility allows a partner at 20 m to be visible; no hidden hazards on a gentle slope. |
| Visibility 5 m, ridge with cornice on one side | **Pole-and-cord** | Low visibility plus terrain hazard plus need to move. All three conditions met. |
| Visibility zero, group at a hut | **Simpler (shelter and wait)** | If the candidate can avoid moving, that is preferable to any navigation technique. |
| Visibility 8 m, descending from a col with cliff band 200 m below | **Pole-and-cord** | Low visibility plus terrain hazard (cliff band) plus need to descend. All three conditions met. |

Mark for the right judgement on each. A candidate who uses pole-and-cord for the gentle slope has missed the conditional use; coach the simpler-when-possible principle.

### Exercise 3 - Practical drill on snow

There is no fixed answer; the drill produces the candidate's own measurements. Mark by checking that:

- the candidate practised the technique with a partner,
- the cord was visibly on the bearing during the drill,
- the scout stopped reliably at the cord end,
- the time for the 30 m leg is recorded (typically 8 to 12 minutes),
- and the candidate has reflected on what would change in real flat light (the drill in good visibility is much faster and easier than the same procedure in actual whiteout).

If the practical drill is not possible, the trainer should demonstrate or show the Glenmore Lodge YouTube reference (linked from the IMS Nav Programme PDF source list). The technique is hard to fully grasp without seeing it.

### Exercise 4 - The "know it even if you never use it" framing

A strong answer should make two points:

1. **Why "know it":** the assessor may probe with "how would you handle X" and the candidate needs the technique in their answer. The technique is also the right answer in the rare conditions where it applies.
2. **Why "rarely use":** the conditions for appropriate use are narrow (very low visibility plus hidden hazard plus need to move). In most conditions, simpler techniques (intermediate aiming points; shelter and wait) are preferable.

The candidate's answer to the assessor's "how would you handle a whiteout descent above a cornice?" should reference the conditional logic: assess whether all three conditions hold; if yes, deploy pole-and-cord; if not, use the appropriate simpler technique.

A candidate who answers "I would use pole-and-cord for any whiteout" has missed the conditional. A candidate who answers "I would never use pole-and-cord" has missed the appropriate use.
