### Exercise 1 - 24-hour stationary drill

There is no fixed answer; the readings depend on the candidate's altimeter and local weather. Mark by checking that:

- the candidate has set the altimeter to their known home elevation as the start,
- four (or more) readings are recorded at meaningful intervals,
- the apparent altitude change from start is calculated for each reading,
- and the readings vary across the 24-hour period (any altimeter on a normal day shows some drift; complete consistency suggests the altimeter has not been read carefully).

For trainer reference, typical patterns:

- **Stable high pressure:** total drift over 24 hours under 5 m. Altimeter behaves as a reliable altitude reference.
- **Approaching weather system (falling pressure):** drift of 20 to 50 m, all in the same direction (apparent altitude rising).
- **Passing front (variable pressure):** drift of 30 m or more with direction change during the period.

A candidate whose readings show no drift may have been reading the same digit each time without checking; coach the careful re-read.

### Exercise 2 - Calculate the implied pressure change

There is no fixed answer; the calculation depends on the candidate's largest apparent change. Mark by checking the arithmetic.

For example: a 24 m apparent altitude change implies a pressure change of approximately 24 / 8 = **3 hPa**.

For the direction:

- **Apparent altitude rising** (altimeter reading higher than start): implies pressure **falling**. Weather expected: deteriorating.
- **Apparent altitude falling** (altimeter reading lower than start): implies pressure **rising**. Weather expected: improving.

A candidate who reverses the direction has the relationship inverted. Drill the mnemonic: "weather going down, altimeter going up".

### Exercise 3 - Distinguish barometric from GPS

A strong answer should:

- name **barometric altimeter** as measuring atmospheric pressure (mechanical, drifts with weather, more accurate when calibrated),
- name **GPS altimeter** as measuring altitude via satellite signals (electronic, does not drift with weather, less accurate, requires satellite signal),
- and identify the IMS / EMS-relevant primary tool as the **barometric altimeter**, with GPS as backup.

The reason for the primary-tool choice should mention either the accuracy advantage when calibrated, or the no-satellite-signal-needed advantage in steep terrain or under cloud, or both.

If the candidate names GPS as the primary tool, coach: GPS altimeters are useful but their lower accuracy and signal dependency make them backup rather than primary in the IMS / EMS toolkit.

### Exercise 4 - When the altimeter is preferred over GPS

Acceptable scenarios include:

- **Steep terrain or under cloud** where satellite signal is poor or unavailable.
- **Battery management** on long tours where GPS battery life is a concern.
- **High accuracy required for a specific task** (such as the aspect-of-slope relocation in D7.2, where the altimeter's 1-to-3 m accuracy is part of the technique).
- **Cross-check** when the GPS altimeter is showing a reading that does not match expectations.

Mark for any specific scenario with a clear reason. A vague "the altimeter is more reliable" is coaching opportunity; push for the specific situation.
