The D7 triplet is the integration block. After D7 the candidate has the full instrument toolkit (compass, altimeter, barometer, inclinometer) and knows how to use them in combination.

D7.1 (bearing plus altimeter) is the most commonly used integration on real tours. A candidate who is fluent on D7.1 has the standard poor-visibility waypoint technique.

D7.2 (aspect-of-slope) is the IMS / EMS-relevant alpine-specific technique. The four-stage procedure is the assessor's expected answer to "what do you do when resection does not work?" A candidate who can execute the procedure in under two minutes on a known slope, and who can recognise when it works (and when it does not), is at the working assessment standard.

D7.3 (inclinometer) is the field cross-check on the map estimate. The convex-roll detection is the most route-relevant application; a candidate who can identify a convex roll from a multi-measurement inclinometer pattern has the avalanche-aware terrain reading skill.

If a candidate is borderline at the end of D7, do not push on to D8. The poor-visibility navigation in D8 assumes confident integrated-techniques fluency; without it, the D8 sequence becomes a list of techniques rather than an integrated response.
