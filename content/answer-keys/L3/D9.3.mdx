### Exercise 1 - Identify decision points on your tour

There is no fixed answer; the decision points depend on the candidate's chosen tour. Mark by checking:

- between three and five decision points are identified,
- each is at a meaningful location (col, point of no return, major slope crossing, time / weather checkpoint),
- and each "why this location" reason names a specific terrain or operational factor (not just "good place to stop").

For trainer reference, typical decision points on a six-leg tour:

- The first significant col (gateway to committing terrain below).
- The summit or main objective (descent route choice).
- An afternoon waypoint (time / weather checkpoint).
- A final col before the descent (final committing decision).

A candidate with fewer than three decision points has not engaged with the contingency thinking; coach for additional points. A candidate with more than five may be over-engineering; coach for prioritisation.

### Exercise 2 - Write criteria for each decision point

There is no fixed answer; the criteria depend on the candidate's decision points. Mark by checking that each criterion has all three components:

- a **specific trigger** (a measurable condition: time of day, barometer change, group fitness, snow observation),
- a **specific action** (turn around, take alternative line, abort, etc.),
- a **named decision point** (which point on the route the criterion applies at).

A candidate whose criteria all use the same trigger (typically time of day) has a one-dimensional contingency plan. Coach for trigger variety: time, weather (barometer trend from D6.2), group condition, snow observation, terrain-trap concern.

For trainer reference, examples of strong criteria using different trigger types:

- **Time:** "If arrival at the col exceeds 10:30am, skip the summit and traverse via the south col."
- **Weather:** "If the barometer has fallen more than 2 hPa since departure when checked at the col, descend by the morning route."
- **Group condition:** "If any group member needs more than 5 minutes' rest at the bench at 2,540 m, turn around."
- **Snow observation:** "If fresh wind-loaded snow is found above 2,600 m, do not enter the upper bowl; descend by the south spur instead."
- **Terrain-trap concern:** "If the slope above the gully has visibly cracked overnight, do not cross the gully; reverse to the col."

### Exercise 3 - Recognise the weak criteria

| Criterion | Standard met? | Missing components / rewrite |
|---|---|---|
| If the weather looks bad at the col, turn around. | **No** | Trigger is not measurable ("looks bad"); rewrite: "If the barometer has fallen more than 2 hPa since departure when checked at the col at 2,720 m, turn around and descend by the morning route." |
| If the barometer has fallen more than 2 hPa since departure when checked at the col at 2,720 m, descend by the morning route. | **Yes** | All three components present; specific, measurable, located. |
| Watch the weather. | **No** | No trigger, no action, no decision point. Not a criterion at all. Rewrite as a specific criterion at a named point. |
| If a group member needs more than 5 minutes' rest at the bench at 2,540 m, turn around and descend to the hut. | **Yes** | All three components present. |
| If we are running late, decide what to do. | **No** | Trigger is vague ("running late"); action is undefined ("decide what to do"). Rewrite: "If arrival at the col exceeds 10:30am, skip the summit and traverse directly to the south col." |

Mark for the right yes/no judgements and for rewrites that include all three components.

### Exercise 4 - The "want to continue" test

A strong answer should make at least two of the following points:

1. **Pre-agreed criteria are decided in calm conditions** (at the desk, before departure) when the candidate has full information and no time pressure. The decision is therefore better than one made in the moment under stress.
2. **In-the-moment decisions are vulnerable to "want to continue" pressure** (group expectations, sunk effort, summit attractiveness, weather optimism). The pressure distorts judgement.
3. **The criterion is non-negotiable on the day.** If the candidate writes "if X then Y" before departure, then on the day if X happens, Y happens. There is no fresh decision to make; the decision was made.

If the candidate answers only "criteria are good", coach for the explicit pressure-vs-calm-conditions framing.
