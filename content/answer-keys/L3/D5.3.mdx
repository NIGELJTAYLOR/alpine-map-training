### Exercise 1 - 7-day altimeter day log

There is no fixed answer; the readings depend on the candidate's altimeter and the local weather over the seven days. Mark by checking that:

- the candidate has set the altimeter to their known home elevation each morning,
- the apparent change from known elevation is calculated each day,
- a weather observation is recorded for each day (a single descriptive word or phrase is enough: "stable high", "incoming front", "windy and clearing"),
- and seven days of data are present.

A candidate whose log shows no apparent change on any day (all entries reading the home elevation exactly) has either had seven consecutive days of perfectly stable pressure (rare) or has not been reading the altimeter carefully each morning (more likely). Coach the careful-read habit if the data looks suspicious.

### Exercise 2 - Identify the pattern

There is no fixed answer; the pattern depends on the candidate's data. For trainer reference, typical patterns from a 7-day log:

- **Average apparent change:** typically 5 to 15 m (the altimeter is rarely at zero drift across multiple days).
- **Largest single-day apparent change:** typically 20 to 50 m on an active-weather day; 5 to 10 m on a stable day.
- **Days with the largest change** typically correlate with active weather (front passing through, deep low pressure, stormy conditions).
- **Days with the smallest change** typically correlate with stable high pressure.

Mark for the candidate having identified a meaningful pattern. A vague "weather affects the altimeter" is not enough; push for the specific correlation between weather type and apparent change.

### Exercise 3 - Calculate implied pressure changes

There is no fixed answer; the calculations depend on the candidate's data. Mark by checking that:

- the rule of thumb (1 hPa = 8 m) is applied correctly,
- the direction is correctly identified (apparent altitude rising = pressure falling; apparent altitude falling = pressure rising),
- and the arithmetic is right.

For example: a 24 m apparent gain implies a 3 hPa pressure drop. A 16 m apparent loss implies a 2 hPa pressure gain.

### Exercise 4 - Worked weather scenario

For the scenario (hut at 2,340 m, altimeter set at 6pm, midnight reading 2,420 m):

1. **Apparent altitude change:** 80 m (2,420 - 2,340).
2. **Implied pressure change:** 80 / 8 = **10 hPa** drop.
3. **Time period:** 6 hours (6pm to midnight).
4. **Rate:** 10 hPa / 6 hours = approximately **1.7 hPa per hour**.
5. **Weather indication:** more than 1 hPa per hour falls into the **rapid** band on the IMS Nav Programme decision framework. Fast-moving system; serious deterioration imminent.
6. **Action at midnight:** wake the group, assess the weather conditions outside (wind, snow, temperature), apply pre-agreed go/no-go criteria, consider abandoning the planned tour for the next day in favour of an early descent or shelter. Recalibrate the altimeter (if you can leave the hut and return to a known point) or accept that the morning altimeter readings will need careful cross-checking.

A strong answer to point 6 should not be "panic" or "do nothing". The IMS / EMS-relevant response is calm assessment and pre-agreed criteria. If the candidate proposes only one action, prompt for the wider response (group, conditions check, criteria, decision).

### Exercise 5 - Reflect on trust

A strong answer should make at least two of the following points:

- **In stable weather (small daily drifts in the log)**, the candidate would trust the altimeter heavily as an altitude reference and recalibrate at the standard known points without urgency.
- **In active weather (large daily drifts in the log)**, the candidate would trust the altimeter less as an altitude reference and recalibrate at every available reliable point. The altimeter would also be read more often as a weather signal.
- **The 7-day log gives the candidate the instinct** for which kind of day they are on, even before they look at a forecast.

If the candidate answers only "trust it less in bad weather", coach the specific behaviours (more frequent recalibration; reading drift as weather rather than as altitude error).
