### Exercise 1 - Walk through the technique at a known starting point

There is no fixed answer; the work depends on the candidate's chosen position. Mark by checking that all four stages are completed:

| Stage | What to check |
|---|---|
| 1. Aspect bearing (magnetic) | Should match the slope's aspect read from the contour pattern (perpendicular to contours, pointing downhill) |
| 2. Grid bearing after variation | Magnetic minus variation for east; magnetic plus variation for west |
| 3. Possible positions on the map | Should identify all places where the grid bearing is perpendicular to contours; usually a small number on a typical alpine extract |
| 4. Position fix from altimeter | Should identify the unique point where the perpendicular-to-contour direction crosses the contour at the chosen altitude |

A candidate who skips the magnetic-to-grid conversion in stage 2 will work entirely in magnetic on the map, which is an error if the map is gridded in true / grid north. Drill the conversion as part of the procedure.

A candidate whose position fix in stage 4 is more than 100 m from the actual position has had an error somewhere in the four stages; identify which stage by re-running the procedure with the candidate alongside.

### Exercise 2 - When the technique works

A strong answer should make at least three of the following points:

- The slope is **uniform enough** to give a single, clear aspect bearing (the fall line is consistent across the area visible to the candidate).
- The slope is **distinctive enough** that not many other slopes in the area have the same aspect at the same altitude (the technique returns a unique position rather than a list of candidates).
- The altimeter is **recently calibrated** so the altitude reading is reliable to within a few metres.
- Variation has been correctly applied in the magnetic-to-grid conversion.

If the candidate names only "you can take a clear bearing", coach for the additional conditions.

### Exercise 3 - When the technique fails

| Scenario | Reliable? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Whiteout, uniform north-facing slope, altimeter calibrated 30 minutes ago | **Yes** | All conditions met: uniform slope, recent calibration. |
| Whiteout, bottom of a curving valley, fall line varies | **No** | No single aspect bearing exists; the technique requires a uniform fall line. |
| Flat light, wide plateau, ground essentially flat | **No** | No meaningful fall line; aspect is ambiguous on flat ground. |
| Whiteout, north-facing slope, altimeter not calibrated for 4 hours, weather actively deteriorating | **No** | Altimeter has likely drifted; the altitude reading is unreliable, so the position fix is unreliable. |
| Whiteout, north-facing slope of a long ridge, many points have same north aspect at same altitude | **No** | Technique returns multiple possible positions; cannot give a unique fix. |

Mark for the right yes/no judgements and for reasons that name the specific failure mode. The fifth scenario is subtle (the technique works in the local sense but does not give a unique position); coach the recognition.

### Exercise 4 - Time the procedure

There is no fixed answer; the time and accuracy depend on the candidate's practice. Mark by checking:

- the time is recorded,
- the position-fix accuracy is checked against the known position,
- and any error is traced to a specific stage of the procedure.

The IMS / EMS working target is under two minutes. A candidate consistently above three minutes has not yet automated the procedure; drill the four stages individually until each is fast.

A candidate whose position fix is consistently wrong despite reasonable timing has a procedural error (commonly: forgetting the magnetic-to-grid conversion in stage 2, or aligning the wrong lines in stage 3). Identify the stage and recycle.
