### Exercise 1 - Identify escape routes for each leg

There is no fixed answer; the escape routes depend on the candidate's tour. Mark by checking:

- every leg has at least one escape route identified,
- the escape route is a different route from the planned route (not just "reverse the planned route" unless reversal is meaningfully safer),
- and the escape route is realistic (the candidate could actually take it from the planned route).

For trainer reference, typical escape routes:

- **From the start of a leg:** turn back along the previous leg.
- **From the end of a leg:** stop at the leg's destination (a hut, a col with no committing descent, a point where the candidate can wait or shelter).
- **From mid-leg:** the alternative line, often a longer or more sheltered route to the same destination or to a closer destination.

A candidate whose escape routes are all "reverse the planned route" has not engaged with the alternative-line thinking. Coach for genuine alternatives.

### Exercise 2 - Identify escape routes that would not work in worsening conditions

There is no fixed answer; the unavailable escape routes depend on the candidate's tour. Mark by checking:

- one escape route is identified that becomes unavailable in worsening conditions,
- the conditions are specific (deteriorating weather, fresh avalanche, group member injured),
- and an alternative escape is named.

For trainer reference, typical patterns:

- A morning ascent line via a north-facing slope: becomes unavailable as a descent line if the slope is wind-loaded by afternoon.
- A col descent via a steep west face: becomes unavailable if visibility drops below 30 m.
- A return via a long contour: becomes unavailable if any group member is injured (the long route is too far for assisted travel).

A candidate who cannot identify any escape route that becomes unavailable has not stress-tested the contingency plan. Coach the worst-case-thinking habit.

### Exercise 3 - The no-viable-escape recognition

There is no fixed answer; the legs flagged depend on the candidate's tour. Mark by checking:

- if any legs are flagged, the candidate has correctly identified them as committing,
- the planning-stage response is appropriate (recognise the commitment explicitly; tighten the entry criteria; reconsider the route choice),
- and the candidate has engaged with the question of whether the route should be revised.

For trainer reference: a tour with no legs flagged "no viable escape" is a tour with reasonable contingency throughout. A tour with one or two legs flagged is acceptable as long as the candidate has tightened the entry criteria for those legs. A tour with three or more legs flagged is a tour that should probably be revised; the candidate should consider whether the chosen route is the right route for the conditions assumed.

A candidate who cannot identify any committing legs on a complex alpine tour is probably not engaging with the question; press for honest review.

### Exercise 4 - The contingency-and-decision-point connection

A strong answer should make the explicit connection:

- **Decision-point criteria are the triggers** for taking the escape route. The criterion says "if X happens at point Y, take action Z"; the action is to take the escape route.
- **The escape route is the action** named in the criterion. Without an escape route, the criterion has no fall-back; without criteria, the escape route is never triggered.
- **D9.3 and D9.4 are two halves of the same discipline.** A complete contingency plan has both: the conditions under which to abort (D9.3) and the alternative route to take (D9.4).

If the candidate answers vaguely about both pages being "important", coach for the explicit trigger-and-action connection.

### Exercise 5 - Complete route card

There is no fixed answer; the entries depend on the candidate's tour. Mark by checking:

- every leg has decision-point criteria (with the three-component standard),
- every leg has at least one escape route,
- the escape route is consistent with the criteria (the criterion's action is to take the escape route),
- and any leg flagged "no viable escape" has tighter entry criteria than legs with available escape routes.

A candidate whose card now has all eleven fields populated for every leg has produced the complete IMS / EMS-style route card. This is the artefact the assessor reviews.
