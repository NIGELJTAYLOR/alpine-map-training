### Exercise 1 - Identify three altimeter-primary traverses

There is no fixed answer; the traverses depend on the candidate's exam-area map. For trainer reference, typical altimeter-primary traverses on an alpine map:

- **A contour traverse across a uniform north-facing slope** between two cols at the same altitude.
- **A traverse across the back wall of a cirque** at a specific altitude to reach a known col on the opposite side.
- **A descent into a featureless basin** with a target altitude that puts the candidate at a known waypoint.
- **A high-level traverse on a wide plateau** where lateral features are far apart but the altitude (the plateau elevation) is known.

A candidate who lists three slopes that all have abundant lateral features (named summits, ridges, streams) has not understood the altimeter-primary concept. Coach the featureless-terrain criterion.

### Exercise 2 - Plan a contour traverse

There is no fixed answer; the entry depends on the candidate's chosen traverse. Mark by checking that all seven fields are populated and consistent:

- start point and altitude match the calibration point and the target altitude (or are within a known relationship to them),
- end point and altitude are reasonable for the leg,
- target altitude is a single contour value,
- approximate bearing is plausible for the chosen line,
- approximate distance is consistent with the map scale,
- calibration point is a reliable known point (per D5.2),
- and notes name at least one feature for cross-check or one hazard to manage.

A weak entry leaves the notes field blank; coach the candidate to think about what would tell them they are on the right line and what would tell them they have drifted off it.

### Exercise 3 - Apply the aim-high strategy

For the scenario (descent from 2,720 m col to 2,450 m hut, with cliff band at approximately 2,400 m below the hut):

1. **Target altitude for the hut:** 2,450 m.
2. **Aim-high approach altitude:** 2,460 m (10 m above target).
3. **Action at aim-high altitude:** stop, confirm position (look around for any visible features; the hut may now be visible at close range), then follow the altimeter down the remaining 10 m to the exact target.
4. **Why the aim-high strategy is especially important on this leg:** below the hut at 2,400 m is a cliff band. Overshooting the hut by 50 m would put the candidate on or near the cliff band, which is committing terrain. The aim-high strategy prevents the overshoot.

A candidate who proposes aim-high without referencing the cliff band has not engaged with the specific hazard; coach the always-know-what-is-below-your-target principle.

### Exercise 4 - Compare with the compass-primary mode

A strong answer should make two points:

1. **Compass-primary leg:** the candidate uses the bearing to find the destination; the altimeter is a confirmation tool. Typical for legs to a recognisable destination (a hut, a junction, a named feature) where the destination provides the visual confirmation on arrival.
2. **Altimeter-primary leg:** the candidate uses the altitude to stay on the right contour or to stop at the right point; the bearing provides only the rough direction. Typical for traverses on uniform slopes, descents to specific altitudes, and poor-visibility legs across featureless terrain.

The route card should reflect the difference by:

- noting the primary tool for each leg explicitly (this could be a column on the route card),
- listing the target altitude prominently for altimeter-primary legs,
- and making the calibration plan explicit (when and where to recalibrate before each altimeter-primary leg).

A candidate who treats every leg as compass-primary has missed the altimeter-primary mode. Coach the explicit choice for each leg.
