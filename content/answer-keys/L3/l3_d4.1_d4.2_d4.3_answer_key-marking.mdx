The D4 triplet is about practical accuracy, not new technique. The bearing-shapes drill is the calibration test; aiming off is the practical-accuracy enhancer; magnetic interference is the silent error source.

Across all three pages, the IMS / EMS-relevant question is: "is the candidate's compass-and-bearing skill accurate enough to produce a route in the field that matches the route on paper?" The bearing-shapes drill answers this directly with a single number (closure error). Aiming off and magnetic interference management are the habits that make the drill answer hold up in real conditions.

If a candidate is borderline on D4.1 (closure error in the 10-to-20-paces range), do not move on to D5 until the closure improves. The altimeter work in D5 introduces the second navigation tool; without a reliable compass-and-bearing first tool, the combined techniques in D7 will not be reliable either.

The personal interference table from D4.3 is a tool the candidate carries mentally on every tour. Make sure the table is recorded somewhere the candidate can refer to (the front of their route-card pad, for example) until the figures are committed to memory.
