### Exercise 1 - Speed-test exercise

There is no fixed answer; the result depends on the candidate's writing speed and the chosen tour. Mark by checking:

- the candidate set a 20-minute timer and recorded the actual time taken,
- the number of legs completed within the 20 minutes is honestly recorded,
- and the nine basic fields are populated for each completed leg.

Standards:

- **All six legs completed within 20 minutes:** at IMS / EMS working standard. The candidate is ready to layer in decision points and escape routes (D9.3 and D9.4).
- **Four or five legs completed within 20 minutes:** approaching standard. Identify which fields took longest and recycle the underlying skill.
- **Three or fewer legs completed:** not yet at standard. Recycle the underlying skills (D2.1 bearing procedure, D2.2 variation, C6.2 terrain-trap reading) before re-attempting.

A candidate who completes all six legs but skips the under-described fields (vague key-terrain entries, single-phrase avalanche assessments) has met the speed but failed the quality standard. Coach the quality before the next attempt.

### Exercise 2 - Self-mark against a reference

There is no fixed answer; the comparison depends on the candidate's card and the reference card. Mark by checking that:

- the candidate has compared their card field-by-field against the reference,
- the comparison is honest (not just "looks similar"),
- and specific differences are noted for each criterion.

Working standards:

- Bearings within 2 degrees of reference: at least 5 of 6 legs.
- Target altitudes match: 6 of 6 legs (these are read directly from the map; no excuse for variation).
- Distance estimates within 10% of reference: at least 5 of 6 legs.
- Time estimates within 15% of reference: at least 4 of 6 legs (more variation acceptable here because of pace differences between candidates).
- Key-terrain features at least as detailed as reference: at least 4 of 6 legs (this is the under-description test).
- Avalanche assessment at least as detailed as reference: at least 4 of 6 legs (same).

A candidate consistently below the working standards on multiple criteria has produced a card that is not yet ready to use on a real tour. Identify the weakest criterion and recycle.

### Exercise 3 - Identify the three slowest legs

There is no fixed answer; the slow legs depend on the candidate's writing. Mark by checking:

- three legs are identified,
- a specific reason is named for each (not just "it was hard"),
- and the underlying skill is identified (D1.1 compass parts? D2.1 bearing procedure? D2.2 variation? C6.2 terrain-trap reading? D5.2 calibration logic?).

The trainer should recognise the typical patterns:

- **Slow on bearing-taking:** D2.1 not yet automatic. Recycle.
- **Slow on variation:** D2.2 mnemonic not yet automatic. Drill.
- **Slow on key-terrain features:** the candidate has not walked the leg in imagination; coach the feature-identification habit.
- **Slow on avalanche assessment:** C6.2 vocabulary not yet fluent. Recycle Section C6.

A candidate who cannot identify which underlying skill is slowing them is a candidate who has not yet learned to self-diagnose; coach the diagnostic approach.

### Exercise 4 - The under-description test

There is no fixed answer; the result depends on the candidate's card. Mark by checking:

- the candidate has read their card against the under-description criteria,
- they have honestly identified whether their entries are detailed enough,
- and they have named two specific improvements for the next card.

The two improvements should be specific (not "write more"). Examples of specific improvements:

- "I will list at least three key-terrain features per leg in order from start to end."
- "I will reference the C6.2 terrain-trap reading explicitly in every avalanche assessment."
- "I will note the runout zone for any leg that crosses below a steep slope."

If the candidate names vague improvements ("be more detailed"), coach for specificity.
