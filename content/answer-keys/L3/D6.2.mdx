### Exercise 1 - Apply the framework to three scenarios

| Scenario | Trend category | IMS action |
|---|---|---|
| Pressure rising at 0.3 hPa/hr for 4 hours | Rising | Proceed with plan |
| Pressure fallen 4 hPa in 5 hours, rate constant | Moderate fall (rate = 0.8 hPa/hr) | Consider early descent. Assess escape routes. |
| Pressure fallen 5 hPa in 2 hours, still falling | Rapid fall (rate = 2.5 hPa/hr) | Initiate escape route or shelter immediately. |

Mark for the right rate calculation and the right band classification. The most common error is misclassifying a moderate fall as rapid (or vice versa); drill the band thresholds.

A candidate who classifies the second scenario as rapid (because 4 hPa sounds large) has missed the rate calculation. The 5-hour timescale puts the rate in the moderate band.

### Exercise 2 - Write three pre-agreed criteria

There is no fixed answer; the criteria depend on the candidate's chosen tour. Mark by checking that each criterion has all three components:

- a **specific trigger** (a measurable condition, not a vague feeling),
- a **specific action** (turn around, skip summit, initiate escape, etc.),
- a **named decision point** (a specific location on the route).

Examples of strong criteria:

- "If pressure has fallen by more than 2 hPa since departure when checked at the col at 2,720 m, turn around and descend by the morning route."
- "If the rate of pressure fall at the col is moderate or worse, skip the summit and traverse via the south col instead."
- "If pressure falls by more than 5 hPa during the day at any waypoint, initiate the southern escape route immediately regardless of progress."

Examples of weak criteria (coach for specificity):

- "If the weather looks bad, turn around." (no measurable trigger)
- "Pay attention to the barometer." (no action)
- "If the weather worsens, do something about it." (no trigger, no action, no decision point)

### Exercise 3 - Tour duration and the framework

A strong answer should make at least two of the following points:

1. **Half-day tour with bailout options:** the framework can be applied less aggressively because the consequences of staying out are smaller. A slow fall might trigger only "monitor" rather than "early descent" because there is less time at risk and more bailout availability.
2. **Full-day committing tour:** the framework should be applied more conservatively because the consequences of staying out are larger. A slow fall might trigger early descent because there is no easy bailout from the committing mid-section.
3. **The committing-terrain consideration is the critical variable.** The framework's standard responses assume bailout is reasonably available; on a tour where bailout is not available from the mid-section, criteria should be tighter.

Mark for the engagement with the duration-and-commitment trade-off, not for a specific framework adjustment.

### Exercise 4 - Decision criteria on the route card

There is no fixed answer; the entry depends on the candidate's chosen tour. Mark by checking:

- the leg description is specific (start, end, and what makes it a meaningful leg),
- the decision point is named (a specific location, not a vague time of day),
- each criterion has the three components (trigger, action, decision point),
- the escape route is referenced (named, with a brief description of how to access it).

A candidate who writes only one criterion or who omits the escape route reference has not engaged with the route-card practice; coach the full entry.

### Exercise 5 - When the criterion says abort but you do not want to

A strong answer should name at least two of the following pressures:

- **Group expectations:** the group came for the summit; turning back disappoints them.
- **Sunk effort:** the candidate has already invested significant effort getting this far; turning back feels like wasted effort.
- **Weather optimism:** the candidate hopes the deteriorating trend will reverse; the natural human bias is to wait one more hour to see.
- **Summit attractiveness:** the summit (or destination) is visible and close; the goal proximity creates a "just one more push" pressure.

The point of pre-agreed criteria is precisely to override these pressures with a calm pre-departure decision. A criterion written before the day is unaffected by the in-the-moment pressures; a decision improvised under pressure typically yields to them.

A candidate who answers "yes I would abort if the criterion said so" without engaging with the pressures has not internalised why the criteria matter. Push for the explicit recognition of the pressures.
