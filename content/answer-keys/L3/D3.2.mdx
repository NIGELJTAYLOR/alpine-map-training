### Exercise 1 - Convert forward to back

| Forward bearing | Back bearing |
|---|---|
| 045 | 225 |
| 090 | 270 |
| 178 | 358 |
| 220 | 040 |
| 312 | 132 |
| 003 | 183 |

Mark for the arithmetic. The single most common error is failing to wrap (for example, 045 + 180 = 225 is correct; 312 + 180 = 492, which should wrap to 132 by subtracting 360). Drill the rule: less than 180, add 180; 180 or more, subtract 180.

### Exercise 2 - Practical drill

There is no fixed answer; the drill produces the candidate's own measurement. Mark by checking that:

- the candidate has stated their forward bearing,
- the calculated back bearing matches the forward bearing plus or minus 180 (with wrap if needed),
- the actual back bearing read in the field is close to the calculated back bearing (within 5 degrees if the walking was straight),
- and the candidate has used the angular-to-linear relationship from D2.3 to estimate the implied lateral drift.

If the actual back bearing is more than 10 degrees off the calculated, the candidate has drifted significantly during the walk; coach the D2.3 walking habits.

### Exercise 3 - The poor-visibility sanity check

A strong answer should make two points:

1. **Why every 5 to 10 minutes:** at typical walking speeds (3 to 5 km per hour), 5 to 10 minutes is 250 to 800 m of travel. A drift accumulating over that distance is still small enough to correct. A drift accumulated over an hour is large enough to be lost.
2. **What action if the back bearing does not align:** stop, take a fresh forward bearing (not the original one), and re-orient. The corrective action is not to "push harder on the original bearing"; it is to recognise the drift, accept the new position, and recalculate from there.

If the candidate answers only "to check direction", coach the explicit timing (5 to 10 minutes, not arbitrary) and the explicit action (re-orient, not push harder).

### Exercise 4 - Back bearing for relocation

For the scenario (col visible behind, candidate uncertain of position on a broad slope):

1. **The candidate would take a forward bearing to the col itself.** From the candidate's current position, the col is the visible feature; the bearing is taken using the D3.1 procedure.
2. **The bearing identifies the line on which the candidate's position lies.** From the col, drawing the back bearing on the map produces a line; the candidate is somewhere on that line. To find where on the line, cross-reference with altimeter (D7.2) or with a second bearing to another visible feature (D3.3 resection).
3. **More reliable than estimating distance walked:** distance estimation on snow is unreliable (pacing does not work; time is affected by surface conditions and gradient). A bearing to a visible feature is exact in direction even if the distance is unknown.

A candidate who suggests pacing or timing as the alternative has the right idea but the wrong tool for snow. Coach the why-distance-fails point.
