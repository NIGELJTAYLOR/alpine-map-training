### Exercise 1 - Identify the linear features

There is no fixed answer; the linear features depend on the trainer's chosen targets on the Section D4.2 extract. For trainer reference, the standard extract is configured with:

| Target | Standard linear feature for aim-off |
|---|---|
| Target 1: hut at 2,450 m | Stream running east-west across the shoulder, 50 m north of the hut |
| Target 2: col at 2,720 m | The ridge crest itself, running roughly north-south through the col |
| Target 3: junction at 2,180 m | Forest edge running roughly east-west, 100 m above the junction |

Mark by checking the candidate has identified a feature that:

- is unmistakable when the candidate reaches it (a stream is unmistakable; "the slope eases" is not),
- runs through or close to the target,
- and is in a position that allows a clear handrail walk to the target.

### Exercise 2 - Choose the safe side

For the standard extract:

| Target | Standard safe side | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Target 1 | North of the hut (so when the candidate hits the stream they walk south, downhill, towards the hut) | Walking downhill on the stream is easier than walking uphill; the south side of the hut is open ground with no hazard |
| Target 2 | Along the ridge to the south of the col | The ridge crest is the safe handrail; aiming into either valley puts the candidate below the col with no easy way to climb back up |
| Target 3 | Above the junction (so when the candidate hits the forest edge they walk down to the junction) | The forest edge is unmistakable; aiming below the junction means walking up a steeper slope to find it |

Mark for the candidate having considered the practical handrail walk from the linear feature to the target, not just for naming a safe side abstractly.

### Exercise 3 - Calculate the aim-off bearing

There is no fixed answer; the bearings depend on the candidate's chosen targets and offsets. Mark by checking:

- the direct grid bearing is taken correctly (using the D2.1 procedure),
- the offset is in the correct direction (towards the chosen safe side, away from the hazard),
- the offset is reasonable (5 to 10 degrees is typical; more produces a longer walk, less produces less reliability),
- and the aim-off grid bearing is correctly calculated.

For Target 1 (hut at 2,450 m, direct bearing 220 grid, aim off 10 degrees north):

- offset direction: north of the direct line. North of bearing 220 is towards 220 - 90 = 130, which is the perpendicular direction. Subtracting from 220 moves the bearing towards north. So 220 - 10 = 210 grid is the aim-off bearing.

Mark the arithmetic and the direction. A candidate who adds when they should subtract has the offset direction inverted.

### Exercise 4 - Aim-off in poor visibility

A strong answer should make two points:

1. **Aim-off matters more in poor visibility because visual confirmation is unavailable.** In good visibility the candidate sees the target as they approach and can correct; in poor visibility they cannot. The aim-off provides the certainty that visual confirmation would otherwise provide.
2. **The safe-side choice becomes more critical in poor visibility because the candidate cannot see hazards on the wrong side.** A wrong-side aim-off in good visibility is recoverable; in poor visibility it may put the candidate on a hazard before they realise.

If the candidate answers only with the first point, prompt for the safe-side implication.
