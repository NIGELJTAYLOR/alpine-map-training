### Exercise 1 - Plan a three-leg route

There is no fixed answer; the legs depend on the candidate's exam-area map. Mark by checking that:

- each leg has a named start and named end,
- the grid bearing is taken correctly using the D2.1 procedure,
- the magnetic bearing applies the variation correctly,
- the target altitude is the altitude at the end point,
- the distance is plausible (consistent with the map scale),
- and each leg genuinely uses both bearing and altimeter as primary inputs (rather than being a leg where the destination is visible from the start).

A candidate whose three legs all have visible destinations has missed the page's pedagogical intent (which is poor-visibility waypoint navigation). Coach for legs where the destination would not be visible at a distance.

### Exercise 2 - The deviation question

There is no fixed answer; the most dangerous deviation depends on the candidate's chosen legs. Common patterns:

- **Bearing error most dangerous** when the leg crosses terrain where a wrong heading puts the candidate over a cliff band, into a terrain trap, or onto an avalanche-loaded slope. Bearing accuracy is critical for terrain selection.
- **Altimeter error most dangerous** when the leg ends at a waypoint above a cliff (the candidate continues past the waypoint and onto committing terrain) or in a basin with limited bailout (the candidate stops at the wrong elevation and is on the wrong contour).

A strong answer should name a specific terrain consequence, not just "bearing wrong = lost".

### Exercise 3 - Walk through the field execution

A strong answer should describe:

1. Calibration of the altimeter at the start point.
2. Setting the compass to the magnetic bearing for the leg.
3. Walking on the bearing using the D2.3 walking technique.
4. Checking the altimeter at intervals.
5. Stopping when the altimeter reaches the target altitude.
6. Confirming with a terrain feature if possible.

A candidate who omits any step has not internalised the integrated technique. Mark for the complete sequence.

### Exercise 4 - When the altimeter and the bearing disagree

For the worked scenario (50 minutes elapsed, altimeter at target, no waypoint visible):

1. **Two most likely causes:**
   - Bearing drift: the candidate has wandered off the bearing during the walk, so they are at the right altitude but the wrong line.
   - Altimeter drift: the altimeter has drifted (weather pressure changing) since the start calibration, so the altimeter shows the target but the candidate is not at that altitude.

2. **Immediate action:** stop, do not continue forward. The combined-instrument technique has surfaced a problem; continuing without diagnosing it makes the problem worse.

3. **Distinguishing between the two:**
   - Take a back bearing to the start point. If the back bearing aligns with the start, the candidate has not drifted off the bearing; the cause is altimeter drift. If the back bearing does not align, the candidate has drifted off the bearing.
   - Cross-check altimeter against any visible feature with a known altitude (a contour line crossed earlier, a recognisable terrain shape).
   - If neither check resolves the ambiguity, treat both causes as possible and re-orient using the aspect-of-slope technique (D7.2) if conditions allow.

A candidate who proposes "just keep walking" or "trust the bearing more than the altimeter" has missed the diagnostic-first principle. Drill the stop-and-diagnose habit.
