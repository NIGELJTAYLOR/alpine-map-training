### Exercise 1 - Look up the variation

There is no fixed answer; the variation depends on the candidate's chosen map area and date. Mark by checking that:

- the source named is one of the three official sources (BGS WMM, NOAA / NCEI, or swisstopo),
- the coordinates entered match the candidate's map area,
- the date entered is current (within a few months of the workbook session),
- the variation returned is reasonable for the area (1 to 3 degrees east in the Alps; 0 to 3 degrees west in the UK; up to 15 degrees east or west in some North American locations).

A candidate who copies a figure from an old map margin without checking the date has slipped the discipline. Coach the look-up habit.

### Exercise 2 - Convert grid to magnetic

There is no fixed answer; the magnetic bearings depend on the candidate's variation lookup from Exercise 1 and the grid bearings from D2.1.

Mark by checking the arithmetic. For an east variation (positive), each magnetic bearing should equal the grid bearing plus the variation. For a west variation (negative), each magnetic bearing should equal the grid bearing minus the variation.

For trainer reference, with grid bearings from the standard D2.1 extract and a variation of +2 degrees east:

| Leg | Grid bearing | +2E | Magnetic bearing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 218 |  | 220 |
| 2 | 045 |  | 047 |
| 3 | 312 |  | 314 |
| 4 | 178 |  | 180 |
| 5 | 003 |  | 005 |

A candidate who subtracts the variation when they should add (or vice versa) has the rule wrong. Drill the chosen mnemonic until it is automatic.

### Exercise 3 - Reflect on the consequence

For a 1,500 m leg with a 2-degree variation:

- Lateral drift = 1,500 × tan(2 degrees) ≈ 1,500 × 0.0349 ≈ **52 metres**.

Mark for the right order of magnitude (40 to 60 m is acceptable for a 2-degree variation). Adjust for the candidate's actual variation:

- 1 degree → approximately 26 m drift over 1,500 m.
- 3 degrees → approximately 79 m drift over 1,500 m.
- 5 degrees → approximately 131 m drift over 1,500 m.

The point of the exercise is for the candidate to see that the variation is not a small detail. A 50 m drift is enough to miss a col, a hut, or a critical waypoint.

### Exercise 4 - Variation in different parts of the world

For trainer reference, approximate current variation figures (May 2026):

| Location | Approximate variation |
|---|---|
| Scottish Highlands (UK) | 0 to 1 degree W (currently small; was 2 W a decade ago) |
| French Alps | 1 to 3 degrees E |
| Swiss Alps | 1 to 3 degrees E |
| Pacific Northwest (Cascades, US) | 13 to 16 degrees E |
| Southern Alps (New Zealand) | 22 to 24 degrees E |

The numbers will drift over time; the workbook does not bake them in. Mark for the candidate having used a recent source rather than for matching the figures above exactly.

The one-sentence reflection should make the point that **a candidate using the workbook outside the European Alps must look up the local variation** because Alpine figures are unrepresentative of variation elsewhere. A candidate who applies +2 degrees east in the Cascades is more than 10 degrees wrong on every bearing.
