### Exercise 1 — Which way would you turn it?
A good answer would say that the learner may need to rotate the map by about 90 degrees so the stream on the page points the same way as the real stream.

### Exercise 2 — Match the scene
A strong paragraph should show that the learner is comparing the relative position of the wood, stream, open ground and track, and rotating the map until those relationships line up.

### Exercise 3 — Valley and ridge thinking
1. Obvious linear features are helpful because they are easy to recognise and compare between map and ground.
2. Featureless or confusing ground gives fewer strong clues, so it is harder to line the map up confidently.

### Exercise 4 — Personal process
Reflective. A good routine should include:

- choosing obvious features,
- comparing relative positions,
- rotating the map,
- and checking that the overall picture makes sense.
