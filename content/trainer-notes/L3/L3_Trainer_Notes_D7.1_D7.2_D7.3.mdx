## Purpose of these sections

D7 brings compass and altimeter together for the integrated alpine techniques. D7.1 covers bearing-plus-altimeter combined navigation (the standard poor-visibility waypoint technique). D7.2 covers the four-stage aspect-of-slope relocation (the alpine-specific alternative to resection). D7.3 covers inclinometer use as the field cross-check on the contour-spacing rule of thumb.

After D7 the candidate has the full instrument toolkit and knows how to use the instruments in combination. D8 then applies the toolkit to poor-visibility navigation explicitly.

## Coaching priorities

1. The bearing-plus-altimeter technique as the standard poor-visibility waypoint sequence.
2. The four-stage aspect-of-slope procedure as the alpine-specific alternative to resection.
3. The recognition of when aspect-of-slope works (uniform slope, distinctive aspect, calibrated altimeter) and when it does not (curving slope, ambiguous aspect, flat ground, uncalibrated altimeter).
4. The inclinometer as the field cross-check on the C1.2 rule of thumb.
5. The convex-roll detection through multi-point inclinometer measurements.

## Teaching approach

Run the D7 triplet as a single 90-minute block. The pages are tightly coupled and the integration framing depends on seeing all three together.

For D7.1, demonstrate the bearing-plus-altimeter technique on the candidate's exam-area map. Walk through a specific leg, calculating bearing and target altitude, and have the candidate write the route-card entry alongside.

For D7.2, the four-stage aspect-of-slope procedure deserves the most session time. Demonstrate at the desk on a known position (use the candidate's exam-area map). Then have the candidate try the procedure on a different known position they have not seen worked through. The repetition with variation builds the procedure into a reliable habit.

For D7.3, demonstrate inclinometer use on the candidate's compass (most baseplate compasses for mountain use have a built-in inclinometer). The convex-roll detection is the page's most valuable application; spend time on the multi-measurement pattern.

- Two baseplate compasses (trainer and candidate), both with built-in inclinometers if possible.
- The candidate's altimeter.
- The candidate's exam-area map for the worked examples and exercises.
- A timer for the D7.2 practical drill.

- A whiteboard or flipchart for the worked examples.

## Delivery notes - D7.1

**Framing.** Most poor-visibility legs in alpine terrain require both a direction and an altitude. The bearing keeps the candidate on the right line; the altimeter tells the candidate when to stop.

**The technique.** The six-step sequence (note target altitude, set bearing, walk on bearing, watch altimeter, stop at target altitude, confirm with terrain feature) is the IMS standard. Drill it.

**Why both are needed.** Walk through the failure modes: bearing alone (no distance check), altimeter alone (no direction check), both together (unique waypoint identification).

**Worked example.** The hut-to-col leg is the model. Walk through the route-card entry on the board, then the field execution. Have the candidate calculate the intermediate altimeter targets (at 30 minutes, at 60 minutes) so the diagnostic is concrete.

**Exercise 1.** The three-leg route. Mark for legs that genuinely need the combined technique (rather than legs to visible destinations). Coach for poor-visibility waypoint thinking.

**Exercise 2.** The deviation question. Push for a specific terrain consequence, not just "lost".

**Exercise 3.** The field-execution walk-through. Mark for the complete six-step sequence.

**Exercise 4.** The disagreement diagnostic. Coach the stop-and-diagnose habit; the worst response is to keep walking.

## Delivery notes - D7.2

**Framing.** Aspect-of-slope is the IMS / EMS-relevant alpine-specific technique. The candidate has met it in passing in C2.3 (aspect-of-each-flank reading) and C4.2 (aspect formal); D7.2 is where it becomes a position-fix procedure.

**The four stages.** Walk through them slowly the first time. The most error-prone stages are:

- **Stage 2 (magnetic to grid):** the candidate must remember the inverse direction of the variation conversion. A candidate who applies grid-to-magnetic instead of magnetic-to-grid will be 4 degrees off (twice the variation), which is enough to give a wrong position fix.
- **Stage 3 (perpendicular to contours on the map):** the candidate must align the parallel orienting lines with the map's grid lines (not just any vertical lines on the page) and read the perpendicular-to-contours direction.

**Why it works.** Coach the conceptual point: aspect (the slope) and altitude (the contour) together uniquely identify the position. The technique just makes the procedure for using both explicit.

**When it works and when it does not.** Walk through Exercise 3 (the five scenarios) with the candidate. The fifth scenario (long ridge with same aspect at same altitude at multiple points) is the subtlest; coach the recognition.

**Worked example.** The east-flank scenario is the model. Have the candidate calculate the grid bearing from a magnetic of 092 with +2 east variation, then identify the east-facing flank on the map.

**Exercise 1.** The four-stage walk-through at a known position. Mark for completion of all four stages with correct values.

**Exercise 2.** The works-when conditions. Push for the three or four conditions, not just one.

**Exercise 3.** The fail-mode recognition. Mark for the right yes/no judgements and for reasons that name the specific failure mode.

**Exercise 4.** The timed practical drill. The IMS / EMS working target is under two minutes. A candidate consistently above three minutes has not yet automated the procedure.

## Delivery notes - D7.3

**Framing.** The inclinometer is the field cross-check on the map estimate. The IMS / EMS-relevant use is avalanche-aware terrain interpretation: confirming whether a specific slope is in the release range before committing to it.

**The inclinometer.** Demonstrate on the candidate's compass. Most baseplate compasses for mountain use have a built-in inclinometer; some are pendulum-style, some are bubble-style. Both work the same way in principle.

**The procedure.** Hold the compass edge-on to the slope, body in the direction of greatest descent, read the angle. Demonstrate on a slope at hand if possible (a desk tilted to a known angle works; a real slope works better).

**Cross-referencing with the C1.2 rule.** The map estimate and the field measurement should agree within a few degrees on most slopes. Discrepancy is information; investigate.

**The 30-degree threshold.** The C1.2 lower bound of the slab-release range. The inclinometer confirms (or contradicts) whether a specific slope is at, above, or below.

**Convex roll detection.** The page's most valuable application. A convex roll has the steepest gradient at the rollover; the multi-measurement pattern (gentle / steeper / moderate) identifies it.

**Exercise 1.** The practical drill. Best on real slopes; set as homework if not available in the session.

**Exercise 2.** The cross-check. Mark for the discrepancy calculation and any pattern noted.

**Exercise 3.** The diagnostic. Coach the multiple-measurement verification approach.

**Exercise 4.** The 30-degree threshold action. Push for sensible matching of action to map estimate.

**Exercise 5.** The convex-roll detection. Coach the three-point measurement pattern.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Bearing-plus-altimeter agreement assumed.** The candidate trusts both tools and continues when they should diagnose. Drill the stop-and-diagnose habit.
- **Aspect-of-slope variation conversion in the wrong direction.** Magnetic-to-grid, not grid-to-magnetic. Drill explicitly.
- **Aspect-of-slope perpendicular-to-contours reading on the map.** The candidate reads the bearing direction without aligning the parallel orienting lines with the grid lines first. Demonstrate the correct alignment.
- **Aspect-of-slope on a curving slope.** The technique fails; the candidate must recognise this and switch to a different approach.
- **Inclinometer measurement at one point only.** Misses non-uniform slopes and convex rolls. Coach the multi-measurement habit.
- **Inclinometer-vs-map disagreement treated as map error.** Often it is the measurement that is unrepresentative, not the map. Coach the diagnostic approach.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - On D7.1, set a stretch task: plan a leg where the bearing changes mid-leg (because of an intermediate waypoint), with both bearing and target altitude for each segment.
  - On D7.2, run the timed drill on multiple slopes in succession; the rapid switching between contexts builds fluency faster than single-slope practice.
  - On D7.3, ask the candidate to predict the inclinometer reading at three points on a slope from the map alone, then take the measurements and compare. The prediction-and-verification cycle calibrates the candidate's map-reading instinct.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D7.1, walk through the field-execution sequence step by step before the candidate attempts Exercise 3.
  - On D7.2, do the four stages together at the candidate's first known position; have them attempt the second known position alone.
  - On D7.3, demonstrate the inclinometer use on a slope at hand before they attempt their own measurements.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D8 (poor visibility) when they can:

- plan a route leg using both bearing and altimeter as the primary inputs,
- diagnose a bearing-altimeter disagreement,
- execute the four-stage aspect-of-slope relocation in under two minutes on a known slope,
- recognise the conditions under which aspect-of-slope works and the conditions under which it fails,
- measure slope angle with the inclinometer,
- cross-reference inclinometer measurements with the C1.2 rule,
- and detect convex rolls through multi-point inclinometer measurements.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. D8 (poor visibility) assumes confident integrated-techniques fluency; without it, the D8 sequence becomes a list of techniques rather than an integrated response.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The bearing-plus-altimeter technique is the IMS Nav Training Programme standard sequence and is consistent with general alpine navigation practice.

The aspect-of-slope four-stage procedure is verified at primary source on Mountaineering Scotland (the "when navigation goes wrong" page) and Alpine Guides (the altimeter-navigation guide). Both treat it as the standard alpine relocation technique. The verification was performed during the Batch 10 sweep and is recorded in `Sources.md`.

The inclinometer cross-check on the C1.2 rule is workbook-specific framing of standard avalanche-aware terrain interpretation. The convex-roll detection through multi-point measurements is consistent with the broader avalanche-education literature.

The C1.2 30-degree threshold is the lower bound of the slab-release range, consistent with BASI / New Generation EMS materials and the broader avalanche literature. The 35-degree "prime" release angle from C1.2 trainer notes also applies here; the candidate should know both figures.
