## Purpose of these sections

D10.1 and D10.2 close Level 3. D10.1 is a fast diagnostic that samples the full Level 3 vocabulary in a low-stakes format. D10.2 is a sustained virtual day on a tour that exercises the full route card and instrument toolkit in a six-leg narration with "what if" scenarios.

Together they are the readiness check for the next phase of the IMS / EMS pathway: real-tour practice, scenario response with a real group, the practical drill days, and the assessment course itself. The point is honesty about which Level 3 skills are fluent, which are slow, and which are unreliable.

D10 mirrors the role of C7 at the close of Level 2: a mixed quiz plus a sustained integration narration plus a readiness check. The pedagogy is the same; the content is the Level 3 instrument toolkit rather than the Level 2 contour vocabulary.

## Coaching priorities

1. The diagnostic mindset. D10.1 is for finding weak spots, not for scoring well. D10.2 is for rehearsing the working voice, not for showing off.
2. The error log as the action plan. Vague entries are not enough; specific commitments are.
3. The five-element narration structure for D10.2 (route card, instrument plan, decision criteria, escape route, "what if" response) held at every leg.
4. The integration of all Level 3 instruments in the "what if" responses (not just one at a time).
5. The honesty of the Level 4 readiness check. The trainer's role is to verify, not to override.

## Teaching approach

Run D10.1 and D10.2 as two distinct sessions on different days, mirroring the C7 pattern. The reasons are the same:

- D10.1 produces an error log that needs action between sessions.
- D10.2 is more useful after the error-log items have been addressed.
- A candidate who runs both back-to-back will not have time to revisit the weak pages identified in D10.1.

A workable timing:

- **Session 1 (D10.1):** 60 minutes. Quiz, marking, error log, action plan.
- **Between sessions:** the candidate does the work in the error log. Typically 1 to 7 days depending on the size of the log.
- **Session 2 (D10.2):** 90 to 120 minutes. Pre-departure briefing, six legs of narration, closing reflection, readiness check.

If the two sessions absolutely have to run on the same day, allow at least an hour break in between and use the break for the candidate to revisit the most important error-log items.

| Activity | Time |
|---|---|

| Leg 2 | 15 min |
| Leg 3 | 15 min |
| Leg 4 | 15 min |

| Leg 6 | 15 min |

The 15 minutes per leg includes the candidate's narration of all five elements plus the trainer's "what if" injection and the candidate's response. A faster candidate may complete legs in 10 minutes; a slower candidate may take 20.

## Materials checklist

- A timer for the 25-minute quiz window.
- Pencils, ruler, eraser.

### For D10.2

## Delivery notes - D10.1

**Framing.** The quiz is diagnostic. Make this explicit at the start. The IMS / EMS-relevant skill is fluency; the quiz tests whether the candidate has the working vocabulary at speed.

**Time pressure.** Set a 25-minute timer for the quiz overall. The 25 minutes is generous (the questions are short); a candidate consistently above 30 minutes has retrieval problems on the underlying knowledge and should recycle.

**Marking with the candidate alongside.** Do not take the quiz away to mark in private. Sit with the candidate; mark question by question; the conversation is the learning tool.

**Confidence score.** Insist that the candidate fills in all seven skill areas before moving to the error log. The score is the bridge between the quiz performance and the action plan.

**Error log.** Walk through each wrong or slow question:

- Which skill area is this question testing?
- Which Level 3 page taught this skill?
- What specifically went wrong?
- What will you do about it before D10.2?

A vague entry is not enough. Push for specific commitments: page, exercise, deadline.

## Delivery notes - D10.2

**Framing.** Open the session with a brief check on error-log progress since D10.1. If the candidate has not done the work, the D10.2 session will surface the same weaknesses. Decide together whether to proceed or to defer the session.

**Pre-departure briefing.** Insist on all seven elements. A candidate who skips the briefing has missed the IMS / EMS-relevant pre-departure habit.

**Leg structure.** Hold the five-element structure: route card, instrument plan, decision criteria, escape route, "what if" response. Some candidates will want to narrate freely; resist. The structure is what makes the narration assessable.

**"What if" injections.** Inject one scenario per leg. The scenarios should span the Level 3 vocabulary (weather, navigation, group dynamics, terrain). The answer key has six suggested scenarios; substitute as appropriate.

**Watch for:**

- **Generic "what if" responses.** "Stop and reassess" is not enough. The IMS / EMS-relevant response uses the specific procedures (whiteout sequence from D8.1, systematic relocation from D8.3, decision-framework application from D6.2).
- **Vague decision-point criteria.** "If the weather looks bad" is not the three-component standard. Coach D9.3.
- **Escape routes as reversals.** Push for genuine alternatives.
- **Instrument plans not differentiated by leg.** "Compass and altimeter" is generic; coach for the explicit primary / secondary choice for each specific leg.

**Closing reflection.** R1 and R2 should both be specific. R3 surfaces the candidate's specific scenario weakness. R4 is the readiness check; R5 surfaces whether the dual-use altimeter habit (from D5.3) has become automatic.

**The Level 4 readiness conversation.** Do not override the candidate's R4 answer. Verify it against the evidence. The honesty of the readiness check is what makes it useful.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Performance mode in D10.1.** The quiz feels like a test; the candidate freezes. Reset the diagnostic framing.
- **Vague error-log entries in D10.1.** The most common D10.1 weakness. Push for specific commitments.
- **Pre-departure briefing skipped in D10.2.** A candidate who jumps straight to Leg 1 has missed the foundational habit. Insist on the briefing.
- **Five-element structure collapse mid-narration.** The structure feels redundant by Leg 3 or 4 and the candidate compresses. Hold the structure.
- **Generic "what if" responses.** The IMS / EMS-relevant moments. Push for specific Level 3 procedures.
- **Vague Level 4 readiness answers.** "I think I'm ready" is not enough. Push for the evidence-supported answer.
- **Trainer-led readiness.** Resist the urge to declare the candidate ready. R4 is the candidate's call. The trainer verifies, the trainer does not decide.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - In D10.1, run the quiz under stricter time pressure (15 minutes for the fifteen questions). The pressure surfaces weak spots that a 25-minute window may not.
  - In D10.2, add a second "what if" injection per leg, or compress the leg time to 10 minutes per leg. Both increase the realism.
  - Set a stretch task at the end of D10.2: produce a written assessment-style report on the day's narration, formatted for handover to a touring partner or examiner. The report consolidates the narration into a working document.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - In D10.1, untime the quiz. Speed comes after correctness.
  - In D10.2, walk through Leg 1 alongside the candidate, modelling the structure. Then have them do Legs 2 to 6 alone.
  - If the closing reflection is hard, prompt with the explicit comparison: "compared with C7.2 from Level 2, is this stronger, weaker, or the same?"

## Readiness to progress to the next phase of the IMS / EMS pathway

The candidate is ready when:

- the D10.1 quiz score is 12 or more out of 15, with no critical errors on the foundational fields (D2.1 four-step procedure; D2.2 variation rule; D6.2 / D9.3 three-component criteria; D9.1 eleven route-card fields),
- the D10.2 narration holds the five-element structure at all six legs,
- the "what if" responses use specific Level 3 procedures rather than generic responses,
- the closing reflection at R1, R2, R3 is honest and evidence-supported,
- and the D10.1 error log has been acted on or has a specific plan.

The next phase of the IMS / EMS pathway introduces real-tour practice, scenario response with a real group, and the practical drill days that lead to the assessment. The workbook has built the foundation; the next phase is application of the foundation in real conditions.

If the candidate is borderline, hold them at Level 3 for an additional consolidation session (a fresh D10.1 quiz on different questions; a fresh D10.2 walk on a different route) rather than moving on.

## Closing the Level 3 build-out

D10.2 is the final page of Level 3. After the readiness check, the workbook formally hands the candidate over to the next phase of the IMS / EMS pathway.

Three artefacts are worth keeping at the end of Level 3:

- **The error log from D10.1**, with the actions completed.
- **The D10.2 narration notes**, especially the "what if" responses, as evidence of the candidate's planning thinking.
- **The readiness check at R4**, with the trainer's verification note alongside.

All three go into the trainer manual's progress log and into any logbook the candidate is keeping for BASI EMS purposes. They are evidence of the consolidation work and the readiness decision.

## Reference notes for the trainer

D10.1 and D10.2 are consolidation pages and do not introduce any new claims requiring source verification. The full Level 3 vocabulary is taken from D1 to D9 inclusive, where the underlying sources are recorded in `00_Research_Inputs/Sources.md`.

The decision to run D10.1 and D10.2 as separate sessions on different days is pedagogical and mirrors the C7.1 / C7.2 pattern from Level 2. The error-log action plan from D10.1 needs time to take effect before D10.2 can test the integration honestly.

The Level 4 readiness criteria are workbook-specific. They do not map directly to a published BASI EMS criterion (which the workbook does not have full access to; see `Sources.md` section 6 for the open verification items). They are a reasonable working standard for moving from Level 3 (compass and instruments) into the next phase (real-tour practice and scenario response).

The "what if" scenario library can be expanded by the trainer based on local terrain and assessment patterns. The six suggested scenarios in the answer key are a starting set; the trainer should curate scenarios that span the candidate's likely real-world challenges.

Sources for this trainer note are recorded in `00_Research_Inputs/Sources.md`. No new claims are introduced beyond those covered in the Batch 2 and Batch 10 primary-source sweeps.
