## Purpose of these sections

D9.1 and D9.2 cover the basic route card. D9.1 covers the standard fields and what each is for. D9.2 covers writing a complete six-leg card under the IMS Nav Programme 20-minute speed standard.

D9.3 and D9.4 then add the contingency layer (decision points and escape routes). The four D9 pages together produce the complete route card as the integrative artefact of Level 3.

The route card is the central artefact of IMS / EMS-level planning. The assessor reviews route cards as evidence of planning thinking; the route card on the day is the candidate's working reference for the whiteout sequence (D8.1) and the relocation procedure (D8.3). After D9 the candidate has the artefact that makes the rest of the level work.

## Coaching priorities

1. The eleven fields named on sight, with no hesitation about which is which.
2. The two typically under-described fields (key terrain, avalanche assessment) recognised and addressed.
3. The 20-minute speed standard for a six-leg card.
4. The under-description test as the quality check after every card.
5. The procedure-as-procedure habit (run the same sequence every time, do not improvise the order).

## Teaching approach

For D9.1, walk through the eleven fields with the candidate using a real route-card template. Show the trainer's own card (or a published reference card) so the candidate sees what a complete entry looks like.

For D9.2, the speed-test exercise is the heart of the page. Set the 20-minute timer; have the candidate produce a card; mark immediately. The first card is usually slow and incomplete; the second and third improve rapidly with the procedural awareness from the marking.

The under-description test is the quality check. A candidate who hits the 20-minute target but produces vague entries has the wrong trade-off; coach the quality before the next attempt.

## Suggested timings

Treat D9.1 and D9.2 as a single 90-minute block.

| Activity | Time |
|---|---|
| Recap of D8 (route card as foundation); framing of D9 as the supply | 5 min |
| D9.1 the eleven fields walk-through | 15 min |
| D9.1 Exercises 1 to 4 | 20 min |
| D9.2 the procedure walk-through | 10 min |
| D9.2 Exercise 1 (the speed-test) | 20 min |
| D9.2 marking and Exercises 2 to 4 | 15 min |
| Self-check and discussion | 5 min |
| **Total** | **90 min** |

If the speed-test produces a clearly weak card (slow, incomplete, vague), set a second attempt as homework with the marking notes from the first attempt as guidance. A candidate who has had two attempts at the speed-test is much better positioned for D9.3 / D9.4.

## Materials checklist

- Two compasses (trainer and candidate).
- The candidate's altimeter.
- The candidate's exam-area map for the speed-test.
- A blank route-card template (eleven fields per leg, six legs per card).
- A reference card written by the trainer for the same tour, used for the self-mark in D9.2 Exercise 2.
- A timer for the speed-test.
- Pencils, ruler, eraser.
- The IMS Nav Programme Quick Reference Card 3 (route card template) for reference.

## Delivery notes - D9.1

**Framing.** A route card is only useful if it captures the right information in the right form. The eleven fields are the working set; missing fields or vague entries break the card.

**The eleven fields.** Walk through them in order. The two most-confused fields (start vs end altitude, key terrain vs avalanche assessment) deserve extra attention.

**Under-described fields.** The key-terrain and avalanche-assessment fields are the typical failures. Show a complete entry alongside an under-described one; the contrast lands the standard.

**Worked example.** The hut-to-col leg is the model. Walk through it on the board, populating each field. Have the candidate identify which field is which as you write.

**Exercise 1.** Labelling. Mark for all eleven fields; coach on any missed.

**Exercise 2.** Function-matching. Watch for the start/end and terrain/avalanche swaps.

**Exercise 3.** Fill in one leg. Mark for the under-description criteria (multiple terrain features; specific avalanche assessment using C6.2 vocabulary).

**Exercise 4.** The under-description identification. Push for specific examples (the candidate's own entry if it shows the pattern).

## Delivery notes - D9.2

**Framing.** The 20-minute speed standard is the IMS / EMS assessable benchmark. Hold it from the first attempt; coach for the trade-off (speed and quality together, not one or the other).

**The procedure.** Drill the nine-step sequence (identify legs, take bearings, apply variation, target altitude, distance and time, terrain features, avalanche assessment, decision points, escape routes, self-check). Decision points and escape routes come later (D9.3 / D9.4); for D9.2 the candidate works through the first seven steps for each leg.

**The 20-minute target.** Approximately 200 seconds per leg for a six-leg card. The breakdown shows where time should go; a candidate spending 60 seconds on the bearing per leg has D2.1 not yet automatic and should recycle.

**Worked example.** Walk through the timed example on the board with the candidate. Have them write the card alongside as you talk through it; the parallel processing builds the procedural awareness.

**Exercise 1.** The speed-test. Set the timer; do not coach during the writing. After the timer ends, mark immediately with the candidate.

**Exercise 2.** The self-mark against reference. The conversation is more valuable than the score; sit with the candidate and discuss the differences field by field.

**Exercise 3.** Identify the slow legs. Push for the underlying-skill diagnosis (D2.1, D2.2, C6.2 etc.). The candidate's specific slow legs are coaching opportunities.

**Exercise 4.** The under-description test. The candidate reads their own card honestly. If they cannot see the under-description, point at specific entries and ask "is this enough for someone else to follow on the day?"

## Common learner difficulties

- **Forgetting one or more fields.** Drill the eleven-field list; coach the consistent habit.
- **Vague terrain features.** "Col at 2,720 m" is not enough. Multiple specific features in order along the leg is the standard.
- **Vague avalanche assessment.** "Steep, north-facing" is not enough. The C6.2 terrain-trap reading should be referenced.
- **Slow on bearings.** D2.1 not yet automatic. Recycle D2.1.
- **Slow on variation.** D2.2 mnemonic not yet automatic. Drill.
- **Speed without quality.** A card produced in 18 minutes but with vague entries has met the wrong target. Coach the trade-off.
- **Skipping the self-check.** A candidate who produces six entries and immediately moves on has missed the integrity check. Insist on the read-through.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - Set a stretch target: 15 minutes for a six-leg card with no under-description. Achievable for genuinely strong candidates; surfaces the assessable speed at the next level up.
  - On D9.1 Exercise 4, ask the candidate to identify the typical under-description in a sample card from a different candidate (or from the trainer's archive). The diagnostic skill is harder than producing the card.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D9.1, walk through the function-matching together before the candidate attempts Exercise 2 alone.
  - On D9.2, produce the first leg together with the candidate alongside; then time them on the remaining five legs.
  - On the speed-test, untime if necessary; speed comes after quality. Re-attempt under time pressure once the under-description is fixed.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D9.3 (decision points) when they can:

- name the eleven fields of an IMS-style route card on sight,
- match each field to its function without thinking about it,
- write a complete six-leg card within 30 minutes (working towards the 20-minute target),
- populate all nine basic fields for every leg,
- avoid the typical under-description in the key-terrain and avalanche-assessment fields,
- and self-mark their own card honestly against a reference.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. D9.3 (decision points) and D9.4 (escape routes) assume the candidate can produce a complete basic card reliably; without that, the contingency layer becomes paperwork rather than the integrative discipline it is meant to be.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The eleven-field route card is consistent with the IMS Nav Programme template (Quick Reference Card 3) and with broader Mountain Training UK / Mountaineering Scotland published route-card guidance. Specific organisations use slightly different field sets; the workbook holds the eleven fields as the working standard.

The 20-minute speed target is from the IMS Nav Programme assessment-checklist. The breakdown into per-leg time (approximately 200 seconds) is derived from the total target; specific candidates may distribute the time differently across legs but should hit the 20-minute total.

The under-description criteria are workbook-specific framing of standard route-card quality. The two typical fields (key terrain, avalanche assessment) are the trainer's experience-based observation; specific candidates may have different weak spots.
