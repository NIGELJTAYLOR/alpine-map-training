## Purpose of these sections

D1.1 and D1.2 introduce the compass as an instrument before the technique pages in D2 ask the candidate to use it. D1.1 covers the six main parts and what each does. D1.2 covers reading a bearing from the bezel, holding the compass correctly, and recognising sluggish needle behaviour.

These pages are pitched at familiarity, not technique. Everything practical begins at D2.1. The job at D1 is to make the parts and the reading habits invisible so the candidate can focus on the technique without fumbling for the orienting arrow.

## Coaching priorities

1. The six parts named on sight, with no hesitation between the orienting arrow and the direction-of-travel arrow.
2. The 2-degrees-in-60-seconds reading standard from the start.
3. The three holding habits (flat, away from steel, settled needle) as automatic.
4. The known-direction quick-check as a habit before any field use.
5. The three causes of sluggish needle behaviour and the right action for each.

## Teaching approach

Bring a real baseplate compass to the session. Lay it on the desk and walk through the six parts in order, naming each and pointing. Have the candidate name them back. Then ask them to identify each part on their own compass. The hands-on identification cements faster than diagram-only labelling.

For the orienting-arrow vs direction-of-travel-arrow distinction, demonstrate with the bezel set to a non-cardinal bearing (for example, 137 degrees). Show that the direction-of-travel arrow stays put on the baseplate while the orienting arrow rotates with the bezel. Have the candidate physically rotate the bezel and watch which arrow moves.

For the reading habits, demonstrate the wrong way first (compass tilted, held next to a phone) and have the candidate observe the needle behaviour. Then demonstrate the right way and have them try. The contrast between the two readings makes the habits stick faster than the right-way demonstration alone.

## Suggested timings

Treat D1.1 and D1.2 as a single 60-minute block.

| Activity | Time |
|---|---|
| Recap of Level 2 readiness; framing of Level 3 instruments | 5 min |
| D1.1 core idea, six parts, two-most-confused | 10 min |
| D1.1 Exercises 1 to 4 | 15 min |
| D1.2 core idea, reading the bearing, holding habits | 10 min |
| D1.2 Exercises 1 to 5 | 15 min |
| Self-check and discussion | 5 min |
| **Total** | **60 min** |

## Materials checklist

- Two baseplate compasses (one for the trainer, one for the candidate). The Silva Type 4 / 15TDCL family or equivalent is a reasonable working choice; any reliable baseplate compass with a rotating bezel and parallel orienting lines works.
- The Section D1.1 labelled-compass diagram (with labels removed for Exercise 1).
- Three compass photographs for D1.2 Exercise 1, each showing a needle settled inside an orienting arrow on a different bezel setting (the standard photographs are configured at 047, 192 and 318 degrees per the answer key).
- A mobile phone (used as a deliberate interference source for the demonstration).
- Pencils, eraser, plain paper.

## Delivery notes - D1.1

**Framing.** Open by recapping the Level 2 readiness criteria. The candidate moves into Level 3 having mastered contour reading, aspect, visualisation, and route thinking. Now the instruments come in. The compass is the first.

**The six parts.** Walk through them in order. Hold up the real compass and name each part as you point. Have the candidate name them back. Repeat until the candidate can name all six on sight without prompting.

**The two most-confused parts.** Spend extra time here. The orienting-arrow vs direction-of-travel-arrow distinction is the foundation for everything in D2. Demonstrate with the bezel rotated to a non-cardinal bearing so both arrows are visibly different.

**Exercise 1.** A labelling exercise. Watch for swaps between orienting arrow and direction-of-travel arrow; both are arrows, both look similar on a diagram. The fix is to confirm which one moves when the bezel turns.

**Exercise 2.** A function-matching exercise. The most common error is swapping (a) and (f); both involve alignment. The orienting arrow aligns with the magnetic needle (in the field, when walking on a bearing); the parallel orienting lines align with the grid lines on the map (at the desk, when taking a bearing from the map).

**Exercise 3.** The explanatory exercise. Push for the consequence of confusing the two arrows, not just the visual similarity. The candidate who can articulate that confusing them produces a wrong bearing has internalised the distinction.

**Exercise 4.** A quick mental-model check. Watch for candidates who try to rotate the bezel at step 2 (when the right answer is to rotate the body); this is a sign that the bezel-rotation procedure from D2.1 has been anticipated and applied wrongly here.

## Delivery notes - D1.2

**Framing.** Open with the 2-degrees-in-60-seconds standard. This is the IMS / EMS working target and it sets the bar for everything that follows. Hold it from the first reading.

**Reading the bearing.** Demonstrate on the real compass. Have the candidate read it back, then re-read on a different bezel setting. The first few readings often take 20 to 30 seconds; speed comes with practice.

**Holding the compass correctly.** Demonstrate the wrong-way / right-way contrast. Hold the compass tilted next to a phone and let the candidate read; then hold it correctly and read again. The angular difference is usually 10 degrees or more; the contrast lands the habits faster than instruction alone.

**Sluggish needle.** Cover the three causes briefly. The cold-temperature point is most relevant for snow conditions; the magnetic-interference point is most relevant for any near-equipment use; the damaged-compass point is rare but worth mentioning so the candidate knows the failure mode exists.

**Exercise 1.** Reading from photographs. Watch for candidates reading the wrong index mark; some compasses have multiple printed scales (degrees, mils, cardinal points). The bearing is read at the direction-of-travel arrow against the degree scale.

**Exercise 2.** Cardinal cross-check. A four-direction-vs-eight-direction slip from C4.2 will appear here. Recycle C4.2 if persistent.

**Exercise 3.** The explanatory exercise. Push for the failure example for each habit; vague answers ("hold it properly") are coaching opportunities.

**Exercise 4.** The quick-check drill. Insist on the candidate doing this with their own compass at home before D2.1. A compass that disagrees with a known direction is a compass that should not be relied on for bearings.

**Exercise 5.** The sluggish-needle troubleshooting. The most common error is correctly identifying the cause but pairing it with the wrong action. A candidate who says "magnetic interference: replace the compass" has not internalised the troubleshooting hierarchy.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Orienting arrow / direction-of-travel arrow confusion.** Most common D1 error. Drill the bezel-rotation demonstration until the distinction is automatic.
- **Reading the wrong index mark.** Some compasses have multiple printed scales. Confirm the candidate is reading degrees, not mils or cardinal points.
- **Bearings consistently 5 degrees or more off.** Almost always a holding error (tilt, interference, unsettled needle). Run the Exercise 4 drill in person and watch the candidate's grip.
- **Speed sacrificed for accuracy or vice versa.** The standard is both inside the limits. A candidate at 60 seconds with bearings 5 degrees off has the wrong trade-off.
- **Skipping the quick-check.** Once or twice is forgivable; routinely is not. The quick-check is a 30-second habit that prevents a wrong-bearing leg.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - Set a stretch target: 1-degree accuracy in 30 seconds. Achievable with practice; signals readiness for D2 speed work.
  - Add a sighting-mirror compass to the instrument set and demonstrate its use. Sighting compasses are more precise for distant features but slower for general bearings.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - Spend the full 15 minutes on D1.1 Exercises 1 to 4 before moving on. The parts must be solid before the bearing reading makes sense.
  - For Exercise 1 of D1.2, work through the first photograph with the candidate alongside before they attempt the other two alone.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D2.1 (taking a bearing from the map) when they can:

- name the six parts of a baseplate compass on sight,
- distinguish the orienting arrow from the direction-of-travel arrow without hesitation,
- read a bearing from a set compass within 2 degrees,
- hold the compass flat, away from steel, and let the needle settle by habit,
- run a known-direction quick-check before relying on the compass,
- and recognise the three causes of sluggish needle behaviour.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. The D2 procedures compound D1 errors; weak D1 produces weak D2.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The compass-parts vocabulary is general navigation skill and is consistent across BASI, Mountain Training UK, Mountaineering Scotland, and the broader navigation literature. The 2-degrees-in-60-seconds reading standard is drawn from the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF and is consistent with Mountain Leader-level expectations.

The "red to shed" mnemonic for which end of the magnetic needle points north is folk-pedagogy rather than published convention; it is widely used in Mountain Training UK courses and is included for memorability rather than authority.
