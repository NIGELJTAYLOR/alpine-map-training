## Purpose of these sections

D4 closes out the compass section of Level 3 by refining practical accuracy. D4.1 is the bearing-shapes drill, the calibration tool that produces a single number (closure error) for the candidate's compass-and-pacing accuracy. D4.2 is aiming off, the deliberate-offset technique that converts a small bearing error into a reliable handrail walk. D4.3 is magnetic interference and instrument hygiene, the silent-error sources that quietly degrade compass readings.

These three pages are not new technique in the way D2 and D3 were. They are the habits that make the D2 and D3 techniques reliable in real conditions.

## Coaching priorities

1. The bearing-shapes drill as a measurable calibration tool that produces a tracking number across sessions.
2. The IMS / EMS closure-error standards (less than 5 paces for excellent, 5 to 10 for good, 10 to 20 for habit-fix-needed, more than 20 for technique problem).
3. The aiming-off habit as the standard for poor-visibility legs to small targets.
4. The safe-side discipline in aiming off (the side without a hazard).
5. The personal interference table as the working tool for managing magnetic interference.
6. The four instrument-hygiene checks as a routine before any tour.

## Teaching approach

Run the D4 triplet as a single 90-minute block. The pages are independent enough that the order can be flexed if the practical drills require specific conditions (D4.1 needs an open space; D4.2 is desk-based; D4.3 is best done with the candidate's actual equipment).

For D4.1, the bearing-shapes drill is the heart of the page. If outdoor space is not available in the session itself, set the drill as homework with explicit instructions on how to record and report the closure error. The trainer should also have run the drill themselves recently so they can speak from experience about what each closure-error band feels like.

For D4.2, walk through the worked example on the board with a real map extract. The aiming-off concept is straightforward but the safe-side choice is route-specific; demonstrate the thinking on at least two different targets so the candidate sees the variation.

For D4.3, run the interference test with the candidate's own equipment. The personal interference table from a candidate's own equipment is far more useful than generic figures.

## Materials checklist

- Two baseplate compasses (trainer and candidate).
- For D4.1: an open space of at least 100 m by 100 m (a park, a beach, a flat field). Set as homework if not available.
- For D4.2: the Section D4.2 map extract with three named targets, each near a different type of linear feature (stream, ridge, forest edge).
- For D4.3: the candidate's own equipment (mobile phone, ski binding, ice axe if used, avalanche transceiver).
- A timer for the bearing-shapes drill.
- Pencils, ruler, eraser.
- A flipchart or whiteboard for the worked examples.

## Delivery notes - D4.1

**Framing.** The bearing-shapes drill is the calibration tool. It tells the candidate, in a single number, how accurate their compass-and-pacing skill actually is. Track the number across sessions to see whether the candidate's accuracy is improving.

**The square drill.** Walk through the procedure on a whiteboard before the candidate attempts it outdoors. The four sides at 90-degree turns are simple in concept but easy to miscalculate under pressure (especially the wrap from 357 back to 087, for example). Have the candidate calculate the four side bearings before walking.

**Standards for closure error.** The four bands (under 5, 5 to 10, 10 to 20, over 20 paces) are working figures. The IMS / EMS standard is less than 5 paces. Hold the standard but recognise that most candidates start in the 10-to-20 band and improve with practice.

**Pace length calibration.** A candidate who has not calibrated their pace length cannot convert pace closure into metres. If the candidate has not done this, do it before the bearing-shapes drill: walk a known 100 m and count paces; divide 100 by the count to get pace length in metres.

**The triangle and hexagon.** Different turn rhythms expose different errors. The triangle (120-degree turns) is hardest because the turns are unfamiliar; the hexagon (60-degree turns) is gentlest because each turn is small. Run all three to see which exposes the candidate's specific error pattern.

**The deliberate-offset variation.** The 3-degree offset over four sides typically produces 10 to 15 paces of closure error. The point is to make the candidate feel what a small bearing error costs in practical terms.

**Exercise 1.** The square drill. Mark for closure error against the standards. Identify the side that contributed most and coach the cause from D2.3.

**Exercise 2.** The triangle drill. Compare with the square; expect the triangle to be slightly worse for most candidates.

**Exercise 3.** The hexagon drill. Compare with the square and triangle; expect the hexagon to be similar to or slightly better than the square.

**Exercise 4.** The deliberate offset. The candidate should see the error pattern compounds.

**Exercise 5.** The implications-for-tour-legs scaling. Mark for the right order of magnitude and the right scaling.

## Delivery notes - D4.2

**Framing.** Aiming off is the small habit that turns a fragile route card into a robust one. It is one of the IMS / EMS assessor's favourite questions because it tests the candidate's planning thinking, not just their compass skill.

**The technique.** Three components: linear feature, safe side, offset bearing. Walk through each in the worked example.

**The safe-side choice.** This is route-specific. The candidate must think it through for every aim-off. Demonstrate the thinking explicitly: identify the hazard, choose the side without the hazard, then calculate the offset direction.

**The trade-off.** Distance cost is small (about 1.5 percent for a 10-degree offset over a 1 km leg); reliability benefit is large. Make the trade-off explicit so the candidate is not deterred by the additional distance.

**Exercise 1.** Identifying the linear feature for each target. Watch for candidates who name a feature that is not unmistakable when reached (the slope eases, not a stream); coach the unmistakable-feature requirement.

**Exercise 2.** The safe-side choice. The reason for each choice should reference a specific hazard, not a general principle. A candidate who answers "the south side because it is safer" has not engaged with the actual terrain.

**Exercise 3.** The aim-off bearing calculation. The arithmetic is straightforward; the direction (add or subtract) is what catches candidates. Drill the rule: subtract to move the bearing towards a smaller value (north when the direct bearing is south); add to move the bearing towards a larger value.

**Exercise 4.** The poor-visibility implication. Push for both points: visual confirmation unavailable; safe-side choice more critical because hazards are unseen.

## Delivery notes - D4.3

**Framing.** Magnetic interference is the silent error source. A bearing read with a compass next to a steel ski binding is consistently 3 to 5 degrees off, but the candidate may not notice. The personal interference table is the working tool that turns generic guidance into a specific habit.

**The four common sources.** Steel ski bindings, ice axes, mobile phones, radio handsets. The minimum safe distances (50 cm for steel, 30 cm for electronics) are working figures; the candidate's personal table from Exercise 1 may show slightly different figures depending on their specific equipment.

**The test procedure.** Demonstrate with the candidate's mobile phone. Set the compass to a known direction; move the phone progressively closer; note the distance at first deflection. The hands-on demonstration cements the habit faster than instruction.

**Other interference sources.** Avalanche transceivers in transmit mode are worth flagging because they are always within 50 cm of the compass when both are worn. Vehicles and reinforced concrete are worth noting for the calibration / quick-check at the start of a tour.

**Instrument hygiene.** The four checks (baseplate cracks, damping fluid, bezel smoothness, quick-check against known direction) are a 60-second habit. The quick-check is the most important because it catches problems that the visual inspection might miss.

**Exercise 1.** The interference test with the candidate's own equipment. The personal table is the artefact; make sure the candidate writes it down somewhere they can refer to.

**Exercise 2.** The highest-risk source identification. For most candidates this is the steel ski binding. Push for a specific management action.

**Exercise 3.** The four hygiene checks. Mark for all four named with a one-sentence action for each.

**Exercise 4.** The disagreement-with-known-direction scenario. The 2-degree case is acceptable; the 5-degree threshold is the limit. Coach the working judgement.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Closure error consistently in the 10-to-20-paces band.** A habit-fix-needed band; usually a D2.3 walking-technique issue (tilted compass, body off-axis, leaning into slope). Run the diagnostic with the candidate alongside.
- **Forgetting to calibrate pace length.** Without pace length, pace closure cannot be converted to metres. Calibrate before the drill.
- **Aiming off without thinking about the safe side.** A routine offset that does not consider hazards is worse than a direct bearing. Drill the safe-side discipline.
- **Adding when they should subtract on the aim-off offset.** The direction-of-offset arithmetic. Mark and coach.
- **Believing the compass cannot be wrong.** A candidate who trusts every compass reading without considering interference has missed D4.3. The known-direction quick-check is the discipline.
- **Skipping the personal interference table.** A candidate who reads D4.3 but does not run the test with their own equipment has the abstract knowledge without the specific habit. Insist on the table.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - On D4.1, run the square drill at 200 paces per side to see whether the closure error scales linearly. Then run with a deliberately variable pace (alternating long and short steps) to see the effect of pacing inconsistency.
  - On D4.2, ask the candidate to design an aim-off for a target that has multiple possible linear features and choose the best. The choice is not always obvious; the trade-off is between feature distinctiveness and approach length.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D4.1, walk the first square alongside the candidate, calling out each turn explicitly. Then have them attempt the triangle alone.
  - On D4.2, work through the first target together (linear feature, safe side, offset) before they attempt the other two.
  - On D4.3, run the interference test together with the candidate's phone before they attempt the rest of the equipment.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D5 (the altimeter) when they can:

- walk a 100-pace square with closure error in the 5-to-10-paces band or better,
- identify a linear feature for aim-off near any named target on the map,
- choose the safe side for an aim-off based on terrain hazards,
- calculate an aim-off bearing in the right direction,
- name the four common magnetic interference sources and their minimum safe distances,
- run the four instrument-hygiene checks as a routine,
- and produce a personal interference table for their equipment.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. The D5 altimeter work introduces the second navigation tool; the integrated D7 techniques assume both compass and altimeter are reliable.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The bearing-shapes drill is drawn from orienteering pedagogy and is referenced in the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF. The closure-error standards (less than 5 paces for excellent, etc.) are working figures consistent with the practice; published Mountain Training UK figures are similar.

The aiming-off technique is general mountain navigation skill, consistent across BASI, Mountain Training UK, Mountaineering Scotland, and the broader navigation literature. The safe-side discipline is workbook-specific framing of the standard "aim to the side away from the hazard" principle.

The magnetic interference figures (50 cm for steel objects, 30 cm for electronics) are from the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF and are consistent with general navigation guidance. Specific equipment may produce different figures; the personal interference table is the candidate's own working tool.
