## Purpose of these sections

D3 teaches the inverse direction of the D2 work. In D2 the candidate took bearings from the map (compass laid on map, then to body, then to ground). In D3 the candidate takes bearings from the field (compass pointed at feature, then to map for identification or position fix). Together D2 and D3 give the candidate the full map-and-field bearing skill set.

D3.1 is the foundational technique (taking a bearing to a visible feature). D3.2 covers back bearings as a confirmation tool with a specific IMS / EMS-relevant use as a poor-visibility sanity check. D3.3 covers resection, the two-feature position fix that requires good visibility.

## Coaching priorities

1. The inverse-of-D2 framing for D3.1: ground to map rather than map to ground.
2. The parallax-error discipline: consistent sighting habit, not casual eyeballing.
3. The plus-or-minus 180-degree rule for back bearings, with wrap.
4. The 5-to-10 minute back-bearing sanity check as the routine poor-visibility habit.
5. The 90-degree rule for resection feature selection.
6. The recognition that resection fails in poor visibility, with aspect-of-slope (D7.2) as the alpine alternative.

## Teaching approach

Run the D3 triplet as a single 90-minute block. The pages are tightly coupled (D3.1 produces the inputs for D3.2 and D3.3) and break-up dilutes the integrated technique.

For D3.1, demonstrate on a real compass with the candidate alongside. Stand at a window or door with a clear external view; take a bearing to a visible feature; convert magnetic to grid; identify the feature on a local map. The candidate then attempts the same. The hands-on demonstration cements the inverse direction faster than diagram-only instruction.

For D3.2, the back-bearing arithmetic is straightforward; the IMS / EMS-relevant point is the timing (every 5 to 10 minutes in poor visibility) and the action when the back bearing does not align (re-orient, do not push harder). Drill both points explicitly.

For D3.3, walk through the four-step procedure on the board with a real compass and a 1:25,000 extract. The plotting step (drawing the back bearing from the feature on the map) is the most error-prone; have the candidate practise the plotting before the practical drill.

## Materials checklist

- Two baseplate compasses (trainer and candidate).
- A 1:25,000 alpine map of an area both can see (or can imagine), for the desk demonstrations.
- For D3.1 Exercise 1: a high point with a clear view of three or more identifiable features (set as homework if not available in the session).
- For D3.3 Exercise 1: the Section D3.3 map extract with named features at usable bearings.
- For D3.3 Exercise 2: a known position with at least two named features visible (typically a different outdoor location from D3.1).
- A device with internet access for any variation look-up checks (per D2.2).
- Pencils, ruler, eraser.

## Delivery notes - D3.1

**Framing.** Open with the inverse-direction framing. The candidate already knows how to take a bearing from the map (D2.1); D3.1 is the same technique applied in the opposite direction. The procedure differs only in step 2: instead of placing the baseplate edge on the map line, the candidate points the direction-of-travel arrow at the feature.

**The procedure.** Walk through it slowly. The step that catches candidates is sighting along the baseplate edge; demonstrate the chest-height two-handed grip with one eye closed.

**Magnetic-to-grid conversion.** The variation rule reverses for the inverse direction. If the candidate set "grid to mag, add" as their D2.2 mnemonic for east variation, the inverse "mag to grid, subtract" applies here. Drill both directions.

**Parallax error.** The most common D3.1 error. Demonstrate with a deliberate parallax (head visibly off-axis) and read the resulting bearing; then with a careful sighting and re-read. The 5-to-10-degree difference makes the discipline land.

**Exercise 1.** Three field bearings. If desk-only, set as homework. The point is the practical experience of looking at a feature and producing a bearing; without the practical, the technique stays abstract.

**Exercise 2.** Identifying the feature on the map. Watch for candidates who pick the wrong feature on the line; the multiple-features-on-the-same-bearing trap is real.

**Exercise 3.** The multiple-feature distinguishing exercise. Push for at least one specific distinguishing factor (estimated distance, elevation cross-check, shape, third bearing).

**Exercise 4.** The parallax diagnostic. The 50-m-walk-and-re-take pattern is the controlled experiment that rules out compass-side causes.

## Delivery notes - D3.2

**Framing.** Back bearings are the confirmation tool. The IMS / EMS-relevant use is the poor-visibility sanity check.

**The 180-degree rule.** Drill the wrap explicitly. The most common error is failing to wrap (135 + 180 = 315 is straightforward; 312 + 180 = 492, which should wrap to 132 by subtracting 360).

**Worked example.** The bearing-then-back-bearing-after-200m pattern is the model for the routine sanity check. Have the candidate notice that the back bearing was within 1 degree of the predicted figure, which means no significant drift.

**Exercise 1.** Pure arithmetic. Mark for correctness; prompt for the wrap rule if any answer falls outside 0 to 360.

**Exercise 2.** The practical drill. The actual back bearing in the field should be close to the calculated one; if not, the candidate has drifted during the walk. Coach the D2.3 walking habits.

**Exercise 3.** The IMS / EMS habit. Push for the explicit timing (5 to 10 minutes) and the explicit action (re-orient, not push harder). A candidate who gives a generic "to check" has missed the working pattern.

**Exercise 4.** The relocation use. The point is that a back bearing to a known feature behind is more reliable than estimating distance walked, especially on snow where pacing fails.

## Delivery notes - D3.3

**Framing.** Resection is the standard map-and-compass position fix and is taught at every level of mountain navigation. The IMS / EMS-relevant point is recognising when it does not work and switching to aspect-of-slope (D7.2) as the alpine alternative.

**The four-step procedure.** Demonstrate on the board with a real compass and extract. The plotting step (drawing the back bearing from the feature) is the most error-prone; the candidate must align the parallel orienting lines with the map's grid lines (not just any vertical lines on the page) and draw in the correct direction.

**The 90-degree rule.** Two features at 90 degrees produce a sharp intersection; two features close together (or close to opposite) produce a shallow intersection where small bearing errors translate into large position errors. Make sure the candidate selects features with this in mind.

**The cocked hat.** A three-feature resection produces three lines that almost never cross at the same point. The triangle they form (the "cocked hat") is the position uncertainty. A cocked hat with sides longer than about 100 m at 1:25,000 indicates a bearing error worth investigating.

**Why resection fails in poor visibility.** Walk through the four scenarios in Exercise 4 with the candidate. The pattern is: no features visible, only one feature, features too close together, or all features in the same direction. Each is recognisable in advance; the candidate should know when to switch to aspect-of-slope.

**Exercise 1.** Plotting practice. The single most error-prone step in resection.

**Exercise 2.** The practical resection. A within-100 m result at 1:25,000 is at IMS / EMS standard.

**Exercise 3.** The 90-degree rule explanation. Push for the practical implication (small bearing error causing large position error), not just the geometry.

**Exercise 4.** The fail-mode recognition. The aspect-of-slope alternative should be named even if the candidate has not yet read D7.2. Accept "the technique we will cover in D7.2" or similar.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Forgetting the inverse-direction reversal of the variation rule.** A candidate who applied "grid to mag, add" in D2.2 may default to the same in D3.1; the reverse direction needs "mag to grid, subtract" for east variation. Drill both directions.
- **Parallax error.** Casual sighting produces consistent 5-to-10-degree errors. The chest-height two-handed grip with one eye closed is the fix.
- **Failing to wrap back bearings.** The most common D3.2 arithmetic error. Drill the rule.
- **Pushing harder on an original bearing when the back bearing does not align.** The wrong response. The right response is to re-orient.
- **Plotting back bearings in the wrong direction on the map.** The most common D3.3 plotting error. Demonstrate the correct direction explicitly.
- **Choosing two features close together angularly.** Produces a poor resection. The 90-degree rule is the discipline.
- **Believing resection works in any visibility.** It does not. Coach the fail modes explicitly.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - On D3.1, ask the candidate to take bearings to features they cannot immediately identify on the map and use the bearing to identify them. This is harder than the standard exercise because there is no a-priori expectation.
  - On D3.3, run a three-feature resection and have the candidate interpret the cocked hat. A small triangle is a good fix; a large triangle is a sign of a bearing error.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D3.1, demonstrate the parallax effect explicitly with the candidate alongside; have them feel the difference between a casual sighting and a careful one.
  - On D3.2, work through Exercise 1 together before they attempt Exercise 2 alone.
  - On D3.3, plot the worked-example back bearings on the map together before they attempt their own.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D4 (practical accuracy) when they can:

- take a magnetic bearing to a visible feature within 2 degrees,
- convert magnetic to grid using the right rule for their local variation,
- use a grid bearing and a known position to identify a feature on the map,
- calculate any back bearing from a forward bearing using the plus-or-minus 180-degree rule,
- take a back bearing as a routine sanity check in poor visibility,
- perform a resection from two features within 100 m of the known position at 1:25,000,
- and recognise the conditions under which resection fails.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. The D4 practical-accuracy work assumes D3 fluency; the bearing-shapes drill in particular tests the integrated D2-and-D3 skill set.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The bearing-to-feature, back-bearing, and resection techniques are general navigation skills consistent across BASI, Mountain Training UK, Mountaineering Scotland, and the broader navigation literature. The 90-degree rule for resection feature selection is geometric and consistent.

The recognition that resection fails in alpine poor visibility, and the substitution of aspect-of-slope as the alpine alternative, is consistent with the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF and with Alpine Guides' altimeter-navigation guide. Both treat aspect-of-slope (D7.2) as the alpine-specific relocation technique.

The cocked-hat concept is general navigation terminology; the working threshold (sides longer than about 100 m at 1:25,000 indicating a bearing error worth investigating) is a workbook-specific working figure consistent with practical IMS / EMS expectations.
