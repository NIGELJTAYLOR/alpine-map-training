## Purpose of these sections

D9.3 and D9.4 add the contingency layer to the route card. D9.3 covers decision points and go / no-go criteria: where on the route the candidate stops to decide, and what conditions trigger which decision. D9.4 covers escape routes and contingency planning: the alternative route if the planned route cannot be completed.

D9.3 builds on D6.2 (the pressure-to-weather decision framework) and applies the three-component criteria standard to the full route. D9.4 builds on C6.2 (terrain traps and committing terrain) and applies alternative-line thinking to every leg.

After D9 the route card is the complete integrative artefact: basic fields (D9.1), full writing (D9.2), decision points with criteria (D9.3), and escape routes with contingency thinking (D9.4). D10 then closes Level 3 with consolidation.

## Coaching priorities

1. The placement of decision points at meaningful locations (cols, points of no return, major slope crossings, time / weather checkpoints).
2. The three-component criteria standard from D6.2 (specific trigger, specific action, named decision point) applied across the full route.
3. The variety of trigger types (time, weather, group condition, snow observation, terrain-trap concern) rather than only one.
4. The escape route as a different route from the planned route (not just a reversal unless reversal is meaningfully safer).
5. The recognition of escape routes that become unavailable in worsening conditions.
6. The no-viable-escape recognition as a flag that the route choice itself may be wrong.

## Teaching approach

Run D9.3 and D9.4 as a single 90-minute block. The two pages are tightly coupled: the criteria from D9.3 are the triggers for taking the escape routes from D9.4.

For D9.3, walk through the worked example on the candidate's own tour from D9.2 (or a fresh tour if the candidate's earlier card is not suitable). Identify decision points together; then have the candidate write criteria for each. The criteria-writing exercise is the page's most important.

For D9.4, walk through escape-route identification on the same tour. The candidate should identify escape routes for every leg, then stress-test by asking which would not work in worsening conditions. The no-viable-escape question is the closing test.

Throughout both pages, emphasise the decision-point and escape-route connection: the criterion at the decision point triggers the escape route. They are two halves of the same discipline.

## Delivery notes - D9.3

**Framing.** A route card without decision points is a one-way commitment. Decision points turn the card into a managed plan with structured fall-back at specific moments.

**Placement.** Three to five decision points for a typical six-leg tour. Fewer suggests under-engineering; more suggests over-engineering. The placement is at meaningful locations: cols, points of no return, major slope crossings, time / weather checkpoints.

**The three-component criteria standard.** Same as D6.2: specific trigger, specific action, named decision point. Drill the standard.

**Trigger variety.** Coach the candidate towards multiple trigger types. A card with five criteria all triggered by time of day has limited contingency; a card with five criteria triggered by time, weather, group condition, snow observation, and terrain-trap concern has rich contingency.

**Worked example.** Walk through the four-decision-point tour on the board. Identify each location, write the criteria for each, and cross-check with the candidate.

**Exercise 1.** Identify decision points. Mark for three to five at meaningful locations.

**Exercise 2.** Write criteria. Mark for the three-component standard at every criterion.

**Exercise 3.** Recognise weak criteria. The five examples cover the typical patterns. Mark for the right yes/no judgement and for acceptable rewrites.

**Exercise 4.** The "want to continue" reflection. Push for the explicit pressure-vs-calm-conditions framing.

## Delivery notes - D9.4

**Framing.** An escape route is a different route from the planned route. Every committing decision should have one; legs with no viable escape are flagged for review.

**What an escape route is.** A different bearing or different waypoint, not just the planned route in reverse (unless reversal is meaningfully safer). Coach the alternative-line thinking.

**Where to plan escape routes.** From the start of the leg, from the end of the leg, from any committing point within the leg. For most legs, three escape-route considerations.

**When no escape route exists.** A committing leg. The candidate has two responses: tighten the entry criteria, or reconsider the route choice. Coach both.

**Worked example.** Walk through the escape-route identification on the candidate's tour. Each leg should have at least one escape route named; if not, flag the leg as committing.

**Exercise 1.** Identify escape routes for each leg. Mark for genuine alternatives, not just reversals.

**Exercise 2.** Identify escape routes that become unavailable. The stress-test.

**Exercise 3.** The no-viable-escape recognition. Press for honest review; a candidate with no flagged legs may not have engaged with the question.

**Exercise 4.** The connection to decision points. Push for the explicit trigger-and-action framing.

**Exercise 5.** The complete route card. The integration moment; the candidate's D9.2 card plus D9.3 criteria plus D9.4 escape routes is the IMS / EMS-style route card.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Decision points only at the obvious places.** A candidate who places decision points only at cols has missed the time / weather checkpoints and the points of no return. Coach the four placement types.
- **Criteria all using the same trigger type.** Time-only criteria are common; coach the trigger variety.
- **Vague criteria.** Same as D6.2; same fix (drill the three-component standard).
- **Escape routes as reversals.** A candidate whose escape routes are all "reverse the planned route" has not engaged with alternative-line thinking. Coach.
- **No flagged committing legs on a route that obviously has them.** A candidate who cannot recognise a committing leg has not engaged with the no-viable-escape question. Press.
- **Treating decision points and escape routes as separate concepts.** The two are connected (criterion triggers escape route). Coach the integration.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - On D9.3, ask the candidate to write criteria with multi-condition triggers ("if X and Y, take action Z"). The compound criteria are harder but more realistic.
  - On D9.4, run a what-if scenario where two escape routes are unavailable simultaneously (worsening weather plus an injured group member). The forced choice between bad options is the IMS / EMS-relevant edge case.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D9.3, work through the criteria-writing for one decision point together; have them write the rest alone.
  - On D9.4, identify the escape route for the first leg together; have them identify the rest alone.
  - On Exercise 5, provide a worked example for one leg's complete entry (basic fields plus criteria plus escape route); the candidate fills in the other legs to match the pattern.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D10 (consolidation) when they can:

- identify three to five decision points on a typical six-leg tour at meaningful locations,
- write go / no-go criteria meeting the three-component standard,
- use a variety of trigger types across their criteria,
- identify escape routes for every leg as genuine alternatives to the planned route,
- recognise escape routes that become unavailable in worsening conditions,
- recognise legs with no viable escape and respond appropriately (tighten entry criteria or reconsider the route),
- and produce a complete route card with all eleven fields populated for every leg.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. D10 (the virtual day on a tour) exercises the full route card and instrument toolkit; without a complete D9, the D10 narration becomes a list of techniques rather than the integrated working response it is meant to be.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The decision-point placement framework (cols, points of no return, major slope crossings, time / weather checkpoints) is workbook-specific framing of standard route-planning practice. Mountain Training UK and Mountaineering Scotland published guidance describes similar placement principles without using identical language.

The three-component criteria standard is workbook-specific framing of standard pre-agreed-criteria practice; it is consistent with the IMS Nav Programme decision framework (D6.2) and with broader expedition-planning literature.

The escape-route discipline is consistent with general alpine planning practice. The no-viable-escape recognition is workbook-specific framing of the principle that route choice should be revisited when the contingency plan is empty; this is a standard insight in expedition-planning literature.
