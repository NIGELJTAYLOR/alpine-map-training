## Purpose of these sections

D5.3 and D5.4 complete the altimeter section of Level 3. D5.3 covers pressure drift and weather effects, with the 7-day altimeter day log as the central practical drill. D5.4 covers using the altimeter as the primary navigation tool on featureless terrain, with the "aim high" strategy for safe descents.

Together with D5.1 (theory) and D5.2 (calibration), the four D5 pages give the candidate the full altimeter skill set. After D5 the candidate flips between compass-primary and altimeter-primary modes deliberately, with the route card making the choice explicit for each leg.

## Coaching priorities

1. The 7-day day log as the calibration of trust.
2. The dual use of the altimeter: altitude reference and weather signal.
3. The IMS Nav Programme rate bands (under 0.5 hPa/hr mild; 0.5 to 1 hPa/hr moderate; over 1 hPa/hr rapid).
4. The contour-traverse technique with altimeter as the hovering reference.
5. The "aim high" strategy for descents to targets above committing terrain.
6. The compass-primary vs altimeter-primary distinction made explicit on the route card.

## Teaching approach

Run D5.3 and D5.4 as a single 75-minute block, but with the 7-day day log set as homework rather than completed in-session.

For D5.3, walk through the worked example of the hut-overnight scenario on the board. The arithmetic is simple but the response (recalibrate, update route card, apply decision framework) is the IMS / EMS-relevant integration. The candidate should see all three responses, not just the recalibration.

For D5.4, demonstrate the contour-traverse technique on a 1:25,000 extract with the candidate alongside. Identify a contour that runs across the slope; trace it with a finger; calculate the bearing; explain how the altimeter would hover around the contour value during the traverse.

The "aim high" strategy is best taught with a deliberate worked example involving committing terrain (cliff band below the target). The visceral "what if the candidate overshoots" framing makes the strategy stick faster than abstract instruction.

## Delivery notes - D5.3

**Framing.** The altimeter is two instruments in one. A candidate who treats it only as an altitude reference has missed half its value.

**The pressure-altitude relationship.** Drill the 1 hPa = 8 m rule and the direction (weather going down, altimeter going up). Both halves matter; the rule without the direction produces inverted predictions.

**Reading the altimeter as weather.** The three patterns (stationary apparent gain; recalibration discrepancy; apparent rise during a descent) are the typical IMS / EMS-relevant moments. Walk through each on the board.

**Rate bands.** The IMS Nav Programme decision framework gives mild, moderate, and rapid bands. These come back formally in D6.2 but should be introduced here so the candidate has the vocabulary before the day-log analysis.

**The 7-day day log.** This is the calibration of trust. Insist on the drill; it cannot be substituted with a single in-session demonstration. Discuss the data with the candidate when it arrives.

**Worked example.** The hut-overnight scenario is the model for the dual response. Walk through:

- the arithmetic (apparent gain → implied pressure drop → rate),
- the rate-band classification,
- the three responses (recalibrate, update route card, apply decision framework),
- and the decision (continue plan, modify, abort).

A candidate who arrives at only one response has not internalised the integration.

**Exercise 1.** The 7-day log itself. Mark when the data arrives.

**Exercise 2.** The pattern-identification across the seven days. Push for the specific weather correlation (which weather → which drift), not just "weather affects the altimeter".

**Exercise 3.** The pressure-change calculations. Mark the arithmetic and the direction.

**Exercise 4.** The midnight scenario. The IMS / EMS-relevant exercise. Hold the standard for the action at point 6: calm assessment, group involvement, pre-agreed criteria, decision.

**Exercise 5.** The trust reflection. Push for the specific behaviours, not the generic "trust it less in bad weather".

## Delivery notes - D5.4

**Framing.** The altimeter-primary mode is the alpine-specific application. On uniform slopes, in featureless basins, and on poor-visibility plateaux, the altimeter is what makes the navigation work.

**The contour traverse.** Demonstrate on the board with a real extract. Identify a contour running across a slope; trace it; calculate the bearing for the leg; explain the hovering procedure. The visual demonstration cements the concept faster than instruction.

**The hovering procedure.** The candidate walks roughly on the bearing, watching the altimeter. Drift up means turn slightly downhill; drift down means turn slightly uphill. The actual line is a small zig-zag around the target altitude. Within plus or minus 5 m is achievable with attention.

**The aim-high strategy.** Walk through the worked example with the cliff band below the target. The strategy is the discipline that turns a routine descent into a controlled approach. Coach the always-know-what-is-below-your-target principle.

**Exercise 1.** Identifying altimeter-primary traverses. The candidate must understand what makes a traverse altimeter-primary (no lateral features) rather than just any traverse.

**Exercise 2.** The route-card-style entry. Mark for all seven fields populated and consistent.

**Exercise 3.** The aim-high scenario. Push for the explicit reasoning about the cliff band; vague "to be safe" answers miss the specific hazard.

**Exercise 4.** The compass-vs-altimeter primary distinction. The route card should make the choice explicit. A candidate who does not articulate the choice has not internalised the dual-mode flexibility.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Skipping the 7-day day log.** A candidate who skips the drill has the abstract knowledge without the personal calibration. Insist.
- **Inverting the pressure-altitude direction.** Drill the mnemonic.
- **Reading drift only as altitude error, not as weather signal.** The dual-use framing is the fix.
- **Treating every leg as compass-primary.** Coach the explicit choice for each leg.
- **Aiming exactly at the target altitude when descending.** The aim-high buffer is the discipline. Demonstrate the visceral consequence (overshoot onto a cliff band).
- **Assuming altimeter-primary works on any traverse.** It works on featureless traverses; on legs with abundant lateral features, compass-primary is faster.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - On D5.3, ask the candidate to predict the altimeter behaviour overnight given a forecast (improving or deteriorating weather) and check the prediction against the morning reading.
  - On D5.4, plan a mixed route with both compass-primary and altimeter-primary legs, and have the candidate justify the mode choice for each leg.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D5.3, work through the calculation in Exercise 4 together; the IMS / EMS-relevant action sequence is the harder part.
  - On D5.4, walk through the first contour-traverse identification together before they attempt the other two.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D6 (the barometer) when they can:

- explain the dual use of the altimeter (altitude reference and weather signal),
- run the pressure-change arithmetic in both directions,
- classify a pressure rate against the IMS Nav Programme bands,
- complete the 7-day day log and articulate the trust calibration that emerged from it,
- identify altimeter-primary traverses on a 1:25,000 alpine map,
- plan a contour traverse with target altitude, bearing, and calibration plan,
- apply the aim-high strategy to descents above committing terrain,
- and articulate the compass-primary vs altimeter-primary distinction on the route card.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. D6 builds on the altimeter-as-weather-signal use; the integrated D7 techniques assume confident D5 fluency.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The 1 hPa = 8 m rule and the rate bands (mild, moderate, rapid) are drawn from the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF and are consistent with standard alpine navigation practice. The specific band thresholds (under 0.5, 0.5 to 1, over 1 hPa per hour) are the IMS Nav Programme working figures.

The contour-traverse technique with altimeter as the hovering reference is consistent with Alpine Guides' altimeter-navigation guide and with general alpine navigation practice. The aim-high strategy is also referenced in the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF as a standard descending-to-target technique.

The compass-primary vs altimeter-primary distinction is workbook-specific framing of the standard alpine practice of choosing the right primary tool for each leg.
