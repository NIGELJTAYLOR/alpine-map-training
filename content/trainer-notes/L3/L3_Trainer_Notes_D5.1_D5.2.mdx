## Purpose of these sections

D5.1 and D5.2 introduce the altimeter as the second navigation instrument of Level 3. D5.1 covers the mechanism (barometric altimeter measures pressure, converts to altitude using a standard atmosphere model) and the distinction from GPS altimeters. D5.2 covers the practical setup (calibration at known reference points) and the recalibration habit.

These two pages set up D5.3 (drift management) and D5.4 (altimeter as primary tool). The four D5 pages together give the candidate the full altimeter skill set.

The pedagogical move at D5 is from compass-as-direction-tool to altimeter-as-position-tool. The candidate who has been thinking of navigation as "which direction" for D1 to D4 now starts thinking about "which contour".

## Coaching priorities

1. The pressure-to-altitude mechanism explained well enough that drift makes sense as a feature, not a bug.
2. The distinction between barometric and GPS altimeters, with barometric as the IMS / EMS-recommended primary tool.
3. The calibration discipline: precisely known altitude at a precisely identified point.
4. The recalibration habit at every reliable known point on a tour.
5. The reliable-vs-unreliable point distinction (no calibration at approximate altitudes or unmarked junctions).
6. The recalibration moment as both an altimeter correction and a weather observation.

## Teaching approach

Open D5.1 by demonstrating the 24-hour stationary drift on a real altimeter if possible. If the trainer has been recording their own home altimeter for a few days before the session, show the data; it makes the drift concept concrete in a way that no diagram can match.

For D5.2, walk through the calibration procedure on a real altimeter with the candidate alongside. Most digital altimeters have a button sequence for setting altitude; demonstrate it on the candidate's own instrument. The hands-on demonstration builds the habit faster than the read-the-manual approach.

For the reliable-vs-unreliable points distinction, work through Exercise 3 (D5.2) with the candidate before they attempt it alone. The discipline is more nuanced than it looks; a candidate who skips the discipline introduces systematic errors that make the altimeter less useful, not more.

## Suggested timings

Treat D5.1 and D5.2 as a single 75-minute block.

| Activity | Time |
|---|---|
| Recap of D4; framing of altimeter as the second instrument | 5 min |
| D5.1 mechanism, pressure-to-altitude rule, GPS comparison | 15 min |
| D5.1 Exercises 1 to 4 (Exercise 1 may be set as homework if the candidate has not run a 24-hour stationary drill) | 20 min |
| D5.2 calibration procedure and reliable-points distinction | 15 min |
| D5.2 Exercises 1 to 4 | 15 min |
| Self-check and discussion | 5 min |
| **Total** | **75 min** |

If Exercise 1 of D5.1 is set as homework, the in-session total compresses to approximately 60 minutes; the candidate completes the 24-hour drill before the next session.

## Materials checklist

- The candidate's own barometric altimeter (wristwatch or handheld unit). Note the model and the procedure for setting altitude before the session if possible.
- A reference for the candidate's known home elevation (Google Maps, OS Locate, IGN Géoportail, or a local map).
- A 1:25,000 alpine map of the candidate's exam area, for the reliable-known-points exercise.
- Optionally a GPS altimeter (a smartphone with appropriate app is fine) for the comparison demonstration.
- A barometer or a reliable weather forecast source for cross-referencing pressure changes.
- Pencils, ruler, eraser.

## Delivery notes - D5.1

**Framing.** The altimeter is the second instrument. Where the compass tells direction, the altimeter tells height. Both are needed for the alpine-specific aspect-of-slope relocation in D7.2.

**The mechanism.** Walk through it conceptually rather than in detail. The candidate does not need to understand the aneroid capsule mechanics; they need to understand that pressure decreases with altitude, the altimeter measures pressure, and the conversion assumes stable weather.

**The 1 hPa = 8 m rule.** Drill this rule. It comes back in D5.3 (calculating implied pressure changes from drift) and in D6 (barometer). The number is approximate but it is the working figure for IMS / EMS-level work.

**GPS altimeters.** Spend a few minutes on the comparison. The trade-offs (accuracy, signal dependency, battery) are the IMS / EMS-relevant points. The recommendation (barometric primary, GPS backup) should be stated explicitly so the candidate does not default to GPS as the obvious modern choice.

**Worked example.** The 24-hour stationary drift example is the lesson. Walk through it with the candidate, calculating the implied pressure change. The point is to make the candidate feel that the altimeter is doing its job correctly even when the reading changes.

**Exercise 1.** The 24-hour stationary drill. Best done as homework over a real day; the data is more meaningful than any in-session demonstration. If the candidate has not done it before the session, set it as homework and mark Exercise 1 at the next session.

**Exercise 2.** The pressure-change calculation. Mark the arithmetic and the direction. The most common error is reversing the direction (altimeter rising with rising pressure rather than falling pressure). Drill the mnemonic.

**Exercise 3.** The barometric-vs-GPS distinction. Push for both halves: the difference and the IMS / EMS-recommended primary tool.

**Exercise 4.** The when-altimeter-preferred scenario. Push for a specific situation; vague answers indicate the candidate has not internalised the trade-offs.

## Delivery notes - D5.2

**Framing.** Calibration is the discipline that keeps the altimeter useful. Without recalibration, the altimeter is correct at the morning calibration point and increasingly wrong everywhere else.

**The setting procedure.** Demonstrate on the candidate's own altimeter. Most candidates need help with the button sequence the first time; once shown, it becomes automatic.

**Reliable known points.** This is the page's most important concept. A reliable point has a precisely known altitude and the candidate is physically at that point. Approximate altitudes and unmarked junctions are not reliable.

**The recalibration cadence.** Walk through the worked example tour: lift station, col, summit, hut, lift station. Five calibrations through the day, each 15 seconds. The cumulative time cost is small; the cumulative benefit is large.

**The recalibration as a weather observation.** Coach this point explicitly. A candidate who recalibrates and notices the discrepancy as a number to correct, but not as information, has missed the dual use.

**Exercise 1.** The home calibration drill. Mark for the candidate having identified a likely cause for any discrepancy (drift, calibration error, route uncertainty).

**Exercise 2.** The reliable-known-points listing for the candidate's own exam-area map. Mark for five precisely-known-altitude points where the candidate would physically stand at the published altitude.

**Exercise 3.** The reliable-vs-unreliable judgement. The five examples on the page cover the typical patterns. Mark for both the right yes/no judgement and the right reason.

**Exercise 4.** The why-recalibrate-so-often explanation. Push for both the drift mechanism and the discrepancy-as-weather-observation use.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Reversing the pressure-altimeter direction.** The mnemonic ("weather going down, altimeter going up") fixes this. Drill until automatic.
- **Treating GPS as the obvious modern choice.** The IMS / EMS recommendation is barometric primary; coach explicitly.
- **Calibrating at unreliable points.** A candidate who calibrates at "approximately the col" introduces systematic errors. Drill the precisely-known-altitude requirement.
- **Skipping recalibrations.** The morning-only calibration habit. Coach the tour-long discipline.
- **Reading the recalibration discrepancy as a problem rather than as information.** The dual-use point. Coach explicitly.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - Set a stretch task: do the 24-hour stationary drill and then a 7-day day log (which is D5.3 Exercise 1 anyway) in parallel with the D5.1 / D5.2 in-session work. The combined data is more revealing than either alone.
  - On D5.2, ask the candidate to plan a tour with deliberate calibration points placed at the geometrically optimal moments (for example, before a long altimeter-primary traverse) rather than just at the natural rest points.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D5.1, work through the worked example calculation together before they attempt Exercise 2.
  - On D5.2, do the setting procedure together on the candidate's altimeter; have them repeat it twice with you watching.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D5.3 (drift management) when they can:

- explain how a barometric altimeter works at the conceptual level,
- apply the 1 hPa = 8 m rule of thumb in both directions,
- distinguish barometric from GPS altimeters and articulate the IMS / EMS recommendation,
- set the altimeter to a known elevation on their own instrument,
- identify reliable known points for calibration on a 1:25,000 alpine map,
- and understand the recalibration discrepancy as both a correction and a weather observation.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. D5.3 builds on the dual-use framing established here; without it the drift signal becomes noise.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The 1 hPa = 8 m rule of thumb is consistent with standard atmospheric physics for the lower troposphere (the IMS / EMS-relevant altitude range). At very high altitudes the relationship changes; for a candidate working at 1,000 to 3,500 m the figure is reliable.

The barometric-primary, GPS-backup recommendation is consistent with the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF and with the broader navigation literature. Specific GPS altimeters vary in accuracy; the workbook's 10-to-20 m figure is a working approximation that holds for typical handheld GPS units in alpine terrain.

The reliable-known-points discipline is workbook-specific framing of standard navigation practice. Mountain Training UK and Mountaineering Scotland materials describe similar habits without using the exact "reliable known point" terminology.
