## Purpose of these sections

D2 is the foundational technique block of Level 3. It teaches the candidate to take a bearing from the map (D2.1), apply magnetic variation to convert it to a magnetic bearing usable in the field (D2.2), and walk on it accurately (D2.3). The three pages form a single integrated technique: take, convert, walk.

After D2 the candidate has the bearing-and-map skill that every subsequent Level 3 technique builds on. D3 (bearings on the ground), D4 (practical accuracy), D7 (combined techniques), D8 (poor visibility) and D9 (route cards) all assume the D2 skill is fluent.

## Coaching priorities

1. The four-step bearing procedure as a memorised, automatic drill.
2. The cardinal cross-check as the safety net against the 180-degree error.
3. The 60-seconds-per-bearing speed target.
4. The look-up habit for magnetic variation: never bake in a fixed figure; always check current variation for the area and date.
5. The grid-to-magnetic conversion applied automatically (with the candidate's chosen mnemonic).
6. The intermediate-aiming-point technique for walking on a bearing.
7. The 15-m-per-100-m drift target.

## Teaching approach

Run the D2 triplet as a single 90-minute block. The pages are tightly coupled and break-up confuses the integrated technique.

For D2.1, walk through the four-step procedure on the board with a real compass and a 1:25,000 extract. Demonstrate the wrong-way-round error deliberately (place the baseplate with the direction-of-travel arrow pointing from destination back to start) and read the resulting bearing; the 180-degree flip lands the discipline of always checking the arrow direction.

For D2.2, the look-up habit is the most important takeaway. Walk through using the BGS WMM, NOAA / NCEI, or swisstopo online calculator on a real device; show the candidate exactly which fields to enter and how to read the result. The candidate who has actually done the look-up once is much more likely to do it again than a candidate who has only read the procedure.

For D2.3, the practical drill is essential. The walking technique cannot be taught at a desk; the drill in any open space is what builds the body memory. If the session is desk-only, set the practical drill as homework before D3.

## Suggested timings

Treat D2.1 / D2.2 / D2.3 as a single 90-minute block.

| Activity | Time |
|---|---|
| Recap of D1; framing of the integrated technique | 5 min |
| D2.1 four-step procedure walk-through | 10 min |
| D2.1 Exercises 1 to 4 | 25 min |
| D2.2 three norths and variation look-up demonstration | 10 min |
| D2.2 Exercises 1 to 4 | 15 min |
| D2.3 walking technique walk-through | 5 min |
| D2.3 Exercises 1 to 5 (drill set as homework if no outdoor space) | 15 min |
| Self-check and final discussion | 5 min |
| **Total** | **90 min** |

If the session is desk-only and the D2.3 drill is set as homework, the total compresses to 75 minutes; the D2.3 drill should still be done before D3.

## Materials checklist

- Two baseplate compasses (trainer and candidate).
- The Section D2.1 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval, with five pre-drawn legs between named features. The standard configuration is documented in the answer key.
- A device with internet access for the variation look-up demonstration (laptop, tablet, or phone).
- The BGS WMM, NOAA / NCEI, or swisstopo declination calculator URL bookmarked.
- For the D2.3 practical drill: any open space (a park, a beach, a flat field) with at least 100 m of clear ground and a partner to measure drift. If indoor-only, the drill is homework.
- Pencils, ruler, eraser, plain paper.

## Delivery notes - D2.1

**Framing.** The four-step procedure is the IMS / EMS-relevant bearing skill. Drill it until it is automatic. The candidate who has to think about which step comes next under route-card preparation pressure has not internalised it.

**The four-step procedure.** Walk through it slowly the first time, then progressively faster. The trainer should be able to demonstrate a bearing in under 30 seconds; the candidate's target is 60 seconds.

**Direction-of-travel arrow direction.** This is the single most consequential step. Demonstrate the 180-degree error deliberately and read the resulting bearing. Have the candidate check, in person, that the wrong-way arrow produces a wrong bearing. The visceral demonstration sticks better than verbal warning.

**Worked example.** Use the page's worked example or substitute one from the candidate's exam-area map. Either way, walk through the four steps explicitly and have the candidate check each one alongside.

**Exercise 1.** Five bearings under no time pressure. Mark each within 2 degrees. A candidate consistently more than 5 degrees off has slipped one of the four steps; identify which by watching them take a bearing in person.

**Exercise 2.** Cardinal cross-check. The discipline that catches the 180-degree error. Insist on the cardinal call for every bearing.

**Exercise 3.** The diagnostic exercise. Push for the diagnosis (180-degree flip), not just the corrected number. A candidate who notices the discrepancy but cannot diagnose has not yet seen the procedure clearly.

**Exercise 4.** The speed drill. Watch the candidate work and time them with a stopwatch. The first speed-drill bearing usually takes 90 seconds; by the third, 60 seconds is achievable. Hold the standard.

## Delivery notes - D2.2

**Framing.** The aspect work in C4.2 used grid north as the practical reference; the eight-direction resolution made variation negligible. Walking on a bearing is different. Variation matters now.

**The three norths.** Brief recap. Most candidates already understand from C4.2 that the three norths are different things; the new content here is that the difference between magnetic and grid is the variation, and it is what gets converted.

**Look-up habit.** This is the most important section of the page. Demonstrate the BGS WMM, NOAA / NCEI, or swisstopo calculator on a real device. Have the candidate enter the coordinates and date themselves. Confirm the variation figure together. The hands-on look-up makes the habit much more likely to stick than the read-it-and-do-it-later instruction.

**Conversion rule.** Hold the candidate's chosen mnemonic. Do not introduce competing mnemonics in the same session; pick one and drill it. The two given on the page ("grid to mag, add" or "the magnetic needle leans towards the variation") both work; let the candidate pick.

**Worked example.** Use a +2 degrees east variation as the page does, regardless of the candidate's actual look-up, so the worked example matches the page. The candidate's own conversion in Exercise 2 will use their look-up figure.

**Exercise 1.** The look-up exercise. Insist that the candidate uses an official source and a current date. A figure copied from a five-year-old map margin without checking is not acceptable.

**Exercise 2.** The conversion exercise. Mark the arithmetic. A candidate who subtracts when they should add (or vice versa) has the rule wrong; drill the chosen mnemonic until the slip is fixed.

**Exercise 3.** The reflection exercise. The candidate should land on the order-of-magnitude figure (40 to 60 m for a 2-degree variation over 1,500 m). The point is to make the variation feel consequential, not negligible.

**Exercise 4.** The international-variation table. The point is that a candidate using the workbook outside the European Alps must look up the local variation. A candidate who applies +2 degrees east in the Pacific Northwest is more than 10 degrees wrong on every bearing.

## Delivery notes - D2.3

**Framing.** The walking technique cannot be taught at a desk. The practical drill is essential.

**Holding the bearing.** Demonstrate the body-rotation technique in person. Have the candidate try alongside. Watch for the tilted compass; coach the flat-and-square-to-horizontal grip.

**Picking the aiming point.** Discuss what makes a good aiming point. Distinctive, stable, at maximum visible distance. In poor visibility a partner sent forward is the standard substitute (D8.2 covers the pole-and-cord variation).

**Common drift causes.** The three causes (tilted compass, body off-axis, leaning into slope) cover most candidate drift. Run the diagnosis in Exercise 3 with the candidate alongside, watching their grip and body position.

**Worked example.** Use the page's example or substitute one from the candidate's recent practice. Either way, the point is the integrated sequence: bearing on bezel, body rotated, aiming point picked, walked to, re-take.

**Exercise 1.** The practical drill. The 15-m-per-100-m drift target is the IMS / EMS standard. A candidate inside the target on the first attempt is at working level. A candidate outside it should run Exercise 3 to diagnose and re-attempt.

**Exercise 2.** The deliberate-offset drill. The point is to see that small bearing errors produce predictable, calculable drift. The 5-degree offset over 100 paces should produce approximately 8.7 m of drift (predicted by trigonometry).

**Exercise 3.** The diagnostic. Watch the candidate during the drill; the cause of drift is usually visible to the trainer even when not to the candidate.

**Exercise 4.** The explanatory exercise. Push for the body-tracking-target mechanism, not just "looking at the compass is awkward".

**Exercise 5.** The backup-strategies exercise. Both must be named: partner sent forward, and tracks in snow.

## Common learner difficulties

- **180-degree error in D2.1.** The most consequential bearing error. Drill the direction-of-travel-arrow check at every bearing.
- **Forgetting variation in D2.2.** The candidate has done the conversion on paper but the bezel is set to the grid bearing in the field. Quick check before walking: ask "what bearing is on the bezel?"
- **Subtraction-instead-of-addition (or vice versa) on variation.** The mnemonic slip. Drill the chosen mnemonic.
- **Walking with eyes on the compass in D2.3.** Most common D2.3 error. Coach the intermediate-aiming-point technique explicitly every time it slips.
- **Leaning into a slope on snow.** Most common drift cause on real terrain. Flag for the snow drills in D7 and D8.
- **Skipping the cardinal cross-check.** The safety net against the 180-degree error. Once or twice is forgivable; routinely is not.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - Set a stretch target of 30 seconds per bearing on D2.1, with no loss of accuracy.
  - On D2.3, run the deliberate-offset drill at 10 degrees and have the candidate predict the drift before measuring. The match between prediction and measurement is the test of whether the candidate has internalised the angular-drift relationship.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D2.1, walk through the first two bearings with the candidate alongside before they attempt the remaining three alone.
  - On D2.2, complete the look-up together; have the candidate write down the URL and the steps so they can repeat at home.
  - On D2.3, run the drill with the candidate alongside and call out the body-rotation step explicitly.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D3 (bearings on the ground) when they can:

- take a grid bearing from a 1:25,000 map within 2 degrees in under 60 seconds,
- look up current magnetic variation using BGS WMM, NOAA / NCEI, or swisstopo,
- convert grid to magnetic automatically using their chosen mnemonic,
- walk on a magnetic bearing with less than 15 m of drift per 100 m of travel,
- and recognise and correct the three common causes of drift.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. D3 introduces back bearings and resection, which assume D2 fluency.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The four-step bearing procedure is consistent with BASI EMS, Mountain Training UK, Mountaineering Scotland, and the broader navigation literature. The 2-degrees-in-60-seconds speed standard and the 15-m-per-100-m drift standard are both drawn from the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF and are consistent with Mountain Leader-level expectations.

The look-up-the-variation habit (rather than baking in a fixed figure) is a workbook-specific decision driven by the generic-reusability convention. A workbook that bakes in a Courchevel-area variation fails for a candidate at any other venue.

The +2 degrees east used in the worked example is illustrative, drawn from the typical Western Alps figure. Variation drifts over time and varies by location; the workbook explicitly asks the candidate to look up current value rather than use the +2 figure operationally.
