## Purpose of these sections

D6 covers the barometer, often the same physical instrument as the altimeter from D5. D6.1 covers reading and classifying the barometric trend. D6.2 covers the IMS Nav Programme decision framework that converts the trend into route-relevant action.

D6 is the formal weather-instrument application of the altimeter-as-weather-signal use established in D5.3. Where D5.3 noticed pressure drift through altimeter behaviour, D6 reads pressure directly and applies pre-agreed criteria.

## Coaching priorities

1. The five trend categories (rising, steady, slow fall, moderate fall, rapid fall) with their specific rate thresholds.
2. The trend (rate of change) as the diagnostic, not the absolute pressure value.
3. The IMS Nav Programme decision framework as structured input to the candidate's decision.
4. The pre-agreed criteria habit as the IMS / EMS-relevant discipline.
5. The recognition that pre-agreed criteria matter most exactly when the candidate does not want to abort.

## Teaching approach

Run D6.1 and D6.2 as a single 60-minute block. The pages are short on technique and heavy on judgement; the in-session work is mostly walking through the framework and the criteria-writing habit.

The three-day pressure log in D6.1 Exercise 1 must be set as homework if not already running. Without the data, the trend-reading exercises in D6.1 are abstract; with the data, they are personal.

For D6.2, walk through the worked example on the board. The criteria-writing exercise (Exercise 2) is the page's most important; have the candidate draft criteria for a real tour from their exam-area map, then critique each criterion against the three-component standard (specific trigger, specific action, named decision point).

## Delivery notes - D6.1

**Framing.** The barometer is the formal weather-instrument application. The altimeter has been doing this work since D5.3; D6 makes it explicit and adds the framework.

**Pressure units.** hPa and mb are interchangeable. Most modern instruments use hPa. The 1013.25 hPa standard sea-level reference is the calibration baseline; real readings vary around it.

**The five trend categories.** Drill the rate thresholds. The most common confusion is between moderate (0.5 to 1 hPa/hr) and rapid (over 1 hPa/hr); both feel "fast" but the response differs.

**Reading cadence.** Hourly during the tour at major waypoints is the standard. More often if the trend is changing; less often if the trend is steady.

**Worked example.** Walk through it on the board. The 2 hPa per 3-hour pattern is the moderate-fall band; the IMS action would be "consider early descent". Have the candidate identify the band and the action before you confirm.

**Exercise 1.** The three-day log. Best as homework. Insist on the drill; abstract trend-reading is much weaker than personal trend-reading.

**Exercise 2.** The plot. A simple line graph; coach for clear axes and labels.

**Exercise 3.** The trend classification. Mark for the rate calculation and the band assignment. The most common error is misclassifying moderate as rapid (or vice versa).

**Exercise 4.** The 12-hour prediction. Push for specificity (a named expected weather, a time element, possibly an intensity).

**Exercise 5.** The forecast cross-check. Coach for the both-have-their-uses framing. The barometer leads for short-term events; the forecast leads for longer-term events.

## Delivery notes - D6.2

**Framing.** A trend without a decision is just a number. The framework converts trends into actions; the pre-agreed criteria habit makes the decisions objective.

**The framework table.** Walk through it. The IMS actions are recommendations, not rules; the candidate's judgement still applies. But the framework provides the structured input that the candidate's judgement works with.

**Pre-agreed criteria.** The three-component standard (trigger, action, decision point) is the discipline. Walk through the worked example with the candidate; then have them draft their own.

**The "want to continue" pressure.** The IMS / EMS-relevant point. Pre-agreed criteria exist precisely to override the in-the-moment pressures (group expectations, sunk effort, weather optimism). Coach this explicitly.

**Exercise 1.** Apply the framework. Mark for the right rate calculation and the right band classification. The arithmetic is more important than memorising the actions; if the band is right, the action follows.

**Exercise 2.** Write three criteria. The page's most important exercise. Mark each criterion against the three-component standard. Coach for specificity at every weak criterion; vague criteria are worse than no criteria because they create false confidence.

**Exercise 3.** Tour duration and the framework. Push for the engagement with the duration-and-commitment trade-off, not for a specific framework adjustment.

**Exercise 4.** Decision criteria on the route card. Mark for the full route-card-style entry; coach the candidate to think of the criteria as part of the route card rather than as a separate document.

**Exercise 5.** The "want to continue" reflection. The IMS / EMS-relevant moment. Push for the explicit recognition of the pressures (group, sunk effort, optimism, summit attractiveness).

## Common learner difficulties

- **Reading absolute pressure rather than the trend.** A candidate who notes "the pressure is 1010 hPa" without context has missed the trend-reading point. Coach the rate-of-change framing.
- **Misclassifying the rate.** Moderate and rapid are easy to confuse; drill the thresholds.
- **Vague criteria.** "If the weather looks bad" is not a criterion. Drill the three-component standard.
- **Writing criteria that are too easy to skip.** Pre-agreed criteria should be non-negotiable; if the candidate writes "consider doing X if Y", they are not committing to the action. Coach for the harder commitment.
- **Refusing to abort when the criterion says to.** The most consequential failure mode. The criterion exists precisely to override the pressure to continue; if the candidate skips it, the criterion was useless.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - Set a stretch task: write the full set of criteria for a tour, including criteria for weather, group fitness, time of day, and snow conditions. The integrated criteria set is what the IMS / EMS assessor sees on a real route card.
  - On D6.1 Exercise 5, ask the candidate to predict ahead of the next 12-hour weather and verify against the actual outcome. The prediction-and-verification cycle builds the trend-reading instinct faster than passive observation.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D6.2 Exercise 2, draft one criterion together; have them draft the other two alone with the first as a model.
  - On Exercise 4, provide a route-card template with the fields pre-labelled; the candidate fills in the entries without having to design the layout.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D7 (combined techniques) when they can:

- record barometric pressure systematically over time,
- classify a trend into one of the five IMS Nav Programme categories using the rate,
- predict the next 12 hours of weather from the latest trend,
- apply the decision framework to any classified trend,
- write a pre-agreed criterion with a specific trigger, action, and decision point,
- and articulate why pre-agreed criteria matter most when the candidate does not want to abort.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. The combined techniques in D7 assume the judgement framework from D6 is in place.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The five-band trend classification and the decision framework are drawn directly from the IMS Nav Training Programme Quick Reference Card 4. The specific rate thresholds (0.5 hPa/hr, 1 hPa/hr) are working figures consistent with general alpine practice; published Mountain Training UK and Mountaineering Scotland materials use similar but not identical thresholds.

The pre-agreed criteria habit is workbook-specific framing of standard route-card practice. The three-component standard (trigger, action, decision point) is workbook-specific; alternative formulations exist but the workbook holds this one for consistency.

The "want to continue" pressure recognition is workbook-specific framing of well-documented decision-making research in expedition contexts. Mark Twight, Ed Viesturs, and others have written on the same theme; the workbook's compressed treatment captures the essential insight without the broader literature.
