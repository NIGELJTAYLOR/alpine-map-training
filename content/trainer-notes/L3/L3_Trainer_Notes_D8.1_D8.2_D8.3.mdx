## Purpose of these sections

D8 covers poor-visibility navigation: the alpine reality the IMS / EMS candidate must be ready to manage. D8.1 covers the IMS Nav Programme whiteout sequence (the standard procedure when visibility drops mid-tour). D8.2 covers the pole-and-cord technique for extreme low visibility with terrain hazards. D8.3 covers Mountaineering Scotland's calm systematic relocation procedure for when the candidate becomes uncertain of position.

D8 applies the instrument toolkit from D1 to D7 to the specific challenge of poor visibility. After D8 the candidate has the toolkit complete; D9 (route cards) provides the integrative artefact that makes the toolkit work in the field.

The scope distinction is critical and runs through all three pages: the BASI EMS / IMS scope explicitly excludes "setting out with the intention of leading in poor visibility". Managing poor visibility encountered en route is in scope; deliberately setting out in it is not.

## Coaching priorities

1. The IMS Nav Programme whiteout sequence as the standard procedure.
2. The pre-prepared route card as the foundation of the sequence.
3. The pole-and-cord technique as conditional: visibility low, hazard present, need to move.
4. The systematic relocation procedure as the response to uncertainty (rather than the avoidance of all uncertainty).
5. The leadership communication patterns ("temporarily unsure", not "lost").
6. The in-scope / out-of-scope distinction made explicit.

## Teaching approach

Run the D8 triplet as a single 90-minute block. The pages are tightly coupled: D8.1 establishes the standard sequence, D8.2 covers the extreme-conditions extension, D8.3 covers the recovery procedure. The candidate should see all three together to understand the full poor-visibility response.

For D8.1, walk through the worked example on the candidate's exam-area map. The key teaching point is that the pre-prepared route card is the foundation; without it, the sequence has nothing to refer to.

For D8.2, demonstrate the pole-and-cord setup on snow if possible. If not possible, the trainer should have run the technique themselves recently and should be able to describe it from experience. The Glenmore Lodge YouTube material (linked from the IMS Nav Programme PDF) is a good substitute when snow is unavailable.

For D8.3, walk through the eight-step procedure on the board with a worked scenario. The leadership language patterns are usually new to candidates; spend time on Exercise 2 with the candidate.

## Delivery notes - D8.1

**Framing.** Open with the in-scope / out-of-scope distinction. The IMS / EMS pathway tests management of conditions encountered en route, not deliberate setting-out in poor conditions.

**The whiteout sequence.** Drill the six steps in order. The most-skipped step under stress is step 1 (Stop); the candidate's instinct is to keep walking. Coach the explicit stop.

**The pre-prepared route card.** This is the page's most important concept. Without a route card, the whiteout sequence has nothing reliable to refer to. The candidate's bearings calculated under stress in poor visibility are likely wrong; the bearings calculated calmly at the desk are reliable.

**Worked example.** Walk through the cloud-down-at-2pm scenario on the board. Have the candidate identify each step of the sequence as you describe the candidate's actions.

**Exercise 1.** The walk-through. Mark for application of all six steps in order, with specific references to the route card at steps 4 and 5.

**Exercise 2.** The scope distinction. Push for the explicit out-of-scope vs in-scope examples.

**Exercise 3.** Why pre-prepared. Push for both points: bearings calculated in good conditions are reliable; bearings calculated under stress are not.

**Exercise 4.** The most-skipped step. The candidate's specific identification matters; mark for the consequence reasoning.

## Delivery notes - D8.2

**Framing.** The pole-and-cord is the IMS-specific technique. The IMS Nav Training Programme treats it as one to know but rarely deliberately use. The conditional logic (visibility low, hazard present, need to move) is what makes the technique appropriate.

**The technique.** Demonstrate the setup with a real ski pole and cord if possible. If not possible, the candidate may need to see the Glenmore Lodge YouTube reference.

**The procedure.** Six steps: tie cord to pole, scout walks forward paying out cord, scout stops at cord end, lead navigator joins scout, picks up cord, repeat. Drill the sequence.

**The cord's three functions.** Direction confirmation, physical extension, drop detection. All three matter; the cord is not just a leash for the scout.

**Conditional use.** The three conditions all need to hold for the technique to be appropriate. If any does not hold, simpler techniques are preferable. Drill the conditional logic in Exercise 2.

**Exercise 1.** When the technique adds value. Mark for a scenario meeting all three conditions.

**Exercise 2.** When simpler techniques are preferable. The four scenarios cover the typical patterns. Mark for the right judgement on each.

**Exercise 3.** The practical drill. If snow is available, run the drill in the session. If not, set as homework or rely on the trainer's demonstration plus the YouTube reference.

**Exercise 4.** The "know it even if you never use it" framing. Push for the conditional answer to the assessor's "how would you handle X?" question.

## Delivery notes - D8.3

**Framing.** Becoming temporarily unsure of position is normal; the IMS / EMS skill is responding to it calmly and systematically.

**The eight-step procedure.** Drill it in order. Step 1 (Stop) is the same as in D8.1; the rest of the procedure is the relocation work, drawing on D7.2 (aspect-of-slope) and D5 (altimeter calibration).

**The leadership dimension.** Spend time on the language patterns. "Temporarily unsure" vs "lost" is not just style; it is a difference in mental framing that affects the candidate's response and the group's confidence.

**Worked example.** Walk through the col-not-visible-at-expected-time scenario on the board. The candidate should see all eight steps applied with specific reference to the candidate's instruments and the candidate's leadership communication.

**Exercise 1.** The walk-through. Mark for all eight steps applied in order, with specific references at each step.

**Exercise 2.** The leadership communication. The five examples cover the typical patterns. Mark for the right judgement and for better alternatives where the example is unacceptable.

**Exercise 3.** Why "temporarily unsure" works. Push for both the candidate's-own-thinking point and the group-confidence point.

**Exercise 4.** The most common error. The candidate's specific identification matters; mark for the consequence reasoning.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Continuing to walk during a visibility drop.** The most common D8.1 error. Coach the explicit Stop instruction.
- **Trying to take fresh bearings in poor visibility.** A candidate who does not trust the route card will calculate fresh bearings in the field; these are usually wrong. Coach the route-card-as-foundation principle.
- **Over-applying the pole-and-cord technique.** A candidate who learns the technique well may use it for any poor-visibility leg. Coach the conditional use.
- **Treating uncertainty as failure.** A candidate who never admits uncertainty continues on a wrong bearing for too long. Coach the "temporarily unsure is normal" framing.
- **Vague leadership communication.** "I think we might be lost" or "let me check the map while we walk" both signal no plan. Coach the precise language patterns.
- **Skipping the systematic relocation procedure** in favour of GPS coordinates. The GPS works in the moment but does not build the skill. Coach the map-and-compass procedure with GPS as backup.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - On D8.1, set a stretch task: design a tour with multiple decision points where the whiteout sequence would apply, and write the route card so that each decision point has the necessary inputs ready.
  - On D8.3, run a timed drill of the systematic relocation procedure on a candidate-described scenario; the IMS / EMS working target is under five minutes for the full eight steps.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - On D8.1, walk through the worked example step by step with the candidate alongside before they attempt Exercise 1.
  - On D8.2, the YouTube reference is essential; make sure the candidate has seen it before attempting the practical drill.
  - On D8.3, work through the worked example with the candidate, then have them attempt Exercise 1 on a different scenario.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for D9 (route cards) when they can:

- apply the IMS Nav Programme whiteout sequence step by step,
- distinguish in-scope from out-of-scope poor-visibility planning,
- describe the pole-and-cord setup and recognise when it is appropriate,
- apply the eight-step systematic relocation procedure on a worked scenario,
- communicate "temporarily unsure of position" calmly and clearly,
- and understand that the route card is the foundation of poor-visibility navigation.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. D9 (route cards) is the integrative artefact; the candidate must see route cards as the foundation of D8 before D9 will land properly.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The IMS Nav Programme whiteout sequence is drawn directly from the IMS Nav Training Programme PDF. The six-step structure is consistent with general alpine navigation practice and with Mountain Training UK guidance.

The pole-and-cord technique is named in the IMS Nav Programme PDF as an IMS-specific technique. As of the Batch 10 verification sweep, the technique was not directly verified in published Mountain Training UK or BASI EMS text; primary verification is still pending and is recorded as an open item in `Sources.md`. The Glenmore Lodge YouTube material referenced in the IMS Nav Programme PDF is the most accessible primary demonstration; trainers should review it before running the D8.2 session.

The systematic relocation procedure is verified at primary source on Mountaineering Scotland's "when navigation goes wrong" page. The eight-step structure used in D8.3 is consistent with the published procedure; the leadership-language framing is workbook-specific framing of standard navigation pedagogy.

The "temporarily unsure of position" vs "lost" distinction is workbook-specific framing of professional navigation language. Mountaineering Scotland's page uses similar language; the formulation here is one specific articulation.

The in-scope / out-of-scope framing is from the New Generation Ski School BASI Level 4 ISTD exam guide (referenced in `Sources.md`), which states explicitly that EMS tours "must not include... setting out with the intention of leading in poor visibility".

Sources for this trainer note are recorded in `00_Research_Inputs/Sources.md`. The pole-and-cord primary verification remains an open item; the technique is taught here based on the IMS Nav Programme PDF and the Glenmore Lodge YouTube reference.
