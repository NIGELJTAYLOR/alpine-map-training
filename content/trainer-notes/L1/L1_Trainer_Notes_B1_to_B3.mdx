## Purpose of this section
These trainer notes support the early part of Level 1 and should be used alongside the learner workbook pages B1.1 to B3.2. The teaching aim at this stage is not speed or exam performance; it is confidence, clarity and visual familiarity.

## Coaching priorities
At this stage, the trainer should focus on five things:

1. Helping the learner understand what a topographic map is for.
2. Reinforcing that maps are a bird’s-eye view of real terrain.
3. Building fast recognition of the main structural parts of a map.
4. Building early confidence with colours and symbols.
5. Encouraging verbal description of what the learner sees.

## Typical learner difficulties
Common issues at this stage include:

- confusing satellite imagery with topographic interpretation,
- seeing colours and symbols but not knowing what matters,
- forgetting to check the legend,
- struggling to turn a small map scene into a simple ground-level description,
- and feeling overwhelmed by too much information at once.

## Teaching approach
Use very small chunks of map at first. Ask the learner to describe one thing at a time:

- What colour do you notice first?
- What does that colour suggest?
- What symbol stands out next?
- What might this place look like on the ground?

Avoid jumping too quickly into complex mountain extracts. If needed, start with simple valley, river and settlement examples before moving into steeper terrain.

## Prompts for B1.1–B1.2
Useful coaching questions:

- What makes this different from a road map?
- What makes this different from a satellite image?
- If you were standing here, what is the first thing you think you would notice?
- Where is the low ground? Where might the high ground be?

If the learner struggles to describe the ground view, ask them to use plain language first rather than technical terms.

## Prompts for B2.1–B2.2
Useful coaching questions:

- Before you read anything in detail, which parts of the map should you always find first?
- Why is the scale important before measuring anything?
- Why is the legend important before trusting a symbol?
- Which map elements do you still search for too slowly?

Encourage the learner to develop a repeatable opening routine:

1. Check title.
2. Check scale.
3. Check legend.
4. Check north.
5. Check grid.

## Prompts for B3.1–B3.2
Useful coaching questions:

- What do the colours suggest before you look at the legend?
- Which symbols are most useful for navigation in this small area?
- Which features would help confirm your position?
- What does this tiny square seem to show overall?

The trainer should repeatedly remind the learner that colour gives a quick impression, but the legend confirms the exact meaning.

## Marking guidance
At this stage, mark for:

- correct recognition of structure,
- sensible first interpretations,
- use of the legend,
- and growing confidence in describing small map areas.

Do not over-penalise imprecise wording if the learner is correctly seeing the broad meaning.

## Readiness to move on
The learner is ready to progress deeper into Level 1 when they can:

- explain the purpose of a topographic map,
- describe the difference between bird’s-eye view and ground view,
- quickly find title, scale, legend, north arrow and grid,
- recall the broad meaning of the five main colours,
- and use the legend to interpret common symbols on a real map.
