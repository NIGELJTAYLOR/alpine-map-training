## Purpose of these sections

C2.2 and C2.3 are the second and third of the named-terrain-shape pages. They teach the learner to recognise valleys, gullies, ridges and spurs from contour patterns, distinguish broad and narrow forms in each pair, read direction of slope from contour values, and translate every shape into a side profile and a verbal "if I stood here" description.

These two pages are paired in the workbook deliberately. Valleys and ridges are direct inverses of each other on a contour map. Teaching them in succession is faster and more durable than teaching either alone.

C2.2 and C2.3 also continue the visualisation drill begun in C2.1: look at the contour pattern, picture the ground, describe in words, sketch in side profile. This is the cumulative exercise that addresses the core developmental need of any learner who struggles with 2D-to-3D translation.

## Coaching priorities

1. The V-direction rule. V points uphill = valley or gully; V points downhill = ridge or spur.
2. The contour-value check. Always confirm the V-direction call by reading the contour numbers along the feature.
3. The width-and-spacing distinction. Broad bend = valley or ridge; tight bend with close side-wall contours = gully or spur.
4. The aspect-of-each-side reading on ridges and spurs. Foundational for C4 (Aspect) and for avalanche-aware route choice at IMS / EMS.
5. The conditional use of linear features. Valley floors and ridge crests are good handrails in poor visibility; gullies are not, even though they are also linear.
6. The 2D-to-3D drill: sketch and verbalise in every exercise, not just the obvious ones.

## Teaching approach

Open the C2.2 / C2.3 block by drawing a single diagram on the board with two adjacent V shapes (one pointing uphill, one pointing downhill) and annotating the contour values on each. Get learners to call the names before you write them.

Walk through the worked examples on each page in turn, but always come back to the inverse-pairing diagram. The workbook itself reinforces this in C2.3 Exercise 5, but the visual pairing on the board makes the relationship stick faster.

For both pages, encourage the look-imagine-describe-sketch sequence introduced in C2.1:

- look at the contour pattern,
- close the eyes briefly,
- describe in their own words what the ground would look like,
- only then sketch.

Exercises 4 (the "if I stood here" descriptions) are the most important exercises on each page. They are also the exercises learners are most likely to skip or rush. Hold the standard.

## Suggested timings

Treat C2.2 and C2.3 as a single 90-minute block. If there is appetite for more practice, split into two 60-minute sessions on consecutive evenings and use the second to revisit the more difficult exercises from the first.

| Activity | Time |
|---|---|
| Recap of C2.1; framing of the inverse pairing | 5 min |
| C2.2 core idea, drainage direction, valley vs gully | 8 min |
| C2.2 worked example walk-through | 5 min |
| C2.2 Exercises 1 to 5 | 30 min |
| C2.3 core idea, ridge vs spur, aspect of each side | 8 min |
| C2.3 worked example walk-through | 5 min |
| C2.3 Exercises 1 to 5 | 25 min |
| Self-check and consolidation discussion | 5 min |
| **Total** | **90 min** |

If timing slips, the exercise to compress is C2.3 Exercise 5 (the inverse pairing), since it functions as a recap of both pages and can be set as homework.

## Materials checklist

- Section C2.2 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least one broad valley with a visible blue stream line, one tight gully with steep side-wall contours, and at least one feature that is visibly not a drainage line (a ridge or spur, for the deliberate ambiguity test).
- Section C2.3 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least one continuous ridge running across the sheet with multiple spot heights, one or more spurs projecting from a parent summit or ridge, and at least one closed-loop summit (Feature D in the standard configuration) which is deliberately neither a ridge nor a spur.
- Two map extracts can be the same sheet section if it contains all the required features. Otherwise use two adjacent print extracts.
- Pencils, including a soft pencil for sketching.
- An eraser.
- Plain paper for additional sketching practice.
- A whiteboard or flipchart for the inverse-pairing diagram.

## Delivery notes — C2.2

**Framing.** Open by reminding the learner that valleys and gullies are the drainage lines of the mountain. They are where snow, water and (sometimes) avalanche debris collect. Reading them well is route-selection, not just terrain identification.

**Core idea.** Hold the line that the V points uphill in a valley or gully. The contour-value check is the confirming step, not an alternative.

**Valley vs gully.** Some learners will treat the two as a binary. Stress that they are the same family of feature on different scales. The diagnostic is the width of the bend and the spacing of the side-wall contours.

**Worked example.** Walk through both halves of the worked example on the board. Draw the broad V and the tight V side by side and annotate the contour values for each. The visual contrast lands the distinction faster than either example alone.

**Exercise 1.** Watch for learners who name a feature "valley" because it has a stream symbol, without actually checking that the V points uphill. The contour pattern is the primary cue; the stream is the confirmation.

**Exercise 2.** Coach the habit of reading both the highest and lowest contour values, not just one. On a real route card, the IMS candidate writes both endpoints of a leg.

**Exercise 3.** The sketch is the big learning moment. Reassure learners who say they cannot draw; the exercise is about the translation from contour pattern to side profile, not artistic skill.

**Exercise 4.** Watch for descriptions that re-state the contour pattern instead of describing the imagined view. Coach back to "what would your eyes actually see standing on the floor?"

**Exercise 5.** This is the IMS / EMS-relevant exercise on the page. Hold the standard that learners must give a specific reason why a gully is not a useful descent handrail in poor visibility. The answer should not just be "because gullies are dangerous". The mechanism (narrow channel, no exit, possible cliff bands or terminal cone) is what the assessor expects.

## Delivery notes — C2.3

**Framing.** Open by setting the inverse pairing. The V points downhill on a ridge or spur. Everything else flows from this.

**Core idea.** Some learners will struggle to internalise V-direction without the contour-value check. Encourage both checks together rather than choosing one. On the IMS / EMS assessment, the candidate is expected to be fluent enough that they barely think about it, but on the workbook page the discipline of doing both checks builds the fluency.

**Ridge vs spur.** Spurs belong to parent features; ridges are the parents. This relational framing helps learners stop reading individual features in isolation and start reading the high-ground architecture of the map.

**Which side falls where.** This is the foundation of C4 (Aspect) and is one of the most assessable pieces of contour fluency. Spend time on it. The mental drill is: stand on the crest, face along it towards the low end, and the slope on each side falls in the obvious direction. Aspect is then read from the slope direction, not the crest direction.

**Worked example.** Draw the spur and the ridge side by side on the board, annotated with values. Highlight the crest line on the ridge example as a continuous line linking spot heights; this is what makes a ridge a "linear high-ground feature" rather than just a sequence of bumps.

**Exercise 1.** Watch for learners who call a closed-loop summit (Feature D in the standard configuration) a spur because it is high. The discipline is: spurs and ridges show V or U shapes; summits show closed loops.

**Exercise 2.** Coach the habit of giving aspects in compass terms (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW), not relative directions ("the left side"). Compass aspect is what shows up on a route card and is what the EMS assessor expects.

**Exercise 3.** The sketch should make the inverse pairing visual. If a spur sketch looks like a valley sketch flipped upside-down, that is correct. Make this point explicitly.

**Exercise 4.** This exercise drills the most common ridge-walking moment in real touring: standing on the crest, deciding which direction to descend. Push for compass-term aspects on each side and a stated descent direction.

**Exercise 5.** A short consolidation that ties C2.2 and C2.3 together. If learners struggle here, return to the board diagram with the two adjacent V shapes and the annotated contour values.

## Common learner difficulties

- **V-direction confusion under stress.** Learners who can call the rule confidently in the classroom may flip it when they meet an unfamiliar map. The contour-value check is the safety net. Drill it as a habit, not a back-up.
- **Ignoring the contour values when the V direction looks obvious.** This is the trap that catches candidates on awkward map extracts where the V direction is genuinely ambiguous. Hold the discipline.
- **Treating spurs and ridges as the same feature.** Acceptable as a starting point, but the relational distinction (spurs belong to a parent feature) must be in place before C3 (Cols, bowls, plateaux) and before any route-selection work.
- **Naming both flanks of a ridge with the same aspect.** A sign that the learner is reading the crest direction rather than the slope direction. Coach the "stand on the crest, face along it, look down each side" mental model.
- **Substituting written description for visualisation.** Some learners try to satisfy Exercise 4 with a long paragraph instead of an imagined view. The point of the exercise is the visualisation, not the prose. Insist on the look-imagine-describe-sketch sequence.
- **Sketch reluctance.** Persist gently, as in C2.1. The cumulative visualisation work in C2.1 to C2.4 is what builds the 2D-to-3D fluency.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - Ask them to identify all spurs that branch off a ridge on the C2.3 extract, and to give the aspect of each side of each one.
  - Ask them to mark on the C2.2 extract which gullies they would judge to be terrain traps under specific avalanche conditions, and explain why.
  - Set them the C2.4 matching exercise as a stretch task before the formal session.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - In C2.2 Exercise 3 and C2.3 Exercise 3, give them a pre-drawn baseline and ground line and ask them to add the feature profile only.
  - Walk them through C2.3 Exercise 2 verbally before they write, to anchor the compass-aspect habit.
  - Pre-annotate the contour values on the standard extracts so they can focus on V-direction reading without having to extract the numbers themselves.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for C2.4 (Matching exercise) when they can:

- look at any V or U shaped contour bend and call valley, gully, ridge, or spur within five seconds, with the contour-value check available as a back-up,
- distinguish broad valleys from tight gullies and ridges from spurs by spacing and length,
- give the aspect of each side of a crest in compass terms,
- produce a recognisable side profile sketch from a contour pattern,
- and articulate the inverse pairing between drainage features and high-ground features in their own words.

If any of these are still wobbly, recycle the relevant exercise on a new map extract before moving on. The fluency built here is what makes C2.4 a consolidation rather than a fresh learning task.

## Link to next section

C2.4 is the Section C2 matching exercise. It pairs eight contour snippets to eight terrain names: hill or summit, valley, gully, ridge, spur, plus the not-yet-taught col, bowl and cliff feature so the learner has a controlled first encounter with the C3 vocabulary in a low-stakes setting.

After C2.4, Section C3 introduces cols, bowls, plateaux and cliff bands. Cols in particular sit on ridges between two summits, so the ridge fluency built in C2.3 is the prerequisite. Flag this at the close of C2.3 so learners arrive at C3 with the right mental model.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The figures and rules in C2.2 and C2.3 are general topographic conventions and are not contentious. They are consistent with:

- Mountaineering Scotland's navigation guidance, which lists ridges, spurs, valley floors and water features as standard linear handrails for mountain navigation, and notes that contour features are usually identifiable on the ground even when covered in snow.
- BASI's published Mountain Safety Pathway and EMS materials, which require fluent identification of contour features on a 1:25,000 topographic map.
- The IGN TOP25 1:25,000 series, which is the standard topographic series for the French Alps and is the published working sheet for several BASI course venues (Courchevel uses IGN TOP25 Les Trois Vallées Modane 3534OT, for example). For other course venues, the equivalent national 1:25,000 series should be substituted (Swisstopo for Switzerland, Alpenvereinskarte for Austria, IGM for Italy).

The IMS / EMS-specific framing in the "Why this matters for IMS" footers links to:

- The route-identification competency tested at EMS assessment.
- The route-selection competency, particularly the use of ridges and spurs as ascent lines and the avoidance of gullies as descent handrails in poor visibility.
- The aspect-reading skill that is foundational for avalanche-aware terrain interpretation.

A note on terminology: BASI's current public materials use "European Mountain Safety" and "European Mountain Security" interchangeably across pages. Treat both terms as equivalent. The workbook uses "European Mountain Safety / EMS" throughout for consistency.

A note on the 30 degree threshold introduced in C1.2: the BASI / New Generation public materials describe 35 degrees as the "prime" slope angle for avalanche release, while 30 degrees is the lower bound of the slab-release range. The C1.2 rule of thumb (3 mm spacing on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m contours) targets the 30 degree threshold deliberately, because the IMS-relevant question is "could this slope slide?" rather than "is this the most likely angle to slide?". If a learner asks about the discrepancy, this is the answer.
