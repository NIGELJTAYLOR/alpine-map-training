## Purpose of this section

C2.4 is the consolidation page that closes Section C2. It is the first sustained fluency exercise in the workbook. The aim is not to teach new content but to build speed and accuracy on the contour-to-feature recognition skill that has been built across C2.1 (summits), C2.2 (valleys and gullies) and C2.3 (ridges and spurs).

C2.4 is also the diagnostic page that determines whether the learner is ready to start Section C3. The reflection exercise (Exercise 3) and the stretch task (Exercise 4) together give the trainer a clean read on whether the learner can hold the C2 vocabulary fluently and meet new shapes without losing what they have.

## Coaching priorities

1. Speed of recognition. Five seconds per snippet is the working target; under three minutes for all eight is the test.
2. Family before sub-type. Get the broad family (high ground, drainage) right first, then the sub-type.
3. Specific reflection. Vague reflection ("I need more practice") is not a useful diagnostic; specific reflection ("I keep flipping ridge and valley on snippet 6") is.
4. Honest engagement with the stretch task. The stretch task is not graded for correctness but is graded for honest attempt.
5. Preserve the foundation. If the learner is not yet fluent at C2.4, do not push on to C3. The cumulative effect of a strong C2 foundation makes C3 easier; a weak C2 foundation makes C3 a struggle.

## Teaching approach

Run C2.4 as a self-test under light time pressure rather than as a teaching session.

A workable session shape:

- Brief recap of the seven feature names (no new content).
- Hand out the snippet sheet face-down.
- Set a five-minute timer for Exercise 1, but allow longer if needed.
- Mark Exercise 1 immediately, with the learner present, so they can see which family or sub-type went wrong.
- Run Exercise 2 untimed, but mark it the same way.
- Use the gaps in their answers to drive the reflection in Exercise 3.
- Save Exercise 4 for the end, framed clearly as a preview, not a graded item.

The key shift from C2.1 to C2.3 is that in those pages the trainer was explaining and supporting; in C2.4 the trainer is observing. Resist the urge to teach. If the learner is struggling, the right response is to send them back to the page that taught the feature, not to re-teach in the moment.

## Suggested timings

| Activity | Time |
|---|---|
| Recap of Section C2 vocabulary | 5 min |
| Exercise 1 — match snippets to names (light time pressure) | 10 min |
| Marking and discussion of Exercise 1 | 5 min |
| Exercise 2 — match snippets to side-view sketches | 10 min |
| Marking and discussion of Exercise 2 | 5 min |
| Exercise 3 — reflection | 10 min |
| Exercise 4 — stretch task (cols and cliff bands preview) | 10 min |
| Final discussion and readiness check | 5 min |
| **Total** | **60 min** |

If the learner is clearly fluent and finishes Exercises 1 and 2 in under twenty minutes combined, use the remaining time to run a second snippet sheet for additional practice. The fluency target benefits from repetition with fresh examples.

## Materials checklist

- Standard C2.4 snippet sheet, containing eight contour snippets (numbered 1 to 8) and eight side-view sketches (lettered A to H), plus two C3-preview snippets (numbered 9 and 10).
- Each snippet should be approximately 4 cm by 4 cm, drawn at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour intervals.
- The eight snippets should cover all seven feature names from C2.1 to C2.3, with two summit sub-types and two ridge / spur examples to test sub-type discrimination. The standard configuration is documented in the answer key.
- Pencils, including a soft pencil for the stretch-task sketches.
- An eraser.
- A timer.
- The C2.1, C2.2 and C2.3 learner pages should be available for reference. If the learner needs to re-read a page, that is information about their fluency, not a failure.

## Delivery notes

**Exercise 1.** This is the heart of the page. Put the timer on the table where the learner can see it. The point is not to make them anxious; the point is to make them notice when they are slow. If a learner takes more than thirty seconds on a single snippet, that is a sign that the recognition is not yet automatic. Note which snippet and use it in the reflection.

**Exercise 2.** This is a different test. A learner can be fluent at naming features but slow at translating to a side view, or vice versa. Watch for which direction the learner finds harder. Both directions matter at IMS / EMS assessment.

**Exercise 3.** The single most important exercise on the page. A weak answer should be coached towards specificity in real time. Useful follow-up questions:

- Which feature do you find hardest to call?
- What page taught that feature?
- Which exercise on that page felt least solid?
- When are you going to redo it?

Record the learner's answer in the trainer manual's progress log so it can be checked at the start of Section C3.

**Exercise 4.** Hold the line that this is a preview, not a test. Some learners will be uncomfortable with the lack of formal vocabulary; reassure them that the names are coming in C3 and that the sketch is what matters here. Watch for sketches that show no relationship between the two high points on snippet 9 (the col), and for sketches that show the cliff band on snippet 10 as merely steep ground rather than as ground too steep to walk on. These are the moments that prepare the learner for C3.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Slowness without errors.** A learner who gets every snippet right but takes ten minutes is not yet at the working pace required for the IMS / EMS assessment. Recycle Exercise 1 with a fresh snippet sheet rather than moving on.
- **Family errors under time pressure.** A learner who calls a spur a valley because they were rushed has not internalised the V-direction check. The fix is the C2.3 inverse-pairing diagram, not more matching exercises.
- **Sub-type confusion within the summit family.** Sharp / rounded / plateau confusion usually traces to spacing-versus-shape inattention. The fix is C2.1 Exercise 3.
- **Sketch pairing without name confidence.** Some learners can pair sketches by family but cannot name the features. Treat name fluency as the prerequisite; the sketch pairing in Exercise 2 is the application.
- **Vague reflection.** The most common weakness on the page. Coach into specificity in real time, not afterwards.
- **Refusing the stretch task.** A learner who skips Exercise 4 because "I haven't been taught those yet" is not engaging with the workbook's pedagogy. The right response is gentle insistence: "draw what you see, not what you know".

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:** run the snippet sheet a second time with the trainer naming a feature aloud and the learner finding the matching snippet (the reverse direction of Exercise 1). Add a third feature: "name the aspect of the slope" for ridge and spur snippets, as a preview of C4.
- **For learners who need more support:** untime Exercise 1 entirely. Walk through the first three snippets together, then leave them to do snippets 4 to 8 alone. Re-mark in the same session.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for Section C3 (advanced terrain shapes) when, on Exercise 1:

- they make no family-level errors on any snippet,
- they make no more than two sub-type errors across the eight snippets,
- they complete the exercise in under five minutes,
- they pair at least six of the eight snippets correctly to side-view sketches in Exercise 2,
- they produce a specific, actionable reflection in Exercise 3,
- and they engage honestly with the stretch task in Exercise 4 even though it is unfamiliar.

If any of the above is missing, recycle the relevant earlier exercise and re-run C2.4 in a later session. The Section C3 features (cols, bowls, plateaux, cliff bands) all build on the C2 vocabulary, so a shaky C2 foundation will quickly become a wider problem.

## Link to next section

Section C3 introduces cols and saddles (C3.1), bowls / corries / basins (C3.2), and plateaux and cliff bands (C3.3). The two stretch-task snippets in Exercise 4 are deliberate previews of C3.1 (the col) and C3.3 (the cliff band). When the learner reaches those formal teaching pages, return briefly to their Exercise 4 sketches and compare what they drew with what they now know. The before-and-after comparison is itself a teaching moment.

After C3, Section C4 introduces aspect formally. The aspect-of-each-side reading already practised in C2.3 Exercise 2 is the bridge.

## Reference notes for the trainer

C2.4 is a consolidation page and does not introduce any new claims requiring source verification. The seven feature names and their contour-pattern conventions are taken directly from C2.1, C2.2 and C2.3, where the underlying sources are recorded in `00_Research_Inputs/Sources.md`.

The decision to include two C3-preview snippets (col and cliff band) in the stretch task is a deliberate pedagogy choice: it gives the learner a controlled first encounter with the C3 vocabulary in a low-stakes setting. The alternative (leaving C3 entirely for Section C3) was considered and rejected because the leap from a four-feature consolidation to a fresh four-feature section is steeper than it needs to be.

The choice of seven names rather than the four named in the original outline (hills, valleys, ridges, spurs) reflects the sub-type vocabulary actually taught in C2.1 (sharp peak, rounded summit, plateau-like high) and C2.2 (valley vs gully). Treating those sub-types as separate names in the matching exercise is consistent with the IMS / EMS expectation that candidates discriminate between feature sub-types fluently, not just at the family level.
