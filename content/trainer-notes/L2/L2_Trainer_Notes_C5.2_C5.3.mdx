## Purpose of these sections

C5.2 (Map to Real Landscape Matching) and C5.3 ("If I Stood Here" Sustained Practice) are the freer half of Section C5. They build on the technical anchor of C5.1 (cross-sections) and complete the visualisation block.

The progression across the three pages is deliberate:

- **C5.1** builds visualisation as a procedural technique (cross-section construction).
- **C5.2** calibrates visualisation against external imagery (real photographs).
- **C5.3** removes external scaffolding and asks for sustained verbal description from the map alone.

By the end of C5.3 the learner should be able to look at any point on a 1:25,000 alpine map and produce a structured, accurate four-to-six-sentence description of what they would expect to see standing at that point. This is the working voice of an IMS / EMS candidate on the move.

## Coaching priorities

1. The calibration habit. Compare the mental picture with the real image; identify the systematic biases; correct them.
2. The cue articulation. A match is only as good as the named reason for it.
3. The four-part description structure: underfoot, slope and aspect, near features, far features. Used every time.
4. Eight-direction aspect terms in the description, never four.
5. The poor-visibility adaptation: distinguishing what the learner can see from what they infer from the map.
6. Self-criticism as the diagnostic. A learner who can read their own description and identify its weakness has internalised the standard.

## Teaching approach

Run C5.2 and C5.3 as a single 90-minute block, with a clear shift in the middle from "calibration with images" to "sustained description from the map alone".

For C5.2, open by laying out the photo-matching set on the table. Have the learner work through Exercise 1 alone first; do not prompt during the matching. Afterwards, sit with them through Exercises 2 to 4, where the conversation is the learning tool.

For C5.3, hand over the four-part structure as a non-negotiable. Some learners will resist as artificial; insist on the structure for the workbook. The point is not natural prose; the point is a structured voice that holds up under assessment pressure.

The standard P1 / P2 / P3 setup in C5.3 is designed to test three different feature types. Make sure your three points genuinely span the families (one slope, one summit or ridge, one valley or plateau, for example) so the learner has to adapt the structure rather than recycle the same description three times.

## Materials checklist

- The Section C5.2 photo-matching set:
  - six contour snippets (numbered 1 to 6) at 1:25,000 with 10 m contours, each approximately 5 cm by 5 cm,
  - six aerial or oblique photographs (lettered A to F) of the same areas,
  - a key (held by the trainer) showing the correct pairings,
  - and an aspect note for each photograph (which way the camera was pointing, for the oblique shots).
- The Section C5.3 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contours, with three points P1, P2 and P3 marked, each on a different family of feature.
- Pencils, ruler, eraser as standard.
- Plain paper for the C5.2 reverse-sketch exercise.
- The trainer's calibration-bias note, if maintained from previous sessions with the same learner.

If oblique photographs are not available for the local area, vertical aerial photographs (such as those from IGN's Géoportail, Swisstopo's map.geo.admin.ch, or Alpenvereinskarte's online viewer) can be used for snippets 1, 2 and 5; oblique photographs from a guidebook or a phone camera should be used for snippets 3, 4 and 6, where the side-view is the test.

## Delivery notes — C5.2

**Framing.** Open with the calibration framing: maps are abstractions; the skilled candidate reads them back into the real world automatically. C5.2 is the page where that becomes a deliberate practice.

**Exercise 1.** Have the learner do this alone, with the trainer not prompting. The matching itself is the diagnostic; if the learner asks for help, decline gently. They can ask after Exercise 1 is complete.

**Exercise 2.** The cue articulation is the IMS / EMS-relevant skill. Push for specificity. A learner who pairs by intuition without naming the cue has not internalised the contour vocabulary; they have just guessed well.

**Exercise 3.** The surprise exercise is where the calibration biases surface. If the learner cannot identify a surprise, the trainer should prompt: "look at the photograph for a feature you would not have drawn that way".

Common biases to watch for and name:

- mountains drawn too steep (a learner expects sharp peaks where the photograph shows rounded summits),
- bowls drawn too symmetrical (the real bowl typically has one steeper flank and is asymmetric),
- cliff bands drawn too geometric (the photograph shows broken rock, not a clean wall),
- ridges drawn too sharp (the photograph shows a broader crest with a walking line on top).

Maintain a note for each learner so the bias can be revisited at the start of the next session.

**Exercise 4.** The reverse sketch is the test of internalisation. A learner who can match images to contours but cannot produce contours from an image has only one direction of the skill. Insist on the reverse direction.

## Delivery notes — C5.3

**Framing.** Hand over the four-part structure as a non-negotiable for the workbook. If the learner protests, acknowledge that the natural voice is freer, and explain that the workbook structure is a training device. After the workbook, the natural voice can return; for the workbook, hold the structure.

**Exercise 1.** The first sustained description. Watch for:

- structure collapsing into a single paragraph (insist on four labelled sentences),
- four-direction aspect language (insist on eight),
- description of the symbol rather than the imagined view (insist on "what would your eyes actually see"),
- vague feature references ("a mountain over there"; insist on "the summit at 2,650 m to the south-west").

**Exercise 2.** The second description, on a different feature type. The test is whether the structure adapts. Common collapses:

- on a summit, the structure feels redundant ("I'd just see lots of mountains"),
- on a ridge, the learner forgets one of the two flanks,
- in a valley, the far-features sentence becomes "I can't see anything" (which is not the same as "I infer from the map that...").

Coach each collapse as it appears.

**Exercise 3.** The poor-visibility adaptation is the most demanding. Hold the distinction between **seeing** and **inferring from the map**. The phrasing "I cannot see X but the map tells me X is at..." is the IMS / EMS-relevant phrasing. If the learner produces a description that does not change between full visibility and 30 m of useful sight, the visibility constraint has not been engaged.

**Exercise 4.** The self-criticism is the diagnostic. Have the learner read all three descriptions aloud. Reading aloud surfaces:

- repetitive language ("there is", "you can see"),
- missing parts of the structure,
- and four-direction aspect slips.

If the learner cannot identify a weakness, the trainer should not name it directly. Instead prompt: "read aloud once more, slowly, and listen for anything that sounds vague". Self-criticism that the learner produces sticks; self-criticism imposed by the trainer fades.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Photo-matching by intuition without cue articulation.** Most common C5.2 error. Insist on the named cue.
- **Generic biases (too steep, too symmetrical, too geometric).** Predictable and persistent; once named, correctable.
- **Reverse sketch confused with side profile.** A learner who sketches a cross-section instead of contours has slipped to the C5.1 mode. Return to the C5.2 wording.
- **Structure collapse in C5.3.** Most common C5.3 error. Insist on the four labelled sentences.
- **Four-direction aspect slips.** A C4.2 error returning under the pressure of sustained description. Recycle C4.2 if persistent.
- **Symbol-description rather than imagined view.** "Lots of contours" rather than "a steep slope". Coach as in C2.1 Exercise 5; the fix is the same.
- **Poor-visibility description identical to clear-visibility description.** Sign that the visibility constraint has not been engaged. Coach the seeing-vs-inferring phrasing explicitly.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - In C5.2, run a stretch task where the learner predicts the contour pattern from a written verbal description (no image, no map) and then checks against the real contour snippet.
  - In C5.3, set a more demanding scenario (poor visibility plus an injured second person who needs to be talked to a safer location), which forces the description to become functional rather than just accurate.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - In C5.2, allow them to look at a single contour snippet alongside three photographs and pick the matching one, rather than the full six-by-six matrix. Build up to the full matrix.
  - In C5.3, walk through P1 verbally with them before they write, modelling the structured voice. Then have them write P2 and P3 alone.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for Section C6 (Slope judgement and route thinking) when they can:

- match contour snippets to real images and articulate the cues,
- identify their own calibration biases and correct them,
- produce a structured four-to-six-sentence description for any point on a 1:25,000 alpine map,
- adapt the description for poor visibility, distinguishing seen features from inferred features,
- and read back their own descriptions and identify weaknesses.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. Section C6 builds on the structured-description voice; weak descriptions produce weak route narratives.

## Link to next section

Section C6 introduces slope judgement and route thinking formally: comparing two route options on the same slope (C6.1) and recognising terrain traps and committing terrain (C6.2). The cross-section technique from C5.1 supports route comparison; the structured-description voice from C5.3 supports route narration. Together with the aspect work from C4.2, the visualisation block prepares the learner for the route-thinking work in C6.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The photo-matching technique is consistent with mainstream mountain navigation training (Mountaineering Scotland, Glenmore Lodge, Mountain Training UK). The four-part description structure (underfoot, slope and aspect, near features, far features) is a workbook-specific scaffold; it is not a published convention. It is included because workbooks need a teachable structure, and because the four parts together cover the categories the IMS / EMS assessor will probe. After the workbook, the learner can move to a freer voice; for the workbook, the four-part structure is the training device.

The decision to remove all external scaffolding by C5.3 is deliberate. The learner who relies on external imagery indefinitely will not pass IMS / EMS, where the assessor can ask "describe what you see from here" anywhere on a tour without a guidebook to hand. C5.3 is the page that closes the scaffolding gap.
