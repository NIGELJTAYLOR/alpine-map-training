## Purpose of this section

C3.1 is the first page of Section C3 (advanced terrain shapes). It teaches the learner to recognise cols and saddles from contour patterns, distinguish them from summits and valleys, read col height accurately, and reason about route choice between crossing a col versus going over a summit.

C3.1 builds directly on C2.3 (ridges and spurs). A col is a low point on a ridge, sitting between two summits. Without fluent ridge reading, col reading does not work. If the learner is wobbly on C2.3, recycle C2.3 and C2.4 before pressing on with C3.1.

## Coaching priorities

1. The saddle pattern. Two opposite sides rising to summits, two opposite sides falling to valleys.
2. The vocabulary. Col / saddle for the feature; pass for the feature when used as a route. Treat col and saddle as interchangeable.
3. The height-reading habit. Use the spot height where present; use the lowest contour through the saddle as the confirmation.
4. The route-choice instinct. Cross at the col, not over the summit, when the climbing budget allows.
5. The before-and-after comparison with the C2.4 stretch task (snippet 9 col preview).

## Teaching approach

Open by recapping C2.3 ridges. Draw a ridge on the board with two summits along it. Ask the learner to mark where they would walk if they had to cross from one side of the ridge to the other. Most learners will instinctively mark the lowest point on the ridge between the two summits. That point is the col. Name it.

This recap-and-rename approach lands the col faster than starting from a contour pattern, because it makes the col emerge from a route-choice question the learner already has the vocabulary to answer.

Then move to the contour pattern. Draw the saddle on the board: two opposite-side summits with rising contours on each side, two opposite-side valleys with falling contours on each side. Annotate the contour values. Ask the learner to mark the lowest contour through the saddle. Name that as the col height.

Walk through the worked example on the page. Use the route-choice exercise (Exercise 5) as the closing point: the col is not just a feature, it is a route choice.

## Materials checklist

- Section C3.1 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least one clear col with named summits on either side, one secondary col on a side ridge, one summit visibly not a col, and one ridge bend that is deliberately ambiguous (point D in the standard configuration).
- Two routes drawn on the extract: Route 1 over the lower summit, Route 2 over the col, both crossing the same ridge. Both routes should start and end at the same approximate points on opposite sides of the ridge.
- The learner's C2.4 stretch-task sketch of snippet 9, if they have one. This is the before-half of the before-and-after comparison.
- Pencils, including a soft pencil for sketching.
- An eraser.
- A whiteboard or flipchart for the saddle diagram.

## Delivery notes

**Framing.** Open with the C2.3 recap so the col emerges from ridge fluency. If the learner cannot confidently name a ridge feature on the board, return to C2.3 before proceeding.

**Core idea.** Hold the line that the col has the saddle pattern (two-up, two-down). The single most common error on this page is calling a re-entrant or valley head a col, because the low-point intuition is the same but the four-directions test fails. Drill the four-directions test as the diagnostic.

**Vocabulary.** The col / saddle / pass distinction usually does not confuse learners but it does come up at IMS / EMS assessment, where the assessor may use any of the three terms. Make sure the learner can move between them.

**Worked example.** Walk through it on the board. Annotate the contour values, name the col height from the spot height, and run the route-choice maths so the learner sees the climbing-saved figure as a numerical fact.

**Exercise 1.** Watch for learners who pick a low point that is not actually on a ridge. The col has to be a low point between two higher points along the ridge line; a low point in a valley is not a col.

**Exercise 2.** This is a quick technical exercise. Hold the spot-height-as-precise-figure habit established in C2.1 Exercise 2.

**Exercise 3.** The sketch should make the local-minimum nature of the col visible. If the sketch shows the col as a separate feature unconnected to the two summits, return to the saddle diagram on the board.

**Exercise 4.** The wind-funnelling point is the IMS / EMS-relevant detail. Some learners will describe the col as "sheltered" because they associate "low point" with "in the valley" and assume valleys are sheltered. The opposite is true at the col: cols funnel wind across the ridge. Coach this point explicitly.

**Exercise 5.** Push for specificity. Climbing saved in metres. Route distance estimated. A specific reason for the choice. This is the IMS / EMS assessor's expected level of route-card reasoning.

**C2.4 before-and-after comparison.** If the learner did the C2.4 stretch task, lay their snippet 9 sketch alongside the side profile they have just drawn for Exercise 3. Discuss what they got right before they had the vocabulary, and what they would change now. Do not frame this as a marking exercise; frame it as a learning artefact. The point is the visible improvement in their visualisation.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Confusing cols with re-entrants or valley heads.** The four-directions test (rise on two opposite sides, fall on the other two) is the discipline.
- **Reading the col height from the contour rather than the spot height.** Treat the spot height as the precise figure, the contour as the confirmation. The route card uses the spot height.
- **Describing the col as flat ground.** Locally flat in the saddle, but shaped by the rising summits on either side. The descriptive precision matters at IMS / EMS.
- **Missing the wind-funnelling point.** Cols are exposed, not sheltered. Coach this explicitly in Exercise 4.
- **Defaulting to the summit route in Exercise 5.** Some learners assume the highest line is the assessor's expected line. The opposite is true: cross at the col when the climbing budget allows. Push back on summit-default reasoning.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:** ask them to identify a third route choice that goes around the lower end of the ridge (a contour-following line that avoids both summits and the col). This is a real ski-touring route choice and it surfaces the trade-off between climbing saved and distance added. As a stretch, ask them to estimate the time penalty of the contour-following line.
- **For learners who need more support:** in Exercise 5, give them the climbing figure for each route as a starting fact rather than asking them to derive it. This lets them focus on the qualitative reasoning before adding the arithmetic.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for C3.2 (Bowls, Corries and Basins) when they can:

- recognise a col on sight from the saddle pattern,
- give the col height accurately from spot height plus contour confirmation,
- distinguish a col from a summit, a valley, and a re-entrant,
- choose a col over a summit in a route-choice exercise and explain the climbing saving in metres,
- and produce a recognisable side profile sketch.

If any of these are still wobbly, recycle the relevant exercise before moving on.

## Link to next section

C3.2 introduces bowls, corries and basins: enclosed and semi-enclosed hollows on a mountainside. These are the next family of feature in Section C3 and the most route-significant for avalanche-aware terrain interpretation. The headwall of a bowl often shares its rim with a ridge; a col on that rim may be the access point to the bowl. Flag this connection at the close of C3.1 so the learner arrives at C3.2 with the right mental model.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The col / saddle / pass vocabulary is general topographic and is consistent across BASI, Mountaineering Scotland, IGN and Swisstopo materials. The route-choice framing (col over summit when climbing budget allows) is consistent with the IMS / EMS expectation that candidates read the map for energy-efficient route choice rather than peak-bagging.
