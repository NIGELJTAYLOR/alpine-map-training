## Purpose of these sections

C3.2 covers bowls, corries and basins (the enclosed and semi-enclosed hollows that collect snow and concentrate avalanche risk). C3.3 covers plateaux and cliff bands (the flattest and steepest extremes of alpine terrain).

The two pages are paired in a single file because they share a common pedagogical move: each is the application of the C2 vocabulary to a more complex shape, and each forces the learner to read contour spacing as a structured pattern (headwall vs flanks for bowls; absence of contours vs merged contours for plateaux and cliff bands) rather than as a uniform feature.

Together with C3.1 (cols), C3.2 and C3.3 complete Section C3 and the contour-shape vocabulary of Level 2. After these three pages, the learner has every shape they need for aspect (Section C4), visualisation (Section C5) and route thinking (Section C6).

## Coaching priorities

1. The structured reading of bowls. Headwall, flanks, floor, mouth. Not "a slope".
2. The avalanche-relevant detail. Wind-loading in the upper bowl. The 30 degree threshold most often crossed on the headwall.
3. The aspect-and-snow link. Northern hemisphere north-facing for the most consistent winter snow; southern hemisphere south-facing.
4. The qualitative break at cliff bands. Walkable terrain (including very steep) vs impassable terrain (merged contours plus rock symbology).
5. Plateau navigation in poor visibility. Recognising the absence of linear handrails as the navigation problem.
6. The before-and-after comparison with the C2.4 stretch task (snippet 10 cliff band preview).

## Teaching approach

Run C3.2 and C3.3 as a single 90-minute block. They are short pages individually, but the pedagogical move (structured reading of contour patterns) is the same on both, and learners benefit from doing them in succession.

For C3.2, open with a board sketch of an arc of contours opening downhill. Ask the learner to name the back, the sides and the floor. Then layer on the avalanche framing: where would wind-loaded snow accumulate? where would the runout be? This question-led approach makes the structured reading emerge from a route-choice question rather than from definitions.

For C3.3, open with two contrasting board sketches: one showing absent contours surrounded by close contours (the plateau), one showing merged contours with rock hatching (the cliff band). Ask the learner to identify the easiest and hardest ground at a glance. The qualitative break at the cliff band lands fastest when contrasted directly with the easiest possible ground.

Throughout both pages, hold the visualisation discipline established in C2: look, imagine, describe, sketch. The "if I stood here" exercises are the most important on each page, and the most likely to be skipped under time pressure.

## Materials checklist

- Section C3.2 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least one clear bowl with a tight-headwall / wider-flanks pattern, one secondary corrie of different aspect, one valley deliberately included as a non-bowl, and one slope with no enclosing pattern (point D in the standard configuration).
- Section C3.3 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least one plateau (1 km or larger) with a spot height, at least two cliff bands (one continuous, one with a break), and surrounding terrain that gives the learner a clear contrast between easiest and hardest ground.
- A single combined Section C3 extract is acceptable if it contains all the required features.
- The learner's C2.4 stretch-task sketch of snippet 10, if they have one. This is the before-half of the cliff-band before-and-after comparison.
- Pencils, including a soft pencil for sketching.
- An eraser.
- A whiteboard or flipchart for the contrasting C3.3 board sketches.

## Delivery notes — C3.2

**Framing.** Open with the question-led approach: arc of contours, then "where would wind-loaded snow accumulate?" The structured reading emerges naturally from the route-choice question.

**Vocabulary.** Bowl / corrie / cirque / basin all describe the same family. Most learners will be familiar with at least one term; coach the others as synonyms. The trainer should know that on IGN sheets the feature is often labelled as "cirque" and that this is the same thing as a Scottish corrie.

**Worked example.** Walk through it slowly. Annotate the headwall spacing, the flank spacing, and the mouth elevation on the board. Have the learner call the slope angle for each part using the C1.2 rule before you confirm it. This is the moment where the C1.2 / C2 / C3.2 chain becomes visible.

**Exercise 1.** Watch for learners who call any U-shape a bowl, including a valley head with an enclosing curve. The diagnostic is the **enclosed on three sides, open on the fourth** check: a bowl is shut at the back; a valley runs through.

**Exercise 2.** Push for both the headwall reading and the flank reading. A bowl is not a uniform slope and the learner who reads it as one is missing the route-relevant detail.

**Exercise 3.** The wind-loading mark is the IMS / EMS-relevant detail. If the learner produces a sketch that shows even snow distribution, gently coach: the bowl is a terrain trap because the easiest ski line is the part of the slope where the snow is most loaded. Mark on their sketch where they would not stand if a slab released.

**Exercise 4.** The runout-zone awareness is the closing point. A learner who describes the bowl from the mouth without mentioning the runout has not yet integrated the avalanche framing.

**Exercise 5.** Aspect and snow is a brief preview of Section C4. Hold the answer to one specific direction with one specific mechanism. Vague answers ("colder") should be coached into specific ones (low sun angle in winter, less radiation, slower snowpack metamorphosis).

## Delivery notes — C3.3

**Framing.** The two-extremes contrast is the way in. Sketch the plateau and the cliff band side by side on the board and ask the learner to identify the easiest and hardest ground. The contrast lands the qualitative break faster than either feature would alone.

**Plateau.** Some learners will struggle with the idea that the absence of contours is itself a feature. Reassure them: contour absence is information. A flat alpine area is rare; when you find one, it has navigation and route-choice implications.

**Cliff band.** The merged-contours-plus-rock-symbology cue is the diagnostic. Stress that contour spacing is a continuum (gentle to steep) but cliff bands sit qualitatively outside that continuum (impassable). Read this as a category boundary, not a steeper version of the same thing.

**Map symbology.** The rock symbology varies between national mapping agencies. The page lists the IGN, Swisstopo and Alpenvereinskarte conventions. Always check the legend of whatever map the learner is using; the variation is real and matters at IMS / EMS assessment, where the candidate may be assessed on a venue map they have not seen before.

**Worked example.** Walk through it on the board. Sketch the plateau (oval, no contours, spot height in the middle) and the cliff band (thick black line on the steep side, rock hatching). Have the learner mark the easiest ground (plateau) and the hardest ground to descend (cliff band) before you label them.

**Exercise 1.** The shading task is a recognition exercise. If the learner shades an area with visible contours, return to the worked example.

**Exercise 2.** The continuity question is the route-relevant one. A continuous cliff band has to be avoided; a broken cliff band may be navigable through the gap. Push for the continuity reading on every cliff band the learner identifies.

**Exercise 3.** This is the diagnostic exercise. A learner who can pick the easiest and hardest ground at a glance, with a sound reason, has internalised the qualitative break. A learner who hesitates has not.

**Exercise 4.** The route-from-the-valley sketch is the IMS / EMS-relevant detail. The learner should choose either a side without a cliff band, or a gap in a broken cliff band. A learner who routes a line through a continuous cliff band has not yet integrated the impassable framing.

**Exercise 5.** The poor-visibility plateau navigation question is the closing point. The altimeter answer is the most important one; in poor visibility on a plateau, height is often the only available cross-check. Some learners will name GPS first; coach them towards altimeter as the primary tool, with GPS as backup, in line with the IMS / EMS expectation.

**C2.4 before-and-after comparison.** As with C3.1 and snippet 9, lay the learner's snippet 10 sketch alongside the cliff-band side profile they have just drawn for C3.3 Exercise 4. Run the comparison the same way. The visible improvement in visualisation is the learning artefact.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Treating a bowl as a uniform slope.** The structured reading (headwall, flanks, floor, mouth) is the discipline.
- **Missing the wind-loading point on bowls.** Even snow distribution is the most common bowl-sketch error. Coach explicitly.
- **Confusing the bowl with the valley head.** Both have a U or arc shape. The diagnostic is the enclosure-on-three-sides test.
- **Treating cliff bands as merely steep ground.** The qualitative break (impassable, not just steep) is the IMS / EMS-relevant detail.
- **Missing rock symbology because they were focused on contour spacing.** Both cues together are the cliff-band diagnostic; either alone can mislead.
- **Underestimating plateau navigation difficulty.** Some learners assume flat ground is easy. In poor visibility, the absence of features is the navigation problem. Coach this point at Exercise 5.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - In C3.2, ask them to identify the safest skinning track up the bowl flank rather than the headwall, and explain why the flank is safer.
  - In C3.3, ask them to plan a complete route from a named point in the valley to a named point on the plateau, avoiding the cliff bands. This is effectively a Section C6 route-thinking exercise pulled forward.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - In C3.2, pre-annotate the headwall and the flanks on the standard extract so the learner can focus on slope angles without having to find the parts themselves.
  - In C3.3, walk Exercise 3 (easiest and hardest ground) verbally before they write, to anchor the qualitative break.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for Section C4 (Aspect and direction of slope) when they can:

- read a bowl as a structured feature with named parts,
- predict where wind-loaded snow accumulates and recognise the bowl as a terrain trap,
- read aspect-and-snow links at the surface level (one direction, one mechanism),
- distinguish plateau ground from steep ground at a glance,
- distinguish cliff bands from very steep walkable ground using both cues (merged contours plus rock symbology),
- and articulate why a plateau is hard to navigate in poor visibility, and what equipment helps.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle the relevant exercise before moving on. The aspect-reading work in Section C4 builds on the bowl-aspect framing here.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The bowl / corrie / cirque / basin vocabulary is general topographic and is consistent across BASI, Mountaineering Scotland and the alpine literature. The cirque-glaciaire connection is geomorphology rather than navigation, and is included only as a French-Alps-relevant note for the trainer; the learner does not need to know it to pass IMS / EMS.

The plateau and cliff-band content is consistent with BASI Mountain Safety Pathway materials, which require fluent recognition of cliff features on a 1:25,000 topo map. The rock-symbology variation between IGN, Swisstopo and Alpenvereinskarte is documented in each agency's published map legend.

The poor-visibility plateau navigation framing is consistent with Mountaineering Scotland's navigation guidance (linear features as handrails; their absence as the diagnostic poor-visibility navigation problem). The altimeter-as-primary-tool answer for poor-visibility height-checking on a featureless plateau is consistent with the ISIA equipment-familiarity expectation.
