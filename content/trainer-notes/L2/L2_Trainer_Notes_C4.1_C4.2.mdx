## Purpose of these sections

C4.1 (High to low ground) and C4.2 (Reading aspect) introduce direction of slope and aspect formally. Both skills have already been used implicitly in Sections C2 and C3; C4 makes them explicit and gives the learner the compass-aspect vocabulary the IMS / EMS assessor will expect to hear.

These two pages are paired in a single file because:

- C4.1 is a short, single-skill page (the fall line),
- C4.2 builds directly on it,
- in any practical navigation training the two are taught together,
- and the combined material fits comfortably in a 60-minute session.

After C4, the workbook moves into Section C5 (Visualisation). The aspect language built here will appear in every "if I stood here" prompt from C5 onwards.

## Coaching priorities

1. The perpendicular-to-contour rule for the fall line. Right angle, not parallel.
2. Eight-direction aspect reading (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW). Never four.
3. Grid north as the practical reference for aspect (true north is close enough at this resolution; magnetic variation does not matter for aspect, only for bearings).
4. Aspect-and-snow reasoning grounded in hemisphere and prevailing wind, not generic "north is colder" assumptions.
5. Bulletin-to-terrain mapping. The single most IMS / EMS-relevant skill on these pages.
6. Carry-over from C2.3 (aspect of each flank of a ridge or spur) and C3.2 (aspect of a bowl): C4 is where these become formal.

## Teaching approach

Open the C4.1 / C4.2 block with a recap question: "How did you decide which way the ground falls in the C2.2 valleys page?" Most learners will say "by reading the contour values". Confirm: that reading is what C4.1 names explicitly, and the rule that makes it reliable is the perpendicular-to-contour rule.

Draw three contour lines on the board with values 2,500 m, 2,490 m, 2,480 m running roughly horizontally across the board. Ask the learner to draw the fall line. Watch for parallel arrows; coach the perpendicular.

For C4.2, switch from "which way is downhill" to "which way does the slope face". Stress the equivalence: face direction equals fall direction. Then layer on the compass: the fall line points in a compass direction; that direction is the aspect.

Use the worked example in the page to drill the eight-direction reading. Then move to Exercise 4 (bulletin to terrain) as the integration moment. This exercise is the closest thing in Level 2 to a real IMS / EMS assessment task: read a bulletin, shade the danger zones, route around them.

## Materials checklist

- Section C4.1 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least four named slopes with different aspects (one each from the four cardinal directions ideally), one of which is a curving slope where the fall line direction varies across the slope width.
- Section C4.2 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain a clear north arrow or grid lines, at least four named slopes covering at least four of the eight aspects, and a range of elevations including both above and below 2,200 m so the bulletin-to-terrain exercise has shaded and unshaded ground.
- A combined Section C4 extract is acceptable if it covers all the required features.
- A plastic protractor or set square to demonstrate the perpendicular rule on the board.
- The C2.3 extract or its replication on the C4.2 extract, for Exercise 5 (aspect on a ridge or spur). If using a separate extract, make sure it carries a recognisable spur with a known aspect on each flank.
- Pencils, including a soft pencil for shading.
- Coloured pencils (one warm colour for shading the bulletin's heightened-danger zone in Exercise 4 is helpful but not essential).
- An eraser.

## Delivery notes — C4.1

**Framing.** Open with the recap question. The learner already knows how to read direction of slope informally; C4.1 just makes the reading explicit and reliable.

**Core idea.** Hold the perpendicular rule. Some learners default to estimating downhill by eye and get it right on simple slopes; the perpendicular rule is what makes them reliable on complex slopes.

**Worked example.** Walk through the example on the board with annotated contour values. Draw the fall line as a perpendicular from the middle contour. Then rotate the contour lines on the board and re-draw the fall line. Repeat once more. The drill is the perpendicular, applied without thinking.

**Exercise 1.** A quick technical exercise. Watch for learners who read only the highest contour and skip the lowest, or vice versa. Both are needed.

**Exercise 2.** This is the discipline exercise. The arrow has to be perpendicular to the contour and pointing downhill. Mark these criteria explicitly and accept neither without both.

**Exercise 3.** The explanatory exercise. A learner who can draw the right arrow but cannot articulate why has done half the IMS / EMS work. Hold the explanatory standard.

**Exercise 4.** The curving slope exercise sets up the spur reading at C4.2 Exercise 5. Watch for learners who draw all three arrows in the same direction; they have not internalised the local nature of the fall line.

## Delivery notes — C4.2

**Framing.** Open with the equivalence: aspect equals fall-line direction. A north-facing slope falls towards the north. The slope and the aspect describe the same thing.

**Three norths.** The three-norths point can confuse learners. Hold the simplification: for aspect, grid north is the practical reference. Magnetic variation is a Level 3 / compass-bearings issue. The workbook's standard "check current variation" line appears in the C4.2 page in parentheses for completeness, but do not let it derail the aspect work.

**Eight directions.** The eight-direction standard is non-negotiable at IMS / EMS. Coach learners who default to four. The avalanche bulletin uses eight; the assessor will expect eight; the workbook stays at eight.

**Worked example.** Walk through it step by step, annotating the higher and lower contours, the fall line direction in compass terms, and the aspect. Then ask the learner to predict the snow conditions on the slope; this is the bridge into Exercise 3.

**Exercise 1.** A quick check that the learner has internalised grid north as the reference. Watch for arrows pointing to a corner or a feature.

**Exercise 2.** The drill exercise. Watch for four-direction defaults; coach to eight.

**Exercise 3.** The aspect-and-snow exercise. Hold both halves of the answer (the right hemisphere reasoning, and a specific mechanism rather than a generic "colder"). The prevailing-wind point is a useful stretch for confident learners.

**Exercise 4.** The bulletin-to-terrain exercise. This is the IMS / EMS-relevant integration. Coach the route-finding step: shading is the easy part; finding a route around the shaded ground is the assessable skill. Some extracts will not have a viable route around the heightened-danger zone, in which case the right answer is to abandon the chosen objective. Make this point explicit; "the route doesn't go" is a valid IMS / EMS answer.

**Exercise 5.** The carry-over from C2.3 Exercise 2. If the learner cannot remember the C2.3 ridge / spur exercise, this is a sign they have not been re-reading the earlier sections. Coach the habit of re-reading.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Fall-line parallel to contour.** Most common error on C4.1. Drill the perpendicular.
- **Four-direction aspect reading.** Most common error on C4.2. Drill eight directions from the start.
- **Confusing grid north and true north.** A few learners worry about the difference and try to apply magnetic variation to aspect. Reassure: not needed at this resolution.
- **Generic snow reasoning.** "North is cold" is not enough. Push for the mechanism (low sun angle, less radiation, slower metamorphosis) and for the hemisphere-aware framing.
- **Treating the bulletin as a rule rather than a guide.** A learner who reads "danger on north aspects" and then refuses ever to ski a north slope has misunderstood the bulletin. The bulletin describes today's heightened danger; aspect itself is not a permanent property of risk. Coach this nuance gently; it will be revisited in Section C6.
- **Forgetting C2.3 Exercise 2.** Many learners will need a brief re-cap before they can do C4.2 Exercise 5. Build the re-cap into the session rather than treating it as a failure.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:** in Exercise 4, set a more demanding bulletin (for example, "danger level 4; heightened danger above 1,800 m on N through E aspects, lateral propagation possible to NE through SE"). Or ask them to plan a route that maximises ski quality while respecting the bulletin, rather than just any safe route.
- **For learners who need more support:** in C4.1 Exercise 2, draw the first fall line for them as a worked example, then ask them to do the remaining three. In C4.2 Exercise 4, provide pre-shaded bulletin coverage so they can focus on route-finding without the shading step.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for Section C5 (Visualisation) when they can:

- read the fall line on any contour pattern using the perpendicular rule,
- read aspect to the nearest of the eight cardinal / intercardinal directions,
- explain aspect-and-snow links with a specific mechanism in the appropriate hemisphere,
- map an avalanche bulletin onto a specific extract and find a route around the heightened-danger zone (or recognise when no viable route exists),
- and recall the aspect-of-each-flank reading for a ridge or spur from C2.3 Exercise 2.

If any of these are still wobbly, recycle the relevant exercise before moving on. Section C5's "if I stood here" exercises will use aspect language throughout; weak aspect makes weak visualisation.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The fall-line and aspect content is general topographic skill and is consistent across BASI, Mountaineering Scotland, IGN and Swisstopo materials. The avalanche-bulletin framing (aspect-and-elevation as the standard format) is consistent with the major European avalanche services (Météo-France, SLF, ZAMG, EAWS) and with the BASI Mountain Safety Pathway expectation that candidates can read and apply a regional bulletin.

The hemisphere note (northern hemisphere aspect-and-snow defaults inverting in the southern hemisphere) is included so the workbook remains usable for candidates training for assessments outside Europe. If the workbook is being used solely for European-Alps work, the hemisphere note can be skimmed in delivery.

The avoidance of magnetic variation in C4.2 is deliberate. Variation matters for compass bearings (Level 3) and for direct compass-to-map work. It does not matter for reading aspect to the nearest of eight directions, and including it here would muddle the page. The workbook's standard "check current variation" line is included in parentheses for completeness only.
