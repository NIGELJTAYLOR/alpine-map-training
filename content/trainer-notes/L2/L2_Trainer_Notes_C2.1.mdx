## Purpose of this section

C2.1 is the first of the named-terrain-shape pages. It teaches the learner to recognise hills and summits from contour patterns, distinguish three summit shapes (sharp peak, rounded summit, plateau-like high), and read summit height accurately using both contours and spot heights.

It is also the first page where the learner has to translate a contour pattern into an imagined 3D view of the ground (Exercises 4 and 5). This is the core developmental need for any learner who struggles to visualise terrain from a flat map, which is a common weakness in candidates moving up to BASI Level 4 IMS / EMS.

## Coaching priorities

1. The closed-loop pattern as the visual cue for a high point.
2. The role of the spot height as the precise summit elevation.
3. The three summit shapes as a vocabulary for terrain description.
4. The 2D-to-3D translation habit (sketching and verbalising what the ground would look like).
5. The link to IMS map reading: summits are key reference points for route identification.

## Teaching approach

C2.1 is the first place the workbook asks the learner to imagine standing on a feature and describing what they would see. Some learners treat this as a creative writing task; it is not. It is a deliberate drill in spatial visualisation.

Encourage learners to:

- look at the contour pattern,
- close their eyes briefly,
- describe in their own words what the ground would look like,
- and only then sketch or write.

This sequence (look, imagine, describe, draw) is the visualisation drill that will reappear in C2.2, C2.3, C5 and C6.

## Materials checklist

- Section C2.1 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least one sharp peak, one rounded summit and one plateau-like high, plus at least one feature that is visibly not a summit (a slope or ridge).
- Pencils, including a soft pencil for sketching.
- An eraser.
- Plain paper for additional sketching practice.

## Delivery notes

**Framing.** Open by reminding the learner that in C1.1 and C1.2 they learned what contours mean and how to read steepness. C2.1 is the first page where contours form a recognisable shape with a name. From here on, every contour pattern in the workbook will have a name and a route-choice implication.

**Worked example.** Walk through it on the board. Draw the three loop sets (sharp, rounded, plateau-like) and have learners call out the type before you label them.

**Exercise 1.** Watch for learners who pick a high spot on a slope (not a closed loop) and call it a summit. The closed-loop check is the discipline.

**Exercise 2.** This is a quick technical exercise but it lands an important habit: the spot height is the figure to use, the contour is the confirmation. On a real route card, the IMS candidate writes the spot height altitude for a waypoint.

**Exercise 3.** This is where the three summit shapes become real. If a learner is unsure between sharp and rounded, ask them to measure the spacing of the outermost two loops (back to C1.2). Tight spacing equals sharp; wide spacing equals rounded.

**Exercise 4.** The sketch is the big learning moment. Some learners will refuse to sketch because they think they "can't draw". Reassure them: it is a stick-figure exercise, not art. The point is the translation from contour pattern to side profile.

**Exercise 5.** The most challenging exercise on the page, but the most valuable for learners with weak 2D-to-3D translation. Watch for:

- Descriptions that re-describe the symbol rather than the imagined view ("there are lots of contour lines"). Coach back to "what would your eyes actually see?"
- Compass directions used loosely. Encourage "the ground falls away to the south-east" rather than "down that way".
- Mention of neighbouring features. If the learner forgets, prompt: "What else can you see from up there?"

## Common learner difficulties

- **Confusing summits with high spurs.** A spur ends in a high point but does not form a closed loop. C2.3 will deal with spurs explicitly; for now, hold the line that summits are closed-loop features.
- **Reading the lowest contour instead of the highest.** Easy mistake when the loop set is small. Coach the habit of tracing each loop and finding the innermost.
- **Treating spot heights as the only summit indicator.** Some maps omit spot heights for minor summits. The closed-loop pattern remains the primary cue.
- **Sketch reluctance.** Persist gently. The visualisation work in C2.1, C2.2 and C2.3 is the cumulative exercise that learners with this weakness need most.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:** ask them to identify the **second** highest summit on the extract and explain how they know.
- **For learners who need more support:** in Exercise 4, give them a pre-drawn baseline and ground line and ask them to add the summit profile only.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for C2.2 (Valleys and Gullies) when they can:

- glance at a closed-loop contour pattern and call summit confidently,
- read summit height from both the contour and the spot height,
- name the three summit shapes,
- and produce a recognisable side profile sketch from a contour pattern.

## Link to next section

C2.2 introduces valleys and gullies, which are the inverse of summits. Where summits show closed loops with rising centres, valleys show V or U shapes pointing uphill (towards higher ground). Flag this contrast at the close of C2.1 so learners arrive at C2.2 with the right mental model.
