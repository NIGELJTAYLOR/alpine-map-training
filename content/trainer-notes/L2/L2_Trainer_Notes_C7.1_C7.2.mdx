## Purpose of these sections

C7.1 and C7.2 close Level 2. C7.1 (Mixed contour quiz) is a fast diagnostic that samples the full Level 2 vocabulary in a low-stakes format. C7.2 (Virtual terrain walk) is a sustained integration exercise that runs a four-stop route narration across the contour vocabulary, the visualisation block, and the route-thinking work.

Together they are the readiness check for Level 3. The point is not the score; the point is honesty about which skills are fluent, which are slow, and which are unreliable.

## Coaching priorities

1. The diagnostic mindset. C7.1 is for finding weak spots, not for scoring well. C7.2 is for rehearsing the working voice, not for showing off.
2. The error log as the action plan. Vague entries are not enough; specific commitments are.
3. The four-part description structure held under sustained narration.
4. Eight-direction aspect language under pressure.
5. Route-choice reasoning that references the comfortable skinning band, the bulletin, and terrain configurations.
6. The honesty of the closing readiness check (R3). The trainer's role is to verify, not to override.

## Teaching approach

Run C7.1 and C7.2 as two distinct sessions on different days, not as a single block. The reasons:

- C7.1 produces an error log that needs action between sessions.
- C7.2 is more useful after the error-log items have been addressed.
- A learner who runs both back-to-back will not have time to revisit the weak pages identified in C7.1.

A workable timing:

- **Session 1 (C7.1):** 60 minutes. Quiz, marking, error log, action plan.
- **Between sessions:** the learner does the work in the error log. This may be 1 to 5 days depending on the size of the log.
- **Session 2 (C7.2):** 90 minutes. Virtual terrain walk, sustained narration, closing reflection, readiness check.

If the two sessions absolutely have to run on the same day, allow at least an hour break in between and use the break for the learner to revisit the most important error-log items.

## Delivery notes — C7.1

**Framing.** The quiz is diagnostic. Make this explicit at the start. Some learners default to performance mode under quiz conditions and freeze on questions they would otherwise handle. The framing reduces this.

**Time pressure.** Light, not strict. Set a timer for the quiz overall (25 minutes), not for individual questions. The point is that the learner notices their own slowness on specific questions; the timer makes them notice.

**Marking with the learner alongside.** Do not take the quiz away to mark in private. Sit with the learner and mark question by question. The conversation that comes out of the marking is more valuable than the marks.

**Confidence score.** Insist that the learner fills in all six skill areas before moving to the error log. Some learners want to skip the confidence score; the score is the bridge between the quiz performance and the action plan.

**Error log.** This is the most important artefact of the session. Walk through each wrong or slow question and ask:

- Which skill area is this question testing?
- Which Level 2 page taught this skill?
- What specifically went wrong (skipped step, wrong vocabulary, vague reasoning)?
- What will you do about it before the next session?

A vague entry is not enough. Push for specific commitments: page, exercise, deadline.

## Delivery notes — C7.2

**Framing.** Open the session with a brief check on error-log progress since C7.1. If the learner has not done the work, the C7.2 session will surface the same weaknesses. Decide together whether to proceed or to defer the session.

**Stop and leg structure.** The page is structured as a sequence: stop, leg, stop, leg, stop, leg, stop. Hold the structure. Some learners will want to narrate the whole route in a single block; resist. The point of the structured stops is the discipline of the four-part description.

**Stop narration.** Watch for:

- four-part structure collapsing into a paragraph (insist on the four labelled sentences),
- four-direction aspect language returning under pressure (insist on eight),
- description of the symbol rather than the imagined view (insist on "what would your eyes actually see"),
- generic feature references ("a mountain over there"; insist on "the summit at 2,650 m to the south-west").

**Leg narration.** Watch for:

- line type identified but gradient eye-balled (insist on the C1.2 contour-spacing reading),
- bulletin or terrain-trap check skipped (insist on the additional check for that leg),
- route choice justified by aesthetics rather than by the comfortable skinning band, the bulletin, or the terrain configuration (insist on the named reasoning).

**Closing reflection.** R1 and R2 should both be specific. R3 is the honesty test. Do not override the learner's R3 answer; verify it against the evidence:

- a "yes" answer with weak C7.1 evidence is a sign of over-confidence,
- a "not quite" answer with strong evidence is a sign of healthy self-doubt (which is valuable; encourage the named-pages habit),
- a "no" answer with strong evidence is rare but real (the learner may know something about their own learning style that the workbook cannot see).

In all cases, the goal is an honest readiness check that the learner can defend.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Performance mode in C7.1.** The quiz feels like a test; the learner freezes. Reset the diagnostic framing.
- **Vague error-log entries.** The most common C7.1 weakness. Push for specific commitments.
- **Structure collapse in C7.2 stops.** The four-part structure feels slow on stop A and the learner tries to compress by stop B. Hold the structure.
- **Eye-balled gradients in C7.2 legs.** The C1.2 reading slips under the pressure of sustained narration. Reset to contour-spacing reading.
- **Skipping the additional checks on legs.** The bulletin check on B → C is the most often skipped. Insist.
- **Generic readiness answers in R3.** "I think I'm ready" is not enough. Push for the evidence-supported answer.
- **Trainer-led readiness.** Resist the urge to declare the learner ready. R3 is the learner's call. The trainer verifies, the trainer does not decide.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - In C7.1, run the quiz under stricter time pressure (15 minutes for the fifteen questions). The pressure surfaces weak spots that a 25-minute window may not.
  - In C7.2, add a fifth stop or a more demanding bulletin scenario.
  - Set a stretch task at the end of session 2: produce a written route card for the same A → B → C → D route, formatted for handover to a touring partner. The route card consolidates the narration into the IMS / EMS-relevant working artefact.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - In C7.1, untime the quiz. Speed comes after correctness.
  - In C7.2, walk through stop A and leg A → B alongside the learner, modelling the structure. Then have them do B onwards alone.
  - If the closing reflection is hard, prompt with the explicit comparison: "compared with C5.3 Exercise 4, is this stronger, weaker, or the same?"

## Readiness to progress to Level 3

The learner is ready for Level 3 when:

- the C7.1 quiz score is 12 or more out of 15, with no family-level errors on the feature-identification questions,
- the C7.2 narration holds the four-part structure at all four stops,
- the leg narrations include the line type, the gradient from contour spacing, and the additional check for that leg,
- the closing reflection at R1, R2, R3 is honest and evidence-supported,
- and the C7.1 error log has been acted on or has a specific plan.

Level 3 introduces compass bearings, magnetic variation, and altimeter / barometer work. It builds on the contour-reading foundation that Level 2 has just consolidated. A learner who is wobbly on Level 2 will compound the wobble at Level 3, where the precision required is higher.

If the learner is borderline, hold them at Level 2 for an additional consolidation session (a fresh C7.1 quiz on a different extract; a fresh C7.2 walk on a different route) rather than moving on. The IMS / EMS pathway has time built in for this kind of consolidation; the workbook should use it.

## Closing the Level 2 build-out

C7.2 is the final page of Level 2. After the readiness check, the workbook formally hands the learner over to Level 3.

Two artefacts are worth keeping at the end of Level 2:

- **The error log from C7.1**, with the actions completed.
- **The readiness check at R3**, with the trainer's verification note alongside.

Both go into the trainer manual's progress log and into any logbook the candidate is keeping for BASI EMS purposes. They are evidence of the consolidation work and the readiness decision.

## Reference notes for the trainer

C7.1 and C7.2 are consolidation pages and do not introduce any new claims requiring source verification. The full Level 2 vocabulary is taken from C1 to C6 inclusive, where the underlying sources are recorded in `00_Research_Inputs/Sources.md`.

The decision to run C7.1 and C7.2 as separate sessions on different days is pedagogical: the error-log action plan from C7.1 needs time to take effect before C7.2 can test the integration honestly. If the trainer's schedule does not allow two sessions, the single-session option is documented above with the recommended break.

The Level 3 readiness criteria are workbook-specific. They do not map directly to a published BASI EMS criterion (which the workbook does not have full access to; see `Sources.md` section 6 for the open verification items). They are a reasonable working standard for moving from contour reading into compass bearings, and they will be revisited if and when the BASI EMS published criteria become available for primary verification.
