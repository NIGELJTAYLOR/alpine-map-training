## Purpose of this section

C5.1 is the technical anchor of Section C5. It teaches the cross-section as a precise, repeatable technique for converting a contour pattern into a side profile. The exercises walk the learner through the full procedure once: drawing a section line, recording every contour crossing, building the cross-section with a stated vertical exaggeration, reading the finished profile, and comparing with a model.

C5.1 is also the page where the workbook stops being mostly about recognition and starts being about construction. From here on the learner is producing artefacts (cross-sections, descriptions, route lines) rather than just naming features on a page.

## Coaching priorities

1. The procedure as a procedure. Section line, contour crossings, axes, plotted points, smooth line. In that order, every time.
2. Horizontal scale matched to the map. Always. Never approximate.
3. Vertical exaggeration stated explicitly. 2:1 is the default; whatever the choice, label it.
4. Reading slope angles from contour spacing on the section line, not from the visual angle of the cross-section drawing.
5. Diagnosing your own errors against a model. The improvement on the second attempt is the learning artefact.

## Teaching approach

C5.1 is best run as a guided first attempt followed by an unguided second attempt.

A workable session shape:

- Walk through the procedure on the board with a worked example (the page's worked example is fine), step by step. The board version is fast; the learner's first attempt should not feel like fresh territory.
- Hand out the standard C5.1 extract and walk the learner through Exercises 1 to 3 with you alongside, prompting at each step. Do not draw for them, but do prompt them to label axes, list every contour crossing, and choose a vertical exaggeration before plotting.
- Have them complete Exercises 4 and 5 alone.
- If time permits, run a second section line on the same extract and have them do it unguided. This second attempt is where the technique becomes their own.

Resist the temptation to pre-label the axes for the learner. Axis labelling is part of the technique; learners who skip it produce cross-sections that misread under handover and need to relearn the habit later.

## Materials checklist

- Section C5.1 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval, with two named points X and Y between which the section line is drawn. The line should be 7 to 10 cm long on the map and should cross at least 8 contours (so the cross-section has enough plotted points to show shape).
- Optionally a second pair of named points for the second-attempt run.
- Grid paper or plain paper for the cross-section drawing.
- A ruler (essential for the section line).
- Pencils with a sharp point. The cross-section work rewards precision.
- An eraser.
- A whiteboard or flipchart for the worked example walk-through.
- The trainer's model cross-section of the X-to-Y line, on a separate sheet, for the comparison in Exercise 5.

## Delivery notes

**Worked example walk-through.** This is the most important part of the session. Walk through the procedure on the board, step by step, naming each step. The procedure is the technique; once it is in the learner's head as a procedure, the cross-section is reliable.

**Exercise 1.** A quick check. Insist on the ruler. A wobbly section line will produce wrong contour-crossing distances and the cross-section will be wrong from the start.

**Exercise 2.** This is the most error-prone step. Watch for skipped crossings, especially in steep sections where contours are close together. The skipping is unconscious and the correction is to count along the line slowly with a sharp pencil tip.

**Exercise 3.** The cross-section drawing itself. Watch for:

- axes not drawn or not labelled,
- horizontal scale not matched to the map,
- vertical exaggeration not stated,
- contour crossings plotted at uniform spacing on the horizontal axis instead of at their actual distances (this destroys the shape),
- smoothing through important breaks.

Each of these is a small error individually but produces a misread cross-section in combination. Coach each one as it appears.

**Exercise 4.** A reading exercise. Hold the discipline of reading slope angle from contour spacing on the section line, not from the visual angle of the cross-section drawing.

**Exercise 5.** The comparison with a model is the diagnostic exercise. Hand the model to the learner only after they have completed Exercises 1 to 4. Then sit with them while they compare. The conversation that comes out of the comparison is more valuable than the exercise text itself.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Skipped contour crossings.** The most common cause of wrong cross-section shape. Coach slow counting with a sharp pencil.
- **Vertical scale not labelled.** A small omission with a large impact on readability. Insist.
- **Horizontal scale not matched to the map.** Watch for cross-sections drawn on smaller paper at a different scale; the learner has unconsciously redrawn at a horizontal scale that fits the page.
- **Reading slope angle from the visual angle of the cross-section.** The cross-section is exaggerated; the contour spacing on the section line is the truth. Coach the C1.2 reading every time.
- **Smoothing too much.** A learner who joins all the dots with a single smooth curve may smooth through a small plateau or a small step. The cross-section should show the breaks the data shows.
- **Smoothing too little.** The opposite error: a learner joins all the dots with straight lines, producing a polygonal cross-section. Smooth between the dots, but preserve the breaks where they exist.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - Run a second section line at right angles to the first to produce an L-shaped pair of cross-sections through the same area. The pair gives a richer 3D picture.
  - Add a **true-scale cross-section** as a stretch task (no vertical exaggeration). The dramatic flatness of the result lands the importance of vertical exaggeration as a visualisation tool.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - Pre-draw the axes for them with scale labels in place.
  - Walk them through Exercise 2 (contour crossings) crossing by crossing rather than leaving them to count alone.
  - In Exercise 3, mark the first three contour crossings on the cross-section together, then have them complete the remaining ones alone.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for C5.2 (Map to Real Landscape Matching) when they can:

- produce a cross-section with horizontal scale matched to the map,
- label the vertical exaggeration explicitly,
- record every contour crossing along the section line,
- read steepness from contour spacing on the section line rather than from the visual angle on the drawing,
- and identify their own errors against a model.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. The cross-section technique is one of the few techniques in Level 2 that requires precision; the precision is what makes the result useful.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The cross-section technique is general topographic skill and is consistent across BASI, Mountaineering Scotland, IGN and Swisstopo materials. The 2:1 vertical exaggeration default is conventional in mountain navigation atlases and is consistent with the standard practice in the BASI Mountain Safety Pathway.

The choice to teach the cross-section as a procedural technique rather than as a freeform sketch is deliberate. Procedural construction produces reliable, repeatable, comparable artefacts; freeform sketching produces variable results that are harder to coach. The freeform sketch returns in C5.2 Exercise 4 and in C5.3, where the cross-section confidence built here supports the freer mode.
