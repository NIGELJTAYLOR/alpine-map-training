## Purpose of this section

These notes support the first two pages of Level 2. The learner is moving from general map literacy (Level 1) into reading the shape of the land from contour lines, and starting to use contour spacing to judge steepness. C1.2 is where the alpine specifics begin: the 30 degree rule of thumb, the importance of contour interval, and the foundation for all later route-choice work.

## Coaching priorities

At this stage, the trainer should focus on:

1. The equal-height meaning of contours (C1.1).
2. The idea of contour interval (C1.1) and why it varies between alpine map series.
3. The link between contour spacing and slope steepness (C1.2).
4. The 30 degree rule of thumb on 1:25,000 with 10 m contours (C1.2).
5. The habit of checking the contour interval on every new map (C1.1 and C1.2).

## Teaching approach

Keep the explanation visual. Avoid rushing into named terrain features (hills, valleys, ridges, spurs) - those land in C2.

For C1.1, the goal is the conceptual leap: contours are not random brown squiggles, they are organised height information. Use simple language:

- "Each line is one height level."
- "The next line up is a little higher."
- "Together the lines build the shape of the hill or slope."

For C1.2, the goal is automaticity: by the end of the page, the learner should be able to glance at a contour pattern and say "gentle / moderate / steep / very steep" without doing the maths each time. The maths exists in the worked example to make the rule defensible, not to be memorised.

## Materials checklist

- Section C1.2 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval (one per learner; A4 colour print recommended).
- Rulers with millimetre markings.
- Pencils and an eraser.
- Optional: a second extract at 1:50,000 or with a 25 m interval, for the differentiation exercise below.

## C1.1 — Delivery notes

**Framing.** Open by reminding learners that the previous map skills (anatomy, colours, scale, grid references) all sit on top of a flat picture of the world. C1.1 is where the map starts to acquire height and shape.

**Worked example.** Walk through it on the board. The mental image of "stepping up a hillside, crossing one contour line at a time" is the one to plant.

**Exercises.** Most learners get Exercises 1, 2 and 4 quickly. Exercise 3 is the one to listen to: the learner's own words give you a quick read on whether the conceptual leap has happened.

**Common stumble.** Some learners describe contours as "lines on a hill" without registering that they are levels of equal height. If you hear this, return to the equal-height idea before moving on.

## C1.2 — Delivery notes

**Framing.** Tell learners up front: this is the most important rule in alpine map reading. Spacing equals steepness. The 30 degree threshold is the rule that will appear in their assessment material later.

**Worked example.** Do the maths once on the board so the rule has visible roots. Then drop the maths and use the rule of thumb.

**Exercise 1.** Quick confidence check. If learners are struggling, do one example on the board with everyone watching.

**Exercise 2.** This is the heart of the page. Watch for:

- Learners estimating spacing by eye instead of measuring with the ruler. Insist on the ruler at this stage.
- Learners measuring at a single point along a long contour rather than at the closest pair near the marked slope. Demonstrate.
- Learners forgetting to check the contour interval at the top of the extract. Make this the first thing they do.

**Exercise 3.** Tests the most common trap. If a learner does not write **contour interval** and **legend**, that is the gap to coach.

**Exercise 4.** The reflection should pull the spacing rule together with the avalanche-relevance from the "Why this matters" section. Learners do not need to mention all of it, but a strong answer should at least name the spacing-to-steepness link.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Treating spacing as universal.** A learner might apply the 3 mm rule to a Swisstopo 1:25,000 sheet with 20 m or 25 m contours and reach the wrong conclusion. Reinforce the legend-check habit.
- **Maths anxiety.** The atan calculation in the worked example can put learners off. Stress that the rule of thumb is what matters, not the maths.
- **Confusing slope angle with slope direction.** Steepness is about how fast height changes. Direction (aspect) comes later in C4. Keep the two ideas separate at this stage.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:** hand them a second extract at 1:50,000 (or with a 25 m interval) and ask them to derive the equivalent rule of thumb for that map. The answer is roughly 1.5 mm spacing for the 1:50,000 / 10 m case, or roughly 8 mm for the 1:25,000 / 25 m case.
- **For learners who need more support:** pair them with a confident learner for Exercise 2, then have them do the rule-of-thumb conversion independently in Exercise 3.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for C2 when they can:

- explain what a contour line represents,
- explain why contour interval varies between maps and why it matters,
- glance at a contour pattern and call gentle / moderate / steep / very steep without measuring,
- and apply the 30 degree rule of thumb on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m contours.
