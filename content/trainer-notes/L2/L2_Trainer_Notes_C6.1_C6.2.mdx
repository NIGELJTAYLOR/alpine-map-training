## Purpose of these sections

Section C6 turns the contour vocabulary built across Sections C2 to C5 into route thinking. C6.1 (Which Line Looks Easier?) is the constructive page: choose the best line up a slope, place kick turns, hold the gradient inside the comfortable skinning band. C6.2 (Spot the Terrain Trap) is the critical page: identify terrain that deserves extra caution, recognise committing terrain, and route around it.

The two pages are paired because they are the two halves of the same on-the-day decision: where to go and where not to. A candidate strong on C6.1 but weak on C6.2 picks efficient lines through dangerous ground. A candidate strong on C6.2 but weak on C6.1 avoids the dangerous ground but does not get up the mountain. Both halves are needed.

Section C6 stays inside the map-reading scope of the workbook. It does not teach formal avalanche curriculum, snow profiling, or rescue. That content belongs to the rest of the EMS pathway. C6 teaches the map-reading interpretation of route-relevant terrain.

## Coaching priorities

1. The comfortable skinning band as a deliberate constraint. 8 to 12 degrees as the working figure; 15 degrees as the upper limit on a sustained line.
2. The kick turn as a placement decision, not just a manoeuvre. Place on the gentlest available ground.
3. The line-types vocabulary: direct, contour-following, zig-zag. Most real lines mix all three.
4. The terrain-trap framing: features whose consequence is greater than their size suggests.
5. The configuration reading: a 30 degree slope is not a trap; a 30 degree slope above a cliff is. The combination produces consequence.
6. The hold-the-line on map-reading scope: avalanche curriculum is taught elsewhere.

## Teaching approach

Run C6.1 and C6.2 as a single 90-minute block, with a clear shift in the middle from "where to go" to "where not to go".

For C6.1, open with the constraint framing: a skin track holds best in 8 to 12 degrees. The route choice problem is to find a line that stays inside that band. Walk through the worked example on the board, calculating gradients for each of the three line types and demonstrating that the zig-zag with kick turns produces the sub-band gradient.

For C6.2, switch the framing: same map, different question. "Where would I not stand or pass through, and why?" The answer comes from configurations rather than features. Walk through the C6.2 worked example as a configuration read: 30-degree slope, plus convex roll, plus tight gully, plus rock walls, plus terminal basin. Each on its own is fine; together they are a textbook trap.

Throughout both pages, hold the discipline that the IMS / EMS-relevant skill is the reasoning, not the specific line. A learner who picks the right line for the wrong reason has not yet internalised the standard.

## Materials checklist

- Section C6.1 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain a slope of approximately 300 m vertical between two named points A and B, with three pre-sketched lines: a direct line, a contour-following line round the side, and a zig-zag line with three kick turns. The slope should be uniformly steep enough that the direct line sits at the upper end of the comfortable skinning band, forcing the zig-zag.
- Section C6.2 map extract at 1:25,000 with 10 m contour interval. The extract should contain at least four terrain-trap configurations (one narrow gully, one single-exit bowl, one slope-above-cliff, one convex-roll-into-gully), at least one runout zone, and at least one safer alternative line for Exercise 5.
- A combined Section C6 extract is acceptable if it contains all the required features.
- The avalanche-bulletin scenario language for Exercise 4 of C6.1 is provided in the page; no external bulletin needed for the workbook.
- Pencils (for sketching kick turns and shading terrain traps).
- An eraser.
- A whiteboard or flipchart for the worked examples.

## Delivery notes — C6.1

**Framing.** Open with the constraint: 8 to 12 degrees. The route choice problem is to find a line that stays inside that band. This single framing makes the rest of the page emerge naturally; without it, the line types and the kick-turn placement feel like arbitrary choices.

**Line types.** Direct, contour-following, zig-zag. Make sure the learner has the vocabulary before doing the exercises. The three types are not exclusive; most real lines mix them.

**Skin-track gradient figures.** The 8 to 12 degree comfortable band and the 15 degree upper limit are working figures, not hard rules. They vary with snow conditions, ski-skin combination and load. For workbook purposes, hold the figures; in the field, the candidate adjusts.

**Worked example.** Walk through it on the board with explicit gradient calculations. The arithmetic (300 m climb over 1,200 m horizontal = 14°; over 2,000 m horizontal = 9°) is what makes the line choice quantitative rather than intuitive.

**Exercise 1.** A measurement exercise. Watch for learners who eye-ball gradient instead of measuring contour spacing. Insist on the measurement.

**Exercise 2.** The justification exercise. The IMS / EMS-relevant skill is on this page. Hold the standard that the justification must reference the comfortable band, the total length, and at least one terrain feature. A justification of "it's the easiest" is not enough.

**Exercise 3.** The kick-turn placement is the BASI EMS-named skill. Push for specificity: each kick turn placed on a named feature (a bench, a slope-crest transition, a shoulder), not "somewhere along the line".

**Exercise 4.** The conditions-change exercise is where the route choice becomes adaptive. The first scenario (heavy fresh snow, danger 3, north aspect) is the test of whether the learner can recognise that the slope itself may be the wrong choice, not just the line on the slope. Coach this point if missed.

**Exercise 5.** The descent-vs-ascent exercise builds the asymmetry. Skin tracks favour gentle gradients; ski lines favour steeper ones. Most learners get this immediately; some assume the same line works both ways. Coach the asymmetry.

## Delivery notes — C6.2

**Framing.** Switch the question. Same map, but now: "Where would I not stand, and why?" The terrain-trap framing is constructive: it gives the learner a vocabulary for the dangerous ground rather than just "avoid steep slopes".

**Terrain trap, committing terrain, runout zone.** Three different concepts with overlapping but distinct meanings. Spend a minute each on the distinctions:

- **Terrain trap:** consequence is greater than slope angle suggests. Often a small slide that would be inconsequential on an open slope.
- **Committing terrain:** ground that cannot be reversed once entered. Not necessarily dangerous, but always worth recognising.
- **Runout zone:** flat ground at risk from terrain above. The standing-in-a-runout-zone point is counter-intuitive for some learners.

**Convex rolls.** Reading slope transitions is just contour-spacing reading. The new content is the route-relevance: a convex roll above a terrain trap is a textbook trigger configuration.

**Worked example.** Walk through the configuration read on the board. The point is to see that each individual feature (slope, roll, gully, walls, basin) is benign on its own; the combination is what produces consequence.

**Exercise 1.** A recognition exercise. The four standard traps on the extract are listed in the answer key. A learner who finds three out of four is at the working level; one who finds fewer than three needs to look again.

**Exercise 2.** Committing terrain is a smaller list than terrain traps. Watch for learners who confuse the two; not every terrain trap is committing, and not all committing terrain is a trap.

**Exercise 3.** The runout-zone exercise is the counter-intuitive one. Some learners describe a basin as safe because it is flat; the right reading is that the basin is the runout for the slope above. Coach this distinction.

**Exercise 4.** The convex-roll exercise is fast. Watch for learners who name a slope-shape change but cannot quantify the contour-spacing change. Insist on the measurement.

**Exercise 5.** The safer-alternative exercise is the planning skill the IMS / EMS assessor tests most directly. Push for the trade-off articulation: every safer alternative has a cost (length, ascent, aspect, exposure to a different feature). The candidate who can name the trade-off is the candidate who can defend the choice in an assessor's debrief.

## Common learner difficulties

- **Eye-balling gradient on C6.1.** The C1.2 contour-spacing rule is the discipline. Reset every time.
- **Picking the direct line because it is shortest.** Distance-minimisation is not the IMS / EMS standard. Coach the energy-cost argument.
- **Placing kick turns by aesthetics.** A kick turn should sit on the gentlest available ground, not at the visually neat spot.
- **Treating terrain features individually rather than as configurations.** A 30-degree slope is not a trap; a 30-degree slope above a cliff is. The configuration reading is the C6.2 diagnostic.
- **Standing in a runout zone because the ground is flat.** Counter-intuitive but predictable error. Coach the upstream-risk reading.
- **Refusing to identify a "no safer alternative" as an answer.** Some learners feel obliged to find an alternative even when none exists. The right IMS / EMS response to a no-good-line extract is to abandon the day's objective. Hold this point.
- **Drift into avalanche curriculum.** The page deliberately stops at map reading. If a learner asks about slab structure, weak layers, transceiver searches, or rescue, redirect to the EMS pathway material. The workbook does not teach those.

## Differentiation

- **For confident learners:**
  - In C6.1, ask them to plan a complete day: ascend slope by zig-zag, traverse the col, descend on a different line, return to the start. The day-planning is a Section C7 stretch task pulled forward.
  - In C6.2, ask them to identify the most dangerous configuration on the extract and rank the others against it. The relative-risk ranking is harder than the binary identification.
- **For learners who need more support:**
  - In C6.1, provide pre-calculated gradients for each line so they focus on the line-choice reasoning rather than the arithmetic.
  - In C6.2, walk through Exercise 1 trap by trap, showing them where to look. Then have them do Exercises 2 to 5 alone.

## Readiness to progress

The learner is ready for Section C7 (Level 2 consolidation) when they can:

- read line gradient against the comfortable skinning band as a deliberate constraint,
- place kick turns on the gentlest available ground,
- adapt route choice to changing conditions (snow, avalanche bulletin, surface),
- identify terrain traps, committing terrain and runout zones from contour patterns,
- recognise configurations rather than just individual features,
- plan a safer alternative line and articulate the trade-off,
- and recognise when no safer alternative exists.

If any of these are wobbly, recycle. Section C7 integrates everything; weak C6 produces weak C7.

## Reference notes for the trainer

The skin-track gradient band (8 to 12 degrees comfortable; 15 degrees upper limit) is consistent with mainstream ski-touring guidance, including BASI / New Generation EMS materials and the broader ski-touring literature. Specific figures vary slightly between sources (some give 10 to 15 as the comfortable band; some go down to 7 to 10); the 8 to 12 figure is a reasonable working consensus.

The terrain-trap and committing-terrain framing is consistent with the EAWS (European Avalanche Warning Services) terminology and with Mountaineering Scotland's published navigation guidance. The decision to treat these concepts at the map-reading level rather than the formal avalanche-curriculum level is deliberate and is documented in the workbook's scope conventions in `00_Research_Inputs/Sources.md` and the Project Status Log operating notes.

The kick-turn-as-placement-decision framing is consistent with the BASI EMS skill list ("route selection including kick turns and setting a skinning track"), which treats kick turns as a route-planning skill rather than just a technical manoeuvre.

Sources for this trainer note are recorded in `00_Research_Inputs/Sources.md`. No new claims are introduced beyond those covered in the Batch 2 primary-source sweep.
