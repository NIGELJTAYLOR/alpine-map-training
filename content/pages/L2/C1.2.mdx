### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to use the spacing between contour lines on a 1:25,000 alpine map to judge whether a slope is gentle, moderate, steep, or very steep, and you should know the rule of thumb for identifying slopes at 30 degrees or more.

### Why this matters

Slope steepness is the single biggest factor in alpine route choice. It governs:

- whether ground is safe to cross,
- whether it can be skinned up,
- whether it is in the avalanche release range,
- and whether the ground is travellable at all.

For BASI Level 4 IMS / EMS candidates, the ability to identify slopes at and above 30 degrees from the map alone is a core assessed skill.

### The core idea

The closer the contour lines are to one another, the steeper the ground.

To put it more carefully:

- **Wide spacing** indicates a gentle slope.
- **Medium spacing** indicates a moderate slope.
- **Close spacing** indicates a steep slope.
- **Merged or near-touching contours** indicate a cliff or extremely steep ground. Snow generally cannot hold a slab here, and it is usually too steep to travel.

This spacing-to-steepness link is the foundation of every later contour skill in this workbook.

### Why contour interval matters again

Spacing on the page only makes sense if you know the contour interval. Always check the legend before judging steepness.

The same physical 3 mm of spacing on a 1:25,000 map with a 10 m interval represents a steeper slope than the same 3 mm on a 1:50,000 map with a 20 m interval. Make checking the interval a habit.

### The 30 degree rule of thumb

On a 1:25,000 alpine map with a 10 m contour interval:

- Contours roughly **3 mm apart or closer** indicate a slope of approximately 30 degrees or more.
- This is the threshold at which avalanche slabs commonly release.
- This is the rule of thumb the IMS candidate needs to internalise.

The rule changes with contour interval. On a 1:25,000 map with a 25 m contour interval, the 30 degree threshold sits at roughly 8 mm spacing instead. Always check the legend.

### Worked example

A path on the map crosses six contour lines over a 12 mm map distance. With a 10 m contour interval, that is 60 m of height gain over (12 × 25) = 300 m of ground distance. The slope angle is approximately atan(60 / 300) ≈ 11 degrees. Gentle ground.

A second path crosses six contour lines over a 4 mm map distance. That is 60 m of height gain over 100 m of ground distance. The slope angle is approximately atan(60 / 100) ≈ 31 degrees. In the high avalanche risk band.
