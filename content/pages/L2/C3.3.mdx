### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to recognise plateaux on a 1:25,000 alpine map from their distinctive absence of contours, recognise cliff bands from merged or rock-symbol contours, distinguish the easiest and the hardest ground on an extract at a glance, and translate both patterns into a side profile and a verbal "if I stood here" description.

### Why this matters

Plateaux and cliff bands are the two extremes of alpine terrain. Plateaux are the easiest ground to walk on and the hardest to navigate in poor visibility; cliff bands are the easiest to spot and the hardest to descend. Reading both fluently is essential for:

- route planning that uses plateaux as recovery and decision points,
- route planning that avoids cliff bands or commits to known descents,
- relocation in poor visibility, where a plateau can produce dangerously few features and a cliff band can be a hard handrail,
- and avalanche awareness, where cliff bands often mark the boundary between manageable and committing terrain.

### The core idea — Plateaux

A plateau shows up on the map as **a high area with very widely spaced or absent contours**, often bounded by a sudden change in spacing where the ground falls away.

The signature is the contrast: contours close together on the bounding slopes, almost none on the plateau itself. Spot heights are usually present to mark the actual highest point because the broad flat top makes the precise peak ambiguous.

You met one form of this in C2.1 as the "plateau-like high". C3.3 covers it as a feature in its own right: a flat or near-flat alpine area with route-choice and navigation implications.

### The core idea — Cliff bands

A cliff band shows up on the map as **contours so close together they merge or are drawn as a single thick line, often with rock symbology overlaid**.

The signature is the merger: where individual contour lines cannot be picked out separately, the ground is steeper than the contour interval can resolve. On 1:25,000 alpine sheets with 10 m contours, this typically means slopes of 50 degrees or above, which is too steep to ski in most conditions and may be impassable on foot.

Most national mapping agencies overlay rock-feature symbols on cliff bands:

- **IGN TOP25 (France)**: black or dark grey rock-pattern hatching across the merged contours.
- **Swisstopo (Switzerland)**: a brown stippled pattern simulating rock faces.
- **Alpenvereinskarte (Austria)**: grey rock shading with prominent rock-edge symbols.

Always check the map legend for the specific symbol used. The combined cue is unmistakable: merged contours plus rock symbology equals cliff.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet with 10 m contours shows two features close together:

- **Feature P:** a roughly oval area approximately 1.2 km by 0.8 km, with no contour lines drawn within it, surrounded by close-spaced contours falling away on all sides; a single spot height at 2,140 m near the centre.
- **Feature Q:** a band approximately 200 m long and 30 m wide, where contours are drawn merged into a thick black line with rock-pattern hatching overlaid, sitting on the steepest part of the slope below the plateau.

Feature P is a **plateau** at 2,140 m. The flat top is bordered by steeper ground on all sides. The plateau itself is easy walking; the bounding slopes are not.

Feature Q is a **cliff band**. It sits on the slope below the plateau, marking the place where the ground transitions from "very steep" to "impassable on foot in normal conditions". A descent route from the plateau to the valley below would have to navigate around this band.
