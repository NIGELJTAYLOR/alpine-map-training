### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to look at any of the contour patterns you have met in C2.1, C2.2 and C2.3 and call the feature confidently and quickly. The aim of this page is fluency, not new content. If you find yourself thinking about each pattern for more than a few seconds, return to the relevant earlier page and re-do the exercise that gave you the pattern in the first place.

### Why this matters

Recognising a contour shape under time pressure is what separates classroom map reading from real route-choice. On a touring day the assessor will not ask you to define a feature. They will point at a contour pattern and expect a name, a direction of slope and a route-relevant comment within seconds. This page builds towards that fluency.

### How to use this page

The exercises on this page draw on three map extracts:

- the Section C2.1 extract (hills and summits),
- the Section C2.2 extract (valleys and gullies),
- the Section C2.3 extract (ridges and spurs).

Use whichever extracts your trainer has given you. If you only have one combined Section C2 extract that contains all the relevant features, use that.

The eight contour snippets in Exercise 1 below are labelled **1** to **8** on your trainer's snippet sheet. Each snippet is a small (roughly 4 cm by 4 cm) extract from a 1:25,000 alpine map with 10 m contour intervals.

### Worked example

Imagine a snippet labelled **0** showing closed contour loops, with the highest contour at the centre, loops 6 to 8 mm apart, and a spot height of 2,200 m at the centre.

Working through the C2.1 to C2.3 vocabulary:

- It shows closed loops with a high point at the centre, so it is in the **summit** family (C2.1).
- The loops are widely spaced (6 to 8 mm), which gives a **rounded summit** rather than a sharp peak.
- Confirmed by the absence of any V-shaped contour bend that would point to a valley, gully, ridge or spur.

The answer is **rounded summit**.

This is the level of internal commentary you should be running through for each of the eight snippets in Exercise 1. After a few snippets, the commentary should fall away and the answer should arrive on sight.

The C2.3 inverse-pairing rule is the key check: if the V points downhill on the contour, the sketch should show a crest, not a drainage line.

### Exercise 4 — Stretch task: preview of Section C3

Two of the snippets on your trainer's sheet (snippets **9** and **10**) show features you have not yet met. They are previews of two features taught formally in Section C3.

Without using the formal Section C3 vocabulary, do your best with the following:

1. Snippet 9 shows a low point sitting between two high points. The contours form a distinctive saddle shape. Sketch what you think the ground would look like from one of the two high points, looking towards the other.

   [Sketch space - approximately 8 cm by 5 cm]

2. Snippet 10 shows a section of the map where contours are merged or near-touching across a band 3 to 5 mm wide. Sketch what you think the ground would look like from a position one kilometre away on the slope below the band.

   [Sketch space - approximately 8 cm by 5 cm]

Do not worry about getting the names right. The two features are taught in C3.1 (cols and saddles) and C3.3 (cliff bands). The point of this stretch task is to give you a controlled first encounter with the contour pattern before the formal teaching, so that when C3 introduces the names you will already have the visual right.

### Self-check

- [ ] I can call summit, valley, gully, ridge or spur on sight from a 1:25,000 contour snippet within five seconds.
- [ ] I can distinguish sharp, rounded and plateau-like summits.
- [ ] I can distinguish broad valleys from tight gullies.
- [ ] I can distinguish long ridges from shorter spurs.
- [ ] I can match each snippet to its side-view sketch with confidence.
- [ ] I have identified at least one feature where my recognition is still slow or unreliable, and I have a specific plan to fix it.

### Why this matters for IMS

This page is the first sustained fluency exercise in the workbook. The IMS / EMS map reading competency expects candidates to identify contour features on sight on a 1:25,000 topographic map; a candidate who hesitates over basic shapes during route-card preparation or in-field relocation is a candidate who is not yet at assessment standard. The reflection in Exercise 3 is the diagnostic that turns this page from a quiz into a learning tool: it tells you exactly which earlier page to revisit before Section C3.
