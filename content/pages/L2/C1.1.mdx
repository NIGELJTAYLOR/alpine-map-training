### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should understand that contour lines show height and terrain shape, and that each contour joins points of equal elevation above sea level.

### Why contours matter

Contour lines are the most important tool in mountain navigation. They take you from reading symbols and map structure into understanding the actual shape of the land.

Without contours it is hard to judge whether a route crosses steep ground, follows a ridge, enters a valley, or climbs gradually. On an alpine map, the contour pattern is what tells you whether ground is travellable, whether a slope is in the avalanche release range, and where natural lines of ascent and descent lie.

### The core idea

A contour is a line that joins points of equal height above sea level.

That means:

- every point on the same contour line is at the same elevation,
- different contour lines show different heights,
- and together they build a picture of the land's shape.

### Contour interval

The contour interval is the vertical height difference between one contour line and the next. It varies by map type. Always check it from the map legend before using contours to judge anything.

Common alpine values:

- **France IGN 1:25,000** — typically 10 m contour interval.
- **Switzerland Swisstopo 1:25,000** — typically 20 m contour interval.
- **Austria Alpenvereinskarte** — varies by sheet; check the legend.

### Worked example

Imagine a hillside with contour lines at 100 m, 110 m, 120 m and 130 m. If you walk uphill and cross each line in order, your height is increasing 10 m at a time.

The map is not showing a wall or a path. It is showing levels of height.
