### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to look at a contour pattern and an aerial or oblique photograph of the same area, identify which photograph matches which contour pattern, and articulate the visual cues you used. You should also be able to identify recurring patterns where your visualisation differs from the real image so you can correct the bias.

### Why this matters

Maps are abstractions. A skilled candidate at IMS / EMS level reads the abstraction back into the real world automatically. C5.2 is the calibration page that turns the contour vocabulary you have built in Sections C2 to C4 into a reliable mental imagery generator.

The exercise here is also the antidote to a common over-confidence: most learners think their mental picture of a contour pattern is more accurate than it actually is. Comparing the mental picture with a real photograph reveals the systematic biases (most people draw mountains too steep and bowls too symmetrical, for example), and the comparison drives correction.

### How to use this page

The exercises on this page draw on the **Section C5.2 photo-matching set**, prepared by your trainer. The set contains:

- six contour snippets (numbered 1 to 6) at 1:25,000 with 10 m contours,
- four oblique photographs (lettered A, B, C and E) taken on a ski tour at the Hintertux Glacier, Austria, in April 2022,
- and a key, held by the trainer, showing the correct pairings.

This is a four-pair iteration of the set. Photographs D and F are deferred to a later refresh, so two of the six contour snippets will not have a matching photograph in this round. Your task is to match each of the four available photographs to the correct snippet.

Each photograph is taken from a known position with the camera roughly horizontal. The viewpoint coordinates are noted in the caption of each photograph in the digital workbook.

### Self-check

- [ ] I can match contour snippets to aerial or oblique photographs of the same area.
- [ ] I can name the visual cue I used for each match in contour-vocabulary terms.
- [ ] I have noticed at least one place where my mental image differed from the real ground, and I can articulate what to change.
- [ ] I can work backwards from a photograph to a sketch of the expected contour pattern.

### Why this matters for IMS

Calibrating mental imagery against real images is what produces a route-choice instinct. The IMS / EMS assessor will not test photo-matching directly, but candidates who have practised it produce more accurate route-card descriptions and more reliable in-field route adjustments. The skill is invisible at assessment but visible in everything the candidate does on the day.
