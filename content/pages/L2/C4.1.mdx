### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to look at any contour pattern and identify the direction of the downhill, draw an arrow showing the line of greatest descent (the fall line), and explain how you know.

### Why this matters

Reading direction of slope is the foundation of every later route-choice skill. Without it you cannot:

- read aspect (C4.2),
- judge fall-line ski descents,
- predict where water and avalanche debris run,
- choose ascent lines that follow gentler ground,
- or transcribe direction of travel onto a route card.

In Section C2 you read downhill informally as part of valley, gully, ridge and spur recognition. In Section C3 you read downhill again as part of bowl and col recognition. C4.1 takes the implicit skill and makes it the explicit subject of the page.

### The core idea

The direction of slope is given by the contour values:

- contours with **higher values** are uphill,
- contours with **lower values** are downhill,
- the line at right angles to the contours, pointing from high to low, is the **fall line**.

The fall line is the steepest line down the slope. It is the line water would take running off the slope. It is also the line a ball would roll down. On a uniform slope, every point has a single fall line.

### The fall line in practice

Two practical points to keep in mind:

- **The fall line is local.** It is the steepest descent at the point you are standing. Two points on the same slope can have slightly different fall-line directions if the slope curves.
- **The fall line is at right angles to the contour line.** This is the geometric definition. To find the fall line at a point, look at the contour line passing through that point; the fall line points downhill, perpendicular to the contour.

This perpendicular-to-contour rule is what makes the reading reliable. Eye-balling the downhill works on simple slopes; on complex slopes, drop a perpendicular from the contour and you have the right answer.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet with 10 m contours shows three contour lines on a slope, with values 2,500 m at the top, 2,490 m in the middle, and 2,480 m at the bottom of the visible section.

The downhill direction is from 2,500 m towards 2,480 m. At any point on the 2,490 m contour, the fall line runs perpendicular to that contour, pointing towards the 2,480 m contour.

If the contours run east-west across the slope on the page, the fall line runs north-south. Whether it points north or south depends on which side has the higher contour: if 2,500 m is to the north, the fall line points south; if 2,500 m is to the south, the fall line points north.

### Why this matters for IMS

Every aspect reading, every route choice, every fall-line ski descent and every avalanche-runout estimate at IMS / EMS level rests on a fluent reading of direction of slope. This page is short because the skill is short, but the IMS / EMS competency depends on it being instinctive.
