### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to read the aspect of any slope from a 1:25,000 alpine map in compass terms (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW), explain what aspect means and why it matters, and apply aspect reading to map an avalanche bulletin onto the terrain.

### Why this matters

Aspect is the single most useful piece of slope information after steepness. It governs:

- snow conditions (cold, dry, wind-loaded, sun-affected, melt-freeze),
- the avalanche bulletin (forecast danger is given by aspect and elevation band),
- the ski quality of a descent at any given time of day or season,
- the safety of a route choice in conditions where some aspects are dangerous and others are not,
- and the route-card translation of the bulletin into "go" and "no-go" terrain.

For a BASI Level 4 IMS / EMS candidate, fluent aspect reading is what turns a generic avalanche bulletin into a specific terrain decision.

### The core idea

The aspect of a slope is the **direction the slope faces**.

Equivalently, it is the direction of the fall line you found in C4.1: the slope faces in the direction it falls towards. A north-facing slope falls towards the north. A south-facing slope falls towards the south.

To read aspect from the map:

1. Find the fall line at the point of interest (using C4.1).
2. Identify the compass direction the fall line points in, using the map's north arrow.
3. Name that direction in cardinal or intercardinal terms.

For IMS / EMS-level work, **eight directions are usually enough**: N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW. Finer divisions (NNE, ENE, etc.) are sometimes used in avalanche bulletins; the workbook stays at eight directions for clarity, with a note on the finer divisions where they appear.

### Map north and grid north

For aspect reading on a topographic map, you read the fall line against the map's grid. Three norths exist:

- **True north.** The geographic North Pole. The notional reference for all maps.
- **Grid north.** The "up" direction of the map's grid lines. Usually within a degree of true north on the alpine sheets used at IMS / EMS.
- **Magnetic north.** Where a compass needle points. Differs from true north by the magnetic variation, which depends on location and date.

For aspect reading, **grid north is the practical reference**. Aspect read off the map is given to the nearest of the eight cardinal / intercardinal directions, and the difference between grid and true north is too small to change the answer at this resolution.

For compass bearings (Level 3 onwards), magnetic variation matters. For aspect at IMS / EMS, it does not.

(If your map area or your assessment requires it, check current magnetic variation for your map area and date using the map margin and an official calculator such as BGS WMM, NOAA / NCEI, or swisstopo. Aspect alone does not need this; bearings will.)

### Aspect and snow in the northern hemisphere

For a slope in the northern hemisphere:

- **North-facing slopes** receive the least direct sun in winter. They typically hold colder, drier, longer-lasting snow, with slower melt-freeze cycles and a higher risk of persistent weak layers.
- **South-facing slopes** receive the most direct sun. They typically have wetter, denser snow, more melt-freeze cycles, and are the first to spring-condition in late season.
- **East-facing slopes** receive morning sun and afternoon shade. Often the best skiing on a sunny mid-winter day.
- **West-facing slopes** receive afternoon sun and morning shade. Often more wind-affected because prevailing winter winds in the northern hemisphere alpine are typically from the west or north-west.

In the **southern hemisphere** (relevant if a candidate is using this workbook for a New Zealand, South American, or Antarctic context) the relationship inverts. South-facing becomes the cold, shaded, snow-holding aspect; north-facing becomes the sun-affected aspect.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet shows a slope in the northern hemisphere where:

- the contour values rise from 2,400 m at the bottom of the slope to 2,700 m at the top,
- the contour lines run roughly east to west across the page,
- the higher contours are on the south side of the page (towards the bottom in the conventional map orientation, but check the north arrow on the actual map),
- and grid north points up the page.

The fall line points from the higher contours towards the lower contours. Higher is south; lower is north. So the fall line points north. The slope **faces** north. The aspect is **north**.

Northern-hemisphere implications: this is a cold, shaded slope that holds dry snow. In winter the avalanche bulletin will treat north aspects as a likely persistent-weak-layer aspect.
