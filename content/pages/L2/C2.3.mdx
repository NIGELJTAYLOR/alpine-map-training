### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to recognise ridges and spurs on a 1:25,000 alpine map from their V or U shaped contour patterns pointing downhill, distinguish a long ridge from a short spur, identify which side of the crest falls away in which direction, and translate the contour pattern into a side profile and a verbal "if I stood here" description.

### Why this matters

Ridges and spurs are the high-ground equivalent of valleys and gullies. They are some of the most useful features on an alpine map because:

- they are linear features that can be followed in poor visibility,
- they are often safer skinning and ascent lines because they shed snow rather than collect it,
- they give you commanding views for relocation,
- they are predictable: if you know the line of the crest, you know which way the ground falls on each side,
- and they let you avoid travelling under or through avalanche-prone slopes.

For the IMS / EMS candidate, fluent ridge and spur reading is what turns a flat map into a viable route choice.

### The core idea

Ridges and spurs show up on the map as **contour lines that bend into a V or U shape, with the V or U pointing downhill**.

This is the direct inverse of the valley pattern in C2.2.

The clearest test is to look at the contour values:

- If the contour values rise as you move towards the point of the V, the V points uphill and the feature is a valley or gully.
- If the contour values fall as you move towards the point of the V, the V points downhill and the feature is a ridge or spur.

This single check resolves almost every confusion between the two shapes. Train yourself to do it before naming the feature.

### Ridge vs spur

Ridges and spurs are the same family of feature on different scales:

- **Ridge.** A long high line connecting high points, often forming the crest between two valleys. On the map a ridge runs across the sheet as a continuous line of paired contour bends, sometimes for several kilometres. The ridge is itself the watershed.
- **Spur.** A shorter projection of high ground that runs out from a larger mass, often from a summit, a ridge, or the side of a high plateau. On the map a spur is a single V or U pointing downhill from a higher feature, often only a few hundred metres long, ending where the contour bend is no longer recognisable.

Every spur belongs to a parent feature. Every ridge is the parent of multiple spurs. Once you start reading them this way, the high ground on a map organises itself into a small number of named lines instead of a wash of orange contours.

### Which side falls where

A ridge or spur is a crest. From the crest, ground falls away on both sides.

To work out which direction the ground falls on each side:

1. Stand mentally on the line of the crest, facing along it towards the point of the V (downhill end).
2. The contours immediately to your left fall away to the left.
3. The contours immediately to your right fall away to the right.
4. The aspect of each side is determined by the direction the slope faces, not the direction the crest runs.

This is the foundation of aspect reading, which is treated formally in C4.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 IGN sheet with 10 m contours shows a contour pattern in which:

- contours bend into a clear V shape,
- the point of the V is at 2,200 m,
- the contours upslope of the bend rise to 2,380 m,
- the bend covers about 5 mm of map width,
- and the V projects out from a larger summit.

That is a **spur**. The crest line drops from 2,380 m down towards 2,200 m. Standing on the crest and facing the 2,200 m end, the ground falls away on both your left and your right sides; the steepness of each side is given by the contour spacing on each side independently.

A second pattern shows:

- a continuous line of paired contour bends running across most of the map,
- spot heights at intervals along the line,
- the line dividing two large drainage systems,
- and the contour values falling on both sides of the line.

That is a **ridge**. It is the watershed between the two drainage systems and the natural high-ground line for any route that wants to stay out of the valleys.

### Why this matters for IMS

This page directly supports the IMS / EMS map reading competency: identify all key contour features (spur, re-entrant / valley, col, convex roll, concave slope, cliff) on a 1:25,000 topo map. Spur and ridge recognition also underpins route selection, since they are the safest natural ascent lines in many alpine settings and the primary handrails for poor-visibility navigation. The aspect-of-each-side reading is a foundation for the avalanche-aware terrain interpretation tested at EMS assessment.
