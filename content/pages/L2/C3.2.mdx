### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to recognise bowls, corries and basins on a 1:25,000 alpine map from their distinctive contour pattern, distinguish the headwall from the side flanks, predict where snow is likely to accumulate, and translate the contour pattern into a side profile and a verbal "if I stood here" description.

### Why this matters

Bowls, corries and basins are some of the most route-significant features in alpine terrain. They are:

- the natural collection points for wind-loaded snow,
- the prime terrain in which avalanches release and run,
- often the most attractive ski lines on the descent (open, sustained, fall-line),
- and a well-known terrain trap when conditions are wrong, because the bowl shape funnels moving snow towards the lower exit and offers few safe lines once you are inside.

For a BASI Level 4 IMS / EMS candidate, the ability to read a bowl from the map and predict its loading and runout is a foundation of avalanche-aware route choice.

### The core idea

A bowl shows up on the map as **a half-cup or U-shape of contours that opens downhill, with the highest contour forming the back of the bowl and the contours fanning out and falling away as the bowl opens**.

A simpler test:

- the back of the bowl (the **headwall**) is where contours are tightly packed and curved, often arc-shaped,
- the flanks of the bowl (the **sides**) are where contours run more or less parallel to the fall line,
- the floor and exit of the bowl (the **mouth**) is where contours space out and the U opens downhill.

The bowl is enclosed on three sides (the headwall and two flanks) and open on the fourth (the mouth, falling away to lower ground).

Three terms describe the same family of feature with slightly different connotations:

- **Bowl.** The general alpine term, used widely in skiing and ski-touring.
- **Corrie.** Scottish / Gaelic for the same feature; common in UK navigation literature, including Mountaineering Scotland's terrain vocabulary. The Welsh equivalent is "cwm".
- **Cirque.** French and standard scientific term, common on IGN maps and in alpine geomorphology. A "cirque glaciaire" is a corrie that has been carved by a former glacier.
- **Basin.** Broader and shallower than a corrie; often used for larger, less steep enclosed areas higher up.

For map work, treat all four as the same family. Pay attention to size and steepness rather than the name.

### Where snow accumulates

Bowls collect snow in two characteristic ways:

- **Wind-loading.** Snow blown over the headwall lip from the upwind side is deposited on the lee side, building cornices on the rim and a thicker pillow on the upper bowl.
- **Gravitational settling.** Snow falling on the upper slopes slides or sloughs into the bowl, deepening the snowpack at the back and building a smoother surface in the middle.

The combined effect is that the **upper part of the bowl** (just below the headwall) is typically deeper, more wind-affected and more avalanche-prone than the lower mouth. This is what makes the bowl a terrain trap: the easiest ski line is also the part of the slope where the snow is most loaded.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet with 10 m contours shows the following pattern:

- contours form a clear arc opening to the south,
- the back of the arc (the headwall) sits between 2,800 m and 2,900 m, with contours roughly 1 to 2 mm apart,
- the flanks of the arc fall away on the east and west sides, with contours 4 to 6 mm apart,
- the mouth opens at around 2,500 m onto a broad, gently sloping basin below,
- and the floor of the bowl shows widely spaced contours and no visible water symbol.

That is a **bowl** (or "corrie", or "cirque", depending on which map and which guide you read). The headwall is steep enough to trigger your 30 degree alarm from C1.2 and likely above the prime release angle. The flanks are moderate. The bowl faces south, which means in the northern hemisphere it receives strong winter sun and may have melt-freeze cycles affecting the snowpack.

### Why this matters for IMS

This page directly supports the IMS / EMS competencies in route selection and avalanche-aware terrain interpretation. Bowls are the classic combination of attractive ski terrain and elevated avalanche risk, and the assessor expects the candidate to read bowl shape, headwall steepness and aspect from the map fluently before stepping onto the snow. The 30 degree threshold from C1.2 is most often crossed on the headwall of a bowl.
