### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to look at any contour pattern on a 1:25,000 alpine map and identify terrain that deserves extra caution: terrain traps, committing terrain, runout zones, and configurations where a small problem can become a large one. You should also be able to suggest a safer alternative line where one is available, or recognise when no safe line exists.

### Why this matters

The map-reading skill is not only "where to go". It is also "where not to go", and "where the ground will not let you back out if it goes wrong". The IMS / EMS assessor expects candidates to read the second skill as fluently as the first, especially on the descent and especially in conditions where the avalanche bulletin or the local snowpack is uncertain.

This page does not teach formal avalanche curriculum. It teaches the map-reading interpretation of terrain features that have route-relevant safety significance. Formal avalanche education (slab structure, weak layers, rescue) sits outside this workbook.

### Terrain traps

A **terrain trap** is a piece of ground where a small avalanche or fall produces disproportionate consequences. Three classic patterns:

- **Narrow gully.** Already met in C2.2. The narrow channel concentrates moving snow; the gully often ends in a terminal cone or a cliff. A small slide that would be inconsequential on an open slope can be life-threatening in a tight gully.
- **Bowl with a single exit.** Already met in C3.2. A bowl that opens onto a narrow mouth funnels everything (skiers and snow) towards a single point. If the mouth is constricted by rock or a cliff band, a slide that runs the bowl produces a terminal cone with no outflow.
- **Slope above a cliff band.** Already met in C3.3. A slope that ends in merged contours plus rock symbology is committing: a slide on the slope cannot run out gently. The same applies to a slide on the slope above a tight gully or above a bowl with a constricted exit.

Each of these has been named individually in earlier sections. C6.2 is where they are read as terrain traps, that is, as features whose consequence is greater than their size suggests.

### Committing terrain

**Committing terrain** is ground that cannot be reversed once entered. Classic patterns:

- **Slope above a cliff.** Once you ski onto the slope, the cliff below means you cannot stop and walk back up. The slope must be skied through.
- **Bowl with a single mouth.** Once you ski into the bowl, the mouth is the only exit. If conditions change inside the bowl, you have to ski out through the mouth.
- **Tight gully on the descent.** Once you commit to the line, exiting laterally may be impossible. The gully has to be skied through.

Committing terrain is not necessarily dangerous. A clean cliff with a known exit and stable snow is committing but skiable. The point is to recognise the commitment so you can decide consciously whether you accept it, rather than discovering it halfway through.

### Runout zones

A **runout zone** is the area where avalanche debris from above would come to rest. On a 1:25,000 map, runout zones are predictable from the contour pattern:

- below a steep avalanche-prone slope, where the gradient eases out (look for the contour spacing widening below a tight band),
- in the floor of a bowl, where the mouth opens onto a flatter basin,
- in the lower part of a gully where it widens onto a debris fan,
- and in any location where a slide from above could reach the ground you are standing on, including across a flat traverse below a steep slope.

A learner standing in a runout zone is at risk from terrain they are not on. This is why the bulletin-to-terrain mapping in C4.2 included aspect-and-elevation: heightened danger on a 35-degree north slope above 2,200 m affects you even on the 5-degree flat below it.

### Convex rolls and slope transitions

A **convex roll** is a slope shape where the gradient increases as you descend. On the map, contour spacing tightens going downhill across the roll. Convex rolls concentrate stress at the steepest section and are common avalanche-trigger points.

The opposite shape, a **concave slope**, has gradient decreasing as you descend. Concave slopes spread stress and tend to be less reactive.

Reading slope transitions on a map is the same skill as reading contour spacing. Where the spacing tightens or widens going downhill, the slope shape changes.

For route choice, the take-away is: **a convex roll above a terrain trap is a configuration that deserves explicit caution**, even on a stable day. Reading the configuration from the map gives you the chance to plan around it.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet with 10 m contours shows the following pattern:

- a slope at approximately 30 degrees descending from 2,400 m to 2,200 m,
- below 2,200 m the contour spacing tightens further, indicating a steeper section to 2,150 m,
- below 2,150 m a 20 m wide gully with merged side-wall contours runs for 300 m down to 1,900 m,
- the gully narrows further at 2,000 m where rock symbology appears on both walls,
- and the gully terminates at 1,900 m on a flat basin with a stream symbol.

That configuration is a textbook terrain trap. From top to bottom:

1. The 30-degree slope at the top is in the avalanche release range.
2. The slope steepens below 2,200 m (a convex roll), concentrating stress at the transition.
3. The slope drops into a tight gully with rock walls.
4. The gully narrows further before the runout.
5. The runout is the basin at 1,900 m.

Even on a stable day, a skier on the upper slope is committing to a line that ends in the gully. On an unstable day, the configuration is the kind a candidate should avoid by choosing a different line entirely. A safer alternative might be a line down a wider open slope with a flat runout, even at the cost of extra skinning to access it.

### Why this matters for IMS

Identifying terrain traps and committing terrain from the map is the prerequisite for the avalanche-aware route choice that the BASI EMS assessment expects. The page deliberately stops short of formal avalanche curriculum (snow profiling, rescue, slab analysis) because that content is taught elsewhere on the EMS pathway and falls outside the map-reading scope of this workbook. What this page does is build the map-reading half of the conversation: the candidate looks at the terrain on paper and recognises where the consequences are greater than the slope angle alone would suggest.

After C6.2 the workbook moves to Section C7 (consolidation), where the route-choice and terrain-trap skills built here are integrated with everything that came before.
