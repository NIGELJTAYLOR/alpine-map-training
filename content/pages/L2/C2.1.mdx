### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to recognise hills and summits on a 1:25,000 alpine map from their closed-loop contour patterns, distinguish three common summit shapes (sharp peak, rounded summit, plateau-like high), and read summit height accurately from contours and spot heights.

### Why this matters

Summits and high points are key reference features in alpine navigation. They are often:

- the turning point on a tour,
- the obvious goal,
- the rendezvous point at the top of a skinned ascent,
- or the unambiguous landmark used to relocate when other features are unclear.

For a BASI Level 4 IMS / EMS candidate, recognising summits quickly from the map is a building block of route identification, which is one of the assessed competencies.

### The core idea

A hill or summit shows up on the map as a set of **closed contour loops** with the highest contour at the centre.

If you imagine pouring water onto the page, water at the centre of a closed loop has nowhere lower to flow to. The centre is the highest point.

### Reading height from the map

Two cues confirm the height of a summit:

- The **centre contour value**. This is the highest contour drawn on the loop set.
- A **spot height**. This is a small dot or cross with a precise elevation number printed beside it. Spot heights are more accurate than the centre contour because they mark the actual highest point, which usually sits between two contour lines.

On a 1:25,000 alpine map, almost every named summit will carry a spot height. Use the spot height as the precise figure; use the centre contour to confirm the general elevation band.

### Three common summit shapes

A summit is not always a sharp peak. Three patterns are worth recognising on sight:

- **Sharp peak.** Tightly spaced closed loops with a single, clear highest point. Steep on most or all sides. Common in the higher alpine. The contour spacing here will usually trigger your 30 degree alarm from C1.2.
- **Rounded summit.** Widely spaced loops, often with a broad central area. The high point can feel ambiguous on the ground. Common on grass-covered or weathered tops.
- **Plateau-like high.** Very widely spaced or even absent contours across a flat area, sometimes with a small spot height marking the actual peak. Common on Austrian Alpine plateaux and broad shoulders.

### Worked example

On a 1:25,000 alpine map you see three closed-loop patterns near each other:

- **Loop set A** has loops 1 to 2 mm apart, with the highest contour at 2,840 m and a spot height of 2,847 m. This is a **sharp peak**.
- **Loop set B** has loops 6 to 8 mm apart with the highest contour at 2,200 m. This is a **rounded summit**.
- **Loop set C** has a single closed loop at 1,920 m surrounding a 0.5 km wide nearly contour-free area, with a spot height of 1,932 m. This is a **plateau-like high**.

### Why this matters for IMS

This page underpins the IMS / EMS competency: identify all key contour features (spur, re-entrant, col, convex roll, concave slope, cliff) on a 1:25,000 topo map. Summit recognition is also a foundation for route identification beyond the ski area boundary, where high points are commonly used as turning points and rendezvous markers on the route card.
