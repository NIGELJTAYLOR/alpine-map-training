### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to recognise cols and saddles on a 1:25,000 alpine map from their contour pattern, distinguish a col from a summit and from a valley, read the height of the col accurately, and explain why cols are the natural crossing points between two valleys. You should also be able to translate the contour pattern into a side profile and a verbal "if I stood here" description.

### Why this matters

A col is a low point on a ridge, sitting between two summits. In navigation terms it is the doorway between two valleys. In a working alpine route, cols are:

- the natural crossing points from one drainage system to another,
- the easiest place to climb from one side of a ridge to the other,
- a recognisable landmark in poor visibility because of the distinctive saddle shape on the skyline,
- a target for the assessor's "where would you cross this ridge?" question on a route card,
- a feature where wind funnelling and snow build-up are common, with implications for route choice and avalanche awareness.

For a BASI Level 4 IMS / EMS candidate, fluent col reading is what turns a map full of summits and ridges into a map full of route options.

### The core idea

A col shows up on the map as a **low point on a ridge**, with rising contours on both sides leading up to two summits.

The clearest signature is a contour pattern that looks like a saddle:

- on two opposite sides, contours rise to higher ground (the two summits),
- on the other two opposite sides, contours fall away to lower ground (the two valleys the col joins),
- and at the centre, a small open area where neither pattern dominates.

If you imagine pouring water onto the col, water at the centre has two directions it could flow (towards either valley) and two directions it cannot flow (towards either summit). That is the saddle.

The terms "col", "saddle" and "pass" overlap in everyday alpine vocabulary:

- **Col** is the standard map term for the low point itself.
- **Saddle** describes the same feature but emphasises the shape rather than the function. The two are interchangeable in most contexts.
- **Pass** describes the col when it is used as a route. A col becomes a pass when it is the chosen crossing.

For map work, treat col and saddle as the same feature. Use "pass" when the route card has chosen to cross there.

### Reading the height of the col

A col has a specific height: the elevation of the lowest contour line that crosses the saddle.

Two cues confirm this:

- The **lowest contour value** at the centre of the saddle. This is the highest contour you can stand on while still being able to walk in any of the four cardinal directions of the saddle (towards either summit, towards either valley).
- A **spot height** at the col itself. Many named cols carry a spot height; on French IGN sheets the col is often labelled with its name and elevation alongside the spot.

Use the spot height as the precise figure, and confirm with the surrounding contour pattern. The col height is what goes onto the route card as the high point of the leg if the route crosses there.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet with 10 m contours shows the following pattern:

- a continuous ridge running north to south,
- a summit at the north end at 2,840 m,
- a summit at the south end at 2,920 m,
- between the two summits a clear saddle, with the lowest contour through the saddle at 2,720 m, and a spot height at the centre marked 2,718 m,
- to the west of the saddle, contours fall steadily into a valley with a stream symbol at 2,400 m,
- to the east of the saddle, contours fall less steeply into a different valley at 2,500 m.

That is a **col** at 2,718 m. The two summits are 122 m and 202 m higher than the col respectively. The col joins the western valley at 2,400 m to the eastern valley at 2,500 m. As a pass, it would be the natural crossing point between those two drainage systems and would save approximately 200 to 300 m of climbing compared with going over either summit.

### Why this matters for IMS

This page directly supports the IMS / EMS map reading competency: identify all key contour features (spur, re-entrant / valley, col, convex roll, concave slope, cliff) on a 1:25,000 topo map. Cols are also the route-card hinge point on most ski-touring days that involve crossing a ridge; the assessor expects the candidate to choose cols over summits where the climbing budget allows, to read col height accurately for the route card, and to factor wind exposure and snow accumulation at the col into the avalanche-aware terrain interpretation.
