### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to mark any point on a 1:25,000 alpine map and produce a sustained four-to-six-sentence description of what you would expect to see standing at that point, without external scaffolding. The description should cover steepness, aspect, neighbouring high and low ground, and the direction of the main slope, in the vocabulary built across Sections C1 to C4.

### Why this matters

This page is the consolidated form of the "if I stood here" prompt that has appeared on every named-feature page since C2.1. There it was a short three-sentence prompt embedded in a longer exercise. Here it is the entire exercise, sustained and unscaffolded.

The point is not to produce creative writing. It is to produce a structured, accurate, repeatable description that:

- could be transcribed into a route-card description column,
- could be read out to a second person as a position fix,
- and could be checked against the ground when you arrive.

This is the working speech of an IMS / EMS candidate on the move. C5.3 is where it becomes sustained.

### Structure for the description

Use the following four-part structure. Each description should cover all four:

1. **Underfoot.** The ground immediately around you (flat, sloping, narrow, broad, snow surface, rock, vegetation if visible from the map).
2. **Slope and aspect.** The direction the ground falls, expressed in compass terms; the steepness in the C1.2 vocabulary (gentle, moderate, steep, very steep).
3. **Near features.** What you would see within a few hundred metres: the nearest summit, ridge, valley, col, bowl, cliff band, or other named feature.
4. **Far features.** What you would see in the wider view: a distant skyline, a recognisable peak, a major valley, a town or hut visible on the map.

Four sentences is the minimum; six sentences gives you room for two near features or a more nuanced steepness description.

### Worked example

Imagine you are standing at a point on a 1:25,000 alpine sheet marked at 2,300 m, on the northern flank of a spur projecting east-north-east from a 2,650 m summit. The contours around you are spaced at approximately 5 mm. To the south the ground rises towards the summit; to the north it falls away into a broad valley with a stream at 1,950 m. About 800 m to the north-east, a ridge rises again to 2,500 m; beyond that ridge, on the far horizon, the spot height for a major peak at 3,250 m is just visible.

A four-sentence description in the structured form:

1. **Underfoot.** I am standing on a moderate snow slope on the northern flank of a spur, with the ground falling away below me and rising up to my left.
2. **Slope and aspect.** The slope falls north, at approximately 18 to 22 degrees on the C1.2 rule of thumb, well below the 30 degree avalanche threshold.
3. **Near features.** The spur crest is about 60 m above me to the south; the broad valley with the stream is 350 m below me to the north.
4. **Far features.** Across the valley, a ridge rises to 2,500 m; on the horizon beyond, a peak at 3,250 m is just visible.

That is the working voice the workbook is building towards.

### Why this matters for IMS

The IMS / EMS assessor's most common in-field test is "describe what you see from here". The candidate who replies in the structured four-part form sounds professional, is hard to catch out, and produces a response the assessor can actually check against the ground. The candidate who replies "there's a slope and some mountains over there" sounds untrained and is.

C5.3 is where the workbook builds the structured voice. The page is the consolidated form of every "if I stood here" prompt back to C2.1 and is the bridge into Section C6's route-thinking work.
