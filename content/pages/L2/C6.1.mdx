### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to look at any slope on a 1:25,000 alpine map, sketch two or three possible lines up or down it, justify your choice of preferred line using contour spacing and terrain shape, and explain why a touring skin track typically sits in a specific gradient band rather than following the direct fall line.

### Why this matters

Route choice on a slope is the most common decision on a touring day. It is the decision the IMS / EMS assessor watches most closely. The candidate who picks lines well:

- skins efficiently up gradients that hold grip,
- spaces kick turns to manage steep transitions,
- avoids unnecessary exposure to avalanche-prone aspects,
- arrives at cols and summits with energy left,
- and produces a route card that reads as deliberate rather than improvised.

The candidate who picks lines badly does the opposite. The map-reading work in Sections C2 to C5 is the foundation; C6.1 is where the foundation becomes a decision.

### The core idea

A route line is not just a line. It is a series of choices about gradient, exposure, distance and terrain features. Three line types appear in almost every touring day:

- **Direct line.** The straightest path between two points. Shortest distance. Often the steepest gradient. Useful for short legs or descents on safe ground; rarely the right ascent line on a sustained slope.
- **Contour-following line.** A line that traverses across the slope rather than climbing it. Long but gentle. Useful for entering or exiting a feature without losing or gaining height.
- **Zig-zag line.** The standard skinning-track shape on sustained slopes. A series of traverses linked by kick turns. Manages gradient on the traverse legs; concentrates the steep moments at the kick turns themselves, where the skis are stationary.

Most touring lines are a mix of all three. The art of route choice is choosing which type to use where.

### Skin-track gradient

A skinned skin track holds best in a specific gradient band. Typical figures across published touring guidance are:

- **Comfortable skinning gradient: 8 to 12 degrees.** Most efficient for sustained climbing.
- **Acceptable upper limit: around 15 degrees** on a single traverse leg, with kick turns to break the gradient.
- **Above 15 degrees on a sustained line:** skin grip becomes unreliable, edge engagement increases, the track tires the legs faster.

These figures are guides, not rules. Snow conditions, ski-skin combination, and the carry weight all shift the bands. But for map work the take-away is: **a skin track that climbs at more than 15 degrees in a sustained way is the wrong line**. The route choice problem is to find a line that holds the gradient inside the band.

On a 1:25,000 alpine map with 10 m contours, the C1.2 rule of thumb gives:

- 8 to 12 mm contour spacing: comfortable skinning gradient,
- 6 to 8 mm contour spacing: at the upper end of comfortable,
- 5 mm or closer: too steep for a sustained skin track; needs zig-zags or a different line.

### The zig-zag and the kick turn

A zig-zag line trades length for gradient. By traversing across the slope rather than up it, the skinner adds horizontal distance and reduces gradient. The kick turn is the manoeuvre that changes direction at the end of each traverse leg.

Two quality criteria for a zig-zag line:

- **Each traverse leg should sit inside the comfortable gradient band.** A zig-zag that traverses at 20 degrees is barely better than a direct line; the point is a sub-15-degree leg.
- **Kick turns should sit on the gentlest available ground.** A kick turn on a steep concave roll is awkward and slow; on a gentle bench it is quick. Map-reading lets you place the kick turns deliberately.

The kick-turn placement decision is the one BASI / EMS materials specifically call out as a route-card-relevant skill. Practice it deliberately.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet with 10 m contours shows a slope between two points: A at 1,800 m at the bottom and B at 2,100 m at the top. The horizontal distance from A to B is approximately 1,200 m. The slope is uniformly steep, with contours at approximately 4 mm spacing throughout.

A direct line from A to B would climb 300 m over 1,200 m horizontal distance: an average gradient of approximately 14 degrees. That is at the upper end of the comfortable skinning band. The line would also be very long-feeling because the gradient is sustained.

A zig-zag line with three traverses would convert the 1,200 m horizontal distance into approximately 2,000 m of skin-track distance, but each traverse leg would climb only 100 m over approximately 670 m of skin-track length: an average gradient of approximately 8 to 9 degrees. Three kick turns would be needed; placing them on the gentlest sections of the slope (where contour spacing is locally wider) keeps them efficient.

The right choice is the zig-zag. The total horizontal distance is the same; the energy cost is much lower because the gradient is in the comfortable band throughout.

### Why this matters for IMS

Setting a skinning track and executing kick turns is a named skill on the BASI EMS training course content. Choosing route lines that respect the comfortable skinning band, place kick turns deliberately, and adapt to conditions is the working substance of the IMS / EMS route-card preparation. After this page, the candidate has the contour-reading vocabulary plus a deliberate process for turning that vocabulary into a route choice.
