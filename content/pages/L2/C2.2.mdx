### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to recognise valleys and gullies on a 1:25,000 alpine map from their V or U shaped contour patterns, predict the direction of water flow from the contours, distinguish a broad valley from a tight gully, and translate the contour pattern into a side profile and a verbal "if I stood here" description.

### Why this matters

Valleys and gullies are the drainage lines of the mountain. On the map they tell you:

- where water and snow naturally collect,
- where ascent and descent lines tend to be funnelled,
- where avalanche debris is most likely to run and stop,
- where you would find a clear linear feature to follow in poor visibility,
- and where a route can be exposed to overhead terrain even when the line itself looks gentle.

Mountaineering Scotland's navigation guidance is explicit on the point that valleys, streams and gullies count as linear features that can be followed even in poor visibility or at night. For an IMS / EMS candidate, that is a working tool, not a piece of trivia.

### The core idea

Valleys and gullies show up on the map as **contour lines that bend into a V or U shape, with the V or U pointing uphill**.

That last word is the one to remember. The point of the V points towards higher ground; the open side of the V points towards lower ground. Water flows from the point of the V outwards, downhill.

This is the inverse of the spur pattern you will meet in C2.3, where the V points downhill.

### Reading the drainage direction

To work out which way water flows in a valley:

1. Find the contour bend (the V or U).
2. Identify the highest contour value at the point of the V.
3. Identify the lowest contour value at the open end of the V.
4. Water flows from the point of the V towards the open end, from high to low.

If the map shows a blue line running through the contour bends, that is the visible stream and it confirms the direction. On dry ground or ground covered by snow, the contour shape alone tells you the same thing.

### Valley vs gully

Valleys and gullies are the same family of feature on different scales:

- **Valley.** A broad drainage feature, often with a flat or near-flat floor, gentle to moderate side walls, and a stream or river along the bottom. On the map the V or U is wide and the bend covers many millimetres of map distance.
- **Gully.** A narrow, steep-sided drainage feature, typically with no flat floor and very tight side walls. On the map the V is sharp, the contours that form the V are close together, and the feature often cuts directly across a slope.

In avalanche terms, gullies behave very differently from broad valleys. A gully concentrates moving snow into a narrow channel; a broad valley spreads it across a wide runout. This is why a candidate working at IMS / EMS level needs to read the difference at a glance.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 IGN sheet with 10 m contours shows a contour pattern in which:

- contours bend into a clear V shape,
- the point of the V is at 2,180 m,
- the open end of the V is at 2,040 m,
- the bend covers about 6 mm of map width,
- and a thin blue line runs through the bend.

That is a **valley**. Water flows from 2,180 m down towards 2,040 m. The 6 mm width and the blue stream tell you the floor is broad enough to walk along comfortably.

A second pattern on the same sheet shows:

- contours bend into a tight, sharp V,
- the point is at 2,520 m,
- the open end is at 2,460 m,
- the bend covers only 1 to 2 mm of map width,
- and the contours that form the V are 2 mm apart on the side walls.

That is a **gully**. Water flows from 2,520 m down towards 2,460 m. The narrow width and the close side-wall contours tell you the feature is steep and tight, and that any moving snow on the slope above will be funnelled into it.

This page directly supports the IMS / EMS map reading competency: identify all key contour features (spur, re-entrant / valley, col, convex roll, concave slope, cliff) on a 1:25,000 topo map. It also supports the route-selection competency, because gully recognition is a building block of avalanche-aware terrain interpretation.
