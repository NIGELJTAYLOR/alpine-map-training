### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to draw a cross-section line across any contour pattern on a 1:25,000 alpine map, project the contour crossings onto a side-profile sketch beneath the line, choose a sensible vertical scale and vertical exaggeration, and read your finished side profile back as a representation of the actual ground.

### Why this matters

The cross-section technique is the most reliable way to convert a contour pattern into a quantitative side view. It is the technique that:

- turns a wash of orange contour lines into a recognisable mountain silhouette,
- lets you compare two route options on the same axis,
- supports route-card preparation for a tour with significant ascent or descent,
- and gives you a piece of paper you can hand to someone else to explain the day's terrain.

For a BASI Level 4 IMS / EMS candidate, the cross-section is the bridge between the contour vocabulary built in Sections C1 to C4 and the route-thinking work that begins in Section C6. It is also the technique that closes the 2D-to-3D gap most directly: there is no eye-balling, no interpretation; the contour data goes onto an axis and a profile emerges.

### The core idea

A cross-section is the side view you would see if you cut the mountain along a chosen line and looked at the cut face.

To build one from a map:

1. Draw a straight line on the map between two points (the **section line**).
2. Note where the section line crosses each contour.
3. Below the map, draw a horizontal axis the same length as the section line on the map.
4. Above that axis, draw a vertical axis with elevation marked at the contour interval (10 m for most IGN sheets).
5. For each contour crossing on the section line, drop a vertical line down to the matching elevation on the vertical axis.
6. Connect the points smoothly to produce the side profile.

That is the technique. The principle is just that of plotting (distance, height) points and joining them up.

### Horizontal scale

The horizontal scale of a cross-section should match the map. On a 1:25,000 map, 1 cm on the section line equals 250 m on the ground, so 1 cm on the cross-section's horizontal axis also equals 250 m. Keep the two scales locked.

A common error is to draw the cross-section at a different horizontal scale and then misread the slope angles. Always use the same horizontal scale as the map.

### Vertical scale and vertical exaggeration

The vertical axis is where most learners make a choice. Two standard options:

- **True scale (1:25,000 vertically as well as horizontally).** Mathematically honest, but produces a cross-section that looks almost flat because alpine relief is small relative to horizontal distance. A 600 m climb over 2 km looks like a gentle ramp at true scale.
- **Vertically exaggerated (typically 2:1 or 3:1).** The vertical scale is multiplied by 2 or 3 relative to the horizontal. The cross-section looks more like the mountain feels. Standard practice in mountain navigation; almost universal in topographic atlases.

Both are valid. For workbook practice, **use 2:1 vertical exaggeration as the default**. If you need a true-scale cross-section for a specific calculation (route gradient, sight lines), draw a true-scale version separately.

When you label your axes, write the scale and the vertical exaggeration explicitly. A cross-section without these labels can be misread.

### Slope angle from a cross-section

The slope angle on a cross-section is governed by the contour spacing along the section line. The C1.2 rule of thumb still applies:

- if the contour crossings on the section line are 3 mm apart or closer (on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m contours), the cross-section section there shows a slope at or above 30 degrees,
- if they are 8 mm or wider, the slope is gentle,
- and so on.

If you draw the cross-section at 2:1 vertical exaggeration, the angles on the page will look steeper than the real slope angles. This is the trade-off: visible relief at the cost of angular accuracy. Read steepness from the map (contour spacing on the section line), not from the angle of the line on the cross-section.

### Worked example

A 1:25,000 alpine sheet with 10 m contours shows a slope crossed by a section line 8 cm long. The line crosses contours at the following positions along its length:

| Distance along line (cm) | Contour value (m) |
|---|---|
| 0.0 | 2,200 |
| 0.5 | 2,210 |
| 1.5 | 2,220 |
| 2.8 | 2,230 |
| 4.2 | 2,240 |
| 6.0 | 2,250 |
| 8.0 | 2,260 |

The section is 8 cm = 2,000 m on the ground (1:25,000), with a height gain of 60 m from start to end. The cross-section drawn at 2:1 vertical exaggeration would show the contour crossings at the listed distances along the horizontal axis, with the height plotted on a vertical axis where 1 cm represents 5 m of elevation (so 60 m of total ascent occupies 12 cm vertically, and the slope reads visually as much steeper than the true 1.7 degrees).

This is a gentle slope: 60 m gain over 2,000 m horizontal distance is approximately 1.7 degrees. The cross-section shows a smooth concave line, with the line pulling away from level slightly more towards the start (closer-spaced contours at the bottom) and flattening towards the end.

### Why this matters for IMS

Cross-section thinking sits behind every route-card decision the IMS / EMS assessor expects to see: total ascent, total descent, the steepest section of the day, the location of cols and bowls along the line of travel. The candidate does not have to draw a formal cross-section for every leg of every tour, but the cross-section technique is what makes the mental version reliable. After C5.1 the workbook moves to less formal visualisation (C5.2 with images, C5.3 with verbal description); the cross-section is the technical anchor underneath both.
