### Learning aim
By the end of this page, the learner should be able to recognise the main colour groups used on outdoor topographic maps and explain what each colour usually represents.

### Why colour matters
Topographic maps use colour as a fast visual language. Before reading small symbols or detailed text, a skilled navigator often gets a quick first impression from the map’s colours.

Although different map agencies may vary slightly, the broad pattern is usually consistent enough to be very useful.

### The five-colour system
On many outdoor maps, these colours usually suggest the following:

- **Brown** – terrain shape and height information, especially contour lines.
- **Blue** – water, such as rivers, lakes, streams, marshes or glaciers.
- **Black** – man-made features, including buildings, roads, railways, walls and many boundaries.
- **Green** – vegetation, especially forest, woodland, scrub or areas of thicker plant cover.
- **White** – open or less vegetated land, often easier to cross than dense woodland (but not always).

The learner should treat these as strong visual clues, then confirm details from the legend.

### Worked example
Imagine you open a map and immediately notice:

- a broad white area,
- a narrow blue line,
- a block of green,
- several black shapes,
- and many brown lines.

A quick first interpretation might be:

- open ground,
- a stream or river,
- woodland or dense vegetation,
- buildings or other man-made features,
- and shaped terrain with height information.

This first visual scan helps you understand the area before you read finer detail.
