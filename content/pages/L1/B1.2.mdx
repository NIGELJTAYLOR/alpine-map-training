### Learning aim
By the end of this page, the learner should understand that a map is a **bird’s-eye view** of the land and should be able to begin turning simple map information into a mental picture of what the ground might look like.

### The key idea
A map shows the landscape **from above**.

When you stand on the ground, you do not see the world that way. You see it from the side, at eye level. Good map reading depends on learning to translate between these two views.

This is one of the most important skills in mountain navigation.

### Bird’s-eye view and ground view
Think of a simple valley with a stream, a bridge, a few buildings and a slope rising on one side.

From above on a map, you may see:

- A blue line for the stream.
- A black crossing for the bridge.
- A cluster of small black shapes for buildings.
- Brown contour lines showing rising ground.

From the ground, you may experience:

- Water at the low point.
- Buildings grouped together near flatter ground.
- Slopes rising away from the valley floor.
- Limited visibility depending on where you stand.

### Worked example
Imagine you are standing next to a bridge shown on the map.

If the contour lines are close together to the east of the bridge and further apart to the west, that suggests:

- The ground rises steeply to the east.
- The ground rises more gently to the west.
- The bridge is likely to be near a low point where the stream passes through.

This is the beginning of visualising map data as reality.

### How to practise this skill
When looking at a small map area, ask yourself four questions:

1. Where is the lowest point?
2. Where is the highest ground?
3. What man-made or natural features are easy to recognise?
4. If I stood here, what would I probably see around me?
