### Learning aim
By the end of this page, the learner should understand what map scale means, how to interpret 1:25,000 and 1:50,000, and why scale affects navigation detail.

### What scale means
Map scale tells you how much the real world has been reduced to fit on the map.

For example:

- **1:25,000** means 1 unit on the map equals 25,000 of the same units on the ground.
- **1:50,000** means 1 unit on the map equals 50,000 of the same units on the ground.

In practical terms:

- On a **1:25,000** map, 1 cm on the map = 250 metres on the ground.
- On a **1:50,000** map, 1 cm on the map = 500 metres on the ground.

### Why scale matters in navigation
A larger-scale map (such as 1:25,000) usually shows more detail. A smaller-scale map (such as 1:50,000) shows a wider area but usually with less detail.

This matters because in mountain navigation you often need both:

- fine detail for precise route choice,
- and wider overview for bigger planning.

### Worked example
If two points are 4 cm apart on the map:

- On a **1:25,000** map, the ground distance is 1,000 m (1 km).
- On a **1:50,000** map, the ground distance is 2,000 m (2 km).

The same line on two different maps can therefore represent very different ground distances.
