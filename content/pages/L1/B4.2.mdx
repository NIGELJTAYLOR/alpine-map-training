### Learning aim
By the end of this page, the learner should be able to measure simple route lengths on printed map extracts using a ruler, straight edge or string, and convert those measurements into ground distance.

### Why this matters
Knowing scale is useful, but navigation becomes practical when the learner can actually measure a route and turn that into a real-world distance. On printed extracts and PDFs, the learner must always check that the print size is correct before trusting measurements.

### Before measuring
Before starting, check three things:

1. The printed extract has been printed at the correct size.
2. The scale bar or scale statement is visible.
3. You know whether the map is 1:25,000 or 1:50,000.

If the print has been resized, use the scale bar rather than assuming the original scale is still exact.

### Straight and curved distances
There are two common cases:

- **Straight line distance** – measure directly with a ruler.
- **Curved route distance** – use a piece of string, paper edge or flexible strip to follow the bends, then measure that length with a ruler.

### Worked example 1 — Straight line
Two points are 5 cm apart on a 1:25,000 printed extract.

- 1 cm = 250 m
- 5 cm = 1,250 m
- So the ground distance is **1.25 km**

### Worked example 2 — Curved route
A winding path is traced with a piece of string and the string measures 7 cm on a 1:50,000 extract.

- 1 cm = 500 m
- 7 cm = 3,500 m
- So the route length is **3.5 km**
