### Learning aim
By the end of this page, the learner should be able to use obvious terrain and feature clues to orient a printed map in a simple situation, without yet relying on advanced compass work.

### The basic principle
A map can often be oriented by comparing a few strong clues between the paper and the ground, such as:

- a valley,
- a stream,
- a road,
- a ridge,
- a settlement edge,
- or a junction.

The learner is trying to make the picture on the paper line up with the picture in the landscape.

### Worked example
Suppose you are standing near:

- a stream in a shallow valley,
- woodland to one side,
- open ground to the other,
- and a path crossing a bridge.

If the printed extract shows the same arrangement, you can rotate the map until those features line up in the same relative directions.
