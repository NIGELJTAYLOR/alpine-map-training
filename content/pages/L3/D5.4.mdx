### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to use the altimeter as the primary navigation reference on a contour traverse where lateral features are not reliable, apply the "aim high" strategy for descending to a target altitude safely, and identify on the map the kinds of traverses where altimeter is the primary tool rather than the supporting tool.

### Why this matters

Most route legs use the compass for direction and the altimeter for confirmation. A subset of legs invert the relationship: the altimeter becomes the primary tool, and the compass plays a supporting role. These are legs where lateral features (named summits, ridges, rivers) are not visible or not reliable, but the candidate's altitude is still meaningful.

Examples:

- A contour traverse across a uniform north-facing slope, holding 2,250 m to reach a known waypoint at the same elevation.
- A descent into a featureless basin where the candidate must stop at a specific altitude to avoid a cliff band below.
- A poor-visibility leg across a wide plateau where the altimeter is the only available cross-check on position.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, the ability to flip between compass-primary and altimeter-primary modes is one of the assessable competencies. The route card should make the choice explicit for each leg.

### The contour traverse

A contour traverse is a route leg where the candidate maintains a constant altitude while moving across a slope. The altimeter is the primary tool because:

- the slope itself provides no lateral landmarks (one stretch of north-facing slope looks much like another at the same altitude),
- the candidate's gradient is the diagnostic: if the altimeter reading rises, the candidate is climbing; if it falls, the candidate is descending,
- the compass provides only the rough direction; the altimeter provides the precise contour.

The procedure:

1. Note the target altitude from the map (the contour you intend to follow).
2. Set the compass to the rough bearing of the traverse (the direction the contour runs).
3. Walk on the bearing while watching the altimeter.
4. If the altimeter rises, turn slightly downhill to drop back to the target.
5. If the altimeter falls, turn slightly uphill to climb back to the target.
6. The actual line followed is a slight zig-zag that hovers around the target altitude.

In practice the line stays within plus or minus 5 m of the target altitude with attention. That is more accurate than the candidate could achieve by visual contouring on an unfamiliar slope.

### The "aim high" strategy

When descending to a target altitude, the candidate may overshoot if they do not stop at the right point. Overshoot can be inconvenient (extra climbing back) or dangerous (overshooting onto committing terrain below).

The "aim high" strategy:

1. Identify the target altitude from the map.
2. **Approach the target from slightly above** (target + 10 m typically).
3. Stop at target + 10 m and confirm position (look around, cross-check with any visible features).
4. Then **follow the altimeter down** to the exact target.
5. Stop precisely at the target.

The strategy works because going slightly too high is recoverable (drop the extra 10 m) but going too low may not be (cliff band, terminal cone, runout zone). The 10 m buffer is small enough not to add much effort and large enough to give a safe stop.

Apply the aim-high strategy especially for:

- descents above cliff bands or runout zones,
- approaches to cols from above (overshoot drops the candidate into the wrong valley),
- and descents in poor visibility where the candidate cannot see what is below.

### Worked example

A candidate plans a leg across the upper east-facing flank of a summit at 2,650 m. The contour at 2,250 m runs roughly north-south across the flank for approximately 1.5 km. The candidate's destination is a cairn at 2,250 m at the south end of the contour.

The leg:

1. **Target altitude:** 2,250 m. Set on the route card.
2. **Bearing for the traverse:** roughly south, let's say 175 grid (177 magnetic with +2 east variation).
3. **Calibration before starting:** at the col at the north end of the flank, calibrate the altimeter to the col spot height (let's say 2,310 m).
4. **Walk south on the bearing.** Drop the 60 m from 2,310 m to 2,250 m in the first few minutes; then maintain 2,250 m for the rest of the traverse.
5. **Hovering around 2,250 m:** if the altimeter reads 2,255 m, turn slightly downhill to drop 5 m; if it reads 2,245 m, turn slightly uphill to climb 5 m.
6. **Approach the cairn:** in the last 100 m, watch for the cairn visually. The altimeter should still read approximately 2,250 m when the cairn comes into view.

If the cairn is not at exactly 2,250 m on the candidate's altimeter when reached, the discrepancy is information: either the altimeter has drifted (recalibrate at the cairn if the cairn altitude is marked) or the cairn is at a slightly different altitude than the candidate expected (cross-check the map).

### Why this matters for IMS

The altimeter-as-primary-tool framing is the alpine-specific application that distinguishes the candidate from a non-alpine navigator. On open snow, in featureless basins, and on poor-visibility plateaux, the altimeter is what makes the navigation work. The aim-high strategy is the discipline that turns a routine descent into a controlled approach, especially above terrain hazards.

After D5 the candidate has the full altimeter skill set: theory (D5.1), calibration (D5.2), drift management (D5.3), and primary-tool use (D5.4). D6 covers the barometer, often the same physical instrument as the altimeter but used as a weather indicator rather than an altitude reference. D7 brings compass and altimeter together for the integrated alpine techniques.
