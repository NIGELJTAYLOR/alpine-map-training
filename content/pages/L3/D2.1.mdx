### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to take a grid bearing accurately from a 1:25,000 alpine map between two named points using the four-step procedure, and you should be able to write that bearing on a route-card-style entry within 60 seconds.

### Why this matters

A bearing taken from the map is the route-card backbone of every poor-visibility leg you will ever walk or ski. The IMS / EMS assessor expects every leg of the day to have a written bearing on the route card before departure. The map bearing is what converts a planned line on the map into a heading you can hold in the field.

The four-step procedure is not difficult, but it has to be drilled until it is automatic. A candidate who has to stop and think about which arrow goes where during a route briefing has not yet internalised it.

### The four-step procedure

Take the compass and lay it on the map. Then:

1. **Place the baseplate edge along the line from your start point to your destination.** The direction-of-travel arrow must point from start to destination, not the other way. This step alone is the most common error: a baseplate placed correctly but with the direction-of-travel arrow pointing the wrong way produces a bearing that is exactly 180 degrees wrong.
2. **Rotate the bezel until the parallel orienting lines are aligned with the grid lines on the map**, with the orienting arrow (and the N on the bezel) pointing to the top of the map (which is grid north on most alpine sheets). The compass body itself does not move; only the bezel rotates.
3. **Take the compass off the map.** The bearing is now set on the bezel.
4. **Read the bearing from the direction-of-travel arrow against the bezel scale**. This is the grid bearing from your start point to your destination.

The bearing read at step 4 is a grid bearing, not yet a magnetic bearing. D2.2 covers the conversion to magnetic. D2.3 covers walking on it.

### Worked example

A candidate plans a leg from a col at 2,720 m to a hut at 2,450 m on a 1:25,000 alpine sheet. Working through the procedure:

1. Place the baseplate edge along the line from the col (start) to the hut (destination). Direction-of-travel arrow points towards the hut.
2. Rotate the bezel until the parallel orienting lines align with the grid lines on the sheet. N on the bezel points to the top of the map.
3. Take the compass off the map.
4. Read the bearing: 218 degrees.

The leg's grid bearing is 218 degrees. Cross-check: 218 degrees is south-south-west. The hut sits to the south-west of the col on the sheet, which is consistent. The bearing is plausible.

The bearing is recorded on the route card as "Grid bearing 218 degrees". The conversion to magnetic happens in D2.2.

### Self-check

- [ ] I can take a grid bearing from a 1:25,000 alpine map using the four-step procedure.
- [ ] I always check that the direction-of-travel arrow points from start to destination, not the reverse.
- [ ] I cross-check every bearing against the closest cardinal direction.
- [ ] I can take a bearing in under 60 seconds with practice.

### Why this matters for IMS

The map-bearing skill is the foundation of every route card and every poor-visibility navigation leg. The 60-seconds-per-bearing speed is the working standard for completing a six-leg route card inside the 20 minutes the IMS Nav Training Programme assessment-checklist allows. The cardinal cross-check is the safety net against the 180-degree error, which is the most common compass error and the most consequential.
