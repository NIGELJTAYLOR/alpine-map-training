### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should understand that a barometric altimeter measures atmospheric pressure and converts it to altitude using a standard atmosphere model, distinguish a barometric altimeter from a GPS altimeter, and recognise that altimeter readings drift with weather changes.

### Why this matters

The altimeter is the second navigation instrument in the IMS / EMS toolkit. Where the compass tells you direction, the altimeter tells you height. Together they form the position-fixing pair that does not require visible features, which is what makes them the alpine-specific alternative to resection.

Understanding how the altimeter works is the prerequisite for using it well. A candidate who treats the altimeter as a magic box will be confused by drift; a candidate who understands the pressure-to-altitude conversion will read drift as information.

### The barometric altimeter mechanism

A barometric altimeter measures atmospheric pressure with a small aneroid capsule (a flexible metal chamber that expands and contracts with pressure changes). The altimeter then converts the measured pressure into an altitude figure using a standard atmosphere model.

The standard model assumes:

- pressure decreases predictably with altitude,
- the rate of decrease is approximately **1 hectopascal (hPa) per 8 metres** of altitude gain in the lower troposphere (the relevant range for alpine work),
- and the conversion is calibrated to a sea-level reference pressure of 1013.25 hPa under standard temperature.

The mechanism is mechanical and reliable. It does not require batteries (in mechanical altimeters) or satellite signal. It is the same technology used in aircraft altimeters at lower altitudes.

### Why altimeters drift

The conversion from pressure to altitude assumes stable weather. Real weather is not stable. Two factors produce drift:

- **Weather pressure changes.** A passing weather system changes atmospheric pressure independent of the candidate's altitude. A falling barometer (incoming low pressure) makes the altimeter over-read; a rising barometer (high pressure building) makes it under-read.
- **Temperature.** The standard atmosphere model assumes a specific temperature profile. In very cold conditions the air is denser at altitude than the model assumes, so the altimeter may under-read. The error is small (typically less than 1 percent) and rarely matters for IMS / EMS work, but it is present.

For practical IMS / EMS work, weather pressure drift is the dominant error source. D5.3 covers it in detail. The take-away here is that an altimeter reading at any given moment depends on both your altitude and the current weather pressure; if either changes, the reading changes.

### GPS altimeters

A GPS altimeter measures altitude directly using satellite signals. It does not depend on atmospheric pressure and does not drift with weather.

The trade-offs:

- GPS altimeters are typically **less accurate than barometric altimeters** in stable conditions (often 10 to 20 m error vs 1 to 3 m for a properly calibrated barometric).
- GPS altimeters require satellite signal, which can be poor in steep terrain, dense forest, or under low cloud.
- GPS altimeters are generally more battery-dependent.

The IMS / EMS recommendation is to use both:

- **Barometric altimeter as the primary tool** because it is more accurate when calibrated and works in any weather.
- **GPS altimeter as a backup** for cross-check and for use when the barometric has drifted significantly without the opportunity to recalibrate.

### Worked example

A candidate sets a barometric altimeter at home in the morning. The reading at 8am is 90 m above sea level (the candidate's known home elevation).

The candidate does not move from home all day. Readings:

- 8am: 90 m
- noon: 95 m
- 4pm: 105 m
- 8pm: 115 m

The altimeter has not moved, but the reading has changed by 25 m over 12 hours. This implies a barometric pressure change of approximately 25 m / 8 m per hPa = approximately **3 hPa drop** over the period.

The candidate cross-checks against a weather forecast or a separate barometer. A 3 hPa drop in 12 hours is consistent with a slowly approaching weather system.

The point of the exercise is to demonstrate that the altimeter is doing its job correctly: the reading change accurately reflects the pressure change. The error appears only if the candidate assumes the reading is altitude when the pressure has shifted.

### Why this matters for IMS

The altimeter is the second navigation instrument in the IMS / EMS toolkit, paired with the compass. Together they enable the alpine-specific aspect-of-slope relocation technique (D7.2) that does not require visible features. Understanding how the altimeter works is the prerequisite for using it well; a candidate who treats it as a magic box will misread drift as altitude error and lose confidence in the instrument when they should be reading the weather.
