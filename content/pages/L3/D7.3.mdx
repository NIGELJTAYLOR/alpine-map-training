### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to measure slope angle directly with an inclinometer, cross-reference the measurement with the contour-spacing estimate from C1.2, and recognise the systematic differences between map-derived and field-measured slope angles.

### Why this matters

The C1.2 contour-spacing rule of thumb gives an estimate of slope angle from the map. The inclinometer gives a direct measurement in the field. Both have their place; both have their limits.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, inclinometer use is the field cross-check on the map estimate. The map says the slope is approximately 30 degrees; the inclinometer at the top of the slope confirms (or contradicts). The discrepancy is information: either the map estimate was wrong, or the slope is non-uniform, or the inclinometer measurement is at an unrepresentative point.

The IMS / EMS-relevant use is avalanche-aware terrain interpretation. A slope at 30 degrees or above is in the avalanche release range; the inclinometer is the field instrument that confirms this for the specific slope the candidate is about to commit to.

### The inclinometer

An inclinometer measures slope angle directly. Most baseplate compasses sold for mountain use have a built-in inclinometer; standalone inclinometers also exist.

Two common designs:

- **Pendulum inclinometer.** A small pendulum suspended inside the compass capsule. When the compass is held edge-on to the slope, the pendulum reads the slope angle against a scale.
- **Bubble inclinometer.** A small bubble in a curved tube. When the compass is tilted to match the slope, the bubble reads the angle.

Both work the same way in principle: the inclinometer measures the angle between the compass body and the horizontal.

To use:

1. Hold the compass edge-on to the slope, with one edge on the slope surface and the body pointing in the direction of greatest descent (the fall line).
2. Read the slope angle from the inclinometer scale.

Some compasses require the candidate to sight along the inclinometer towards a distant point on the slope; others use a built-in pendulum that gives the reading directly.

### Cross-referencing with the C1.2 rule of thumb

The C1.2 rule of thumb (3 mm contour spacing on a 1:25,000 map with 10 m contours indicates approximately 30 degrees) gives a map-derived estimate. The inclinometer gives a field measurement. For most slopes the two should agree within a few degrees.

When they disagree, three explanations:

- **The map estimate was averaged over a longer slope** that includes both gentler and steeper sections. The inclinometer measures the specific point; the map averages.
- **The slope is non-uniform.** The inclinometer reading at one point may be different from the average for the whole slope.
- **The measurement is at a poor point.** A measurement at a small bump or hollow gives an unrepresentative reading. Take three or four measurements at different points and average.

A discrepancy of more than 5 degrees between the map estimate and a representative inclinometer measurement is worth investigating. Check the contour-spacing measurement against the legend; check the inclinometer reading at a different point.

### The 30-degree threshold

The C1.2 30-degree threshold (the lower bound of the slab-release range) is one of the most assessable boundaries in alpine navigation. The inclinometer is the field instrument that confirms whether a specific slope is at, above, or below this threshold.

For practical IMS / EMS work:

- A slope estimated at less than 25 degrees on the map: the inclinometer should confirm. If it reads above 30, the map estimate was wrong; recheck.
- A slope estimated at 25 to 30 degrees on the map: inclinometer measurement is the deciding figure. If above 30, treat as avalanche-prone; if below, treat as safer.
- A slope estimated at 30 degrees or above: the inclinometer is a confirmation rather than a diagnosis. The candidate already knows the slope is in the release range.

The inclinometer is also useful for **convex roll detection**. A convex roll has the steepest gradient at the rollover point. The inclinometer at the top of the roll may read 25 degrees; at the rollover, 35 degrees; below the rollover, 30 degrees. Three measurements identify the convex-roll pattern that the map shows as tightening contours.

### Worked example

A candidate is at the top of a slope that the map suggests is approximately 32 degrees (contour spacing approximately 2.5 mm on a 1:25,000 with 10 m contours).

The candidate takes inclinometer readings at three points:

- At the top of the slope: 28 degrees.
- 50 m down the slope: 35 degrees.
- 100 m down the slope: 33 degrees.

Average: 32 degrees. Map estimate: 32 degrees. Agreement.

The variation across the three readings (28 to 35) tells the candidate the slope is non-uniform: gentler at the top, steepening into a roll, then settling to a moderate-steep gradient. The 35-degree mid-slope reading is in the prime avalanche release range; the 28-degree top is not.

The inclinometer has added information beyond the map: the candidate now knows where the steepest section is and can plan the route accordingly.

### Why this matters for IMS

Inclinometer use is the field cross-check on the map estimate. The IMS / EMS-relevant use is avalanche-aware terrain interpretation: the candidate confirms (or contradicts) the map estimate before committing to a slope in the release range. The convex-roll detection is the most route-relevant application; rolls are classic avalanche-trigger points and the inclinometer pattern (gentle / steeper / moderate) is the field signature.
