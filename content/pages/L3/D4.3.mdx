### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to identify the common sources of magnetic interference on a ski tour, know the minimum safe distance from each, and produce a personal interference table for the equipment you carry. You should also know the basic instrument-hygiene checks to keep your compass reliable.

### Why this matters

A compass is a magnetic instrument. Anything magnetic nearby pulls the needle off true magnetic north. The pull may be small but it is consistent: a 5-degree deflection from a phone in a chest pocket affects every bearing the candidate takes that day until the phone is moved.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, magnetic interference is one of the silent error sources. A bearing read with a compass next to a steel ski binding produces drift on every leg, and the candidate may not notice until the cumulative effect is large.

### Common interference sources

The four most common interference sources on a ski tour:

- **Steel ski bindings.** The largest interference source most candidates encounter. Bindings are within 50 cm of the compass when the compass is held at chest height with skis on. Minimum safe distance: **50 cm**.
- **Ice axes.** Steel head and shaft. Minimum safe distance: **50 cm**.
- **Mobile phones.** The internal magnets of the speaker and camera produce a small but consistent field. Minimum safe distance: **30 cm**.
- **Radio handsets.** The internal antenna magnets produce a similar field to phones. Minimum safe distance: **30 cm**.

Other interference sources to be aware of:

- Avalanche transceivers in transmit mode. (In search mode they are designed to detect signals and are even more sensitive to interference; never use a compass alongside a transceiver in active search.)
- Electronic watches and altimeters. Generally negligible at the wrist when the compass is held at chest height.
- Vehicles. Steel chassis and engine. Minimum safe distance: **2 m** when standing next to a parked car.
- Reinforced concrete buildings. Reinforcement bars produce local fields; useful to know when calibrating a compass at a hut or visitor centre.

### The test

The test for interference is simple: with the compass set to a known direction, move the suspect object closer. If the needle deflects as the object approaches, the object is the source.

For a personal interference table, run the test for every piece of equipment you routinely carry. Note the distance at which deflection becomes detectable. Add a margin (typically 20 to 30 cm) for safety.

### Instrument hygiene

The compass itself needs occasional checks:

- **Crack in the baseplate.** A cracked baseplate may flex and tilt the compass when held flat. Replace.
- **Cloudy or pierced damping fluid.** A compass with a damaged needle pivot or compromised damping fluid will not settle reliably. Replace.
- **Stuck or sluggish bezel.** A bezel that does not turn smoothly will produce inconsistent bearings. Try cleaning gently; if the problem persists, replace.
- **Confirmation against a known direction.** Run the D1.2 quick-check at the start of every tour. A compass that disagrees with a known direction on the quick-check is a compass that should be inspected before being relied on.

### Worked example

A candidate runs the interference test at home. With the compass set to a known direction (a bearing taken to a building edge known to be due south, so the compass should read 180 degrees magnetic):

| Object tested | Distance at first deflection | Personal safe distance |
|---|---|---|
| Mobile phone | 25 cm | 50 cm (with margin) |
| Ski binding (held in hand) | 40 cm | 70 cm (with margin) |
| Ice axe | 35 cm | 60 cm (with margin) |
| Avalanche transceiver (transmit) | 15 cm | 40 cm (with margin) |
| Avalanche transceiver (search) | not tested (do not test in active search mode) | n/a |

The candidate notes that the steel ski binding has the largest interference range. On tour the candidate keeps the compass at chest height (50 cm above the binding tops when standing) and is careful not to place the compass on the snow next to the skis when removing them.

### Why this matters for IMS

Magnetic interference management is one of the small habits the IMS / EMS assessor expects without prompting. A candidate who places the compass on a steel ski binding while taking a bearing has not internalised the discipline. The personal interference table is the working tool that turns generic guidance into a specific habit; the candidate carries the table mentally and adjusts compass placement accordingly.

After D4 the candidate has the full compass-and-bearings skill set: parts and reading (D1), bearings from the map (D2), bearings from the ground (D3), and practical accuracy plus instrument hygiene (D4). D5 introduces the second instrument: the altimeter.
