### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to walk closed shapes (square, triangle, hexagon) using compass bearings only, measure your closure error, and use that error to assess your practical compass-and-pacing accuracy. You should also be able to interpret the closure error in terms of the IMS / EMS 15-m-per-100-m drift target from D2.3.

### Why this matters

A closed shape walked on bearings is a self-test. If the candidate's compass and pacing are accurate, the shape closes back on the starting point. The gap between where the candidate finishes and where they started is the closure error: the cumulative effect of every bearing inaccuracy and every pacing inconsistency over the four (or three, or six) sides.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, the bearing-shapes drill is the calibration tool. It gives a single number (closure error) that can be tracked across sessions. A candidate whose closure error is improving is becoming more accurate; a candidate whose closure error is stuck has a habit to fix.

### The square drill

The standard drill is a square: four sides of 100 paces each, with 90-degree turns at each corner.

Procedure:

1. Mark a starting point.
2. Set any bearing on the compass (for variety, use a bearing that is not a cardinal direction, for example 137 degrees).
3. Walk 100 paces along that bearing using the D2.3 walking technique.
4. At the corner, add 90 degrees to the bezel (137 + 90 = 227).
5. Walk 100 paces along the new bearing.
6. Repeat steps 4 and 5 twice more (227 + 90 = 317; 317 + 90 = 047 or equivalently 047, the original bearing minus 90 since 137 - 90 = 047 wraps).
7. After the fourth side, you should be back at the starting point.

Measure the closure error: the distance from the actual end point to the starting point.

### Standards for closure error

For 100-pace sides on a square (about 70 to 100 m of ground):

- **Less than 5 paces of closure error** indicates excellent practical accuracy. The candidate is at IMS / EMS working standard.
- **5 to 10 paces** indicates good accuracy. Identify which leg contributed most to the error and refine.
- **10 to 20 paces** indicates a habit to fix. Run the diagnostic from D2.3 (tilted compass, body off-axis, leaning into slope) and re-attempt.
- **More than 20 paces** indicates a bearing or pacing problem. Recycle D1.2 (compass holding) and D2.3 (walking technique) before trying again.

A pace is the distance covered by one foot in normal walking. Pace length varies by candidate (typically 70 to 90 cm for adults). A candidate's pace length should be calibrated separately by walking a known 100 m and counting paces; the figure is then used to convert pace counts into metres.

### The triangle and hexagon drills

The square drill uses 90-degree turns. The triangle uses 120-degree turns (3 × 120 = 360); the hexagon uses 60-degree turns (6 × 60 = 360). Each is a different turning rhythm and a different test.

For the triangle:

1. Three sides of 100 paces.
2. Turn 120 degrees at each corner.
3. Should close on the starting point.

For the hexagon:

1. Six sides of 100 paces.
2. Turn 60 degrees at each corner.
3. Should close on the starting point.

The triangle is the harder drill because the 120-degree turn is less intuitive than 90 degrees and small errors compound faster over fewer, longer-feeling legs. The hexagon is the gentler drill because each turn is small and there are more legs to average over. Run all three to see which turn rhythm exposes your specific error pattern.

### The deliberate-offset variation

To see what a 3-degree compass error looks like in practical drift, run the square drill with the bezel deliberately set 3 degrees off the calculated bearing on every side. Compare the closure error with your accurate-bezel run.

Predicted drift from a 3-degree error over a 100-pace side: approximately tan(3 degrees) × 100 paces = approximately 5 paces of lateral drift per side. Over a four-sided square the errors compound, but they also partially cancel; the closure error from a uniform 3-degree offset is typically 10 to 15 paces.

### Worked example

A candidate walks a square with sides of 100 paces, starting on bearing 137 degrees:

- Side 1: 137 degrees, 100 paces.
- Side 2: 227 degrees, 100 paces.
- Side 3: 317 degrees, 100 paces.
- Side 4: 047 degrees, 100 paces.

End point: 7 paces south-east of the starting point. Closure error: 7 paces.

Interpretation: in the 5-to-10-paces band. Good but not excellent. The candidate notes which corner contributed most (visibly side 2 had the most awkward feel) and resolves to focus on the body-square-up habit at corner turns next time.

### Why this matters for IMS

The bearing-shapes drill is the assessable practical-accuracy standard from the IMS Nav Training Programme. A candidate at less than 5 paces closure error on a 100-pace square is at the IMS / EMS working level. A candidate at 20 paces or more is not yet at working level; the route-card bearings will not produce a route in the field that matches the route on paper. The drill is the diagnostic that tells the candidate whether their compass-and-bearing skill is good enough to rely on.
