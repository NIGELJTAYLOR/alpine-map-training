### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to convert any forward bearing into a back bearing (forward bearing plus or minus 180 degrees), use back bearings to confirm direction or to relocate from a known feature behind you, and recognise the IMS / EMS use of back bearings as a routine sanity check in poor visibility.

### Why this matters

A back bearing is the bearing from where you are now back to where you came from. If your forward bearing was correct and you have walked it correctly, the back bearing should align with your starting feature. If it does not, you have drifted.

Back bearings are useful in three IMS / EMS-relevant ways:

- **Confirming direction.** Take a back bearing every five to ten minutes in poor visibility; if the back bearing aligns with where you came from, you are still on the line.
- **Relocating using a known feature behind you.** When the feature ahead is invisible, the feature behind may not be; take a bearing to it and apply the back-bearing rule.
- **Confirming you have not drifted.** A simple sanity check that takes 30 seconds and catches a slow drift before it becomes a long one.

### The 180-degree rule

A back bearing is the forward bearing plus or minus 180 degrees:

- If the forward bearing is **less than 180 degrees**, **add 180** to get the back bearing.
- If the forward bearing is **180 or more**, **subtract 180** to get the back bearing.

The rule keeps the back bearing in the 0 to 360 range. Both add-180 and subtract-180 produce mathematically equivalent results; the rule above is just the cleaner way to express it.

The rule applies equally to grid bearings and to magnetic bearings, as long as you do not mix them. A grid forward bearing produces a grid back bearing; a magnetic forward bearing produces a magnetic back bearing.

### Worked example

A candidate has set off on a leg from a col with a magnetic bearing of 220 degrees (from the D3.1 worked example). After 200 m of walking, they stop to confirm direction.

The back bearing from their current position to the col is:

- forward bearing 220 is greater than 180, so subtract 180 to get back bearing.
- 220 - 180 = **040 degrees magnetic**.

The candidate turns around (mentally; they do not need to physically face the col), takes a magnetic bearing to the col itself, and reads 041 degrees. Within 2 degrees of the predicted 040; they have not drifted.

If the candidate had read, for example, 055 magnetic, that would tell them they had drifted approximately 15 degrees off the intended line. At that point they would take a corrective heading rather than continuing on the original 220 forward bearing.

### Why this matters for IMS

The back-bearing sanity check is the IMS / EMS-relevant habit for poor-visibility navigation. A candidate who takes back bearings every five to ten minutes is the candidate who catches drift early and corrects before it becomes a problem. The skill is small but the habit is what counts; the assessor will watch for it during route narration in flat light or whiteout simulation.
