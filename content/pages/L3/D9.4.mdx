### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to plan one or more escape routes for each leg of a route card, identify a generic safe-line strategy, and recognise when no viable escape route exists and what that implies for the route choice.

### Why this matters

An escape route is the alternative line when the planned route cannot be completed. Every committing decision should have one. The route card with escape routes is a card with options; the route card without them is a card with one path and one outcome.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, escape routes are the contingency layer that closes the route card. The assessor reviews them as evidence that the candidate has thought about what could go wrong, not just what should go right.

The most important escape-route insight is the negative case: when the candidate cannot identify a viable escape route for a leg, the route choice itself may be wrong. A leg with no escape route is a committing decision; the candidate should know that before committing.

### What an escape route is

An escape route is a different route from the planned route, used when the planned route fails. "Different route" means:

- a different bearing or direction of travel,
- a different waypoint or destination,
- or both.

Examples:

- Planned route: ascend via the north ridge to the summit. Escape: descend via the morning route to the lift station.
- Planned route: traverse the upper bowl. Escape: drop into the bowl floor and descend via the safer south flank.
- Planned route: cross the col and descend the west slope. Escape: turn back at the col and descend via the south slope.

A "different route" does not mean "the same route in reverse" unless reversing is meaningfully safer. A reversal is sometimes the right escape (when the candidate has come up a safe line and is now committing to a less safe descent), but often it is not (when the safe line on the way up was via a slope that is now wind-loaded and avalanche-prone for the descent).

### Where to plan escape routes

For each leg of the route card, the candidate identifies:

- **The escape route from the leg's start.** If the candidate stops at the start of the leg and does not commit, where do they go?
- **The escape route from the leg's end.** If the candidate completes the leg and conditions then change, where do they go from the leg's destination?
- **The escape route from any committing point within the leg.** If the leg has a point of no return mid-way, where does the candidate go from there?

For most legs, the escape route from the start and the escape route from the end are different routes; the within-leg escape route may be the same as one of the others or distinct.

### When no escape route exists

A leg with no viable escape route is a committing leg. Examples:

- A traverse below a cornice with steep ground to one side and a cliff to the other: there is no safe alternative line.
- A couloir descent: once the candidate enters, the only way out is down the couloir.
- A long contour at high altitude with no safe descent in either direction for several kilometres.

When the candidate identifies a leg with no escape route, two responses are appropriate:

1. **Recognise the commitment explicitly.** The route card notes "no viable escape; committing leg" and the decision-point criteria for entering the leg are tightened accordingly.
2. **Reconsider the route choice.** A committing leg in stable conditions may be acceptable; a committing leg in marginal conditions is usually not. The "no viable escape" finding is a flag that the route may need to be revised.

The IMS / EMS-relevant insight: a route with multiple legs flagged "no viable escape" is a route that will not work in any conditions short of perfect. The candidate's response to this finding is part of the planning skill the assessor tests.

### Worked example

The candidate's tour from D9.2 / D9.3 has four decision points. Escape routes for each leg:

- **Leg 1: Lift station to col at 2,720 m.** Escape: turn back along the ascent line to the lift station. Always available; the ascent line is on a broad shoulder with no committing terrain.
- **Leg 2: Col to summit at 2,980 m.** Escape: turn back at any point along the leg to the col, then descend via the morning route. Available unless conditions make the col itself dangerous.
- **Leg 3: Summit to hut at 2,450 m.** Two routes exist: the planned north route (steeper, faster, exposed) and the alternative south route (longer, more sheltered, more bailout opportunities). The south route is the escape from the north.
- **Leg 4: Hut to second col at 2,610 m.** Escape: stop at the hut and shelter. The leg is optional; the candidate is not committed to it.
- **Leg 5: Second col back to lift station via planned line.** Two routes exist: the planned line and the alternative line. The alternative line is longer but more sheltered; it is the escape from the planned line.

For each leg the candidate has named at least one escape. No leg is flagged "no viable escape", which suggests the route choice is reasonable for the conditions assumed.

### Why this matters for IMS

Escape routes and contingency planning are the closing layer of the route card. A complete card with escape routes is the artefact that demonstrates the candidate has done the full planning thinking. The IMS / EMS assessor scrutinises the contingency layer carefully; a card without it signals that the candidate has planned a one-way commitment.

After D9 the route card is the integrative artefact of Level 3. Every previous instrument and technique feeds into it; the route card on the day is the candidate's working reference for the whiteout sequence (D8.1) and the relocation procedure (D8.3).

D10 closes Level 3 with consolidation: a mixed quiz (D10.1) and a virtual day on a tour (D10.2) that exercises the full route card and instrument toolkit in a sustained narration.
