### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to place decision points on a route card at meaningful locations, write go / no-go criteria that meet the three-component standard from D6.2 (specific trigger, specific action, named decision point), and recognise the typical decision-point locations on an alpine route card.

### Why this matters

A route card without decision points is a one-way commitment: the candidate plans to complete the tour and has no structured way to abort. A route card with decision points is a managed plan: the candidate plans to complete the tour but has pre-agreed criteria for stopping or changing course at specific places.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, decision points are the discipline that turns a route card from a plan into a managed plan. The assessor will look for them; their absence is a meaningful gap.

### Where to place decision points

Decision points are placed at specific kinds of locations:

- **Cols and ridges where committing terrain begins below.** Once the candidate descends from the col, the descent commits them; the col is the last pre-commitment decision point.
- **Points of no return.** A traverse above a cliff band, a couloir entry, a gully descent. The decision is made before entering the committing terrain, not during.
- **Major slope crossings.** Where the route crosses a slope that is in the avalanche release range, the decision point is at the slope edge before crossing.
- **Time / weather checkpoints.** Specific points on the route where the candidate has agreed to assess time elapsed, weather trend (D6.2), and group condition before continuing.

For a typical six-leg full-day tour, the candidate places three to five decision points. Fewer than three suggests the candidate has not engaged with the contingency thinking; more than five suggests the candidate is over-engineering the card.

### The three-component criteria standard

From D6.2: every pre-agreed criterion has three components:

1. A **specific trigger** (a measurable condition).
2. A **specific action** (turn around, take the alternative line, abort, etc.).
3. A **named decision point** (the specific location where the criterion is checked).

Examples of strong criteria:

- "If the barometer has fallen by more than 2 hPa since departure when checked at the col at 2,720 m, descend by the morning route."
- "If the time of arrival at the col exceeds 10:30am, skip the summit and traverse via the south col instead."
- "If any group member is unable to maintain the planned pace at the bench at 2,540 m, turn around and descend to the hut."

Examples of weak criteria (coach for specificity):

- "If the weather looks bad, turn around." No measurable trigger; no specific action; no decision point.
- "Watch the weather." Not a criterion at all.
- "If we are running late, decide what to do." No specific trigger; no specific action.

The three-component standard is non-negotiable for IMS / EMS assessment. A candidate's route card with vague criteria signals incomplete planning thinking.

### Worked example

A candidate's tour from D9.2 (lift station at 1,850 m through col at 2,720 m, summit at 2,980 m, hut at 2,450 m, second col at 2,610 m, back to lift station) has four natural decision points:

- **Decision Point 1: at the col at 2,720 m.** The col is the gateway to the summit attempt; the descent from the col commits to the summit-and-hut sequence.
- **Decision Point 2: at the summit at 2,980 m.** The descent route choice (planned north line vs alternative south line) is made here.
- **Decision Point 3: at the hut at 2,450 m.** The afternoon decision: continue to the second col, or stop here for the day.
- **Decision Point 4: at the second col at 2,610 m.** The final descent line choice: planned line vs alternative.

Pre-agreed criteria for each:

- **DP1 (col at 2,720 m):**
  - If the time is past 10:30am: skip summit; traverse via south col.
  - If the barometer has fallen more than 2 hPa since departure: descend by the morning route.
  - If any group member is struggling: descend by the morning route.

- **DP2 (summit at 2,980 m):**
  - If the weather is rising or steady: descend by the planned north route.
  - If the weather trend is in slow or moderate fall: descend by the south route (more reliable bailout).
  - If the weather trend is in rapid fall: descend by the south route immediately.

- **DP3 (hut at 2,450 m):**
  - If the time is before 2pm and conditions are stable: continue to the second col.
  - If the time is between 2pm and 3pm and conditions are stable: continue but use the shorter alternative route from the second col.
  - If the time is after 3pm or conditions are deteriorating: stop at the hut for the day.

- **DP4 (second col at 2,610 m):**
  - If conditions allow: descend by the planned route.
  - If conditions have deteriorated since the hut: descend by the alternative route (longer but more sheltered).

The card now has decision points named, located, and equipped with criteria. The plan is no longer a one-way commitment; it is a managed plan with structured fall-back at four specific moments.

### Why this matters for IMS

The decision points and pre-agreed criteria are one of the IMS / EMS-relevant route card features the assessor will look for. A card without them is a one-way commitment; a card with them is a managed plan. The candidate who has the discipline to write the criteria and the discipline to apply them on the day is at the working assessment standard.
