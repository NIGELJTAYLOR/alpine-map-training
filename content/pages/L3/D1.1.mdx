### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to identify the six main parts of a baseplate compass on sight, explain what each part does, and recognise the two parts that are most commonly confused with each other.

### Why this matters

The compass is the primary instrument for converting a map bearing into a direction of travel in the field. At IMS / EMS level the candidate is expected to handle the compass without thinking about its parts; the assessor will not pause for the candidate to inspect the instrument before taking a bearing. Familiarity with the parts is the prerequisite for the speed and accuracy that come later.

### The six main parts

A baseplate compass is built around six functional parts. Most beginners can name three or four; the assessor will expect all six.

- **Baseplate.** The flat transparent body of the compass. The edges are usually marked in millimetres or centimetres so the baseplate doubles as a ruler for map work. The baseplate is the working surface that lies on the map when a bearing is taken.
- **Direction-of-travel arrow.** The arrow printed permanently on the baseplate, pointing forward (away from the user when the compass is held in the standard orientation). This is the arrow that points where you are going.
- **Rotating bezel.** The dial that turns around the compass body, marked in degrees from 0 to 360. The bezel is what you set to a specific bearing.
- **Orienting arrow.** The arrow printed on the floor of the rotating bezel (visible through the transparent base). When the bezel is turned, the orienting arrow turns with it. This is the arrow you align with the magnetic needle when walking on a bearing.
- **Magnetic needle.** The free-floating needle that always points to magnetic north (the red end). The needle is what makes the compass a compass.
- **Parallel orienting lines.** The lines printed on the floor of the rotating bezel, parallel to the orienting arrow. These are what you align with the grid lines on the map when taking a bearing from the map.

A handful of compasses also include a clinometer (for slope angle measurement) and a sighting mirror (for high-precision bearings on distant features). Both are useful but neither is essential at IMS / EMS level.

### The two most-confused parts

The single most common error among new compass users is confusing the **direction-of-travel arrow** with the **orienting arrow**. Both are arrows on the same instrument; both point. The distinction matters and is worth memorising:

- The **direction-of-travel arrow** is on the baseplate and is fixed. It points where you are going.
- The **orienting arrow** is on the floor of the rotating bezel and turns with the bezel. It is the alignment target for the magnetic needle.

When a bearing is set on the bezel, the direction-of-travel arrow shows the heading and the orienting arrow shows where the magnetic needle should sit. The needle goes into the orienting arrow, like a thumb into a glove. The body then rotates until both align with the direction-of-travel arrow. At that point the compass is set to the bearing and the user faces the right way.

The other common confusion is between the red end and the white end of the magnetic needle. The red end points to magnetic north. A useful mnemonic is **red to shed**: red points north, where in the northern hemisphere the shed (or any reference object known to be north) sits. Get into the habit of confirming the red end early; on a poorly-lit map at dusk the colour can be hard to read.

### Worked example

A candidate picks up a baseplate compass for the first time and is asked to identify the parts. A correct identification names:

1. The transparent flat body as the baseplate, with the millimetre scale on its edge.
2. The arrow printed permanently on the baseplate (pointing forward) as the direction-of-travel arrow.
3. The numbered dial as the rotating bezel, currently set with 0 (north) at the top.
4. The arrow inside the dial floor as the orienting arrow.
5. The free-floating needle as the magnetic needle, with the red end pointing north (verified by asking which way is north in the room).
6. The lines printed inside the dial floor, parallel to the orienting arrow, as the parallel orienting lines.

If the candidate names "the arrow" without specifying which arrow, return them to the orienting-vs-direction-of-travel distinction.

### Why this matters for IMS

Compass familiarity is the prerequisite for every D2 technique that follows. The assessor expects the candidate to handle the compass fluidly during route narration, position fixing, and poor-visibility navigation. A candidate who fumbles for the orienting arrow under stress sounds untrained. After D1.1 the parts should be invisible: you reach for the compass and set the bearing without thinking about which arrow is which.
