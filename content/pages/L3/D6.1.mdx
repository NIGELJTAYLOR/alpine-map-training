### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to record barometric pressure readings over time, classify the trend into one of five categories (rising, steady, slow fall, moderate fall, rapid fall), and explain the weather meaning of each category.

### Why this matters

The barometer is the standard short-term weather-forecasting instrument. The trend (rate of change over time) matters more than the absolute value for any practical decision. A pressure reading of 1010 hPa means little on its own; the same reading falling at 1 hPa per hour means a fast-moving weather system is approaching.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, the barometer trend feeds directly into the route-card decision criteria. A rising trend means proceed; a rapid fall means initiate an escape route. The trend is the input; the decision framework in D6.2 is the output.

### Pressure units

Barometric pressure is measured in **hectopascals (hPa)** or equivalently **millibars (mb)**. The two units are interchangeable; 1 hPa = 1 mb. Most modern altimeters and weather forecasts use hPa.

The standard sea-level reference pressure is **1013.25 hPa**. Real readings vary around this value depending on weather and altitude. A reading of 950 hPa at sea level indicates very low pressure (a deep depression); 1030 hPa indicates high pressure (a stable anticyclone).

For trend reading, the absolute value is less important than the direction and rate of change.

### The five trend categories

The IMS Nav Training Programme classifies trends into five categories:

| Trend | Rate | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Rising** | Any rate | High pressure building; weather improving. |
| **Steady** | Stable for more than 3 hours | Stable conditions; pressure systems not changing fast. |
| **Slow fall** | Less than 0.5 hPa per hour | Mild deterioration likely. |
| **Moderate fall** | 0.5 to 1 hPa per hour | Significant system approaching. |
| **Rapid fall** | More than 1 hPa per hour | Fast-moving system; serious deterioration imminent. |

The rate is what matters. A 6 hPa fall over 12 hours (0.5 hPa per hour) is moderate; the same 6 hPa fall over 3 hours (2 hPa per hour) is rapid. The decision response to each is very different.

### Reading the trend

To read the trend, the candidate needs at least two pressure readings separated by enough time to detect a change. Practical guidance:

- **At least 1 hour between readings** to detect any change at all.
- **3 hours of consistent direction** before classifying as a settled trend.
- **Multiple readings over 6 to 12 hours** to confirm the rate.

A single reading is a snapshot; a trend requires sequence.

For practical IMS / EMS work, the typical reading cadence is:

- a baseline reading the evening before a tour,
- a reading at departure in the morning,
- hourly readings during the tour at major waypoints (cols, lunch stops, decision points),
- a final reading at the end of the tour for the next-day baseline.

### Worked example

A candidate records the following barometric readings on the day of a tour:

| Time | Pressure (hPa) | Change since previous |
|---|---|---|
| 6am (departure) | 1018 | (baseline) |
| 9am (col) | 1016 | -2 hPa over 3 hours |
| Noon (summit) | 1014 | -2 hPa over 3 hours |
| 3pm (hut) | 1012 | -2 hPa over 3 hours |

Each 3-hour window shows a 2 hPa drop, which is approximately 0.67 hPa per hour. That sits in the **moderate fall** band.

The candidate has been seeing significant deterioration through the day. The IMS Nav Programme decision framework (D6.2) recommends "consider early descent; assess escape routes" for moderate fall. By 3pm the candidate has already arrived at the hut, but if the trend continues into the evening it will be the input for tomorrow's go / no-go decision.

### Why this matters for IMS

Barometric trend reading is the input to the IMS / EMS-relevant decision framework in D6.2. A candidate who can classify a trend rapidly and reliably has the input for the framework; a candidate who cannot has nothing to feed the framework with. The three-day log is the practical drill that builds the trend-reading instinct.
