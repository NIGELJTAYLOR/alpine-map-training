### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to deploy the pole-and-cord technique for navigating in extreme low visibility (less than 5 to 10 m) with terrain hazards, identify the scenarios where the technique adds value beyond a partner used as an intermediate aiming point, and recognise the BASI / IMS framing of this as a technique to know but rarely deliberately use.

### Why this matters

In extreme low visibility (whiteout, dense fog, flat light on a featureless snow slope) the candidate may not have any visible feature within walking distance. A partner sent forward becomes the aiming point, but the partner can only be sent as far as they remain visible.

Where the terrain has hidden hazards (a drop, a cornice margin, a crevasse - though crevasse risk is out of IMS scope, the technique transfers), sending a partner forward alone is risky. The pole-and-cord technique provides a physical extension that confirms direction and helps detect the hazard before the partner steps into it.

This is an IMS-specific technique. The IMS Nav Training Programme references it explicitly. The Glenmore Lodge YouTube material demonstrates it on snow. The technique is one to know even if the candidate rarely uses it; the assessor may probe.

### The technique

The setup:

- Tie 5 to 6 m of thin cord to the end of a ski pole.
- The lead navigator holds the pole; one group member (the "scout") holds the other end of the cord.

The procedure:

1. The lead navigator sets the bearing on the compass.
2. The scout walks forward on the bearing, paying out the cord as they go.
3. The cord lies on the snow in the direction of travel; this provides a physical confirmation that the scout is walking on the correct line.
4. At the end of the cord (5 to 6 m), the scout stops.
5. The lead navigator walks forward to join the scout, picking up the cord as they go.
6. From the new position, the procedure repeats.

The cord on the snow serves three functions:

- **Direction confirmation:** the cord lies on the bearing, visually confirming the scout's line.
- **Physical extension:** the scout is at the end of the cord, not at the end of the partner's reach. The cord allows the scout to test the ground forward of their position before the lead navigator arrives.
- **Drop detection:** if the scout steps onto a sudden drop or cornice margin, the cord tension changes; the lead navigator can pull the scout back.

The technique is slow (5 to 6 m at a time over potentially hundreds of metres) but it is reliable in conditions where no other technique works.

### When to use it

The pole-and-cord technique is appropriate when **all three** of the following hold:

- visibility is **less than 5 to 10 m** (the partner is not visible at the standard intermediate-aiming-point distance),
- the terrain has **hidden hazards** (drops, cornices, crevasse margins, sudden steepening),
- and a **bearing-walked approach is the only available navigation option** (no GPS available; aspect-of-slope relocation has fixed position but the next leg requires walking on a bearing).

If any of these conditions does not hold, simpler techniques are usually preferable:

- If visibility allows a partner to be seen at 20 m or more, use the partner as an intermediate aiming point (D2.3).
- If the terrain is benign (no hidden hazards), the cord adds no value over a partner alone.
- If the candidate can avoid moving entirely (shelter and wait for visibility), that is often the better choice.

The IMS Nav Training Programme treats this technique as one to know but rarely deliberately use. The assessor may ask about it; the candidate's answer should reflect the conditional use.

### Worked example

A candidate is leading a group on a descent in flat light. Visibility drops to approximately 5 m. The map shows a cornice along the ridge crest the group is crossing, with steep ground to the east.

The candidate stops the group and assesses. The bearing for the next leg (away from the cornice and into a safer descent line) is 195 magnetic. The visibility is too low for a partner to walk safely as an aiming point; the cornice risk means stepping forward without verification could put the scout into the cornice or onto the steep slope.

The candidate deploys the pole-and-cord technique:

1. The pole is tied to the cord end; a group member is briefed as the scout.
2. The scout sets off on the 195 bearing, paying out the cord. After 5 m the scout stops.
3. The candidate verifies the cord lies on the bearing (visual confirmation in the few metres of visibility available).
4. The scout probes the snow ahead with their pole before stepping. The cord margin gives the lead navigator time to react if the scout finds a drop.
5. The candidate walks forward to join the scout.
6. The procedure repeats for the next 5 m.

After approximately 30 m of progress (six iterations), the group reaches a position where the visibility allows a partner to be seen at 20 m. The technique is wound up; the group continues with intermediate aiming points.

Total time for the 30 m: approximately 10 minutes. Slow, but the cornice was successfully avoided and no member was at risk of stepping into it.

### Why this matters for IMS

The pole-and-cord technique is the IMS-specific technique that the assessor may probe with a "how would you handle X" question. A candidate who knows the technique, knows when it is appropriate, and knows when it is not, has the working assessment-standard response. A candidate who has never heard of it cannot answer the question; a candidate who would use it for every poor-visibility leg has misunderstood the conditional use.
