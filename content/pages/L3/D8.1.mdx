### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to apply the IMS Nav Programme whiteout sequence when visibility drops during a tour, distinguish between deliberate setting-out in poor visibility (out of IMS scope) and managing poor visibility encountered en route (in IMS scope), and explain why a pre-prepared route card is the prerequisite for poor-visibility navigation.

### Why this matters

Poor visibility is the alpine reality the candidate must be ready for. Snow, cloud, fog and flat light are common features of mountain weather, especially on long days when the morning forecast no longer matches the afternoon conditions. The IMS / EMS candidate is expected to handle visibility drops without losing the day or the group.

The scope distinction is important. The IMS / EMS pathway does not test the candidate's willingness to set out in deliberately bad conditions; it tests the candidate's ability to manage conditions that develop unexpectedly. A candidate who plans a tour to be skied in zero visibility has misunderstood the scope. A candidate who plans a tour for good conditions and handles a sudden white-down at the col has understood the scope and the test.

### The IMS Nav Programme whiteout sequence

When visibility drops mid-tour, the IMS Nav Training Programme prescribes a six-step sequence:

1. **Stop.** Do not continue navigating without confirming position. The first reaction to a visibility drop is to keep moving on the assumption that things will improve; this is the wrong reaction. Stop first.
2. **Get the group in a tight cluster.** A scattered group in poor visibility loses members quickly. Bring the group together and keep them together.
3. **Confirm the last known position on the route card.** The route card was prepared in good conditions before the day; it is the trusted reference. Identify the last waypoint the group passed through.
4. **Take the bearing to the next waypoint from the route card.** The bearing was calculated and written before departure; it is reliable. The candidate sets the compass to that bearing now.
5. **Note the target altitude from the route card.** Same principle: the target altitude was determined before the day. The altimeter is the arrival check.
6. **Move forward on the bearing**, with the group moving together. Use intermediate aiming points where any features are visible at all; use the pole-and-cord technique (D8.2) for extreme low visibility with terrain hazards.

The sequence works because the route card was prepared in advance. A candidate who has not prepared a route card has nothing reliable to fall back on; the in-the-moment improvisation that follows is the worst possible response to a visibility drop.

### The pre-prepared route card as the foundation

The candidate's route card is the foundation of poor-visibility navigation. Before the day, in good light at the desk, the candidate has:

- identified each leg of the tour,
- calculated the grid and magnetic bearing for each leg,
- noted the target altitude for each waypoint,
- recorded the distance and estimated time for each leg,
- identified key terrain features along each leg,
- and noted decision-point criteria.

In poor visibility on the day, the candidate refers to the route card and follows it. The bearings are pre-calculated; the target altitudes are pre-noted; the candidate's job is to execute, not to plan.

A candidate who tries to take a fresh bearing from the map in poor visibility, with cold fingers and a panicked group, will produce a bearing that is wrong. The pre-prepared route card removes this failure mode entirely.

D9 (route cards) covers the pre-preparation in detail. D8.1 assumes the route card exists.

### Worked example

A candidate is leading a group on a tour. The morning forecast was for clear conditions until late afternoon. At 2pm, mid-tour, cloud descends rapidly and visibility drops to approximately 30 m.

The candidate applies the whiteout sequence:

1. **Stop.** Calls the group together at the current position.
2. **Group in a tight cluster.** All four members within arm's reach of each other.
3. **Last known position confirmed.** The group passed the col at 2,720 m approximately 15 minutes ago. Current position is approximately 200 m past the col on the bearing for the next leg.
4. **Bearing to next waypoint from the route card.** The next waypoint is the hut at 2,450 m. The route card has the bearing as 218 grid, 220 magnetic.
5. **Target altitude noted.** 2,450 m. The current altimeter reads 2,650 m, so approximately 200 m of descent remaining.
6. **Move forward.** The group sets off on the 220 magnetic bearing. The candidate uses the second group member as an intermediate aiming point at 20 m visibility distance. Pace count maintained for distance estimation. Altimeter checked every 50 m of altitude descent.

The group reaches the hut after approximately 35 minutes. The altimeter at arrival reads 2,455 m (within 5 m of the target; within calibration tolerance). The hut is identified visually at approximately 15 m range. Leg complete.

The IMS / EMS-relevant detail: at no point did the candidate attempt to take a fresh bearing or re-plan the route. The route card was the reference; the field execution was mechanical.

### Why this matters for IMS

The whiteout sequence is the IMS / EMS-relevant standard for poor-visibility management. The assessor will probe with weather-deterioration scenarios; the candidate who refers to the pre-prepared route card and applies the sequence is at the working assessment standard. The candidate who improvises under pressure is not.

The route card is the artefact that makes the sequence work. D9 covers route cards in detail; D8.1 assumes the candidate is moving towards the discipline of writing them.
