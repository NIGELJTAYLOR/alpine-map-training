### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to take a magnetic bearing in the field from your current position to any visible feature, convert it to a grid bearing using magnetic variation, and use that grid bearing to identify the feature on the map.

### Why this matters

Taking a bearing to a feature is the inverse of taking a bearing from the map. It is what the candidate does when:

- standing at a known position and trying to identify an unfamiliar feature on the skyline,
- confirming that a visible feature is the one they expected from the route card,
- or providing the inputs to a resection (D3.3) for a position fix.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, this is the field-side complement of the route-card preparation skill. The route card has the bearing on it before the day; the field check has the bearing taken on the day.

### The procedure

Standing on the ground, looking at the feature:

1. **Hold the compass flat in front of you.**
2. **Point the direction-of-travel arrow at the feature.** Sight along the baseplate edge and align the arrow with the feature.
3. **Rotate the bezel until the orienting arrow aligns with the magnetic needle.** The needle goes into the orienting arrow; the bezel rotates around it.
4. **Read the bearing from the direction-of-travel arrow against the bezel scale.** This is the magnetic bearing from your position to the feature.

To convert this magnetic bearing to a grid bearing for use on the map, apply the variation in the opposite direction from D2.2:

- **Variation is east:** subtract the variation from the magnetic bearing to get the grid bearing.
- **Variation is west:** add the variation to the magnetic bearing to get the grid bearing.

The mnemonic from D2.2 ("grid to mag, add" when east) reverses for the inverse direction ("mag to grid, get rid" when east means subtract).

### Common error: parallax

The single most common D3.1 error is parallax: the candidate's eye is not aligned with the feature when they sight along the baseplate edge. The result is a bearing that is several degrees off, with no obvious cause.

The fix is a sighting habit: hold the compass at chest height with both hands, square to the body, and look along the baseplate edge with one eye closed. A sighting compass (with a built-in mirror) makes this easier but is not essential.

### Worked example

A candidate stands at a col at 2,720 m on a 1:25,000 alpine sheet. A distinctive rock outcrop is visible to the south-west. The current magnetic variation for the area is +2 degrees east (verified using BGS WMM).

Working through the procedure:

1. Holds the compass flat, points the direction-of-travel arrow at the rock outcrop.
2. Rotates the bezel until the orienting arrow is aligned with the magnetic needle.
3. Reads the magnetic bearing: 222 degrees.
4. Converts to grid: 222 - 2 = **220 degrees** grid bearing from the col to the rock outcrop.

The candidate then takes the compass to the map. Placing the baseplate edge from the col with the direction-of-travel arrow on the 220 grid bearing, the rock outcrop on the map should lie along that line. Confirms: yes, a rock outcrop is marked on the map at approximately 1.4 km from the col on the 220 line.

The unidentified feature on the skyline is now identified.

### Self-check

- [ ] I can convert magnetic to grid using the right rule for my local variation.

- [ ] I can distinguish two features on the same bearing line.

### Why this matters for IMS

Field-bearing reading is the IMS / EMS-relevant skill for confirming that visible features match the route card. The assessor may point at a distant ridge and ask the candidate to identify it; a candidate who can take a bearing, convert to grid, and identify on the map within a minute is at the working standard. After D3.1 the candidate has the inverse-of-D2 technique; D3.2 adds back bearings as a sanity check.
