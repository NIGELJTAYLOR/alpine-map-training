### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to read a bearing in degrees from a set compass to within 2 degrees, hold the compass correctly (flat, away from steel objects), and recognise when the magnetic needle is sluggish or settling rather than reading the figure.

### Why this matters

A bearing is only useful if it has been read accurately. The assessor will accept a bearing read within about 2 degrees of the correct value at IMS / EMS level; further than that, errors compound rapidly over the length of a leg. A bearing that is 3 degrees off produces approximately 26 m of lateral drift over a 500 m leg, which is enough to miss a col, a hut, or a critical waypoint.

The reading itself is simple; the discipline around the reading is what makes it reliable.

### Reading the bearing

The bearing is shown by the angular position of the direction-of-travel arrow against the bezel scale. To read:

1. Hold the compass flat in front of you.
2. Identify the line on the bezel that aligns with the direction-of-travel arrow. (Most compasses have a small index mark at this point.)
3. Read the bearing in degrees from the bezel scale at that line.

A bearing is always a number between 0 and 360 degrees. Cardinal directions correspond to:

- North: 0 (or 360, equivalently).
- East: 90.
- South: 180.
- West: 270.

Intercardinal directions sit between: NE = 45, SE = 135, SW = 225, NW = 315.

The C4.2 eight-direction aspect vocabulary maps onto these figures directly. A north-facing slope from C4.2 has an aspect bearing of 0 (or 360). A slope falling to the south-east has an aspect bearing of 135.

### Holding the compass correctly

Three habits make compass readings reliable:

- **Hold the compass flat.** A tilted compass floor lets the magnetic needle drag against the bezel, producing slow or sticky readings. Use the back of one hand as a working surface, or rest the compass on a glove or notebook.
- **Hold the compass away from steel.** Ski bindings, ice axes, mobile phones and radio handsets all create magnetic fields that deflect the needle. Minimum safe distance: 30 cm from a phone or radio; 50 cm from steel objects. (D4.3 covers magnetic interference in detail.)
- **Wait for the needle to settle.** A freshly-handled compass needle takes several seconds to come to rest. Reading the figure before the needle has stopped moving produces a wrong bearing. In cold conditions, settling can take longer than usual; allow up to 10 seconds in temperatures well below freezing.

### Recognising a sluggish needle

A magnetic needle that swings sluggishly, fails to settle, or settles in different positions on repeated readings is telling you something. Three causes worth knowing:

- **Cold temperature.** Compass damping fluid thickens in cold. Allow extra settling time; if the needle is so sluggish that the compass is unusable, warm it inside a glove for a minute.
- **Magnetic interference.** A nearby steel or electronic object is deflecting the needle. Move the compass away from the suspect source and re-read; if the bearing changes, the source is confirmed.
- **Damaged compass.** A compass that has been dropped, sat on, or pierced may have a damaged needle pivot or compromised damping fluid. A compass that no longer settles reliably should be replaced.

The quick-check before any field use is to set a known direction (a building edge, a road, a ridge crest you can see) and confirm the compass agrees. Disagreement is the cue to investigate before the bearing matters.

### Worked example

A candidate sets their compass to take a bearing in still air at room temperature. The bezel is rotated until the needle settles inside the orienting arrow. The direction-of-travel arrow now points to the index mark on the bezel.

1. The candidate reads the bezel value at the index mark: 142 degrees.
2. The candidate cross-checks against a sighting on a wall feature known to be roughly south-east. 142 degrees is consistent with south-east (135 degrees), so the reading is plausible.
3. The candidate re-takes the bearing from the same position to confirm it falls within 2 degrees of the first reading: 143 degrees on the second take. Acceptable.

The bearing is recorded as 142 degrees, with the second-take confirmation noted in the candidate's working.

### Why this matters for IMS

A bearing read within 2 degrees in under 60 seconds is the working standard at IMS / EMS level. The reading discipline (flat, away from steel, settled needle, quick-check) is what produces that standard reliably. After D1.2 the candidate is ready to apply the compass to the map in D2.1, where the bearing is no longer an arbitrary figure but a route-relevant direction between two named points.
