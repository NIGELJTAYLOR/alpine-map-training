### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to identify the standard fields of an IMS-style route card, explain the function of each field, and recognise the two fields that most commonly get under-described in candidate route cards.

### Why this matters

A route card is only useful if it captures the right information in the right form. A card with vague entries (or with key fields missing) is no better than no card at all in poor visibility. A card with complete, specific entries is the foundation of every subsequent IMS / EMS-relevant practice from D8 onwards.

For the candidate, knowing the fields makes the writing in D9.2 efficient. The writing time in D9.2 is targeted at 20 minutes for a six-leg card; that target only works if the candidate knows which fields go where without thinking about them.

### The standard fields

The IMS Nav Training Programme route card template (Quick Reference Card 3) uses the following per-leg fields:

- **Leg number.** The position of the leg in the day's tour (Leg 1 of 6, Leg 2 of 6, etc.).
- **Start point and altitude.** The named feature where the leg begins and its elevation.
- **End point and altitude.** The named feature where the leg ends and its elevation.
- **Compass bearing (magnetic).** The bearing the candidate sets on the compass to walk the leg. This is the magnetic bearing; the underlying grid bearing may also be recorded for cross-check.
- **Distance (map).** The horizontal distance of the leg as measured on the map.
- **Estimated time.** The expected duration of the leg in minutes.
- **Altimeter trigger height.** The altimeter reading at which the candidate stops or changes action. For a leg ending at a waypoint, this is the target altitude. For a leg with an intermediate trigger (descend to 2,500 m then bear right), this is the trigger altitude.
- **Key terrain features.** The recognisable features the candidate expects to encounter or pass; used for confirmation in good visibility and for orientation in poor.
- **Avalanche assessment.** The slope angle, aspect, and avalanche-relevant terrain considerations for the leg. Built using the C1.2, C4.2, and C6.1 / C6.2 vocabulary.
- **Decision point criteria.** The pre-agreed criteria for go / no-go decisions during the leg. Covered in D9.3.
- **Escape route.** The contingency route if the leg cannot be completed as planned. Covered in D9.4.

A complete card has all eleven fields populated for every leg. A six-leg tour produces six rows of eleven entries each.

### Two most commonly under-described fields

Across most candidates' route cards, two fields are typically under-described:

- **Key terrain features.** Many candidates note one obvious feature (the col, the summit) and skip the smaller features that would actually confirm position en route (a stream crossing at 2,400 m, a slope-angle change at 2,650 m, a forest edge below the col). The fix is to walk the leg in imagination and note every feature the candidate would expect to see.
- **Avalanche assessment.** Many candidates write a single phrase ("30 degrees north-facing") and skip the route-relevant detail (where on the leg the slope is steepest; whether the leg crosses a runout zone; whether the line is above or below a cornice-prone ridge). The fix is to apply the C6.2 terrain-trap reading to the leg explicitly.

These two fields are also the ones the IMS / EMS assessor scrutinises most carefully. A candidate whose key-terrain field reads "col at 2,720 m" has done one tenth of the work; a candidate whose field reads "stream crossing at 2,400 m, bench at 2,540 m, slope steepens past 2,600 m, col at 2,720 m" has done the work the assessor expects.

### Worked example

A candidate plans a leg from a hut at 2,340 m to a col at 2,720 m. Distance approximately 1.4 km; expected ascent rate 400 m per hour for a moderate ski-touring pace. Bearing 047 magnetic.

A complete route-card entry for the leg:

| Field | Entry |
|---|---|
| Leg number | Leg 2 of 6 |
| Start | Refuge des Ecrins, 2,340 m |
| End | Col du Grand Plan, 2,720 m |
| Compass bearing (magnetic) | 047 |
| Distance | 1.4 km |
| Estimated time | 60 minutes |
| Altimeter trigger height | 2,720 m (target altitude at col) |
| Key terrain features | Stream crossing at 2,400 m; slope steepens past 2,540 m; bench at 2,610 m; col saddle visible from 2,700 m |
| Avalanche assessment | NW-facing slope; angle 22 to 25 degrees on lower section, 28 to 32 degrees from 2,540 m; danger Considerable; stay on ridge crest above 2,600 m to avoid the bowl on the left |
| Decision point criteria | (covered in D9.3) |
| Escape route | (covered in D9.4) |

The eleven fields are populated; the candidate has a working entry for one leg. Six such entries make the card.

### Why this matters for IMS

The route card is the IMS / EMS assessor's primary planning artefact. A candidate whose card has all eleven fields populated for every leg is at the working assessment standard; a candidate whose card has gaps or vague entries is not. After D9.1 the candidate has the field vocabulary; D9.2 covers using it under speed pressure.
