### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should understand that altimeter readings drift with weather pressure changes even when the candidate is stationary, run a 7-day altimeter day log to build the instinct for trusting or distrusting the altimeter, and read altimeter behaviour as a weather signal alongside its use as an altitude reference.

### Why this matters

The altimeter drift discussed in D5.1 is not just an error to be corrected at calibration; it is information about the weather. A falling barometer makes the altimeter over-read, which means the apparent altitude is rising when the candidate is stationary or descending. A rising barometer makes it under-read, so the apparent altitude is falling.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, the altimeter is therefore two instruments in one: an altitude reference (when calibrated and read against known points) and a weather indicator (when its drift is noticed and interpreted). The route card should reflect both uses.

### The pressure-altitude relationship

The standard rule of thumb from D5.1: **1 hectopascal (hPa) of pressure change is equivalent to approximately 8 metres of apparent altitude change**.

The relationship is most accurate in the lower troposphere (the relevant range for alpine work). At very high altitudes the relationship changes because air density falls; at very low altitudes the rule is also slightly off. For 1,000 m to 3,500 m alpine work, 1 hPa = 8 m is reliable.

The direction of the relationship:

- **Falling pressure** (incoming low pressure, deteriorating weather): altimeter reads **higher** than the true altitude. A stationary altimeter shows apparent altitude gain.
- **Rising pressure** (high pressure building, improving weather): altimeter reads **lower** than the true altitude. A stationary altimeter shows apparent altitude loss.

The mnemonic is: **the altimeter overstates altitude when the weather understates the pressure.** Or more memorably: weather going down, altimeter going up.

### Reading the altimeter as weather

The IMS / EMS-relevant use is to notice altimeter drift and apply it. Three patterns:

- **Stationary candidate noticing apparent altitude gain.** Pressure is falling. Weather is deteriorating. Note the rate (m per hour) and apply the IMS Nav Programme decision framework (covered formally in D6.2).
- **Recalibration showing the altimeter reads higher than the known point.** Same diagnosis: pressure has fallen since the last calibration; weather is deteriorating.
- **Apparent altitude rising during a known descent.** Pressure is falling fast enough to overcome the descent. Significant weather event in progress.

The rate of drift is the diagnostic. A 5 m drift over an hour is unremarkable. A 20 m drift over an hour is significant. A 50 m drift over an hour is a fast-moving system; consider an early descent or shelter.

### The 7-day altimeter day log

The day log is the calibration of trust. The candidate sets the altimeter to home elevation each morning for 7 days and records the reading. Over the week, the candidate sees:

- which days the altimeter was steady (stable weather: trust the altimeter heavily on tour),
- which days the altimeter drifted significantly (active weather: recalibrate frequently on tour),
- and what kinds of weather correlate with what kinds of drift.

The candidate emerges from the 7-day log with an instinct for when to trust the altimeter as an altitude reference and when to read it primarily as a weather signal.

### Worked example

A candidate is at a hut overnight at a known elevation of 2,340 m. The candidate sets the altimeter to 2,340 m at 6pm.

The next morning at 6am, before leaving the hut, the candidate reads the altimeter: 2,395 m.

The candidate has not moved. The apparent altitude gain of 55 m implies a pressure drop of approximately 55 / 8 = nearly 7 hPa over 12 hours. That is a significant drop; weather is deteriorating, possibly substantially.

Three responses:

1. **Recalibrate** the altimeter to 2,340 m before departing.
2. **Update the route card** with the weather observation. The day's plan may need to change.
3. **Apply the decision framework** (D6.2): a 7 hPa drop in 12 hours is a moderate-to-fast deterioration; consider an early turn-around criterion or an alternative day plan.

The candidate now has both the corrected altimeter (for the day's altitude readings) and the weather observation (for the day's route decisions).

### Why this matters for IMS

The altimeter as a weather signal is one of the IMS / EMS-relevant integration moments: a single instrument provides both an altitude reading and a weather observation, and the candidate uses both on the route card. A candidate who notices a 50 m apparent gain at the morning hut without responding has not internalised the dual use. The 7-day day log builds the instinct that turns the abstract knowledge into a working habit.
