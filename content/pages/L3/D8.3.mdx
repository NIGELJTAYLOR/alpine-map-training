### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to apply Mountaineering Scotland's calm, systematic relocation procedure when you become uncertain of position, communicate "temporarily unsure of position" to a group without losing their confidence, and recognise that calm systematic relocation is the IMS / EMS-relevant skill rather than the avoidance of all uncertainty.

### Why this matters

Becoming temporarily unsure of position is normal in mountain navigation. It happens to experienced navigators in good visibility; it happens more often in poor visibility. The IMS / EMS skill is not avoiding uncertainty entirely; it is responding to uncertainty calmly and systematically.

The candidate who never admits to being uncertain is a candidate who continues on a wrong bearing for ten minutes longer than they should. The candidate who panics in front of the group is a candidate the group loses confidence in. The candidate who stops, communicates calmly, and works through a relocation procedure is the candidate the assessor is looking for.

Mountaineering Scotland's "when navigation goes wrong" page is the published source for the procedure. It is consistent with general navigation pedagogy at Mountain Leader and IMS / EMS levels.

### The systematic relocation procedure

When uncertain of position:

1. **Stop.** Do not continue walking. Continuing means moving further from the last known position, making relocation harder.
2. **Mark the position** mentally and (if available) with a GPS waypoint. The GPS waypoint is for backup, not for primary use; the relocation should be possible with map and compass alone.
3. **Note the last confirmed position and the elapsed time** since that position. This is the search starting point: the candidate is somewhere within the area reachable from the last confirmed position in the elapsed time.
4. **Take a fall-line bearing of the slope.** This is the aspect-of-slope technique from D7.2. The aspect bearing constrains the position to a specific kind of slope.
5. **Match to the map** using the compass perpendicular to contours (the D7.2 procedure). This identifies the possible positions consistent with the aspect.
6. **Check the altimeter.** The altitude reading refines the position fix to a unique point on the matching slope.
7. **Confirm with any visible feature.** If even one feature is visible (a small rock, a colour change in the snow, a distant ridge), use it as a cross-check.
8. **Plan the next leg from the new fix.** Once the position is established, the route resumes from the new known point.

The procedure typically takes 2 to 5 minutes. It is slower than continuing on the assumption that everything is fine, but it is the only way to recover when "everything is fine" turns out not to be true.

### The leadership dimension

A trip leader's response to uncertainty affects the whole group. A panicked leader produces a panicked group; a calm leader produces a calm group, even when the situation is genuinely uncertain.

Useful language patterns:

- **"I am temporarily unsure of position. I am stopping to relocate."** This is the professional formulation. It admits the uncertainty (which builds trust) and states the action (which builds confidence).
- **"The last confirmed position was [X], approximately [Y] minutes ago. I expect to relocate within a few minutes."** This frames the situation as a managed problem, not a crisis.
- **"In the meantime, please stay together at this point."** This gives the group a clear instruction and prevents them from wandering.

Avoid:

- **"I think we might be lost."** "Lost" is not professional language; it suggests there is no plan. "Temporarily unsure" suggests a known process is underway.
- **"Let me just check the map."** Vague; does not communicate the action.
- **Continuing to walk while consulting the map.** Suggests the candidate is hoping the situation will resolve itself.

The IMS / EMS assessor will watch for the leadership dimension during route narration. A candidate who can communicate "temporarily unsure" calmly is at the working assessment standard.

### Worked example

A candidate is leading a tour. After approximately 40 minutes of walking on a bearing in moderate visibility (approximately 100 m), the candidate notices that the expected col is not visible at the expected time. The altimeter reads 2,650 m; the col was expected at 2,720 m at this point.

The candidate applies the procedure:

1. **Stop.** Calls the group together.
2. **GPS waypoint marked** as a backup. (The candidate does not consult the GPS for the relocation; it is a backup if all else fails.)
3. **Last confirmed position:** the bench at 2,540 m, 40 minutes ago.
4. **Fall-line bearing of the current slope:** 095 magnetic. Variation +2 east; grid bearing = 093 (east-facing).
5. **Match to the map.** The east-facing slope below the col is the candidate-set; the perpendicular-to-contour bearing of 093 grid is consistent with that slope.
6. **Altimeter reads 2,650 m.** On the east-facing slope below the col, 2,650 m is the contour the candidate is on.
7. **Confirm with visible feature.** A small rock outcrop is visible at approximately 80 m to the south. The candidate identifies it on the map: a marked rock at 2,640 m on the east-facing slope. Cross-check confirms the position.
8. **Plan the next leg.** The col is approximately 250 m to the north-west, climbing 70 m. The candidate sets a new bearing to the col and continues.

The relocation took approximately 4 minutes. The candidate's communication to the group was: "I am temporarily unsure of position. The last confirmed point was the bench at 2,540 m. I am stopping to relocate; expect a few minutes." After the fix, the candidate confirmed: "We are at 2,650 m on the slope below the col. The col is approximately 250 m to the north-west. We continue."

The group remained calm throughout. The candidate's confidence after the fix was higher than before because the fresh position was now confirmed rather than assumed.

### Why this matters for IMS

The leadership dimension of poor-visibility navigation is one of the IMS / EMS-relevant moments the assessor will watch for. A candidate who can communicate "temporarily unsure" calmly, run the systematic procedure, and resume the route with confidence is at the working assessment standard. A candidate who panics in front of the group, or who continues walking on a wrong bearing rather than admit uncertainty, is not.
