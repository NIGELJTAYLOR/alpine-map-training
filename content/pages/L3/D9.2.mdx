### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to write a complete route card for a six-leg tour from your exam-area map within the IMS Nav Programme target of 20 minutes, self-mark against a reference card written by an experienced navigator (or by your trainer), and identify the three slowest legs and what made them slow.

### Why this matters

The route card writing speed is one of the IMS Nav Programme assessment-checklist standards: complete a full tour route card within 20 minutes total. The 20-minute target works out to approximately 200 seconds per leg for a six-leg tour, which is achievable with practice but not without it.

For the IMS / EMS candidate, the speed standard matters because route cards are written under time pressure on real tour days (the morning of the tour, the evening before, sometimes during a brief stop mid-tour to revise the plan). A candidate who needs an hour to write a card has a card that is often unfinished; a candidate who needs 20 minutes has a card that is reliably complete.

### The procedure

Writing a complete route card for a six-leg tour:

1. **Identify the legs.** Decide where the tour starts, where it ends, and the meaningful waypoints in between. Six legs is a typical full-day tour; some are shorter, some longer.
2. **For each leg, take the bearing** using the D2.1 four-step procedure. Apply the variation (D2.2). Record both grid and magnetic if the candidate's card has space; magnetic is what the candidate uses in the field.
3. **Record the target altitude** for each leg. This is the altimeter trigger height for arrival at the next waypoint.
4. **Estimate distance and time.** Distance is measured on the map at the map's scale; time is calculated using the candidate's typical ski-touring pace (often 300 to 400 m per hour ascent rate; 1 to 2 km per hour horizontal on snow).
5. **Note key terrain features.** Walk the leg in imagination and list every feature the candidate would expect to see, in order from start to end.
6. **Write the avalanche assessment.** Apply C6.2 terrain-trap reading to the leg explicitly.
7. **Identify decision points** (D9.3).
8. **Identify escape routes** (D9.4).
9. **Self-check the card.** Read through every leg; confirm all eleven fields are populated; confirm the bearings and altitudes are consistent with the start and end points.

The procedure is deliberate; the speed comes from familiarity. A candidate who has written a dozen route cards can run the procedure on autopilot; a candidate writing their first card will find every step takes longer than they expected.

### The 20-minute target

The IMS Nav Programme assessment-checklist target is to complete a full tour route card within 20 minutes. For a six-leg card, this is approximately 200 seconds per leg.

Within the 200 seconds, time breaks down approximately:

- Identify start and end points: 20 seconds.
- Take bearing using D2.1: 60 seconds.
- Apply variation: 10 seconds.
- Record target altitude, distance, time: 30 seconds.
- Write key terrain features: 40 seconds.
- Write avalanche assessment: 30 seconds.
- (Decision points and escape routes added separately, in D9.3 and D9.4.)

The 20-minute target is the working IMS / EMS standard. A candidate consistently above 30 minutes is not yet at standard; one consistently above 45 minutes has serious procedural inefficiencies and should recycle the underlying skills.

### Worked example

A candidate plans a six-leg tour from a lift station at 1,850 m through a col at 2,720 m, a summit at 2,980 m, a hut at 2,450 m, a second col at 2,610 m, and back to the lift station via a different descent line.

The candidate sets a 20-minute timer and works through the procedure:

- Minute 0 to 3: identifies the six legs and writes start / end points and altitudes for each.
- Minute 3 to 11: takes six bearings using the D2.1 procedure; applies variation; records target altitudes.
- Minute 11 to 15: estimates distances and times for each leg.
- Minute 15 to 19: writes key terrain features and avalanche assessments.
- Minute 19 to 20: self-checks the card.

Total: 20 minutes. The card has six entries with the nine basic fields populated. Decision points and escape routes are added in D9.3 and D9.4.

If the candidate runs over 20 minutes, the slowest sections are usually the bearing-taking (if D2.1 is not yet automatic) and the avalanche-assessment writing (if C6.2 is not yet fluent). Recycle the underlying skill rather than just trying to write faster.

### Exercise 1 - Speed-test exercise

Set a 20-minute timer. Write a complete route card for a six-leg tour from your exam-area map. Use the table format from D9.1 Exercise 3 (eleven fields per leg; decision points and escape routes can be filled in later in D9.3 and D9.4).

The card should have six entries. Time yourself.

1. Time taken: __________ minutes
2. Number of legs completed within the 20 minutes: __________
3. Were all nine basic fields (excluding decision points and escape routes) populated for each completed leg? __________

### Exercise 2 - Self-mark against a reference

Compare your card with a reference card for the same tour, written by an experienced navigator or by your trainer.

| Comparison criterion | Result |
|---|---|
| Bearings within 2 degrees of the reference | ________ legs out of ________ |
| Target altitudes match reference | ________ legs out of ________ |
| Distance estimates within 10% of the reference | ________ legs out of ________ |
| Time estimates within 15% of the reference | ________ legs out of ________ |
| Key-terrain features at least as detailed as the reference | ________ legs out of ________ |
| Avalanche assessment at least as detailed as the reference | ________ legs out of ________ |

### Exercise 3 - Identify the three slowest legs

For your card from Exercise 1, identify the three legs that took the longest to write.

1. Slowest leg: __________ Reason: __________
2. Second-slowest leg: __________ Reason: __________
3. Third-slowest leg: __________ Reason: __________

For each, identify the underlying skill that needs to improve (D1.1 compass parts? D2.1 bearing procedure? D2.2 variation conversion? C6.2 terrain-trap reading? D5.2 calibration logic?).

### Exercise 4 - The under-description test

Read your card from Exercise 1 against the under-description criteria from D9.1 (key-terrain features and avalanche assessment).

1. Did your key-terrain entries name multiple features per leg, or only the obvious one? __________
2. Did your avalanche entries reference the C6.2 terrain-trap reading, or only the slope angle? __________
3. What two improvements will you make to the next route card you write? __________

### Self-check

- [ ] I can write a complete route card for a six-leg tour within 20 minutes (or have a clear plan to reach the standard with practice).
- [ ] I can self-mark my card against a reference card and identify specific differences.
- [ ] I can identify which legs took the longest to write and trace the slowness to a specific underlying skill.
- [ ] I have read my card against the under-description criteria and identified at least two improvements.
- [ ] I treat the 20-minute target as a working IMS / EMS standard rather than as an aspirational goal.

### Why this matters for IMS

The 20-minute speed standard is one of the IMS / EMS assessable benchmarks from the IMS Nav Programme. A candidate who can produce a complete card within 20 minutes has the planning fluency the assessor expects; a candidate who needs an hour will not finish cards on real tour days and will operate without the route-card foundation that D8 depends on.

After D9.2 the candidate has the basic route card. D9.3 adds decision points and go / no-go criteria; D9.4 adds escape routes and contingency planning. Together these complete the route card as the integrative artefact of Level 3.
