### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to navigate to a known waypoint using compass bearing and altimeter together, where neither alone would be sufficient. You should be able to plan a route leg with both inputs and write a route-card entry that makes both explicit.

### Why this matters

Most poor-visibility legs in alpine terrain require both a direction and an altitude. The bearing keeps the candidate on the right line; the altimeter tells the candidate when to stop. Either tool alone leaves the candidate guessing.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, the combined technique is the standard for any leg to a waypoint that cannot be visually identified at a distance. The route card should record both the bearing and the target altitude for each such leg.

### The technique

To navigate to a known waypoint using both instruments:

1. **Note the target altitude from the map.** This is the altitude the candidate expects to be at when they reach the waypoint.
2. **Set the compass bearing for the leg.** Use the four-step procedure from D2.1, then convert to magnetic for the field.
3. **Walk on the bearing** using the D2.3 walking technique with intermediate aiming points.
4. **Watch the altimeter** at intervals. The reading should rise (or fall) consistently as the candidate progresses.
5. **Stop when the altimeter reaches the target altitude.** The candidate should now be at the waypoint.
6. **Confirm with a terrain feature** if possible. The waypoint may have a recognisable feature (a cairn, a junction, a named landmark) that confirms arrival.

This is the IMS-standard technique for navigating to a col, a hut, or any named waypoint in poor visibility. The bearing handles the direction; the altimeter handles the distance (in altitude terms, which is more reliable than horizontal-distance estimation on snow).

### Why both are needed

- **Bearing alone** tells the candidate the direction but not the distance. On a uniform slope, the candidate could walk past the waypoint without knowing.
- **Altimeter alone** tells the candidate when they have reached the right altitude but not which point on the contour. The candidate could be at the right altitude but on the wrong slope.
- **Bearing plus altimeter** uniquely identifies the waypoint: the right line at the right height.

The technique scales to any waypoint with a known altitude and a known direction from a known starting point. The bearing and the target altitude are written on the route card before departure; the field execution is then mechanical.

### Worked example

A candidate plans a leg from a hut at 2,340 m to a col at 2,720 m. The grid bearing from the hut to the col (taken using the D2.1 procedure) is 045 degrees. The current magnetic variation is +2 degrees east, so the magnetic bearing is 047 degrees.

The route-card entry for the leg:

| Field | Entry |
|---|---|
| Start | Hut, 2,340 m |
| End | Col, 2,720 m |
| Grid bearing | 045 |
| Magnetic bearing | 047 |
| Target altitude | 2,720 m |
| Distance | approximately 1.4 km |
| Estimated time | 60 minutes (typical alpine ascent rate) |
| Notes | Watch for the col's distinctive saddle shape; cornice possible on the lee side |

In the field:

1. Calibrate the altimeter at the hut (set to 2,340 m).
2. Set the compass to the magnetic bearing 047.
3. Walk on the bearing using intermediate aiming points.
4. Check the altimeter every 5 to 10 minutes. After 30 minutes, the altimeter should read approximately 2,500 m (mid-leg); after 60 minutes, approximately 2,700 m.
5. Stop when the altimeter reaches 2,720 m.
6. Look for the col's saddle pattern; if visible, confirmed arrival.

If the altimeter reads 2,720 m but no col is visible, the candidate is either drifting off the bearing (compass error) or the altimeter has drifted (recalibration overdue). Both are recoverable; the action is to take a fresh bearing back to the last known feature and re-orient.

### Why this matters for IMS

The bearing-plus-altimeter technique is the IMS standard for poor-visibility waypoint navigation. The IMS Nav Training Programme cards explicitly call this out as the standard sequence: target altitude noted, compass bearing set, walk on bearing, stop at target altitude, confirm with terrain feature. A candidate who can execute this in the field with both tools, and who can diagnose a disagreement when one occurs, is at the working assessment standard.
