### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to set the altimeter to a known elevation reference at a known position, recognise the need to recalibrate at every reliable known point on a tour, and identify the kinds of points that are reliable for calibration.

### Why this matters

An altimeter that has not been calibrated recently is unreliable. Weather pressure has drifted; the altimeter reading has drifted with it; the candidate is reading the wrong altitude.

The recalibration habit is the discipline that keeps the altimeter useful through a touring day. Best practice is to recalibrate at every reliable known point. The cost is small (15 seconds per calibration); the benefit is large (the altimeter stays accurate to within a few metres throughout the day).

For an IMS / EMS candidate, the recalibration habit is one of the small habits the assessor will watch for. A candidate who calibrates at the morning lift station and never again is using a tool that is correct at 9am and wrong by 5pm.

### Setting the altimeter

Most altimeters have a manual altitude-set function. The procedure varies slightly by model but the principle is the same:

1. Stand at a position where you know the altitude precisely (a marked spot height on the map, a lift station, a hut with a published elevation).
2. Note the known altitude.
3. Use the altimeter's set function to enter the known altitude.
4. The altimeter is now calibrated to that pressure-to-altitude relationship for the current weather.

For digital altimeters, the set function is usually a button sequence; for mechanical (analogue) altimeters, the set function is usually a small adjustment screw or knob. Either way, the procedure is fast (10 to 15 seconds) and should become automatic.

### What makes a known point "reliable"

Not all points on a map are equally reliable for calibration. A reliable known point is one where:

- the altitude is precisely known (within 1 to 2 m), not estimated,
- the candidate is physically standing on the marked altitude (not approximately near it),
- the candidate has high confidence they have correctly identified the point on both the ground and the map.

Examples of reliable known points on a typical tour:

- **Lift stations.** Altitude usually published; the candidate is physically at the published level.
- **Huts and refuges.** Altitude marked on the map; the candidate is at the hut level.
- **Summit spot heights.** Marked spot height; the candidate is at the highest point.
- **Col spot heights.** Marked spot height; the candidate is at the lowest point of the saddle.
- **Valley junctions** with marked altitudes (less common; usually less reliable than the above).

Examples of unreliable points:

- **Approximately at a contour line.** The candidate may be 5 m above or below the contour; calibrating to the contour value introduces a systematic error.
- **A general high point** that is not a marked spot height. The candidate may be at the wrong sub-peak.
- **A path junction** without an altitude marker. Easy to misidentify on the map.

The discipline is to recalibrate only at reliable points. A poor calibration is worse than no calibration because it introduces an error the candidate trusts.

### How often to recalibrate

The IMS Nav Training Programme recommends recalibrating at every reliable known point on the tour. In practice this typically means:

- **Morning calibration** at the start point (lift station, car park, hut).
- **One or two recalibrations** at named features during the ascent (a col, a summit, a hut at lunch).
- **Final calibration** if returning to a known point (lift station for descent).

In stable weather (high pressure), drift between calibrations is small and the habit is mostly precautionary. In active weather (falling barometer, fronts moving through), drift can be 20 to 50 m over a few hours and the habit becomes essential. Either way, the discipline is the same: when you reach a reliable known point, take 15 seconds and reset.

### Worked example

A candidate plans a tour:

- Start at lift station at 1,850 m (morning calibration).
- Climb to col at 2,720 m (recalibration at the col spot height).
- Continue to summit at 2,980 m (recalibration at the summit spot height).
- Descend to hut at 2,450 m (recalibration at the hut elevation).
- Return to lift station at 1,850 m (final calibration cross-check).

Five calibration moments through the day. Each takes 15 seconds. Total time cost: under 90 seconds for the full day.

Between calibrations, the candidate uses the altimeter knowing the reading is accurate to within a few metres (in stable weather) or potentially within 10 to 20 m (in active weather). The recalibration at each known point catches and corrects whatever drift has accumulated.

If the candidate notices that the altimeter reads, for example, 35 m higher than the col spot height when they reach the col, they have learned two things:

1. The altimeter has drifted by 35 m since the morning calibration.
2. The pressure has fallen (35 m apparent gain implies approximately 4 hPa drop), suggesting weather is deteriorating.

Both pieces of information are now on the route card.

### Self-check

- [ ] I can set the altimeter to a known elevation at a known position.
- [ ] I can identify reliable known points for calibration on a 1:25,000 alpine map.
- [ ] I know that approximate-altitude points and unmarked junctions are not reliable for calibration.

### Why this matters for IMS

The recalibration habit is one of the small habits the IMS / EMS assessor will watch for during route narration. A candidate who recalibrates at the col without prompting and notes the discrepancy as a weather observation is at the working assessment standard. A candidate who calibrates only at the start and is then surprised by altimeter readings later in the day has the wrong habit.

After D5.2 the candidate has the calibration discipline that makes the altimeter useful. D5.3 covers the drift behaviour between calibrations and how to read it as weather information. D5.4 covers using the altimeter as the primary navigation tool on featureless terrain.
