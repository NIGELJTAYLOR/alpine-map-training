### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to apply the aiming-off technique to reach a known target by deliberately offsetting your bearing, ensuring you hit a known linear feature on a known side and turn towards the target. You should also be able to choose the safe side of the offset and articulate the trade-off between offset distance and total distance walked.

### Why this matters

A direct bearing to a small target (a hut, a col, a junction) is fragile. A small bearing error or a small pacing error means you arrive close to the target but cannot tell which side you are on. Without that information you cannot reliably correct.

The aiming-off technique solves this by deliberately offsetting the bearing so you arrive at a known linear feature (a ridge, a stream, a track, a cliff base, a contour line) on a known side of the target. Once you arrive at the linear feature, you know which direction to turn.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, aiming off is one of the small habits that turns a fragile route card into a robust one. It is also one of the assessor's favourite questions: "how would you reach this hut in poor visibility?"

### The technique

Three components:

1. **Identify a linear feature** through (or close to) the target. The linear feature should be unmistakable when you reach it: a ridge crest, a stream, a track, a cliff base, a contour line at a specific altitude.
2. **Choose which side of the target to aim for.** This is the safe side: the side without a hazard between you and the target.
3. **Calculate the aim-off bearing.** Take the direct bearing to the target and adjust by the angle that places your line on the chosen side. A typical offset is 5 to 10 degrees, equivalent to a lateral offset of about 10 to 20 m per 100 m of travel.

When you arrive at the linear feature, you turn towards the target along the linear feature. The feature is your handrail to the destination.

### Choosing the safe side

The safe side is the side without a hazard. Examples:

- For a hut at the top of a steep slope below a cliff band: aim off to the side of the cliff band that has a walkable approach. Hitting the cliff band is the wrong side.
- For a col on a ridge: aim off along the ridge, not into either of the two valleys.
- For a junction in a stream: aim off upstream of the junction, so when you hit the stream you walk downstream to the junction. Aiming downstream of the junction means you walk upstream to find it, which is harder.

The safe-side choice is route-specific. There is no general rule beyond "the side with no hazard". The candidate should think it through for every aim-off they plan.

### The trade-off

Aiming off adds distance. A 10-degree offset over a 1 km leg adds approximately 1.5 percent to the total distance walked (about 15 m). The cost is small.

The benefit is reliability. A direct bearing that misses the target by 50 m to an unknown side leaves the candidate guessing. An aim-off that hits the linear feature 50 m from the target on the known side is a clean handrail walk to the destination.

The trade-off is almost always worth it for poor-visibility legs. For good-visibility legs where the target is visible from a distance, the trade-off is less compelling and a direct bearing is fine.

### Worked example

A candidate is planning a leg from a col at 2,720 m to a hut at 2,450 m. The hut sits on a flat shoulder; a stream runs roughly east-west across the shoulder, passing about 50 m north of the hut.

The direct bearing from the col to the hut is 220 degrees grid (from D2.1). The candidate decides to aim off to the **north** (so when they hit the stream they walk **south** along it, downhill, towards the hut).

Aim-off calculation:

- Direct bearing 220 degrees.
- Offset 10 degrees to the north (which on this bearing means subtracting 10): 220 - 10 = 210 degrees.
- The candidate sets 210 grid (212 magnetic with +2 east variation) on the compass and walks the leg.

Arrival: the candidate hits the stream approximately 100 m north of the hut. They turn left along the stream and walk downhill until they reach the hut.

If they had walked the direct 220 grid bearing and arrived 50 m north of the hut, they would have to guess which side to walk along. The aim-off removes the guess.

### Why this matters for IMS

Aim-off is one of the IMS / EMS habits that distinguishes a candidate from a strong candidate. The route card with aim-off bearings on the poor-visibility legs is the route card that produces a robust day. The assessor will probe with "how would you reach this hut in flat light?" and the answer that names the linear feature, the safe side, and the offset is the answer at working standard.
