### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to walk on a magnetic bearing in the field accurately, using intermediate aiming points, with a working drift target of less than 15 m per 100 m of travel.

### Why this matters

A bearing taken from the map and converted to magnetic is only useful if the candidate can hold it on the ground. The walking technique is what turns the bearing on the bezel into a route. Two habits make the technique reliable; without them, even an accurate bearing produces drift.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, the working accuracy target is 15 m of lateral drift per 100 m of travel. A 500 m leg should land within 75 m of the intended point. Better is achievable with practice; worse than this and the bearing was effectively useless.

### Holding the bearing

To walk on a magnetic bearing already set on the compass:

1. Hold the compass flat in front of you, at chest or waist height.
2. Rotate your whole body until the magnetic needle sits inside the orienting arrow on the bezel.
3. Read the direction the direction-of-travel arrow now points in the field.
4. Identify a feature (a rock, a tree, a snow feature, a cloud break) at the maximum visible distance in that direction.
5. Put the compass away.
6. Walk to that feature.
7. Repeat from step 1.

The "intermediate aiming point" technique is the single most important habit on this page. Walking with the eyes on the compass is unreliable because the body drifts whenever the eyes leave the path. Walking with the eyes on a fixed feature ahead is reliable because the body tracks the target naturally.

### Picking the aiming point

A good aiming point is:

- on the bearing,
- at the maximum visible distance,
- distinctive enough to recognise on approach (not "that grey rock among many grey rocks"),
- and stable (a cloud break is a poor aiming point because it moves; a rock is a good one because it does not).

In open snow with no features, the candidate's options are reduced. Two backup strategies:

- A **partner** sent forward on the bearing as a moving aiming point. Useful in poor visibility (and is the basis of the pole-and-cord technique covered in D8.2).
- **Tracks in the snow** if the candidate is following a known line; not always available, and not always reliable in fresh snow or wind.

### Common drift causes

Three causes of drift are worth recognising:

- **Tilted compass.** A compass held off-flat lets the needle drag against the bezel and produces a wrong reading. Hold flat.
- **Body off-axis.** A candidate who reads the compass correctly but turns at the moment of stepping forward will drift. The body should square up before walking.
- **Leaning into the slope.** On a side-slope the candidate naturally leans uphill, which rotates the compass against the slope and shifts the needle. Hold the compass square to the horizontal, not square to the slope.

### Worked example

A candidate sets a magnetic bearing of 220 degrees on the compass (from D2.2 worked example, leg 1). On the ground at the col:

1. Holds compass flat at waist height.
2. Rotates body until the needle sits inside the orienting arrow.
3. Identifies a rock outcrop on the slope ahead at approximately 200 m distance, on the bearing.
4. Puts the compass away.
5. Walks to the rock outcrop.
6. At the rock, takes out the compass again.
7. Re-takes the bearing using the same procedure; identifies a new aiming point a further 200 m along the bearing.
8. Continues.

The candidate arrives at the planned destination (the hut at 2,450 m) approximately 30 m east of the intended point on a 1,500 m leg. Lateral drift: 30 m / 1,500 m = 2 percent, or approximately 2 m per 100 m. Well inside the 15 m per 100 m target.

### Exercise 1 - Practical drill in any open space

In any open space (a park, a beach, a flat field) at home, with a partner if possible:

1. Set a magnetic bearing on the compass (any bearing; for variety, use a bearing that is not a cardinal direction).
2. Mark your starting point.
3. Pick an aiming point at maximum visible distance on the bearing.
4. Walk 100 paces along the bearing using the intermediate-aiming-point technique.
5. Mark your end point.
6. With your partner (or by re-tracing), measure the lateral drift from the intended line.

Record:

- Starting bearing: __________
- Number of paces: 100
- Intended distance covered (estimate using your pace length): __________
- Lateral drift from the intended line (metres): __________
- Drift expressed as metres per 100 m: __________

### Why this matters for IMS

The 15 m per 100 m drift target is the working standard for IMS / EMS poor-visibility navigation. A candidate who can hold a bearing to that accuracy can navigate to a col, a hut, or a known waypoint in flat light. The intermediate-aiming-point technique is what the assessor will watch for during route narration; a candidate who walks with eyes on the compass loses points even if they happen to land in the right place.

After D2.3 the candidate has the integrated technique: take the bearing from the map (D2.1), convert it to magnetic (D2.2), walk on it (D2.3). The remaining D3 and D4 pages refine this with on-the-ground bearings, back bearings, resection, bearing shapes drills, and aiming off.
