### Learning aim

By the end of this page, you should be able to convert a barometric trend into a route-relevant decision using the IMS Nav Programme decision framework, apply the framework to hypothetical scenarios, and write decision criteria into a route card template that make the framework objective and pre-agreed.

### Why this matters

A trend without a decision is just a number. The IMS Nav Training Programme provides a framework that converts each trend category into a specific route action. The framework is not a rule; it is a decision aid that makes the action objective rather than subjective.

For an IMS / EMS candidate, pre-agreed decision criteria are the IMS / EMS-relevant habit. A criterion written before departure is a decision made calmly with full information; a criterion improvised under pressure during deteriorating weather is a decision made by adrenaline.

### The framework

The IMS Nav Training Programme decision framework (Quick Reference Card 4):

| Trend | Rate | Meaning | IMS action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rising** | Any | High pressure building; weather improving | **Proceed** with plan |
| **Steady** | More than 3 hours stable | Stable conditions | **Proceed** with plan |
| **Slow fall** | Less than 0.5 hPa/hr | Mild deterioration likely | **Note time. Monitor. Brief group.** |
| **Moderate fall** | 0.5 to 1 hPa/hr | Significant system approaching | **Consider early descent. Assess escape routes.** |
| **Rapid fall** | More than 1 hPa/hr | Fast-moving system; serious deterioration imminent | **Initiate escape route or shelter immediately.** |

The framework does not say "always abort on a moderate fall". It says "consider early descent and assess escape routes". The decision is still the candidate's; the framework provides the structured input.

### Pre-agreed criteria

A pre-agreed criterion is a decision condition written into the route card before departure. Examples:

- "Turn around at the col if barometric pressure has fallen by more than 2 hPa since departure."
- "Skip the summit attempt if the rate of pressure fall is moderate or worse at the col."
- "Initiate the southern escape route if pressure falls by more than 5 hPa during the day."

A pre-agreed criterion has three components:

1. A **specific trigger** (a measurable condition).
2. A **specific action** (turn around, skip summit, initiate escape).
3. A **named decision point** (where on the route the criterion is checked).

The criterion is set before the day and is non-negotiable on the day. The point is to take the decision out of the moment, where pressure (group expectation, sunk effort, weather optimism) distorts judgement.

### Worked example

A candidate is planning a tour with the following decision points:

- **Decision point 1: at the col at 2,720 m.** Pre-agreed criteria:
  - Pressure has fallen by less than 2 hPa since departure: continue to the summit.
  - Pressure has fallen by 2 to 5 hPa: proceed to the summit only if the rate is in the slow fall band; otherwise descend via the south route.
  - Pressure has fallen by more than 5 hPa: descend via the south route regardless of rate.

- **Decision point 2: at the summit at 2,980 m.** Pre-agreed criteria:
  - Pressure trend rising or steady: descend by the planned north route.
  - Pressure trend in slow or moderate fall: descend by the south route (which has more reliable bailout points).
  - Pressure trend in rapid fall: descend by the south route immediately.

The candidate carries this on the route card. At each decision point, the criteria are checked against the actual barometer reading and the actual time elapsed. The decision is then automatic.

If the trend at the col is moderate fall and pressure has fallen by 3 hPa, the criteria say: descend via the south route. The candidate does not need to think about it in the moment; the thinking was done before departure.

### Why this matters for IMS

The IMS / EMS assessor will probe with weather-deterioration scenarios during route narration. A candidate who refers to pre-agreed criteria from the route card and applies them calmly is at the working assessment standard; a candidate who improvises under pressure is not.

The criteria themselves are workbook-specific examples; the real ones are written for each tour with the specific terrain, escape routes, and weather context in mind. The point of D6.2 is the habit of writing them, not the specific numbers.
