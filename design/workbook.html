Domes, peaks, and plateaux as contour patterns.

Learning aim

Identify the three principal summit forms from contour spacing and shape alone, and predict where the steepest descent line will run.

A summit, on a map, is the smallest closed contour. What lies beneath that single ring tells you most of what you need to know about the descent — long before you see the snow.

Three archetypes recur. Read them in this order:

Dome
Peak
Plateau

How the contour spacing reads

  • Dome — concentric rings, evenly spaced. Predictable, every aspect skis similarly.
  • Peak — rings tightening rapidly toward the summit. Steepest line varies by aspect; read which side compresses first.
  • Plateau — a single broad contour with sub-summits. Navigation hazard in poor vis: no obvious fall line.

Summit shapes

Domes, peaks, and plateaux as contour patterns.

Learning aim

Identify the three principal summit forms from contour spacing and shape alone, and predict where the steepest descent line will run before committing to it on the ground.

A summit, on a map, is the smallest closed contour. What lies beneath that single ring tells you most of what you need to know about the descent — long before you see the snow. Read three archetypes, in order:

Dome — even spacing
Peak — tightening rings
Plateau — sub-summits

How the contour spacing reads

  • Dome — concentric rings, evenly spaced. Predictable, every aspect skis similarly. Watch the lower flank for a convex roll.
  • Peak — rings tightening rapidly toward the summit. Steepest line varies by aspect; read which side compresses first.
  • Plateau — broad summit area, often with multiple sub-tops. Navigation hazard in poor vis: no obvious fall line, easy to misread aspect.

DomeEven all roundNoneConvex roll on lower flank
PeakTight at top, looser belowMarkedAspect-driven loading on lee

Answer key (trainer mode)

Q1. Ten concentric ovals, spacing 22m → 18m → 14m → 10m → 6m. Dome with steepening lower flank.

Q2. Three contour rings on the SE side, one on the NW. Peak; NW is the steeper aspect — likely lee-loaded after a westerly storm cycle.

Trainer note — when running this with a group, draw the dome first with eyes closed; most students will spread the rings unevenly and reveal what they actually expect.
